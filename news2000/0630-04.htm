New Report Advocates 'Drilling Under Detroit' As Long-Term Solution to High Gas Prices
 
WASHINGTON - June 30 - Skyrocketing prices at the pump have left American families searching for solutions to high gas prices. While Band-Aid solutions and blame have been plentiful, we must instead examine long-term solutions. A new Sierra Club report, "Drilling Under Detroit: A Long-Term Solution to Gas Prices that Works," offers increased fuel economy as a real solution to stabilize gas prices.

" Rather than searching for a Band-Aid for high gas prices, the time has come to apply the cure: Make America's cars and Sport Utility Vehicles go further on a gallon of gas," said Dan Becker, Director, Sierra Club Global Warming and Energy Program.

"Drilling Under Detroit," shows that by utilizing existing technology, increase Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards would save more oil than we import from Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, United Arab Emirates, estimates of oil in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, and national offshore oil combined. The report also details the potential savings for millions of Americans who drive some of the most popular cars and SUVs, but pay more than they need to at the pump.

"The American people are tired of gas guzzling, polluting and price gouging," said Becker. "They're demanding that Detroit put modern technology on our cars and trucks to break OPEC's stranglehold on our wallets and the threat to our climate."

The last time fuel economy was raised significantly was 1975, when Congress responded to an oil crisis by instituting CAFE standards for cars and light trucks. The result is the most successful energy savings measure ever adopted. It was so successful, so quickly, that America's automobiles used less gasoline in 1980 than 1975.

CAFE standards doubled fuel economy and save 3 million barrels of oil every day. But because the standards have stagnated and Americans are driving more miles than ever before, demand for oil is at an all-time high and growing.

Numerous editorials have commented on the role of fuel economy with our high gas prices:

"We have the same fuel-efficiency standards in effect today as we did in the 1970s, when auto technology was much more crude. And we've taken advantage of a loophole in those standards by turning en masse to gas-guzzling light trucks and SUVs that make up an increasing percentage of our fleet." -Atlanta Journal-Constitution, June 23, 2000

"The reality is that we were overdue for an oil shock for a number of reasons, which have converged to put energy prices back in the news in a big way for the first time since the 1980s. Though automobiles are significantly more gas efficient than when the first oil shock of the 1970s struck, less-efficient light trucks represented only 20 percent of vehicles then. Along with sport utility vehicles, today they account for one of every two new vehicles sold." -Harrisburg Patriot-News, June 25, 2000

"Greater efficiency is the most obvious and most logical answer to an oil pinch. Rejecting it means not only exporting more American dollars but pumping ever-increasing quantities of pollutants and global-warming gases into the atmosphere." --Des Moines Register, June 17, 2000
