Nikitin Must Face New Trial - Again
Press Briefings and Reception on Thursday
 
WASHINGTON - July 19 - In a move believed to be unprecedented in post-Soviet Russia, the FSB (Russia's secret police and the KGB's succesor) has appealed the Supreme Court acquittal of environmentalist Aleksandr Nikitin to the full presidium of the Supreme Court.

Nikitin is in Washington, DC, where he had planned to celebrate his recent victory over charges of espionage with the international groups that had aided him throughout his trials, and to meet with U.S. officials about the continuing environmental problems in Russia.

Nikitin will be available to the press at three events on Thursday, July 20. In the morning, there will be a breakfast briefing at Radio Free Europe and a briefing for Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe in the Rayburn House Office Building. In the afternoon, Sierra Club will be giving Nikitin and the Bellona Foundation it's highest international honor, the Earthcare Award at a reception hosted by Congressman Sam Gejdenson.

8:30 am Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty breakfast briefing 1201 Connecticut Ave., NW Aleksandr Nikitin, Bellona, Amnesty International, Sierra Club and Union of Councils for Soviet Jews Contact: Asta Banionis, 202-828-7210

10:00 am Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe briefing (Helsinki Commission) Aleksandr Nikitin 2255 Rayburn Hoyse Office Building

5:30 pm Reception and Sierra Club Award Ceremony 2172 Rayburn House Office Building Congressmen Gedjenson (CT), Boehlert (NY), and Smith(NJ) Aleksandr Nikitin, Bellona, Amnesty International, Sierra Club and Union of Councils for Soviet Jews contact: David Willett, 202-675-6698

The Sierra Club and Amnesty International are extremely concerned for Nikitin and for the message this move by the FSB conveys about the prospects for democracy and environmental protection in Russia. Sierra Club, Amnesty International and the Union of Councils for Soviet Jews are hosting Nikitin's visit to Congressional and Administration officials in Washington this week.

Nikitin was arrested when he wrote chapters of a Bellona Foundation report detailing the environmental consequences of Russia's aging fleet of nuclear submarines. The FSB attempted to charge that Nikitin had divulged state secrets. he was first acquitted in a St. Petersburg court on December 29, 1999, a ruling that was upheld this April by a three judge Supreme Court panel. His new hearing is scheduled for August 2.

Bellona Foundation Press Release

Russian Secret Police never gives up - Nikitin faces new trial -- again.

Details: http://www.bellona.no/nikitin/

Nikitin case goes to Supreme Court Nikitin not informed about hearing date

July 19, 2000 -- After being rebuffed four times by various Russian courts, the Prosecutor General's office is trying again to convict internationally renowned environmentalist Aleksandr Nikitin.

In a surprise appeal received by Nikitin today, the Prosecutor General's office (Attorney General) requests that the case be re-investigated, after more than four years of investigations, harassment and legal proceedings. The prosecutors revisited Soviet-era laws to resuscitate this case.

Nikitin had been acquitted by the St. Petersburg City Court on December 29, 1999, and the verdict was confirmed on appeal on the April 17 this year by a three-judge panel of the Supreme Court. The City Court had previously criticized the investigators -- Russia's secret police and KGB successor FSB -- for shoddy work and rejected the indictment in October 1998. This verdict was upheld by the Supreme Court in February of 1999.

The verdicts in favor of the former submarine captain has been historical, as it vindicates his work of mapping the dramatic nuclear situation in North West Russia for the Bellona Foundation, against the treason charges of the state security apparatus.

The case started in October of 1995, when Bellona's office and Nikitin's apartment were ransacked. On February 6, 1996, the secret police stormed in early in the morning and arrested Nikitin, who had to spent the next 10 month in an FSB prison in St. Petersburg. Subsequently, the case was sent back and forth between courts and the FSB for further investigations eight times. Six court hearings, including two for procedural matters at the Constitutional and City Court level and the four mentioned above, dealt with the case.

The Supreme Court panel's verdict is a landmark step forward for the Russian legal system, as it evaluated the law rather than rubber-stamping the charges of the secret police. The Supreme Court also rejected the use of secret decrees and laws enacted after the alleged criminal activities had occurred.

Niktin's passport was returned to him only one month ago, after the Supreme Court verdict upholding his acquittal. He arrived in the United States last week to receive his Goldman award, often called the Nobel Prize for the Environment, awarded to him in 1997, when he was not allowed to travel to the ceremony.

This week, Nikitin will meet with a number of Members of Congress, State Department and Clinton Administration officials.

He learned about the appeal during a Wednesday meeting in Washington with Amnesty International, Sierra Club and the Union of Councils for Soviet Jews.

The Prosecutor General's appeal will be heard August 2, 2000. He claims the case should be sent back for further investigation. If approved, Nikitin will face many more rounds in court in this Kafkaesque process.
