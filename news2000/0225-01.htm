Greenpeace Stops Ship Bearing GMO Animal Feed to Britain
 
WASHINGTON - February 25 - Early this morning, Greenpeace successfully intercepted the bulk carrier Iolcos Grace carrying up to 60,000 tons of genetically modified soya into Britain. The vessel was boarded by Greenpeace volunteers off the coast of Anglesey in North Wales. The soy is being shipped by Cargill, one of the world's largest graintraders and purchasers of genetically modified (GMO) soybeans. Cargill's Liverpool mill is the only GMO soya mill in the UK.

"Europe has rejected GMO food, but the biotech industry is still trying to dump its unwanted cargo any way it can," said Charles Margulis, Greenpeace U.S genetic engineering specialist. "American farmers should know that GMO animal feed is considered ‘tainted goods’ on the world market."

Earlier this week, the UK supermarket chain "Iceland" announced that it would be selling only GMO-free animal products starting September 1. Recently, the UK’s largest supermarket chain, Tesco, stated that it would phase out its use of GMO animal feed. A December 10, 1999, letter from Tesco to Cargill and other grain dealers states, "It is clear to us that our customers are concerned about the use of GM crops for animal feed and they would like us to take steps to remove them.… Our target is the eventual elimination of GM ingredients from animal feed." The letter also notes that Tesco expects to secure a source of non-GMO soy "as the next Brazilian soya crop becomes available in Spring 2000."

Several of the UK's other top chains also have stated that they are moving to non-GMO animal feed. Europe's largest poultry producer, Bourgouin, teamed with the largest animal feed supplier, Glon-Sanders, last year to launch a line of non-GMO fed poultry.

"This ship is carrying a cargo that nobody wants," said Jim Thomas, GE campaigner with Greenpeace, "and most people would like to see it sent home. There is no demand for GMO crops and we resent this contamination of the food chain. On the other hand, we would welcome non-GMO soy from American farms."

Notes to Editors:

Greenpeace volunteers at the action can be contacted via the press office. The Iolcos Grace departed New Orleans, La., earlier this month and was heading for the Cargill GMO soya mill in Liverpool docks.

On February 11, 2000, a shipment of 30,000 tons of corn being shipped to Brazil was turned away as a result of a warning from Greenpeace that some of the grain onboard might include GMOs. The Brazilian authorities then announced that it would demand certification of all U.S. corn to stop illegal imports of GMO corn.
