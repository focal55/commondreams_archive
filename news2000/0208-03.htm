National Parties Raise Record $107.2 Million In Soft Money During 1999; Congressional Campaign Committees Triple Their Receipts Over 1995, According to Common Cause Study
 

WASHINGTON - February 8 - The national political party committees raised a record $107,210,420 during the 1999 calendar year - 81 percent more than the $59,161,380 they raised during the last comparable presidential election period in 1995 - according to a Common Cause analysis released today.

Congressional campaign committees of the national parties raised more than three times as much soft money during 1999 than they raised during 1995, $62.0 million compared to $19.4 million.

Overall, national Republican party committees raised $57,811,386 in soft money contributions, while national Democratic party committees raised $49,399,034 in soft money in 1999, according to the study.

According to Common Cause, the national party committees are on pace to break all soft money fundraising records for an election cycle. During the 1996 campaign, Democratic and Republican national party committees raised a record $260 million in soft money, much of it used for "issue advertisements" designed to aid the presidential campaigns.

"The record sums of special-interest soft money raised by the national parties is further evidence of a fundamental disconnect between our political parties and the voters," Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger said. "If the presidential primary results in New Hampshire are any indication, the American people are tired of seeing their democracy sold to the special interests for millions of dollars in soft money contributions. Unless something is done to stop the flow of corporate and union money into the political system, we will likely see a repeat of the 1996 scandals, and the further erosion of the people's trust in our governmental institutions."

CONGRESSIONAL CAMPAIGN COMMITTEES POST BIG GROWTH
The national congressional campaign committees accounted for much of the growth in soft money contributions since 1995. Republican congressional campaign committees - the National Republican Senatorial Committee (NRSC) and the National Republican Congressional Committee (NRCC) - raised $32.0 million in soft money during 1999, double the $15.4 million raised in 1995. The Democratic congressional campaign committees - the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee (DSCC) and the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee (DCCC) - raised $30.0 million during 1999, more than seven times the $4.0 million raised during 1995, according to Common Cause.

"The incredible growth in soft money raised by the congressional committees exposes the fiction that soft money is not used to support federal candidates running for office," Harshbarger said. "The very purpose of these committees is to elect House and Senate candidates to office and the activities they conduct clearly reflect this."

Both of the Senatorial committees have set up joint fundraising committees with Senate candidates facing competitive November elections. These joint committees raise both hard and soft money contributions with hard money proceeds split between the candidate and the party committee, while soft money proceeds go to the party committee. For example, according to DSCC reports filed with the Federal Election Commission and analyzed by Common Cause, the New York Senate 2000 joint fundraiser between the DSCC and Hillary Clinton's Senate campaign took in more than $660,000 in soft money contributions from October to December of 1999. Similar joint fundraising committees between the national parties and candidates have already been established for Senate races in California, Florida, Michigan, Missouri, Pennsylvania, and Washington.

SOFT MONEY
Under current law, corporations and labor unions are prohibited from making contributions in connection with a federal election, while individuals and political action committees (PACs) are subject to federal limits. Hard money means contributions that are legal under federal law for federal elections. Soft money means contributions made outside the limits and prohibitions of federal law, including large individual or PAC contributions and direct corporate or union contributions.

The Common Cause study is based on national political party committee reports of their non-federal, or soft money, accounts from January 1 through December 31, 1999 filed with the Federal Election Commission (FEC). Totals do not include transfers between party committees, bank loans or interest, offsets to operating expenditures, or other business-related receipts.
