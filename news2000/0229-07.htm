WWII Landmine Survivors Join in Appeal to Clinton to Lead in Global Ban
 

WASHINGTON - February 29 -

News Advisory:

WHO:
- WWII Veteran John Wack, a de-miner in the 39th Combat Engineering Unit, lost his leg in Italy in 1943.
- WWII Vet Duane Robey, 415th Infantry Regiment 104th "Timberwolf" Division, lost foot in Germany on Dec. 31, 1944.
- Jerry White lost right leg below the knee during his junior year abroad in Israel on April 12, 1984 and serves as newly elected chair of the U.S. Campaign to Ban Landmines.
- Ken Rutherford lost both legs on a humanitarian mission in Somalia on Dec. 16, 1993.

White and Rutherford co-founded the Landmine Survivors Network, co-recipient of the 1997 Nobel Prize for Peace awarded to the International Campaign to Ban Landmines.

WHAT:
Appeals signed by 1,000 landmine survivors from 12 heavily mine-affected countries ask President Clinton to actualize his vision for a time when families and neighbors can "Walk the Earth in Safety. " Survivors urge President Clinton to take one last courageous step in his Presidency and ban anti-personnel mines in 2000. "As landmine survivors ourselves we cannot feel healed and we cannot feel secure until the scourge of landmines is eliminated."

WHEN:
10:30 a.m., Wednesday, March 1. The Mine Ban Treaty came into force as international law on March 1, 1999. 137 countries have joined.

WHERE:
Press Briefing Room No. 226, United Nations, New York City

DETAILS:
"Today we speak on behalf of hundreds of thousands of brother and sister landmine survivors. We call on all nations to eradicate anti-personnel mines so that everyone in the world can walk without fear. Someone steps on a mine every twenty-two minutes. Between today and President Clinton's last day in the White House, over 21,000 individuals will be murdered or maimed by a mine," said White.

"Landmines are equal opportunity weapons-they do not discriminate or know who is friend or foe. Landmines do not recognize armistices or peace treaties. Secreted deep in the earth, they continue to kill and maim for years after wars have been forgotten and battles won or lost," noted Rutherford.

"After the war, I went to Harvard University and enjoyed challenging and fulfilling work until I retired in 1992. I have a wonderful wife, eight children, and eleven grandchildren. Sometimes I recall the field just North of Florence where I lost my leg and I think I have had a fulfilling life, but how much easier it might have been with my leg," commented Wack, who had been a de-miner in Italy in 1943.

Duane Robey recalls New Year's Eve, 1944. A big 21-year-old guy, 6 feet, 200 pounds, from Minnesota, Robey was on patrol waiting to rush across the road and leap into the German position on the Roer River front. "I moved into position to wait for the signal. I saw a slight rise in the snow. The other six in my unit had stepped over this spot; my subconscious screamed DON'T STEP THERE! Too late! WHOOMP! I was thrown forward and my face plowed into the snow-covered ground. Dazed, I rolled over on my back...I sat up and saw my mangled foot against my shin...The boot was gone and my lower leg felt sticky and wet. I saw my foot was gone. I could hear nothing but the falling snow. I did not make a sound...The Lieutenant called the mission 'aborted. Pick Robey up and head for our lines.' Four guys carried me. Quietly I said, 'For God's sake, don't anyone step on a mine.' WHOOMP! I was thrown upwards. I recall sharp pain in my back, then there was blackness," recalled Robey.

"U.S. silence in the Landmine Ban reminds me of the quiet, listening to the falling snow in 1944. Danger covered us like a blanket. It was so unreal and eerie," said Robey. He hopes Clinton will "stand tall and stop the use of anti-personnel mines. Make the promise of the landmine ban real. My foot has been gone for fifty-five years, a lifetime, Mr. President. Your mission as Commander-in-Chief is drawing to a close. One act of courage that will be your legacy is to ban mines forever. You will honor hundreds of thousands who have met their mine and offer a lifeline to future generations who will 'walk the earth in safety' as you yourself have said."

The Survivor Action Appeal is only one of many survivor initiatives to ban landmines and provide peer support and assistance to others. Other initiatives include:

-- "Surviving Limb Loss" - Just published: 10 fact-based Peer Support Pamphlets designed to educate amputees and families on what to expect, what to do, and how to resume active, productive lives. The series is being translated into several languages for use by survivors around the world.

-- "Survivors Rehabilitation Database" (www.lsndatabase.org) - Developed in partnership with UN Mine Action Service and the UN Office for Project Services, the database profiles rehabilitation services available to mine victims worldwide.

For more information on the Landmine Survivors Network, visit www.landminesurvivors.org. For a copy of the letter sent to President Clinton, contact Diane Murphy at 202-662-1280.
