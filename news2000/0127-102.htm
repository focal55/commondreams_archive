National Parks Group Sues to Block Garbage Dump; Bureau of Land Management Charged with Endangering Joshua Tree National Park
 
WASHINGTON - January 27 - The National Parks Conservation Association (NPCA) sued the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) today in the U.S. District Court for the Central District of California for promoting the establishment of the nation's largest landfill on the border of Joshua Tree National Park.

"If constructed, the Eagle Mountain landfill will bring up to 20,000 tons of garbage per day to the doorstep of Joshua Tree National Park, and do so for 117 years," said Brian Huse, NPCA's Pacific Region Director. "I can think of no greater insult to our nation's natural heritage." Despite strong opposition from the National Park Service, BLM transferred public lands neighboring the park to the dump developers and issued rights-of-way that allow garbage to be hauled by train and truck across other BLM lands critical to the survival of the threatened desert tortoise.

BLM ignored the dangers the dump poses to its national park neighbor, asserts NPCA's attorney. "Had the BLM done an honest job of assessing the impacts these deals will have on Joshua Tree National Park, the project would never have made it off the drawing board," said Deborah Sivas, of Earthlaw. "BLM has fundamentally ignored its responsibility to the public trust."

The proposed Eagle Mountain dump would be surrounded by Joshua Tree National Park wilderness. Established in 1936, Joshua Tree protects some of the most pristine desert habitat in the world. So unique is its flora and fauna, it is recognized as a United Nations Biosphere Reserve. The dump is expected to degrade this sensitive desert region, undermine wilderness protection, jeopardize the desert tortoise, spoil views, and shatter the natural quiet. "The National Parks System is considered the crowning conservation achievement of our nation, and Joshua Tree is one of the jewels," said Huse. "BLM's actions will not only tarnish Joshua Tree, but also the entire park system."

The specter of Eagle Mountain has loomed over Joshua Tree for more than a decade. The site, a former iron mine owned by Kaiser Steel, has remained dormant since 1983. Rather than reclaim the mine site, as required by law, Kaiser is attempting to turn the mine site and more than 2,000 acres of pristine desert into what could be the nation's largest landfill.

To be profitable, Kaiser and its subsidiary the Mine Reclamation Corporation required additional land beyond the mine site. BLM agreed and recently transferred 3,500 acres of public land surrounding the mine to the developers in exchange for several smaller Kaiser-owned parcels. In addition, BLM granted rights-of-way for the transport of garbage across even more public land.

NPCA is asking the Court to invalidate the environmental analysis of the project, acknowledge that BLM has violated its own regulations, and block the land exchange and rights of way.

The National Parks Conservation Association is America's only private nonprofit citizen organization dedicated solely to protecting the U.S. National Park System. "Protecting Parks for Future Generations," NPCA was founded in 1919 and today has more than 400,000 members. Earthlaw is a public interest, non-profit environmental law group dedicated to defending the West's natural resources. A library of national park information, including fact sheets, congressional testimony, position statements, press releases and media alerts, can be found on NPCA's World Wide Web site at http://www.npca.org.
