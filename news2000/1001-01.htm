Mass Slaughter of Forgotten Whales Begins Again in Japan
 
WASHINGTON - October 1 - Despite consistent criticism from the International Whaling Commission, Japan continues to kill more than 20,000 small whales, dolphins and porpoises each year. Japan's annual slaughter of thousands of these "forgotten whales" begins this week in Japan, even as the US government threatens Japan with sanctions for hunting sperm and Bryde's whales under the guise of scientific research.

The dolphin hunting season starts on October 1st , with fishermen in two regions of southern Japan -- Wakayama and Shizuoka -- legally permitted to kill dolphins and small whales in shockingly brutal hunts.

The species targeted include the world's best known dolphin -- the bottlenose dolphin -- along with striped dolphins, spotted dolphins, Risso's dolphins, false killer whales and short-finned pilot whales.

Footage from a dolphin hunt last year was widely circulated at a recent meeting of the International Whaling Commission in Adelaide, Australia. The Japanese delegation strongly opposed any discussion of the methods used to kill small whales, dolphins and porpoises, but was unable to prevent the showing of the video in a separate room during the meeting. The video shocked both pro- and anti-whaling delegates and the Japanese delegation left the meeting in protest, although later admitted on camera that killing methods had to be improved.

With the hunt season opening today it remains to be seen whether any action to reduce the suffering of these "forgotten whales" will be taken, however EIA believes only a complete suspension of the hunt will suffice. Detailed research has shown none of the six species targeted in these hunts are known to have healthy populations that can withstand continued hunting.

EIA Director Allan Thornton said "According to Japanese scientists, striped dolphins in Japan's coastal waters are highly endangered, bottlenose dolphins are threatened, and short-finned pilot whales are rare. There is insufficient data for the other species to even hazard a guess as to their status. The US government has recently done an excellent job at calling attention to the fraudulence of Japan's so called "scientific whaling" for sperm and Bryde's whales. But attention must also be given to these "forgotten whales" before Japan's hunting drives them to extinction."

EDITOR'S NOTES:

1. The Environmental Investigation Agency (EIA) is a non-profit environmental organization with offices in Washington DC and London, England. Set up in 1984, EIA works to expose environmental crimes including illegal trade in endangered or threatened species, illegal logging and timber trade and the illegal trade in ozone-depleting substances.

2. The Government of Japan allows up to 22,000 dolphins, porpoises and small whales to be killed by hunters each year. The status of each population is either threatened, endangered, or unknown.

3. There are no national or regional government enforcement inspectors to ensure compliance with the catch limits set by the Government of Japan's Fisheries Agency. Fisheries co-operatives merely report the number of dolphins and porpoises that have been killed to the regional prefecture governments in Japan, which in turn pass them on to the Fisheries Department. There is no physical inspection of the catches by the Government.

4. There is a flourishing large-scale fraudulent trade in small cetacean products, which are often sold as whale meat thereby vastly inflating their value. The Government of Japan has never acted to counter this widespread fraudulent trade nor has it enacted meaningful enforcement of catch limits or other supposed controls on the commercial hunting of dolphins, porpoises and small whales around its coasts.

5. The United States Marine Mammal Protection Act prohibits the hunting, capturing or killing of dolphins, porpoises and small whales. Thirty four nations are signatories to the Council of Europe's Bern Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife which both outlaws "the indiscriminate killing" of cetaceans and " the use of means capable of causing local disappearance of, or serious disturbance to populations of a species." New Zealand and Australia have similar legislation.

6. The continued unsustainable exploitation of small cetacean populations in coastal waters of Japan contradicts the Government of Japan's frequently stated claim that it pursues a policy of 'sustainable utilization of marine resources'.

7. Dolphins, porpoises and small whales have no legal protection under Japanese law to prevent them being hunted to extinction or killed in the most indiscriminate and brutal manner.

8. Members of the Japanese public and press are generally unaware that such hunts occur in Japan and are often shocked to discover that such large numbers of dolphins, porpoises and small whales are still killed.
