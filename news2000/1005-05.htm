McCain Ads to Close Gun-Show Loophole, Offer Challenge to George W. Bush
 
WASHINGTON - October 5 - The following was released today by Handgun Control Inc.:

Sen. John McCain's appearance in two TV ads supporting the ballot initiatives in Colorado and Oregon issues a special challenge to Gov. George W. Bush.

The two initiatives are based on the language of the Lautenberg amendment, passed by the Senate on Vice-President Gore's tie-breaking vote in May of 1999, shortly after the Columbine tragedy. However, the Colorado and Oregon bills are actually stricter in their definition of a gun show; any venue in which 25 or more firearms are sold would be included under these measures. On May 18, 1999, The Boston Globe reported that Gov. Bush said that his vice-president would not have voted for the Lautenberg measure, implying that he would not have supported the same bill that Sen. McCain is now supporting.

Gov. Bush's campaign reiterates its support for an "instant-check" at gun shows. This is dishonest. The Lautenberg Amendment used the existing federal law on "instant-check" as defined in 18 USC 922(t) and applied it to sales by unlicensed sellers at gun shows. Licensed dealers, both in their stores and at gun shows, currently abide by the current law, including the provision allowing for three business days for problems. Under the current "instant-check" used by licensed dealers, 95 percent of purchasers get their firearms within two hours. According to the FBI it is the last five percent who are most likely to be prohibited purchasers - felons, the mentally ill, and those with a domestic violence restraining order. The three-day period, which is not a waiting period but simply the amount of time the FBI has to check suspect purchasers, is now the standard for all sales by federally licensed firearm dealers, whether at gun stores or gun shows. The Colorado and Oregon initiatives would apply this standard to all sales at gun shows, including those by so-called 'private collectors.'

At the time of the Senate debate on this issue, the Republican Senate leadership (and Sen. McCain) supported an alternate measure, sponsored by NRA Board Member and U.S. Sen., Larry Craig, which would have provided only "voluntary" background checks at guns shows. Gov. Bush also supported the NRA-backed Dingell amendment in June of 1999 that would have provided an inadequate time frame of only 24 hours to conduct background checks on problematic purchasers. Had this been the law for licensed firearm dealers, the ATF estimates that approximately 17,000 prohibited purchasers would have had their firearms sales approved in a year, due to the lack of time to perform an adequate background check. It is this phony "closing of the gun-show loophole" that the Bush campaign supports -- unless they offer evidence to the contrary.

Today's Denver Post explains Sen. McCain's reasons for publicly supporting the two initiatives. "Since (the Senate vote) he said in the interview, he's been swayed by a Treasury Department report showing that nearly all background checks take just minutes, and that the delays often turn up old convictions. Now, he says, 'the 72-hour situation isn't nearly as important to me as preventing sellers from selling to criminals.'"

"Senator McCain has shown courage and just plain common sense in offering his voice in support of these measures," said Michael Barnes, president of Handgun Control, Inc. "This year, we have seen Republican leaders like Governors Pataki and Owens support sensible gun laws. We have seen pro-gun candidates like George Allen in Virginia and Bill McCollum in Florida flip-flop and attempt to hide their pro-gun votes. Senator McCain's public support demonstrates that the Republican party does not have to be owned and operated by the NRA."

"We challenge Governor George Bush to come to the same conclusion."

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, was founded in 1974 by Dr. Mark Borinsky and N.T. "Pete" Shields, two victims of gun violence. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works with law enforcement, public health, religious, and community groups across the country to strengthen and protect federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban all guns. HCI has more than 500,000 members and supporters nationwide, making it the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. More information about HCI and its sister organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on our website at http://www.handguncontrol.org.
