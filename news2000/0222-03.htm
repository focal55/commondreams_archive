New California Peace Action, National Priorities Project Report Raises Questions for Candidates
 
SAN FRANCISCO - February 22 - California Peace Action and the National Priorities Project (NPP) announced the release of NPP's State of California 2000.

The State of California 2000 is part of a nationwide release, which includes individualized reports for all 50 states. The national State of the States 2000 report will be released in mid-March.

The report measures how the people of California are faring in five areas: economic security, education, the environment, health care and housing. It tracks the changes in need and federal expeditures in these areas over a 20-year period and juxtaposes this analysis with a critique of excessive federal spending on the Pentagon.

In California, the level of need exceeds the level of federal spending committed to meet it, according to the National Priorities Project. For exaxple, 2.2 million California children lived in poverty in 1998, up from 947,000 in 1980. At the same time, the federal Head Start program receives funds sufficient to serve only one-third of the children eligible in California.

The report also gives information specific to local cities ranging from San Diego to San Francisco. It points out that while the child poverty rate for California as a whole is 23.6 percent the rate for Los Angeles is 33.7 percent. Other reported facts about California include:

-- 87 percent of schools are in need of significant repair;
-- 7.4 million had no health insurance in 1998, up from 4.7 million in 1987;
-- 1 million California residents drink polluted water.

A proposed six year, $100 billion increase in Pentagon spending will take more money from California programs, according to the report. The increase would include the purchase of the F-22 fighter jet program and more nuclear submarines. According to Lawrence Korb, former assistant secretary of defense under the Reagan administration, these weapons systems are designed to fight a war with a kind of technologically advanced enemy that does not exist.

"It's easy for candidates to say they're committed to education,," said Danielle Babineau, California Peace Action's Southern California political director. "But the fact is, if you're committed to education, health care, housing or the environment, then you cannot continue throwing our tax dollars at unnecessary weapons programs. This is what we mean when we ask elected officials'What are your priorities?"

The National Priorities Project is a privately funded, research and education organization that offers citizens and community groups tools and resources to shape federal budget priorities which promote social and economic justice. For more information about NPP, visit our website at www.natprior.org.

California Peace Action is the state affiliate of a nationwide grassroots citizens' lobby that works to abolish nuclear weapons, stop US weapons sales to dictators and end wasteful miltiary spending. Previously known as SANE/Freeze, the organization has a statewide membership of 33,000 and offices throughout California.
