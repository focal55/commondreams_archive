Texas Kicks Off Opening Day Of Ozone Exceedance Season
 
WASHINGTON - April 17 - Despite Governor George W. Bush's green claims, Texas' air is worse than ever before. On Friday, April 14, Houston suffered its first one-hour ozone exceedance of 2000. And with inaction from the Bush administration and a long, hot summer ahead, the mess in Texas may get a whole lot worse.

Houston broke the national ozone health standard of 125 parts per billion with a reading of 142 ppb on Friday. This is the earliest Houston has ever had such a high rating. Last year, Houston passed Los Angeles as the nation's smog capital, with the Texas city racking up 52 days of violations.

"Texas' air has never been this dirty, this early. Governor Bush's failure to curb air pollution forces Texas families to breathe dirty, unhealthy air that burns their lungs," said Dr. Neil Carman, air scientist, Lone Star Sierra Club. "Our children should be able to play outside, but dirty air can worsen their asthma. Instead of leadership, all the Bush administration does is further pollute the air with false claims."

Today, Bush's Lieutenant Governor Rick Perry and environmental appointees held a press conference to tout so-called cleaner air in Texas. This is similar to a report issued last year by the right-wing Foundation for Clean Air Progress, who cited reduced emissions of pollutants from 1970 to 1996. That report drew the following response from Gregg A. Cooke, Regional administrator, U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, Dallas:

"Certainly, there has been progress toward better air quality since 1970. Cleaner burning cars were identified as one factor... However, a more direct and meaningful way to assess air quality improvement is to measure the quality of the air we breathe. By looking at data from air monitors, we can see that some air quality problems are persistent in Texas. The trends are not encouraging...Ozone is a particularly vexing air quality problem in Texas. Knowing the health impact that ozone has on people with asthma and other respiratory problems, we cannot give up," Cooke wrote in March, 1999.

Cooke's words, written before Houston became the nation's smog capital, proved to be prescient. Problems have only gotten worse for the over 400,000 kids in Texas who suffer from asthma. Additionally, Texas ranks first in a number of troubling environmental categories:

Toxic releases to the environment;
Total toxic air emissions from industrial facilities;
Toxic chemical accidents;
Cancer-causing pollution; and
Number of factories violating clean water standards.
