After Shootings, Gun Lobby and GOP Candidates Try to Talk the Talk About Guns
 
WASHINGTON - March 3 - The following was released today by Handgun Control Inc.:

The recent shootings in Michigan and Pennsylvania have again thrust the issue of gun safety into the national spotlight -- much to the dismay of the National Rifle Association and the pro-gun presidential candidates, who are now being forced to deal with a problem which they don't want to talk about.

As a result, the NRA, Gov. George Bush and Sen. John McCain are in full "spin cycle," using smoke-and-mirrors public relations to claim to be championing gun safety legislation to deflect their poor records on the issue of gun safety. At the same time, what the NRA and these pro-gun candidates say and do on the issue of gun control are two completely different things entirely.

In response to the heavy media coverage of the shootings in Michigan and Pennsylvania, the NRA has toned down its typical anti-gun control rhetoric and has now expressed its alleged support for reasonable gun control laws designed to keep firearms out of the hands of children. Ironically, the NRA's new public relations campaign follows eight months of aggressive lobbying Congress to defeat the package of common-sense gun control amendments to the juvenile justice bill, including proposals closing the gun show loophole, requiring mandatory safety locks for all new guns sold, and banning the possession of semi-automatic assault weapons for juveniles under age 18.

While the "new" NRA and recent gun control converts George W. Bush and John McCain have belatedly come out in support of stronger gun control laws to help protect children, they've got a short memory about their long-standing positions on the proposals they are now claiming to support.

The same NRA that now proclaims that they "support and encourage the distribution of safety locks," last year opposed a Senate floor amendment that simply would have required the sale of a safety lock with every handgun sold by a licensed dealer. Believe it, or not, only a few months ago NRA top lobbyist James J. Baker called the defeat of the safety lock amendment, "a great victory for public safety." In arguing against this common-sense proposal, the NRA uses its familiar scare tactics to convince gun owners that these laws will lead to house-by-house inspections.

This irresponsible and dishonest talk mirrors the misinformed talk of the "trigger lock police" from Governor George W. Bush in last night's Republican presidential debate. While our children continue to be killed by guns that are improperly stored, both the Texas Governor and the NRA continue to use a message of fear to scare the public into believing that these laws are enforced on the gun owner rather than on the gun manufacturer, which is the intent of the proposal.

And, while it now claims to want to do more to protect children from firearms, the NRA has consistently dismissed, and even actively lobbied against, efforts to make guns "childproof," or "smart guns" that cannot be fired by unauthorized users. To underscore its opposition to this innovative safety proposal, it recently launched a radio ad campaign in Washington, D.C. and several other media markets mocking President Clinton's call to provide funding to expand research and development efforts for "smart gun" technologies. The ad, which features a President Clinton impersonator, parodies the television show "Get Smart," with a fake agent saying the "smart gun" proposal "doesn't sound very smart to me."

Just this morning on the Today Show, NRA Executive Vice President Wayne LaPierre said that, "if you have young children in the house, you better lock up your guns and you better make them inaccessible. And if you're criminally negligent, you should be charged and we support that."

Maybe Wayne ought to inform his lobbyists in Washington about the NRA's "new" position. For many years, the NRA has fought laws to hold adults criminally responsible for improperly storing of firearms so that they are not accessible to children, called Child Access Prevention (CAP) laws. Right now in Ohio, in fact, the NRA is fighting a CAP bill that would make it a crime for an adult to leave a loaded firearm where it is accessible by children, even going as far as calling the legislation the "(Governor) Taft Burglar Protection Act."

Their efforts haven't been limited to Ohio. The NRA has lobbied against CAP legislation across the country. Just this year, they have fought it in New Hampshire. They have fought CAP in Rhode Island, Washington and Indiana too. Here again, the powerful gun lobby uses dishonest rhetoric to imply that these laws would require people to lock up their guns all of time, even if there were no children in the house, and would also make families vulnerable to crime. A 1997 study published in the Journal of American Medical Association found that state safe storage laws are particularly effective at reducing gun injuries and deaths among children.

The NRA came to the Today Show to promote its message of enforcement over stronger laws and express its newfound interest in the safety of our children, but it failed to mention that, in its zeal to defeat all new gun control laws, no matter how reasonable, they have helped to make it easier for children and criminals to get guns. In December, the NRA led the fight in Illinois to reduce the penalty for illegally carrying a gun from a felony to a misdemeanor, and recently defeated a proposal in Utah that would have strengthened penalties for violent gun misdemeanants. Last April in Missouri, the NRA spent more than $4 million in an unsuccessful attempt to pass a referendum that would have allowed people convicted of violent misdemeanors, including stalking and child molestation, to carry a hidden handgun almost anywhere in the state.

Talk is cheap. After each horrible shooting in America, the NRA talks about its support for laws making it more difficult for children and criminals to get guns. Then they fight them when lawmakers try to pass these laws.

When John McCain talks about his support for new gun safety laws, he doesn't mention that he voted against the Brady bill, the assault weapons ban, mandatory background checks for all sales at gun shows, even legislation banning the manufacture, sale and importation of undetectable plastic guns. When George W. Bush talks about protecting children from guns, he doesn't want to tell people that he opposed closing the gun show loophole in his state that still allow children and criminals to easily access guns, and signed into law one of the nation's most lax concealed-carry laws that allow permit holders to carry hidden handguns into most public places, even churches and amusement parks in Texas. Governor Bush also won't tell you how, in June 1999, he signed into law a bill that preempts Texas cities and counties from suing the gun industry to recover damages.

Until the "Johnny-come-lately's" on gun control -- NRA, Governor Bush and Senator McCain -- put their money where their mouths are, the American people can expect nothing less than business-as-usual from our pro-gun "friends."

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, is the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works to enact stronger federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban handguns. Founded in 1974, HCI has more than 400,000 members nationwide and works with local groups around the country to enact and protect reasonable gun control laws. More information about HCI and its affiliated organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on our Web site at http://www.handguncontrol.org.
