Youth Group Fights Bush Social Security Privatization
 
WASHINGTON - May 15 - Arguing that a safety net for all generations is more important than investment accounts, 2030 Action, an advocacy organization for young adults, launched a campaign to educate the public about the destructive potential of Gov. Bush's Social Security privatization and tax cut schemes.

As Gov. Bush delivered a key speech in Southern California outlining his principles for Social Security privatization, an airplane towing a banner sponsored by 2030 Action circled the skies above. It read: "DON'T PRIVATIZE SOCIAL SECURITY, WWW.2030.ORG".

Hans Riemer, Director of 2030 Action (a 501(c)4 non-profit, non-partisan organization affiliated with the 2030 Center, founded in 1997), made the following statement in response to the Bush speech:

Bush wants funds that should go into Social Security to go into the stock market instead. So where will he get the money to continue paying Social Security benefits? He doesn't say. In truth, benefits will have to be cut and the retirement age will have to be increased. And the cuts will be biggest for today's young workers. But guaranteed benefits for all generations are more important than private accounts.

Now is the time to put Social Security on sound financial footing for the future. If we reduce the debt and credit the savings to Social Security, we can guarantee solvency-and full benefits for all workers-until the year 2050. Making the payroll tax fairer by applying it to all income up to $150,000, instead of just $75,000, as now, would get us to 2065. Why gamble on privatization and a colossal tax cut instead?

Young workers could get slammed by Bush's plans. Social Security privatization on top of a huge tax cut risks a budget nightmare-a massive Social Security bailout-that would make the deficits of the 80s and early 90s look like petty change. Our actions throughout this campaign will demonstrate our concerns.

In response to the Bush speech, 2030 Action mobilized activists in California and Pennsylvania, and joined a coalition event in Washington calling on Bush to "sign the pledge" to protect Social Security (www.signthepledge.com). Following are several key arguments designed to clarify debate about the future of Social Security and its impact on young workers:

ECONOMICS OF SOCIAL SECURITY AND YOUNG WORKERS

The core idea of privatization is that some workers will stop paying their full Social Security contribution, and invest instead in the stock market. While the idea has superficial appeal, it courts disaster for younger generations.

-- If young workers stop paying into Social Security so they can invest in stocks, somebody has to pay the piper. Despite reducing Social Security's income dramatically, Bush promises no cuts for current and "near" retirees. If these obligations were kept, a "modest" privatization plan like Bush's would cost an extra one trillion dollars over the next 10 years (Center on Budget and Policy Priorities). Of course, Bush has already spent the budget surplus on a colossal tax cut.

-- Privatization risks a massive government bailout-financed by today's young workers. A plan like Bush's has only a 60 percent chance of remaining solvent under "ideal conditions" (Employee Benefits Research Institute). If economic projections turn out differently, the entire house of cards will collapse-just as the boomers retire-and today's young workers get the bill (benefit cuts, massive debt, and a shredded safety net).

-- Benefit cuts for today's young workers will be enormous. Investing one-sixth of payroll taxes into private accounts leaves Social Security at least 20 percent short on its benefits (CBPP). Since Bush does not propose cuts for current retirees, cuts will be much higher than 20 percent for today's young workers and the retirement age may also have to increase (Bush says he would consider raising the age).

-- Privatization can never match what Social Security provides-a rock solid guarantee that promotes economic security for everyone. Social Security is our most important safety net-it's not just for people who are lucky or know how to invest. Without Social Security, millions of seniors, people with disabilities, survivors, and children would live in poverty. Even with "average returns," many will lose under privatization-and it's not fair to "average out" the lives of real people.

PUBLIC OPINION ABOUT SOCIAL SECURITY AND YOUNG WORKERS

The 2030 Center, a 501(c)3 affiliate of 2030 Action, has conducted two polls with the national survey research firm Peter Hart Research Associates. Important findings from a recent poll:

-- Young adults support using the surplus to strengthen Social Security over paying for a tax cut. Young people want Social Security to be there for them: 59 percent prefer strengthening Social Security to providing a tax cut. Yet Gov. Bush's massive tax cut leaves no funds for Social Security.

-- Young people place more importance on providing guaranteed benefits than receiving a "higher rate of return" on their payroll taxes. Young people say that "making sure that people receive a decent, guaranteed monthly retirement benefit" is a higher priority than "making sure that people receive a better rate of return" (55 percent vs. 39 percent). Yet Gov. Bush would trash the safety net for privatization.

-- Privatization's benefit cuts are very unpopular with young people. While some are attracted to the idea of private accounts in isolation, they strongly oppose the reductions in Social Security's guaranteed benefits that are required if young people stop contributing fully to the Trust Fund. 76 percent of young Americans oppose making a 20 percent reduction in guaranteed monthly benefits, and 81 percent oppose raising the eligibility age for receiving benefits. Gov. Bush must explain how he will finance promised benefits.
