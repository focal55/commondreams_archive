Florida Supreme Court Yesterday Gives Powerful New Grounds for Seminole Appeal
Ruling Would Put Gore in the Lead Regardless of Pending U.S. Supreme Court Decision

 
TALLAHASSEE - December 12 - While the world waits for the U.S. Supreme Court's decision in the recount controversy, the Florida Supreme Court could still change the vote count and put Vice President Gore in the lead.

Attorney Gerald Richman filed supplemental papers today to the Florida Supreme Court in the appeal of the Seminole County voter fraud case. The "Notice of Supplemental Authority" filed today is based on yesterday's Florida Supreme Court clarification. This is a major new grounds for victory on appeal in the Seminole County case.

"The court's decision in the Palm Beach case is a huge boost for our chances to win on appeal," said Richman. The Florida Supreme Court yesterday emphasized that courts are unable to diverge from the plainly articulated intent of the legislature. "It is very clear in our case that the legislature in 1998 intended to provide for strict compliance with Florida's election requirements, especially those pertaining to absentee ballots," he added.

Earlier yesterday before the U.S. Supreme Court, Joseph P. Klock, counsel for Secretary of State Katherine Harris, argued that voters must follow precise instructions in order for their vote to be legally counted. Klock argued "the fact is that a legal vote at the very basics has to at least be following the instructions you are given…" This also provides important support to the argument we are making in the Seminole County case. Voters who failed to include voter registration numbers did not follow the instructions they were given and the law makes clear these should not be counted as legal votes.

The appeal is based on the judge's error in determining a series of actions to be illegal but unintentional. The key issue in this case is that election supervisor Sandra Goard arranged for GOP operatives to alter absentee ballot requests. Numerous similar Democratic requests were rejected. Florida law requires that only a voter, a family member or guardian may complete all information on the absentee ballot application.

The Seminole County Board of Elections separated rejected absentee ballot applications and gave special privilege to Republicans. Republican requests that were incomplete or incorrect received ballots, resulting in almost 2,000 additional Republican votes being cast.
