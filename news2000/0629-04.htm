Jubilee 2000 Bicyclists Complete Cross-Country Journey
 
LOS ANGELES - June 29 - As leaders of the industrialized world continue to debate the merits of debt cancellation for the planet's most impoverished nations, three Anglican priests (or vicars, as they are known in Great Britain) are winding up their tour across the heartland of the U.S. to educate Americans on the urgent need for change.

The bicyclists will be at two main events in Los Angeles, their last stop before boarding a plane for the G-8 Summit of world leaders in Okinawa, Japan.

1) Sunday, July 2, 10:15 am -- a presentation at All Saints Episcopal Church in Pasadena. In Sweetland Hall, 132 N. Euclid Avenue. Contact: Chris Burdette Parr, 626-583-2762 x734

2) Monday, July 3, 5:00-8:00 p.m -- Informal picnic. Hancock Park, in Los Angeles (behind LACMA -- Los Angeles County Museum of Art -- corner of 6th and Ogden (one block north of Wilshire) (picnickers will gather near the small amphitheatre) Contact: Rev. Diane Kenney, 323-933-5179

"The cycle ride is to help publicize the campaign to highlight the struggle of many impoverished countries that are burdened by unpayable debts to richer countries and financial institutions," said rider Nick Howe.

The campaign is inspired by the scriptural idea of the Jubilee Year: periodically, every 7 x 7 years or 50 years, debts are forgiven and slaves set free, and "liberty is proclaimed throughout the land". Jubilee is a time to apply self-righting mechanisms for restoring balance to society.

Economists say cancellation of the debt would present virtually no financial hardship to industrialized nations since all of the debts are due to be repaid over a long period, 20 years or more. The burden of cancellation could be spread over an identical period -- over 20 years, this would cost each citizen of the industrialized nations less than four dollars per year.
