Bush Debate Environmental Fictions Vs. Facts
 
WASHINGTON - October 12 - Gov. George W. Bush made a number of misleading and inaccurate statements about his environmental record and other issues during the debate last night in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. The following are some of his misleading statements from the transcript of the debate as printed in the New York Times (10-12-00), and the real facts.

* "Our water is cleaner now." FALSE: The discharge of industrial toxic pollution into surface waters in Texas increased from 23.2 million pounds in 1995 to 25.2 million pounds in 1998, the last year with data available. (Toxics Release Inventory, Environmental Protection Agency, May 11, 2000)

* "We're [sic] reduced our industrial waste by 11 percent." FALSE: First, Gov. Bush is probably referring to the oft-stated assertion that air pollution in Texas has declined by 11 percent. This assertion is incorrect. It is based on a Texas Natural Resources Conservation Commission (TNRCC) calculation that emissions of six criteria air pollutants from industry fell by 11% during the last five years. EPA statistics dispute this number, instead showing a net increase in emissions of these six criteria pollutants. The TNRCC statistics are misleading because large amounts of the claimed reductions are simply a change in reporting methodology for NOx emissions -- giving the appearance of reductions of almost 100,000 tons, which are reductions on paper only. In fact, emissions of NOx, the key ozone smog precursor emitted by industry, has increased during the last five years.

* "The electric decontrol bill that I fought for and signed in Texas has mandatory emission standards." MISLEADING: In fact, Governor Bush initially opposed any mandatory cleanup of 68 grand fathered power plants in S.B. 7. His position changed after the US EPA Region 6 letter of May 3, 1999 informing Texas that $1.3 billion in federal highway funds could be withheld as a sanction due to noncompliance with ozone health standards in the Dallas-Ft. Worth area. Bush finally agreed to support these mandatory reductions under pressure from Dallas-Ft. Worth elected officials such as State Rep. Steve Wohlens, who chaired the House Committee with jurisdiction over electric utility deregulation under SB 7.

* Mandatory emission reductions are "what we ought to do at the federal level when it comes to grant [sic] for other plants, for utilities." MISLEADING: As a candidate for President, Gov. Bush has not proposed mandatory pollution reductions from aging power plants. Instead, he has said: "I don't think you can litigate clean air and clean water. I don't think you can legislate clean air and clean water." (Seattle Post-Intelligencer, 8-12-00) This philosophy would preclude such mandatory emission reductions.

* "They took 40 million acres of land out of circulation without consulting local officials... I just cited an example of the administration just unilaterally acting without any input." FALSE: Gov. Bush is referring to the Administration's pending proposal to protect 40 million acres of roadless areas in our national forests from additional road building and logging. In fact, the Forest Service conducted 600 public meetings about the proposal nationwide and more than one million Americans urged the Administration to strengthen the proposal. There was ample opportunity for local officials and others to comment on the proposal, which has not been issued in final form yet.

* "China and India were exempted from that [Kyoto] treaty." FALSE: China and India are not exempt from the Kyoto Treaty to reduce global warming pollution. Under the agreement, both countries are required to report their emissions. In fact, both nations have committed to reducing their global warming pollution. China has erased its emissions growth and may have actually reduced its emissions.

* "I just found out the other day an interesting fact, that there's a national petroleum reserve right next to Prudhoe Bay that your administration had opened up for exploration in that pristine area." MISLEADING: The National Petroleum Reserve in Alaska was set aside specifically for potential oil and gas production. Last year, the Administration opened a portion of the NPR-A to leasing and development, being careful to prohibit drilling in environmentally sensitive areas, such as the Colville River Delta and Teshekpuk Lake. The NPR-A lies far to the west of the Arctic National Widllife Refuge and is separated from the coastal plain by the Prudhoe Bay oil fields. Bush would like to open the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge to oil drilling even though it contains many sensitive areas with unmatched ecological diversity. The coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge is home to polar bears, caribou, musk oxen and other rare species.
