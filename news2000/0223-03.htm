New Study Reveals Surprising Data on Air Quality: Air Pollution in the Midwest is Worse Than in the Northeast
 
CINCINNATI - February 23 - Scientists and concerned citizens gathered today for simultaneous press briefings in three Midwest cities -- Cincinnati, Ohio; Marietta, Ohio; and Huntington, W.Va.

They were there to release a new report titled "Ohio Valley -- Ozone Alley, a comprehensive study on smog pollution and power plants in the Ohio River Valley and what can be done to clean them up.

The "Ohio Valley-Ozone Alley" report reveals that contrary to popular opinion, air pollution can be worse in the Midwest than it is in the Northeastern United States. A Harvard University researcher reports that parts of the Ohio River Valley typically experience health-damaging ozone smog more hours per year than Northeast cities, even though peak pollution levels are higher on the East Coast. This increased health risk correlates with a higher per capita rate of hospital admissions in the Ohio River Valley due to respiratory disorders. According to a recent study conducted by Abt Associates, communities in the Ohio River Valley had up to 17 percent more hospital admissions during the 1997 summer smog season than cities such as Boston or New York.

"It's not the peaks of ozone that do the most damage; it's the sustained exposure," noted Dr. George Leikauf, professor of environmental health at the University of Cincinnati. "By that measure, the Ohio River Valley truly is a unique alleyway of high ozone."

The report also establishes for the first time that emissions from older-coal and oil-fired power plants in the Ohio River Valley are the major source of the problem. Power plants in the Ohio River Valley emit more than 1 million tons of nitrogen oxide (NOx) per year, a primary culprit in ozone pollution.

"We knew that power plants are a big factor in Midwest air pollution, but this study surprised us with just how big it really is," says Dr. Sandy Sillman, a professor of atmospheric studies at the University of Michigan and a contributor to the report. "As much as 50 percent of ozone pollution in the Ohio River Valley is caused by power plants in the region."

Authors of the report maintain that utilizing up-to-date pollution controls on some plants and coverting others to newer, cleaner technologies would dramatically improve air quality not only in the Northeast, but also in the Midwest and South. Nitrogen oxide emissions could be reduced by adding off-the-shelf control technologies to smokestacks, replacing coal-fired plants with cleaner natural gas plants, increasing the use of renewable energy, and investing in energy conservation.

"Despite the rhetoric on both sides, smog is not really an inter-regional fight," says Kurt Waltzer of the Ohio Environmental Council. "The pollution is in everyone's back yard and the Midwest stands to gain even more from cleaning up our region's older plants than the Northeast will."

The report was commissioned by the Ohio Environmental Council with primary funding from the W. Alton Jones Foundation. Research was conducted by scientists from Harvard University, the University of Michigan, and Abt Associates. The report will be posted online on Feb. 23. See www.theoec.org/ohio-valley or call 614-487-7506 to obtain a free copy of the report.
