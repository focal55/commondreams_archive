Sierra Club Blasts Senate for Vote Moving Toward Oil Drilling in Arctic Refuge
Clinton Veto Expected
 
WASHINGTON - April 6 - Despite a certain veto expected from President Clinton, Big Oil's Senate allies have advanced a backdoor attempt to exploit the coastal plain of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge for oil and gas development. A majority of Senators has threatened a treasured American landscape, home to grizzly and polar bear, wolves, migratory birds and caribou.

By the narrow margin of 51-49, the Senate tabled an amendment by Senator William Roth (R-DE) and Senator Barbara Boxer (D-CA) to strip out a provision in the FY 2001 Budget Resolution that assumed $1.2 billion in anticipated revenue from the from as yet unauthorized oil drilling in the coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge.

"Drilling the Refuge would be as shortsighted as damming the Grand Canyon for hydroelectric power or tapping Old Faithful for geothermal energy," said Sierra Club Legislative Director Debbie Sease. "Last month President Clinton said he would reject `drilling in the protected and treasured natural habitats of Alaska.' We hope he will follow through on that promise and veto any budget that contains Arctic drilling money. Rest assured we will continue to vigorously defend this American treasure."

The coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge is the last sliver (5 percent) of Alaska's North Slope not currently available for oil and gas development. It is currently off-limits to oil and gas development. But drilling advocates, led by Senator Frank Murkowski (R-AK) are using backdoor methods and red herrings like high gas prices to pass legislation to exploit the Refuge. Their latest avenue of attack is to include revenues derived from future Arctic lease sales in the federal budget bill.

The oil industry and their Congressional allies have seized upon drilling the Arctic as a way to lower oil prices. However, opening the Refuge would have no impact on prices at the pump. It is not clear that there is any economically recoverable oil in the Arctic Refuge at all. At best, the Refuge would yield only a six-month supply of oil. And it would take a decade before the first drop of gas made it to the pump.

"America can never drill its way to energy independence. We import more than 55% of our oil and America sits on less than 3 percent of the world's known energy reserves," said Melinda Pierce, Sierra Club Washington Representative. "The answer to the country's energy needs lies in energy efficiency and renewables, not beneath the surface of the coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge. This national treasure must not be sacrificed."

The coastal plain of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge is referred to America's Serengeti. Nestled between the towering mountains of the Brooks Range and the Beaufort Sea in northeast Alaska, the narrow 1.5 million acre coastal plain is the biological heart of this untamed wilderness. It is home to unique and abundant wildlife. The coastal plain is also the calving grounds of the 129,000 member Porcupine River Caribou Herd, which migrates over 400 miles each year to this same place to give birth to their young.

More information about Arctic drilling can be found at (www.sierraclub.org/wilderness/arctic/index.asp).
