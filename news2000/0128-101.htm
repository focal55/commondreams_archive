TV Preacher Kennedy Spreads Misinformation About Church And State
 
WASHINGTON - January 28 - Television preacher D. James Kennedy aired a nationwide television special Jan. 23, 2000, that attacked the separation of church and state. During the hour-long edition of his "Coral Ridge Hour," Kennedy maligned the concept of church-state separation numerous times, calling it, among other things, "one of the greatest deceptions of the 20th century."

Kennedy's analysis was full of standard Religious Right distortions about the separation of church and state and contained numerous inaccuracies. This document, prepared by Americans United for Separation of Church and State looks at some of Kennedy's biggest mistakes.

Kennedy asserted that the separation of church and state was intended to bar only the establishment of a national church.

RESPONSE: No serious scholar of the Constitution or the Bill of Right believes this. The historical record and the debate over the wording of the First Amendment show clearly that the amendment was intended to prevent government from endorsing or promoting specific religious denominations or religion generally. Early drafts of the First Amendment that specifically barred only the establishment of a national church were rejected as too weak. Clearly, the Founding Fathers had something more far-reaching in mind, which is why the First Amendment says in part, "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion...."

Kennedy said that James Madison, the acknowledged Father of the Constitution, believed the First Amendment was designed only to bar a national church.

RESPONSE: This claim is false, as Madison's actions and writings prove. As president, Madison vetoed two bills that he said would violate the First Amendment--a measure giving federal land to a church and a proposal to officially incorporate an Episcopal church in Washington. In his veto messages, Madison specifically stated that he believed both bills would run afoul of the First Amendment. Madison would not have made these statements if he believed all the First Amendment did was bar the establishment of a national church. In writings after he left office, Madison made clear his support for church-state separation and an expansive interpretation of the First Amendment.

Kennedy stated that, "Nowhere does the Constitution mention a separation of church and state."

RESPONSE: The literal words "separation of church and state" do not appear in the Constitution, but the concept is firmly ingrained therein. Thomas Jefferson spoke of the First Amendment as creating a "wall of separation between church and state." James Madison wrote that the First Amendment created the "total separation of the church from the state." The phrase "religious freedom" appears nowhere in the Constitution, yet the document surely grants that concept. So it is with separation of church and state.

Kennedy said that Thomas Jefferson's views on the First Amendment are not important since he was in France when the Bill of Rights was drafted.

RESPONSE: The First Amendment was clearly influenced by Jefferson's "Statute for Religious Liberty," which he wrote and helped make law in Virginia. James Madison, who was Jefferson's protégé, took Jefferson's ideas of religious liberty nationwide through the First Amendment. Jefferson's ideas were so powerful that he did not need to be in the room during the debate over the First Amendment. In fact, Jefferson's ideas and beliefs still influence America today, more than 170 years after his death.

Kennedy said that the U.S. Supreme Court applied the doctrine of separation of church and state for the first time in 1947.

RESPONSE: This is simply incorrect. There were other church-state cases at the high court before 1947. In one of them, 1879's Reynolds v. United States, the court spoke favorably of Jefferson's "wall of separation" metaphor, saying it "may be accepted almost as an authoritative declaration of the scope and effect of the amendment...."

Kennedy interviewed David Barton, who stated that Jefferson's 1802 letter to the Danbury (Conn.) Baptists, which contains the famous "wall" metaphor, was intended only to state Jefferson's belief that the church should be protected from the state.

RESPONSE: David Barton is a "Christian nation" propagandist whose fallacious view of early U.S. history has been definitively debunked by several legitimate scholars. The Danbury Baptists wrote to Jefferson because they were angry that they still had to pay taxes to support the Congregational Church in Connecticut. It is clear from the context of Jefferson's reply that he wrote to agree with their concerns and to state his belief that government had no business promoting religion or taxing citizens to pay for religion. Jefferson frequently used letters to make policy statements, and he told his attorney general that he planned to use the response to the Danbury Baptists to make a major statement of his views on church-state relations.

Kennedy insisted that the Founding Fathers wanted the Ten Commandments to be the foundation of our government.

RESPONSE: If this is what the Founders wanted, why isn't it in the Constitution? The Ten Commandments tell believers, among other things, not to worship false gods, not to make graven images, not to take the Lord's name in vain and to keep the Sabbath holy. However, the Constitution includes none of these provisions. Instead, the Constitution guarantees religious freedom to everyone--Christian and non-Christian--and bars all "religious tests" for public office. Claims that the Founders based our government on the Ten Commandments are commonly heard from Religious Right leaders, but they are based on inaccurate history. (For example, many in the Religious Right point to an alleged quotation from Madison that says, "We have staked the future of all of our political institutions upon the capacity...to control ourselves, to sustain ourselves according to the Ten Commandments of God." This quote is false. Madison scholars agree that it is completely unsubstantiated by the historical record.) The Founders gave us a secular republic, not an officially "Christian nation," as even a casual reading of the Constitution and the debate surrounding the Bill of Rights proves.

Kennedy asserted that the Supreme Court's decisions have removed God from public schools. "God was removed and metal detectors were brought in," he said.

RESPONSE: Kennedy is ill-informed about the long history of religion in public schools and the conflict mandatory prayer and worship sparked in many states. Nor does he seem to be aware of the string of state courts that struck down coercive school devotions in the 19th century, holding that they were incompatible with state constitutions.

Kennedy seems to believe that public schools were better in the 1940s and '50s when mandatory prayer existed in many states. Yet graduation rates for those periods were dismal, the number of students who went on to college was a fraction of what it is today. Furthermore, public education at that time was racially segregated in many states. It certainly was no golden age.

* * * * *

D. James Kennedy is a Religious Right propagandist, not a scholar. While Kennedy holds several degrees, they are from seminaries and religious institutions and do not qualify him as an expert on U.S. history or constitutional law. His analysis should be viewed with extreme skepticism.

It is also worth noting that Kennedy uses attacks on church-state separation to raise money. Three times during his Jan. 23 broadcast he appeared on camera to appeal for donations. His Ft. Lauderdale, Fla.-based Coral Ridge Ministries Media is a successful fund-raising operation, taking in $28.3 million in 1997.

Kennedy also urged his viewers to sign a petition asking the Supreme Court to reverse course on the separation of church and state. He did not tell his flock that such petition drives are a waste of time, as the high court is required to make its decisions on the basis of what the Constitution says, not what one group of people may want or even demand. If our civil liberties were left up to the whims of mobs, we would have few rights indeed.

Americans United is a religious liberty watchdog group based in Washington, D.C. Founded in 1947, the organization represents 60,000 members and allied houses of worship in all 50 states.
