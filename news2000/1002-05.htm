Tens of Thousands of Americans Unite to End Gun Violence on First Monday 2000
 
WASHINGTON - October 2 - Attorney General Janet Reno, U.S. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development Andrew Cuomo, talk show host Rosie O'Donnell, Senator Frank Lautenberg, and attorneys general, mayors, police chiefs, college and graduate school students, moms, doctors, lawyers, social workers, civic leaders, and thousands of others joined forces across America today to take a stand for common sense gun laws. First Monday 2000: Unite to End Gun Violence is a national day of education and action, and the beginning of a two-year campaign to stop gun violence.

Events were scheduled for 50 major cities and 300 colleges and schools of law, medicine, nursing, public health and social work. The first Monday in October is a day when Americans traditionally focus on justice, as the Supreme Court begins its new term. First Monday 2000 is coordinated by the Alliance for Justice, in conjunction with Physicians for Social Responsibility.

The centerpiece of every event is the powerful new documentary, "America: Up In Arms," by award-winning filmmakers Rory Kennedy and Liz Garbus. It focuses on three people who lost their lives to guns: robbery victim Songha Willis of Philadelphia, who died at age 28; Kenzo Dix of Berkeley, who died as the result of an accidental shooting at age 15; and 19-year-old Sean Woods of Houston, who committed suicide using a handgun.

"In every one of these stories, I saw my own children and grandchildren, and the danger to all American families," said actor Martin Sheen in closing the film. "That's why First Monday 2000 is so important. We're fighting the gun lobby by proposing simple, sensible solutions."

"First Monday 2000 is generating a new wave of activism for common sense gun laws," said Alliance for Justice President Nan Aron. "Ten children die each day in the U.S. from gun violence. A new generation of activists is sending a message that lawmakers can't ignore. We want guns regulated more closely than tires, and the gun industry scrutinized more closely than the entertainment industry. We are determined to stop gun violence."

Attorney General Reno and Secretary Cuomo helped announce the campaign in July, along with Members of Congress from both political parties. "It is easier in this society to buy a gun than it is to buy a McDonald's hamburger," Secretary Cuomo said in discussing the issue. "There are seven times more gun dealers than there are McDonald's -- 12,000 McDonald's; 84,000 gun dealers. We have to do something about it."

"The First Monday 2000 campaign will help mobilize Americans to stop the scourge of gun violence. I am proud to support this important campaign," Senator Edward Kennedy said last week.

"Gun violence in the United States is a true public health epidemic," said Robert K. Musil, PhD, Executive Director and CEO, Physicians for Social Responsibility. "It's time to save lives, re-set our priorities, and hold the gun industry accountable."

First Monday 2000 co-sponsors include the National Education Association, American Academy of Pediatrics, American Bar Association, American Public Health Association, Children's Defense Fund, Family Violence Prevention Fund, Million Mom March, National Association of Children's Hospitals and Related Institutions, National Association of Social Workers, National Black Police Association, and United States Student Association.
