Environmental Issues Key to Moving Independent and Undecided Voters in Presidential Contest
 
WASHINGTON - October 16 - As the presidential race rounds into the home stretch, polling analysis released today shows that clean air and water issues could be key to moving Independent and undecided voters in what is expected to be a photo-finish presidential race. While Independent and undecided voters are relatively uninformed about the distinctions between presidential nominees Vice President Al Gore (D) and Texas Governor George W. Bush (R) on environmental issues, they move away from Bush and toward Gore in significant numbers when presented with each candidate's record.

According to the national poll commissioned by the League of Conservation Voters (LCV) and conducted by Peter D. Hart Research Associates, the issue of clean air and water is very important to 74 percent of Independents, forming a swing block of 14 percent of all voters. A strong majority of these clean air and water Independents (60 percent) believe environmental laws do not go far enough and should be strengthened, higher than Independents in general (52 percent) and Democrats (49 percent).

"All signs in the presidential election say that we are in for a photo finish," said pollster Peter Hart. "In an election in which the candidates are looking for new toe holds, the environment is likely to be a key issue in persuading two constituencies-Independents and undecided voters. These groups care intensely about the environment as an issue and when they learn the positions of Al Gore and George W. Bush, they move significantly away from George W. Bush and to Al Gore."

According to the poll, clean air and water is very or fairly important to 86 percent of voters, rating just below education (93 percent) and health care (89 percent). Although clean air and water rated higher in importance than both the economy (83 percent) and cutting taxes (71 percent), 37 percent of voters said they did not know enough to distinguish between the environmental positions of the two candidates. More importantly, 51 percent of Independents who say clean air and water are very important are unable differentiate the environmental records of Bush and Gore.

At the outset of the survey, Gore led Bush by a 44 to 41 margin with 60 percent of voters saying they trusted Gore on environmental issues and only 46 percent saying they trusted Bush. After learning about the records of the candidates, 63 percent said they trusted Gore, while 42 percent said they distrusted Bush on the environment. In the final head-to-head match-up, Gore widened the margin, beating Bush by 47 percent to 39 percent. Most of the movement came from Independent and undecided voters who are motivated by environmental concerns, in particular clean air and water.

"With three-quarters of Independent voters saying that clean air and water are very important, the Gore campaign is well-served to expose Governor Bush's polluting record in Texas and to highlight the fact that Bush's 'polluter-friendly' approach to clean air and water policy would jeopardize 30 years of American environmental progress," said Deb Callahan, LCV president. "When they know the facts about his poor environmental record, Independent and undecided voters are unwilling to risk voting for Bush."

According to the polling analysis, there is very little change in how partisan Democrats and Republicans say they will vote after hearing about the candidates' environmental positions. However, Hart said that the poll reveals by moving Independent and undecided voters "the environmental issue could take Al Gore from a neck-and-neck race to a solid front-runner position."

The national survey of 1,023 likely voters (including an oversample of 225 voters in several battleground regions) was conducted Sept. 13-17, 2000, by Peter D. Hart Research Associates for the League of Conservation Voters. The margin of error is plus-or-minus 3.5 percent for a sample this size and higher for specific subgroups. Survey analysis and polling questions can be found on-line at www.lcv.org/presidential/.

The League of Conservation Voters is the political voice for the national environmental and conservation community. LCV was the first national environmental group to endorse Gore for president on May 30, 2000. For more information about the environmental positions of the presidential candidates, visit http://www.lcv.org.
