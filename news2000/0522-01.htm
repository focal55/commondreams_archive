In New TV Ads, Texans Testify On Bush's Pollution Policies
Airing in Michigan, Missouri, Ohio and Wisconsin; Texans Condemn Unhealthy Air and Polluted Water
 
WASHINGTON - May 22 - The Sierra Club today released a new TV ad running in key states that examines Texas Governor George W. Bush's record on fighting air and water pollution. The ad includes testimony from a mother from suburban Houston and an angler from rural Texas.

"We have too many children on breathing machines, too many people with asthma. We are in a crisis situation," said Tamaro Maschino from Seabrook. She opposes granting a permit to a new chemical plant that would add more pollution to Houston's already smoggy skies. She has joined with other Republican mother from Seabrook to oppose the permit.

The ad will run in St. Louis, Milwaukee, Detroit, and Lansing this week. And in Ohio next week. The ad asks viewers to contact Texas Gov. George W. Bush to urge him to oppose Federal legislation to weaken penalties for clean air and water violations.

Ed Parten, an avid angler from Kingwood, Texas is also featured. He and other anglers have become increasingly concerned about Texas' increasing water pollution and the effect it's having on their sport.

Beta and VHS copies are available by calling Jon Schneider at 202-675-2383.

Below is the ad script and documentation:

Sierra Club-- Haddow Communications TV :30-- Final "Texas Testimonials" Script

VO: Ed Parten: "All of our lakes -- all have water quality problems..." (CG: Ed Parten, Kingwood, Texas)

Documentation: Recently released EPA data found that Texas continues to rank third in the nation for the discharge into lakes and rivers of industrial toxic pollution. In fact, the discharge of toxics into surface waters increased by nearly 20 percent, to approximately 25 million pounds in the most recent Toxics Release Inventory for 1998. -1998 TRI, Environmental Protection Agency, released May 11, 2000

Documentation: Pollution has to some degree impacted all of Texas's 15 inland river basins and 8 coastal basins, several of its reservoirs, and all of its estuaries, coastal wetlands, and bays. -Texas Natural Resources Conservation Committee, Water Quality Inventory

VO: Narrator: Texas leads the nation in the number of factories violating clean water standards. (EPA Compliance Data) (CG: Texas leads in factories violating clean water standards, EPA Compliance Data)

Documentation: Texas leads the nation in the number of facilities that violate clean water standards. A February 2000 report by the U.S. Public Interest Research Group based on EPA data found that Texas had the most major facilities in "significant noncompliance" with pollution discharge limits in their permits. Between October 1997 to December 1998, 178 facilities were in significant noncompliance, compared to 141 facilities in Florida (ranked 2nd). This was 25% less than Texas. --"Poisoning Our Water: How the Government Permits Pollution," US PIRG, February 2000

Documentation: EPA data for January 1995 through March 1996, indicates that 22 percent of all major municipal wastewater treatment and industrial facilities were in significant noncompliance with clean water permits, making Texas first in the nation for the number of major facilities in significant noncompliance. --Clean Water Network, A Prescription for Clean Water: How to Meet the Goals of the Clean Water Act, p20

VO: Tamara Maschino: "We have too many children on breathing machines, too many people with asthma... (CG: Tamara Maschino, Seabrook, Texas)

Documentation: Texas has 401,289 cases of pediatric asthma, while New York with an almost identical population has only 338,844 cases; nearly 16% less. -- Incidence of Lung Disease by Lung Association Territory--American Lung Association, April, 1999

VO: Narrator: Texas has the most industrial air pollution in the nation. (CG: Texas has the most industrial air pollution in the nation. EPA latest Toxic Release Inventory)

Documentation: Texas continues to have the most industrial air pollution in the nation. According to the latest data from EPA, released on May 11th, the emission of industrial toxic air pollutants emissions rose from 108 million pounds in 1997 to approximately 110 million pounds in 1998. The next closest state was Louisiana, with 75 million pounds of industrial toxic discharges into the air. This is nearly 50% less than Texas. -1998 TRI, Environmental Protection Agency, released May 11, 2000

Documentation: As of May 17, 2000, Houston has already three days that violated clean air health standards. "Houston's ozone levels have surpassed federal requirements three times so far this year, including April 17, when it had the highest one-hour ozone level in the nation."

--Associated Press, April 27, 2000

Documentation: For the majority of Texans, air quality has substantially diminished under the Bush Administration. Two-thirds of Texans-- 12 million people-- live in the state's top eight urban areas. From 1990-94, they suffered 508 eight-hour ozone violations, from 1995-99 under Bush, this jumped to 679 violations, a 34% increase. --Ozone data from the Texas Natural Resource Conservation Commission website, City of Houston's Bureau of Air Quality, NESCAUM's website on US ozone data, US EPA AIRS database.

VO: Tamara: We're in a crisis situation.

Tamara Maschino is from Seabrook, Texas, which is in the Houston metropolitan area. She is very concerned about Houston's horrible smog problems, which ranked first in the nation last year according to TNRCC data. She is opposed to the state's plan to further increase emissions in the Houston area by issuing three new emissions permits to American Acryl, a new chemical plant located in Pasadena, Texas, a suburb of southwest Houston. The permits are expected to allow the plant to emit about fifty toxic chemicals into the air, which would add pollution equal to 9000 cars. Local residents called on Gov. Bush and the Texas Natural Resources Conservation Commission to conduct a study of the cumulative effects of this additional toxic pollution. So far, the government has refused to do so. --Associated Press, April 27, 2000

VO: Ed: We've contacted George Bush's office and he has the attitude he's not interested.

Documentation: Ed Parten is an expert angler who has participated in numerous fishing tournaments. He is involved in efforts to protect water quality in Texas. Mr. Parten has worked to educate the public about the TNRCC's proposal to down grade water quality in Lake Sam Rayburn. He has discussed water pollution issues with John Howard of Gov. Bush's staff, and the latter indicated little interest in addressing water pollution problems in Texas.

VO: Narrator: Call George Bush. Tell him to oppose legislation in Congress that weakens penalties for clean air and water violators. For our families, for our future. (CG: Tell George W. Bush to oppose S. 1053 and H.R. 3570. Call 512-463-2000. Paid for by Sierra Club.)

Documentation: S. 1053 is sponsored by Sens. Kit Bond R-MO, Gramm R-TX, Hutchinson R-TX. This bill would weaken the sanctions provisions of the Clean Air Act of 1990 (signed into law by President George Bush). Under existing law, communities suffering from unhealthful levels of air pollution cannot receive federal highways funds for new highway construction that would add to congestion, sprawl and smog until they had taken steps to reduce existing levels of air pollution, and account for the increase of new pollution caused by the new highways. (Federal funding for highway safety measures are exempt from this sanction.) If this sanction is lifted, it would remove incentives for cities to adopt clean air plans, and encourage them to build highways that make problems worse.

Documentation: Last year, Texas state officials lobbied the Texas Congressional delegation to support this bill. -Boston Globe, December 17, 1999

Documentation: H.R. 3570 is sponsored by Rep. Steve LaTourette R-OH. It would delay and weaken Clean Water Act enforcement against municipal waste treatment facilities that. allow the discharge of raw sewage into rivers and lakes. This bill would create an incentive for communities to ignore their sanitary sewer overflow problems. These overflows threaten human health and the environment.
