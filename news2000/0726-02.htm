Agreement in NC May Lead to Change in National Hog Industry
Smithfield Foods, Attorney General Agree to Phase Out Open Air Lagoons Within Five Years
 
RALEIGH, NC - July 26 - An agreement reached yesterday by Smithfield Foods and North Carolina Attorney General Mike Easley could lead to a chance in how the hog industry across the country manages its waste. Yesterday's announcement was applauded by a range of North Carolina groups including Environmental Defense, the Southern Environmental Law Center (SELC), and the North Carolina Chapter of the Sierra Club. North Carolina has led the nation in the explosive growth of factory-styled hog operations.

Smithfield, the world's largest pork producer, agreed to fund a two-year, $15 million North Carolina State University study to test alternative waste treatment technologies on a commercial scale and $50 million over 25 years for an environmental fund. Under the agreement, this will lead to a phase-out of open air lagoons. Once a determination is made of which alternatives meet environmental, economic and other standards, Smithfield is required to convert to these alternatives on its North Carolina farms within three years from that date.

"What happened in North Carolina today may have major national implications down the road," said Ed Hopkins, Sierra Club Senior Washington Representative. "We look forward to an industry-wide switch to improved technology which will result in cleaner air and water in North Carolina and around the country."

Second only to Iowa, the state has an estimated 10 million hogs located in a handful of counties in the eastern part of the state.

"Mike Easley has gone where the General Assembly has feared to tread," said Molly Diggins, state director of the North Carolina Chapter of the Sierra Club. "Easley has taken the most concrete step to date to break the impasse over the future of animal waste lagoons. We hope the legislature will follow Easley's lead and require environmentally sound waste systems for all large livestock operations."

The vulnerability of the lagoon and sprayfield system was highlighted last year when Hurricane Floyd deluged the eastern part of the state with its worst ever natural disaster. State regulators estimate that over 180 hog farms are located in the 100 year floodplain. Media accounts of the disaster highlighted hogs trapped on the roofs of hog houses and hog carcasses floating downstream.

The agreement applies to hog farms owned by Murphy Farms, Carroll's Foods, Brown's of Carolina, and Quarter M Farms, all subsidiaries of the Virginia-based Smithfield. Although the hundreds of hog farmers who produce hogs for these companies under contract are not covered by the agreement, Smithfield has agreed to provide its contract growers with financial and technical assistance to enable them to convert to alternative systems.
