Array of Groups Alert Voters their Rights Are At Risk If Supreme Court Nominations Move Majority Far Right
 
WASHINGTON - October 2 - While the U.S. Supreme Court opened their 2000-01 session just across the street, a group of national leaders and lawmakers came together today to sound an alarm for voters: The President you choose on Nov. 7 may end up choosing Supreme Court Justices who could drastically curtail or abolish Americans' fundamental rights for many years to come.

At a news conference with the Court as the backdrop, Voters For Choice President Gloria Steinem and People For the American Way President Ralph G. Neas were joined by leaders of major national civil and constitutional rights, abortion rights, labor, environmental, and gun control organizations and by national lawmakers in announcing their plans to mobilize millions of voters across the country to consider the Court when they cast their ballot.

The Supreme Court is widely recognized as among the highest stakes in this presidential year. Most of the current Justices are at or past the age when most Americans retire, and Court-watchers are predicting that the next President could have the chance to nominate as many as three out of the nine Justices. It has now been over six years since a new Justice was named to the Court, the longest interval between appointments in 177 years. With the Court narrowly divided and already edging toward the far right's position on some issues, George W. Bush's statements that he would model his nominations after the Court's two farthest-right Justices, Clarence Thomas and Antonin Scalia, are especially troubling.

"The 2000 elections are the most important elections for choice in two decades because the Supreme Court hangs in the balance," said Steinem. "The next President will likely appoint two to three Supreme Court Justices. After the Roe v. Wade decision, anti-choice leaders implemented a strategy to stack the Court with anti-choice Justices. The result: today's Court is very divided. Right now, three Justices support the right to choose, three Justices consistently oppose the right to choose, and three Justices are somewhere in the middle. Just one or two more anti-choice votes could overturn Roe v. Wade."

"In this election there are three critically important issues," said Neas. "The Supreme Court, the Supreme Court, and the Supreme Court. The Court is the issue that makes this the most important election in two-thirds of a century. The Christian Coalition and the rest of the Radical Right know it. That's why they're mobilizing their voters and we have to do the same thing."

Joining Steinem and Neas at today's news conference were speakers from the NAACP, The Feminist Majority, American Federation of Teachers, Americans For Democratic Action, Human Rights Campaign, United Auto Workers, Handgun Control, the National Education Association, the National Gay and Lesbian Task Force, Sierra Club, NARAL, the Interfaith Alliance, and the American Association of People with Disabilities.

National lawmakers, including Reps. Stephanie Tubbs Jones, D-OH, Bernard Sanders, I-VT, Frank Pallone, D-NJ, Louise Slaughter, D-NY, Major Owens, D-NY, and Del. Eleanor Holmes Norton, D-DC, took time during the closing days of the Congressional session to join PFAW and Voters For Choice in kicking off their fall campaign urging voters to take seriously the future of the Supreme Court when choosing the next president in November.

Neas noted that the current Court, led by its right wing, has already begun turning back the clock by curtailing Congress' ability to protect American's rights. Neas and the other speakers urged voters to consider, before casting ballots on Nov. 7, the future Court's impact on fundamental rights ranging from free speech and religious liberty to the ability of workers and minorities to seek and win justice.
