Sierra Club Cheers Monument Recommendations
Salmon Runs, Native American Cliff Dwellings, Wildlife Habitat To Be Protected
 
WASHINGTON - May 31 - WASHINGTON-- U.S. Department of the Interior Secretary Bruce Babbitt today recommended that President Clinton protect four majestic landscapes as National Monuments, earning ringing applause from the Sierra Club. Babbitt urged that the President safeguard Hanford Reach in Washington, Canyons on the Ancients in southwest Colorado, Ironwood National Monument in Arizona, and Soda Mountain in southwest Oregon. Treasured for their historic, wildlife and recreational values, these areas could be lost unless the President uses the power of his office to safeguard these examples of our nation's vanishing natural heritage.

"By designating these four monuments, President Clinton can protect areas throughout the spectacular American West from destruction," said Carl Pope, Sierra Club Executive Director. "Each of these areas is threatened by oil and gas drilling, development, grazing, or off-road vehicle use, but with the President's action, they will be protected for our families and our future. Because Americans see our wildlands so rapidly dwindling, people want action now to save what's left for future generations to explore and enjoy."

Washington's Hanford Reach is one of the nation's natural wonders, because this 51-mile stretch of the Columbia River is the only free-flowing stretch of the Columbia left in the U.S. A run of wild fall chinook salmon is abundant enough to support a commercial and tribal fishery and attract hundreds of recreational anglers to the region. Irrigation projects and polluted run-off threaten to load up the river and choke the salmon, unless the President ensures the future of this treasure.

"There are areas of the Columbia River at the Hanford Reach that remain as wild as it did two hundred years ago when Lewis and Clark explored the breath-taking American West," Pope said. "With a National Monument designation, President Clinton can ensure the Hanford Reach continues to amaze Americans and nurture salmon for generations to come. The time has come to protect our salmon by protecting the Hanford reach."

Babbitt's proposal will also grant protection for Canyons on the Ancients, the rich cultural and archeological resources of remote Anasazi sites in southwest Colorado. National Monument designation will maintain the pristine character of these undisturbed canyon areas and the integrity of the irreplaceable archeological sites that include cliff dwellings, towers, petroglyphs, and other evidence of 1,000-year-old communities.

"The Canyons on the Ancients National Monument would encompass a one-of-a-kind, world class archeological resource," said Mark Pearson, Chair of the Sierra Club Wildlands Committee. "National monument designation would bring long-needed management attention to the problems of vandalism, resource impacts, and rapidly expanding recreation that threaten the longevity of these cultural resources."

This announcement comes at a time when the Canyons on the Ancients are under extreme ecological destruction from oil and gas leasing, off-road vehicle use, mining and grazing.

Soda Mountain represents a breathtaking landscape of high ridges and steep canyons that harbors a biological treasure house of plants and wildlife. Rich with trout streams, ancient stands of pine and fir, towering cliffs, and rich grasslands dotted with majestic oaks, scientists have long recognized this area for its amazing array of plant and wildlife diversity.

"Soda Mountain's majesty is in danger. Abuse by off-road vehicles, over-grazing, and logging, could push Soda Mountain's trout streams and ancient pine forests to the brink." said Anne Mitchell, chair or Sierra Club's High Desert Committee. "We welcome today's action which gives us hope that it's not too late to protect the values of this unique landscape."

The proposed Ironwood National Monument is located in the Silver Bell Mountains, currently home to ASARCO's Silver Bell copper mine. The strip mine uses 2 million gallons of limited desert water a day and is endangering plant and animal life in the process.

"The desert big horn sheep and Saguaro cactus are both in danger of extinction without national monument protection," said Rob Kulakofsky from the Sierra Club's Rincon Group. "Ironwood trees provide shade for new growths of trees and habitat for species which is important in a desert climate."

The land is also sacred to native tribes and has territorial and Spanish-colonial historical significance.

Every President this century except for Nixon, Reagan and Bush has protected American landscapes as National Monuments.
