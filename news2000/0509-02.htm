Forest Service Recommendation Fails to Protect Wild Forests
But Americans Can Demand Full Protection at Nearly 400 Public Hearings
 
WASHINGTON - May 9 - Six months after being asked by President Clinton to protect the remaining wild pockets of America's National Forests, the U.S. Forest Service has laid out a range of options to accomplish this vision. While some options in the draft plan could offer real protection for our wild forests, the agency's preferred alternative would fall short of the President's bold vision and squander this historic opportunity.

"Three out of four Americans want their wild forests protected. But no one option recommended today will provide lasting protection to our National Forests' remaining wild areas," said Sierra Club Executive Director Carl Pope. "The good news is that the Forest Service has recommended ending road building and has included policy options that could -- if added together -- realize the President's vision."

Two gaping loopholes glare in the draft plan. First, the preferred alternative does not end logging in unspoiled forest areas, but only prevents roadbuilding. While a ban on roadbuilding makes logging more difficult, it does not halt destruction of these lands. Second, the preferred option delays until 2004 any decision on protecting Alaska's Tongass National Forest, allowing logging to continue to destroy the Earth's largest intact temperate rainforest while the agency stalls.

Noting that the release of the Draft Environmental Impact Statement (DEIS) kicks off an unprecedented round of nearly 400 public hearings across America, Pope urged Americans to "speak up and demand a final plan that protects our forests for our families and for our future. This fight isn't over yet and we have every reason to hope that we can end logging in the wild fragments of our National Forests and protect Alaska's Tongass National Forest right now.

"Delay means decimation for the Tongass," Pope said. "In our hands, we hold the key to protecting this majestic wonder: Go to the public hearing in your community and demand that the final rule, due out this year, include an end to logging in this land of wild salmon, ancient trees and grizzly bears."

Polling shows most Americans want to ban logging, mining, off-road vehicles and other destructive activities from unspoiled areas in our National Forests. The Republican firm American Viewpoint found 76% of voters nationally favor that goal, with only 19% opposed. That poll found support held across geographic region (80% support in the East; 73% in the South; 78% in the Midwest; and 72% in the West), sex (78% of women and 73 % of men support) and party lines (86% of Democrats, 78% of independents and 62% of Republicans support).

To fully safeguard the unspoiled areas of our National Forests, the final plan must protect all roadless areas larger than 1,000 acres from logging and other destructive activities, and must immediately provide this protection for Alaska's Tongass National Forest as well.
