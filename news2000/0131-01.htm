CONGRESS 2000: MORE ATTACKS ON THE ENVIRONMENT LIKELY
 

WASHINGTON - January 31 - As the second session of the 106th of Congress begins, the Sierra Club is preparing for some tough battles. At stake is the clean water we drink and the ability for our communities to adequately clean up toxic waste sites. During the State of the Union Address, President Clinton laid out a bold agenda to safeguard our National Forests, stop sprawl, make communities more livable, protect open space and parks and curb global warming. But Clinton is not waiting for Congress to act, he is already using his executive powers to establish new national monuments and protect our wild forests.

Despite overwhelming public support, anti-environmental forces in Congress will likely spend most of this coming year trying to undo or override these and other popular environmental initiatives. Below are some of the actions the Sierra Club expects to see in this year's Congress. We hope you will use this information to monitor the environmental movement in Congress and we encourage you to contact our issue experts for any additional information you need.

* Last year, President Clinton announced a plan to protect America's wild forests. This initiative could protect up to 60 million acres of unspoiled areas in National Forests from logging and other destructive uses. Congress is likely going to try to halt the plan with legislative amendments.

* This month, using his authority under the Antiquities Act, President Clinton announced the designation of three national monuments. This is the first set of what we hope will be a series of national monument designations this year. Congress is expected to fight to block each monument designation.

* Most Congressional action regarding the environment will come in the form of riders on the must-pass funding bills. The practice of attaching anti-environmental riders proved very popular among enemies of the environment in 1999 and we expect this trend to continue. Along with introducing new anti-environmental legislation, riders are also often used to cut or underfund good programs such as management for forests, wilderness, fish and wildlife, recreation, and curbing global warming. Riders to derail the wild forest protection plan and halt monument designations are very likely.

* Another likely legislative vehicle could be an omnibus public lands bill that rolls together many measures that have been considered by the Committee but have not seen floor action. These omnibus bills can be very dangerous to the environment, as a handful of popular measures are held hostage by destructive amendments. The final package will likely still contain some damaging measures.

* Because it is an election year, Congress has scheduled only 114 legislative working days, the remainder of the year will be devoted to "district work periods." This almost guarantees that Congress will not take up much new environmental legislation or reauthorize major environmental statutes Following are more actions and bills we anticipate, broken down by specific issue:

Wildlands/Forests

* One bill that has some chance of seeing congressional action this year is the Conservation and Reinvestment Act-- a bill that would mandate a permanent source of funding for the Land and Water Conservation Fund which provides money to protect land, wildlife, recreation and coastal areas. A compromise version of this bill passed out of the House Resources Committee last year. Conservation organizations want this bill to move forward, but will pursue amendments on the floor to correct several critical flaws in the committee bill.

* Senators Ron Wyden (D-OR) and Larry Craig (R-ID) are backing a bill that would force the Forest Service to increase logging to generate more money -- or else make up for it by taking away money budgeted for fish and wildlife conservation. By allocating 25 percent of the money to local schools, the bill holds schools hostage to logging interests and creates a perverse incentive to log. Now the bill is headed for the Senate (S. 1608) and could be voted on in the next month. For more information contact Melanie Griffin at (202) 675-6273) or Sean Cosgrove at (202) 675-2382

Pollution

* Congress may attempt to weaken the Clean Water Act's requirements for cities to clean up sewage overflows that occur during rainstorms. Combined Sewer Overflows (CSO) dump raw sewage, untreated industrial wastes, and contaminated urban runoff into waterways, closing beaches and shellfish beds, and causing public health problems. Both houses of Congress held hearings last year on legislation to allow cities virtually unlimited time to clean up their sewage overflows.

* Congress may attempt to weaken the Superfund waste clean-up law by lowering cleanup standards that protect health and natural resources, undermining "polluter pays" provisions, and eliminating the federal safety net that protects against shoddy cleanups at abandoned urban brownfield sites. House committees passed bills to weaken Superfund last year. For more information contact Kathryn Hohman at (202) 675-7916

Sprawl

* Congress can act to curb some of the ill effects of suburban sprawl by passing the Community Open Space Bonds Act, S 1558 in the Senate, and HR 2446 in the House. If passed, these bills would allow communities to carry out their own conservation priorities by using zero interest bonds to purchase open space, protect water quality, improve access to parks, and help communities redevelop abandoned industrial city centers. For more information contact Kathryn Hohman at (202) 675-7916

Global Warming

* Since 1996, Congress has attached a rider to the Transportation Funding bill that has frozen the fuel economy standards and has even prevented the U. S. Department of Transportation from examining the need to raise the these standards. This year, Reps. Boehlert (R-N.Y.), Dicks (D-Wash.), Greenwood (R-Pa.), and Waxman (D-Calif.) are circulating a letter in support of higher fuel economy standards. The letter urges the President Clinton to work with Congress to implement the law setting automotive fuel economy standards. Raising fuel economy standards is the biggest single step the United States can take to curb global warming

* Additional riders that could restrict the prevention of global warming could end up in most of next year's appropriations bills. Attacks on funding for energy efficiency and renewable energy are also likely. For more information contact Dan Becker at (202) 675-6694

Population/Family Planning

* Last year Congress greatly reduced funding and increased restrictions on U.S. aid to international family planning agencies through the "global gag rule." We expect a tough battle during the appropriations process to remove the restrictions and restore funding for international aid to 1995 levels. For more information contact Carol Schlitt at (202) 675-7901

The Sierra Club will be actively working on these and other issues in Congress this year. As you are writing and researching your stories we hope you will contact us for more information. Throughout the year, we encourage you to call the experts listed with each issue, or call Allen Mattison at (202) 675-7903.
