National Day of Action on Arctic Refuge
 
WASHINGTON - October 19 - Students across the country today are calling on President Clinton to protect Alaska’s Arctic National Wildlife Refuge from harmful oil development by designating the area a National Monument. As part of a “national day of action,” students at nearly 200 campuses are generating comments by mobilizing people to call, write or fax the White House. More than one million comments to the President are expected to be collected by the end of the week.

Drilling in the Arctic Refuge has become a heated topic in recent months due to the strong positions on this issue by the Presidential candidates. While Vice President Al Gore is against drilling, Texas Governor George W. Bush has pledged to open the pristine coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge to oil development should he win office, despite the fact a majority of Americans are opposed to drilling.

“Students at the University of Wisconsin-Stevens Point are very concerned about the Arctic Refuge,” said student organizer Angela Harper. “We are doing all we can to convince President Clinton to protect the Arctic including generating hundreds of calls to the White House for today’s day of action.”

The students’ nationwide efforts are supported by U.S. Public Interest Groups (U.S. PIRG), Free the Planet!, ecopledge.com, and the Sierra Club Student Coalition. The Alaska Wilderness League has led the call to protect the Arctic Refuge, working closely with members of the Gwich’in Nation, a subsistence people in Alaska, whose culture will be dramatically affected by drilling in the Refuge.

In addition to calling on President Clinton to name the area a National Monument, activists will flood BP Amoco with e-mails urging the company to stay out of the Arctic Refuge.  More than 100 ecopledge.com and PIRG chapters will target BP as part of the PIRG's Arctic Wilderness Campaign.

“The best way for BP to go ‘beyond petroleum’ is to support wilderness designation for the coastal plain of the Refuge,” said Athan Manuel, director of the PIRG Arctic Wilderness Campaign. “BP needs to recognize that the Arctic Refuge is a unique and precious resource that should be protected, not plundered.”

Students at Florida State University in Tallahassee are handing out “black ice” snow cones to symbolize the potential of oil spills should the Refuge be developed. At the University of Connecticut, “Kara the Caribou,” a huge inflatable caribou, has stopped along her migration for today’s events.

The Arctic Refuge is the last great wilderness of the United States. The Arctic Refuge is home to nearly 200 species, including polar bears, grizzlies, wolves, caribou and millions of migratory birds. Each summer, 130,000 porcupine caribou migrate across northeast Alaska and northwest Canada. The herd migrates to the 1.5 million-acre coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge where the cows have their calves. The calving grounds are considered sacred by the Gwich’in, who rely on porcupine caribou to maintain their traditional way of life.

Estimates predict that there is no more than a 6-month oil supply in the Arctic Refuge. This oil would take a decade to make it to market and would have no affect on gas prices.
