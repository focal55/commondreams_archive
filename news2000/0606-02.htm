World Bank Breaks Promises, Approves Africa Pipeline
Oil Project in Chad and Cameroon Could Lead to Environmental Degradation and Violence
 
WASHINGTON - June 6 - The Sierra Club today criticized the World Bank for voting to approve the construction of an oil pipeline project in Chad and Cameroon. The project poses serious threats to the environments of both countries and could exacerbate human rights abuses. The Sierra Club was particularly disappointed that the U.S. Executive Director to the World Bank voted in favor of the project.

Last month, in response to protests in Washington, DC, the World Bank announced it would incorporate greater environmental considerations into development projects. Additionally, in a report released last week, the Bank admitted that its lending policies in Africa had failed over the past 40 years.

"Despite promises, we're still seeing the 'old' World Bank," said Alejandro Queral of the Sierra Club's Human Rights and Environment Program. "There are serious environmental concerns that have yet to be properly addressed."

The Sierra Club supports development projects that are environmentally responsible and prioritize respect for human rights. In its current form, the Chad and Cameroon Oil and Pipeline Project could have disastrous environmental consequences and lead to gross abuses of those who criticize or oppose the project, including environmental activists. In a joint campaign with Amnesty International USA, entitled "Defending Those Who Give the Earth a Voice," the Sierra Club has made the pipeline project a priority case where environmentalists are threatened.

Some of the major concerns include:

* The U.S. State Department discovered harrowing records of abuses to human rights and restrictions of freedom. Additionally, both Chad and Cameroon have made the activities of non-governmental organizations working on social and environmental issues on the project increasingly difficult and possibly life-threatening.

* The World Bank's approval of this loan comes at a time of increased civil unrest and patterns of human rights abuses in Chad, where a civil war in the North is now spreading to southern Chad, where the projected 300 oil wells are to be drilled.

* The 650-mile pipeline poses risks of soil and water pollution from oil spills, and threatens pristine rainforest habitats in Cameroon. According to studies and comments made by foreign and US government agencies, the oil spill response plan is too general and it is severely underfunded to properly respond to an oil spill.

* Local populations have not been properly consulted and farmers in Chad have not been properly compensated for their land and fruit trees.

"The political climates of these countries mean there is a great potential for ecological damage and for violence against those who would protect their environment," continued Queral.

The Sierra Club, along with several other organizations, had asked the World Bank to put a two-year moratorium on the pipeline in order to insure that the project and its host countries would be able to address all environmental and human rights concerns.

In addition to approval from the World Bank, the Sierra Club is concerned that the Export-Import Bank of the United States will rush through a decision to provide loan guarantees for political and commercial risk insurance.

"The Export-Import Bank should consider the outstanding environmental, social, human rights, and institutional capacity issues raised by Chadian, Cameroonian, and international organizations," said Queral.
