IMF Sells Environment Short, According to Friends of the Earth Report
 
WASHINGTON - March 7 - Friends of the Earth today released "IMF: Selling the Environment Short," a report that criticizes International Monetary Fund (IMF) policies for accelerating environmental damage in borrowing countries.

The report looks at eight countries -- Brazil, Cameroon, Cote d'Ivoire, Guyana, Indonesia, Nicaragua, Philippines, and Thailand -- and concludes that significant negative environmental impacts occurred in all countries.

"Every case in the report shows that the IMF pushes short-term profit at the expense of biodiversity and ecological prosperity," said Carol Welch, co-author of the report and International Policy Analyst at Friends of the Earth. "Quality of life and environmental quality go hand in hand. This report shows that the IMF is undermining people's lives by disregarding environmental issues."

Several of the countries in the study received IMF bailouts and suffered severe reductions in government environmental spending after the start of the bailout programs. The IMF's pressure on countries to eliminate budget deficits is a major reason behind these cuts. Although countries undergoing structural adjustment may show some economic gains, these so-called achievements are based on the unsustainable exploitation and export of natural resources. Several of the case studies point to the short-sightedness of the IMF's policies that encourage, and sometimes induce, countries to exploit their natural resources at unsustainable rates.

"It is time for the IMF to wake up and face the long term environmental and economic consequences of its policies. The selection of the IMF's next leader will be key in determining if the IMF is capable of facing this reality," said Welch.

Friends of the Earth is calling on the IMF to institute reforms which would make its practices less environmentally harmful, and advocates pro-active measures which the IMF could adopt to encourage environmental sustainability in countries. Among the recommendations in the report is a call for the IMF to cease its structural adjustment programs and have institutions with more appropriate expertise, such as the World Bank, handle longer-term issues like structural adjustment. This recommendation is expected to be echoed in an upcoming report by a congressionally appointed commission examining the role of the IMF and World Bank. The Friends of the Earth report also calls for environmental impact assessments of structural adjustment at the World Bank.
