Fathers Speak Out for Violence Against Women Act
 
WASHINGTON - September 28 - Dads and Daughters, the national nonprofit membership organization of fathers with daughters, today called on Congress to reauthorize the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) H.R. 1248.

A letter from Joe Kelly, executive director of Dads and Daughters, was delivered today to all 535 representatives and senators, along with a compelling brochure on ways men can combat violence against women. Dads and Daughters' member Hank Shaw of Rochester, N.Y., along with graphic designer Daniel Hon created the brochure.

In his letter to Congress Joe Kelly wrote:

"As the fathers of daughters, we care passionately about ending violence against females. We want to do everything we can to protect our daughters from violence.

"We also support VAWA because of its importance to our sons. If violence against women continues to be acceptable, then our sons will continue to be prejudged as a threat to women, no matter what our individual histories, just because they are male. We have a stake in ending violence for many reasons."

Enacted in 1994, the Violence Against Women Act provides federal funding for state and local programs to combat violence against women, including special police units focused on sex crimes. The reauthorization bill would continue these efforts and expand the law to include funding for transitional housing for women and children who are victims of violence, as well as resources for specific populations, such as the elderly and Native Americans.

Dads and Daughters provides tools to strengthen relationships between fathers and daughters and to transform the pervasive messages that value girls and young women more for how they look than who they are. DADs acts against marketers who undermine girls to sell their products, and applauds those who don't.

In recent weeks, DADs made national news when it successfully pressured Campbell's Soup to drop a commercial telling young girls they should be worrying about their weight and dieting. DADs convinced Chattem, Inc. to cease a girl-hating Sun-In print ad, praised Chevy Truck's positive portrayal of a father-daughter relationship in a TV spot, and sparked a national protest of the Simon & Schuster Interactive CD-ROM game Panty Raider.
