Redeveloping Toxic Sites: A Right Way and a Wrong Way
Gov. Bush Touts Weakened Standards as His Way
 
WASHINGTON - April 3 -The Sierra Club today decried Texas Governor George W. Bush's proposal to weaken the Federal Superfund law for cleaning up abandoned toxic waste sites. Speaking in Pennsylvania today, Bush endorsed weakening cleanup standards for contaminated industrial sites known as "brownfields."

Decontaminating and redeveloping brownfields is a crucial part of revitalizing our cities-- and a major emerging environmental issue. If properly decontaminated, brownfields can be converted from abandoned industrial areas to clean areas for families or for new industrial facilities; if improperly cleaned, they can expose the families or workers to a plethora of poisons. Unfortunately, Governor George W. Bush wants to weaken existing federal standards which protect communities from botched cleanups.

"George W. Bush's promises on the environment are as credible as Rick Rockwell saying `til death do us part,'" said Carl Pope, Sierra Club Executive Director. "Today Gov. Bush is proposing weakening Superfund protections; he's already proposed weakening the Clean Air Act. It's discouraging that he'd begin the 21st Century by proposing to weaken environmental laws passed in the last century."

Bush proposed allowing industrial firms to build new facilities on brownfields without meeting existing cleanup standards adequate to fully protect human health and the environment. The Bush proposals would leave workers at these facilities vulnerable to health risks as well as threatening contamination to groundwater and the environment. Bush would have to weaken the Superfund law to accomplish this goal. Last year, the Texas State Government proposed weakening the Clean Air Act to remove sanctions for cities with unaddressed air pollution, such as Dallas and Houston.

Many Americans are now discovering the issue of brownfields, featured in the movie, "Erin Brockovich." In its years of work on the issue, the Sierra Club has come across many families that are all too familiar with the consequences of improper development:

· Chromosomal damage in children: In Midway Village, CA, housing was built on a site owned by Pacific Gas & Electric Co. in 1976. Nearly 40 percent of the low-income residents have reported serious illnesses from toxic waste left on the site. How was the contamination cleaned up? By paving over those areas that exceeded a certain contamination standard. This sometimes meant only paving over half a lawn; or one home but not a neighbor's. Many of the children have suffered chromosomal damage.

· Mercury in bloodstreams: In Hoboken, NJ, apartments were built in an aging industrial building, which once manufactured mercury vapor lamps. Despite its former use, the New Jersey Department of Environmental Protection never tested the building for mercury. In 1995, a resident laying new floors noticed pools of mercury in the subflooring and crawl spaces. Mercury has a devastating effect on the central-nervous system. For one four-year-old, this has resulted in unusually poor eyesight for his age.

"There are two ways to clean brownfields: a right way, and a quick, cheap and dirty way," said Kathryn Hohman, Sierra Club Environmental Quality Director. "Vulnerable families or workers should be concerned that Governor Bush is choosing the quick, cheap and dirty way, ignoring common-sense safety and not informing the public."

Solutions:
Brownfields should be restored to productive use, this can reduce the public's exposure to dangerous contaminants and make use of existing infrastructure. Good brownfields policies have the following features:

* Strong cleanup standards are essential to protect public health and the environment. The Sierra Club opposes policies that reduce cleanup standards as a way to lower redevelopment costs.

* Retaining federal enforcement authority is a vital safety net for protecting public health.

* Financial assistance and incentives are needed to help communities assess, remediate and redevelop brownfields.

* Strict liability standards must be retained to insure that those responsible for contaminating brownfields are held accountable for the cost of cleanup.

* Substantive and early opportunities for the public to participate in decisions about brownfields remediation and redevelopment are essential.

* Brownfields redevelopment should not disproportionately affect racial minorities or persons of low income.
