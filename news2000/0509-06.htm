Navy Devices Posing as Deadly Threat to Whales, Dolphins
 
WASHINGTON - May 9 - News Advisory:

A press conference will be held at 9:30 a.m. on Wednesday, May 10, in the Zenger Room of the National Press Club. The speakers will be:

-- Joel Reynolds, Natural Resources Defense Council;
-- Ken Balcomb, Center for Whale Research;
-- Dr. Charles Bernard, military expert, U.S. Navy (retired)
-- Dr. Marsha Green, Ocean Mammal Institute;
-- Dr. Naomi Rose, Humane Society of the United States

Environmental and animal welfare organizations and marine scientists will present evidence directly linking the mass stranding of whales and dolphins in the Bahamas in March 2000 to the U.S. Navy use of active sonar devices in the area. These organizations will call for the suspension of further development, testing and deployment of Low Frequency Active (LFA) sonar, a particularly dangerous and far-reaching active sonar technology.

The Navy's LFA sonar emits noise so loud it can destroy the vital organs and tissue of marine mammals. Its potential for deafening, disorienting and disturbing marine mammals over a wide area is considerable. Other active sonar devices, such as those used in the Bahamas, emit sounds in mid-range frequencies that may be particularly dangerous for beaked whale species, the type that stranded in the greatest numbers in the Bahamas incident.

The Navy is turning a blind eye to the evidence that its sonar technologies are posing a direct threat to marine life. The Navy continues to declare its technologies harmless with inadequate and superficial analyses while dead whales and dolphins drift to shore. In a scenario reminiscent of the Department of Defense's "star wars" program, the Navy is pursuing its testing plans for mid-range frequency sonar devices and its deployment plans for LFA sonar despite serious and legitimate doubts about the efficacy of the technology and concerns about the harmful effects on marine life. Further Navy testing involving active sonar of the type used in the Bahamas is scheduled along the east coast of the United States this summer; a Navy-sponsored research project involving low frequency sounds and their effect on whales is planned off the Azores this summer.

The organizations listed above will call for a halt to the East Coast testing plans until a complete and thorough investigation into the Bahamas incident is completed and evaluated by the scientific and environmental communities. Footage of the stranding of marine mammals will be shown.

Press kits and B-roll footage will be available.
