Johnson Hanging Death Sparks Two-Day March for Unity and Hope
“We are working diligently to ensure that the Johnson case is thoroughly reviewed by local, state and federal authorities and that all leads are pursued,” said Jackson.
 
CHICAGO - July 5 - Today, the Reverend Jesse Jackson, Founder and President of the Rainbow/PUSH Coalition, called on people of conscience to step forward to participate in a march against bigotry and racial disparities in Mississippi. The two-day march will take demonstrators from the site of Raynard Johnson’s hanging death to the courthouse in Columbia.

Jackson urged elected officials, ministers, civil rights activists, and people of conscience to step forward to help expose the many racial, gender and economic disparities that exist in the state of Mississippi.

“This coming weekend we are convening leadership from across Mississippi and other southern states to participate in a two-day march and a series of community rallies in an effort to expose the racist underbelly of Mississippi,” said Jackson. “All races need to come together to work towards a new Mississippi. A Mississippi that practices equal protection under the law, a Mississippi that practices due process and encourages economic and social justice.

“There appears to be a bounding fear in Kokomo, Mississippi. There was an ugly epithet on a bridge near the Johnson’s home that stated Kill All Niggers. That slur remained for weeks before Raynard’s death - it was just recently removed, but its sentiment still lingers in Kokomo.”

The first portion of the march will take place on July 8, 2000 in Kokomo, Mississippi where hundreds of supporters will meet and march to the tree where Raynard Johnson was hanged. Then on July 9, 2000, in Columbia, Mississippi, supporters will gather in Ducksworth Park and march to the Columbia Courthouse.

“We have talked with Mississippi’s Governor who assured us of his willingness to look into the Johnson’s case and provide a thorough investigation,” said Jackson. “We have talked to Attorney General Janet Reno, and Deputy Attorney General Eric Holder, and so the Department of Justice and the FBI are involved in this case and are pursuing leads.

“We have not stopped working to expose the dark underbelly of Marian County since we learned of the suspicious hanging death of Raynard Johnson. We stand firmly with the family and do not believe that this is a case of suicide. We know that Kokomo was a very threatening environment for the Johnson family. Raynard Johnson’s social relationships with white female friends were the subject of bitter racial animosity from people in the community toward him and his family. On several occasions Raynard was confronted or threatened with violence.

“Recently the Rainbow/PUSH Coalition began to investigate claims that several other mysterious deaths of African American males within the Marian County have occurred within the last 18-months. Now that we have begun to examine the possibility that Raynard Johnson is one of many African American men who may have died at the hands of local white supremacists, we have to wonder what’s happening in the dark in Mississippi?
