House Resolution, Backed by Amnesty International, Condemns Human Rights Violations Based on Sexual Orientation
 

WASHINGTON - February 29 -

News Advisory:

At a press conference this Wednesday, March 1, U.S. Rep. Tom Lantos (D-Calif.) will introduce a landmark House Concurrent Resolution condemning human rights violations based on real or perceived sexual orientation. Amnesty International hails Lantos for taking this unprecedented and important step.

"Many do not realize that there are more than 80 countries in which homosexuality is officially criminalized. In a number of these countries, conviction can result in harsh prison sentences, cruel and unusual punishment, or even death," said Michael Heflin, Director of Amnesty International USA's OUTfront program. "These violations should be given the same level of consideration as others in the formulation of U.S. policies to protect and promote human rights around the world."

The press conference will be held at 11 a.m. at the House Triangle. During the event, several of the resolution's 12 Congressional co-sponsors and supporting organizations -- such as Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch and the International Lesbian and Gay Human Rights Commission -- will voice their support for the resolution; it documents specific abuses in countries as wide ranging as Saudi Arabia, Mexico, Singapore and El Salvador. In support of the resolution, Lantos cites a number of egregious examples, including:

-- In September 1999, Uganda President Yoweri Museveni ordered police to arrest all homosexuals, with punishment of life in prison.

-- In Brazil, a lesbian couple was tortured and sexually assaulted by civil police. Despite eyewitness testimony, internal pressure from Brazilian citizens, as well as an October 1999 Amnesty International action on their behalf, their case remains unprosecuted.

-- In Afghanistan, men convicted of sodomy by Taliban Shari'a courts are placed next to stone walls by Taliban officials and are subsequently executed as the walls are toppled upon them. They are buried under the rubble.

"Sexual orientation and gender identity is not a special category under human rights law, but it is fully embedded in the overall human rights norms defined in international conventions to which the U.S. is party," said Heflin. "If this resolution is passed, it will go a long way toward helping the U.S. advance its image as a human rights protector."
