House Votes Against Reducing Arsenic, Radon and Smog Levels
 
WASHINGTON - June 22 - The U.S. House of Representatives last night voted against efforts to reduce the amount of arsenic and radon in drinking water, threatening the health of American families. The current standard for arsenic levels in drinking water was last updated in 1942, but under today's vote, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) will be forbidden from reducing the amount of that poison that flows from our taps. In a separate vote, the House approved an amendment, which delays the process of cleaning smog hanging over our dirtiest cities.

By a vote of 216-208, the House rejected an attempt to remove harmful anti-environmental riders from the EPA budget bill. The first of those riders to the VA-HUD-Independent Agencies Appropriations bill prevents the EPA from completing regulations to reduce levels of arsenic in drinking water; the second stops the agency from issuing a new standard for radon levels in drinking water; the third halts cleanup of contaminated sediments in waterways.

"Congress defied public opinion and common sense by voting against reducing the arsenic and radon in our nation's drinking water and smog from our air," said Ed Hopkins, Sierra Club Senior Representative. "You don't need a Ph.D. to know that if you can remove arsenic, radon and smog levels, you vote yes."

The current arsenic standard was set in 1942, yet the rider "strongly urges" the EPA to "cease all actions" enforcing the weak "interim" standard. Several statutory deadlines have passed without the EPA able to update the standard. A 1999 National Academy of Sciences report found that the current EPA standard for arsenic in tap water is unsafe, and should be revised "as promptly as possible." Uninformed Congressional second-guessing of the scientific findings of the National Academy of Sciences, EPA scientists, and virtually every independent expert on arsenic seriously undercuts public health protection.

The radon rider directs the EPA to delay implementation of a drinking water standard. In 1996, Congress' Safe Drinking Water Act amendments ordered EPA to issue a radon rule for tap water by August 2000. As in the case of arsenic, this rider flies in the face of two 1999 National Academy of Sciences studies that found that radon is known to cause cancer in people, and poses very real risks.

"The House's belief that it knows more about safe drinking water than the National Academy of Sciences and the EPA is a threat to our families' health," Hopkins said. "Hopefully, the Senate will show more concern for our nation's drinking water and remove these harmful riders."

A third rider that would have been removed by the amendment is designed to halt EPA's cleanup of contaminated sediments in waterways until the completion of an additional report, delaying action that could endanger the environment and public health. Significantly, the rider is identical to language proposed by the General Electric corporation, which has contaminated 200 miles of the Hudson and large stretches of the Housatonic River with toxic PCBs.

The rider delaying smog cleanup was co-sponsored by Reps. John Linder (R-GA) and Mac Collins (R-GA) and passed by a vote of 226-199. It prohibits the EPA from requiring state and local governments to submit lists of counties for smog designations until after the US Supreme Court rules on EPA's smog and soot standards case next year. This rider would prevent the EPA from taking action specifically authorized by the DC Circuit to name smog areas. Because smog non-attainment designations are the first step in a long process to clean the air, this rider sets significantly delays the process.

"Millions of children, elderly and people with lung diseases will suffer most from this smog designation delay because states will not have to acknowledge that many areas have chronic levels of smog that put them in danger," Hopkins said.
