Protecting The Arctic By Cutting America's Oil Addiction
 

WASHINGTON - February 25 - Statement by Sierra Club Legislative Director Debbie Sease:

 A quarter-century after the OPEC oil crisis of the 1970s, America faces yet another oil crunch. Oil prices are going up, just as the laws of supply and demand tell us they will when we guzzle gas faster than we produce it.

But here's the bottom line: It doesn't matter how much oil is in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge. Drilling there would be as short-sighted and foolish as damming the Grand Canyon for hydroelectric power or tapping Old Faithful for geothermal energy.

Today's oil crunch results from our nation's addiction to oil -- and our failure to face that addiction. As usual, there's good news and bad news for the future. The bad news is that unless we confront our addiction, gas prices will keep going up, no matter where we drill.

The good news is we can curb our addiction by raising fuel economy standards for cars and light trucks, adopting strong energy policies. These steps will set this country on the right course. The solution is not drilling for oil in the Arctic or along our coasts. We can solve our problem by drilling for oil under Detroit.

Every day, Americans guzzle our way through more than 17 million barrels of oil. And America's cars, SUVs and other light trucks burn 40 percent of that oil. Why? Because fuel economy standards for our cars and light trucks have stagnated. Since 1980 we've managed to create the Internet, end the Cold War and clone sheep, but the fuel economy of today's new cars is at its lowest point since then. The consequence is that we are increasing our oil addiction and beginning to pay the price for it now.

Raising our fuel economy standards by 60 percent will save more oil than we import from the Persian Gulf, plus what we could find in the Arctic Refuge and by drilling off the coast of California. The biggest single step we can take to reduce our addiction to oil and curb the threat of global warming is to raise fuel economy standards.

While some people in Congress and their oil industry allies shudder at the thought of raising miles-per-gallon standards, they salivate at the thought of drilling in the Arctic. Most Americans, however, want to protect this northern jewel.

It is a national treasure, protecting the full spectrum of fragile arctic and sub-arctic habitats. A place where grizzly and polar bears still roam the land, and a destination for millions of migratory birds who travel from every state in the Union, to nest and feed their young.

Among the American who oppose oil drilling in the Refuge are the Gwich'in Nation of Alaska. These native people rely on the Porcupine caribou herd for their subsistence and spiritual livelihood. The caribou calve each summer on the coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge. Biologists agree that oil drilling and industrial development within the Arctic Refuge would very likely harm the caribou herd which in turn could permanently alter the Gwich'in culture.

Reasonable estimates of oil reserves in the Arctic would supply only 57 days of oil at current rates of consumption. The Gwich'in have lived on this land for tens of thousands of years. To sacrifice their home for two months of oil displays unthinkable arrogance.

Regardless of how much oil may be under the ground, Americans agree that the coastal plain of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge is too special to be disturbed. America has an oil addiction, and it will only get worse if left unchecked. The solution is not to drill for oil in the Arctic -- we can drill for oil under Detroit.
