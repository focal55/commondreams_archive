Planned Military Package Will Worsen Human Rights Abuses in Colombia, Not End the Drug War
Human Rights Activists Will Join Students to Protest Giant Military Aid Package to Colombia as Secretary of State Madeleine Albright Delivers Commencement Address at UC Berkeley
 
BERKELEY, CA - May 9 - Bay Area human rights and peace activists will join UC Berkeley students Wednesday, May 10 to protest Secretary of State Madeleine Albright's graduation speech at the university's commencement ceremony. The protests will target the Clinton Administration's plan for a huge military aid bill to Colombia that activists say will increase human rights abuses in the conflict-troubled country.

The commencement ceremony begins at 4:00 p.m. at the Greek Theater on the UC Berkeley campus. At 4:30 p.m. a plane will start flying over the graduation trailing a banner with the message "Stop US Military Aid to Colombia." Other signs of protest will appear inside the ceremony. Earlier in the day, a teach-in is planned for noon at Sproul Plaza. Students will begin demonstrating outside the Greek Theater at 2:00 p.m.

In March, the House of Representatives approved a $1.6 billion aid package for Colombia, already the third largest recipient of US foreign aid. The bill is supposed to go to the Senate floor later this month.

Eighty five percent of the planned aid--or $1.3 billion--will go to pay for military hardware and training to halt drug production in the Andean country. But drug policy experts say that further militarization will not decrease drug production and that even if it did succeed in suppressing production, it would not affect the US drug market.

Human rights groups charge that the Colombian military's record of human rights abuses, and its connection with right-wing paramilitaries who are responsible for 75 percent of abuses in the country, should be reason enough to deny an increase in military funding. Rights activists say the aid will only aggravate Colombia's humanitarian crisis. More than 30,000 people have lost their lives during the last 10 years of the country's 40-year civil war. Another 1.5 million people have been displaced, more than 500,000 of those in just the last two years. And up to 1,000 people are fleeing the country every week. Human rights groups fear that the aid package may scuttle the country's struggling peace process.

"This military aid package will not stop the flow of drugs to the US--that's a fact," says Sandra Alvarez, coordinator of the Colombia program at Global Exchange, a San Francisco-based human rights organization. "What this military aid will do is worsen the terrible suffering already occurring in Colombia. US citizens must demand that we don't deepen the conflict in Colombia and call their Senators to tell them to vote against this gross proposal. We must support the peace process in Colombia. Any aid sent to the country should be directed entirely to social investment such as education programs, alternative development programs, crop substitution, aid to the displaced, and strengthening of the judicial system."
