Report Critiquing Sea-Based Anti-Missile Systems Released
 
WASHINGTON - October 11 - Sea-based and boost-phase alternatives to national missile defense (NMD) would be expensive and could not be deployed until 2014, according to a report released today by the Council for a Livable World Education Fund. The 48-page report, Taking National Missile Defense to Sea: A Critique of Sea-Based and Boost-Phase Proposals, concluded that deploying a sea-based NMD system would cost between $30 and $36 billion and could be detrimental to other Navy operations.

"Developing and deploying a global sea-based NMD system would not be cheap, quick or easy, and installing NMD interceptors in AEGIS ships would compete with other Navy fleet ... requirements," according to the report, written by defense analyst Rodney W. Jones.

Taking National Missile Defense to Sea assessed the cost of building and deploying a global sea- based NMD system against long-range missiles, as well as recent proposals for boost-phase missile defenses. The report concluded that:

Sea-based NMD would not be cheap. A conservative cost estimate of even a ‘limited' sea- based NMD places the price tag between $30 and $36 billion, or an estimated $43 billion with the inclusion of additional AEGIS ships to cover simultaneous geographic threats.

Sea-based NMD would not be quick. The technology for sea-based strategic missile interceptors has not been designed or developed. Even a system that uses upgraded Navy theater missile defense technologies could not be deployed before FY 2014, while full deployment could not be completed until FY 2020.

Sea-based NMD would not be easy. Altering AEGIS ships to launch strategic interceptors and upgrading ship-based radars would be expensive and could pose new safety hazards. Moreover, installing boost-phase interceptors on VLS modules would be impractical.

Sea-based NMD would compete with other Navy requirements. The system would either degrade the scope of routine naval operations or require more ships, or both.

"Before the next administration moves any distance down the paths of global sea-based NMD or regional boost-phase interceptor systems, it should demonstrate conclusively that the technologies will work and make public what the costs will be," the report recommends.

"Endorsing sea-based or boost-phase defenses without a thorough understanding of the difficulties and costs is a grave mistake," said John Isaacs, president of Council for a Livable World.

For a copy of the report, contact Lynn Erskine at 202-543-4100, x100. It will also be available on the Council's website: http://www.clw.org/ef/seanmd.html
