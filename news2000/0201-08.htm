Physicians Group Sees DOE Admission as Important Beginning
 
WASHINGTON - February 1 - After decades of denial, the U.S. government has finally acknowledged what Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR) and the rest of the nuclear disarmament community has long held true: the very business of producing nuclear weapons is harmful to workers and citizens. The next step, PSR believes, is to curtail current plants to resume nuclear production and to get out of this hazardous business altogether.

"We've worked hard for years to get this health information into the public eye," said PSR Executive Director Dr. Robert K. Musil. "Even if a nuclear weapon never explodes," Musil said, "the effects of production are like a Hiroshima in slow motion."

PSR believes that the Department of Energy (DOE) must fully compensate nuclear complex workers whose lives were lost or threatened by work they performed throughout the Cold War. Many workers were exposed without their knowledge, and the dangers of exposure were ignored or minimized by the government for the last half-century.

PSR and other groups launched the call for responsible and comprehensive research into the exposure of nuclear complex workers as the Cold War ended, culminating in the publication of a critical review of the DOE's radiation work entitled "Dead Reckoning." The "Dead Reckoning" report exposed severe lapses in the DOE's protection and monitoring of some 600,000 nuclear complex workers over the half-century since the dawn of the nuclear age.

"Almost a decade ago, 'Dead Reckoning' came to the same conclusions and exposed the flawed, defensive studies by DOE that served to obscure the truth," said Dr. H. Jack Geiger, a PSR founding physician and co-author of the report. "There simply is no safe way to produce nuclear weapons. It's time to stop."

Available for interviews:
H. Jack Geiger, M.D., is the Arthur C. Logan Professor of Community Medicine at the City University of New York Medical School and a founding member of Physicians for Social Responsibility. He served as PSR president from 1986-87 and is the co-chair of the Physicians Task Force on the Health Risks of Nuclear Weapons Production. Geiger has authored more than 25 publications on the medical and biological effects of nuclear weapons and is featured in several documentary films including "The Last Epidemic." He is recognized widely for his work in preventing nuclear war and providing health care for the poor.

David Rush, M.D., is professor of nutrition, community health and pediatrics, Emeritus, at Tufts University and the Regional Director of PSR/Greater Boston. He is past president of the Society for Epidemiologic Research and a member of the editorial board of Medicine and Global Survival. He is a member of the Department of Energy's Scientific Advisory Committee on joint U.S.-Russian studies of the health effects of radiation exposure at former Soviet nuclear weapons production sites. He has lectured and written extensively on issues related to maternal and child health, nutrition and aging, and epidemiology research as well as numerous topics related to radiation pollution and nuclear war, particularly the health of nuclear weapons workers and the environment and health consequences of atmospheric nuclear testing in the United States. He is co-author, along with Geiger, of "Dead Reckoning," a 1992 PSR publication on the U.S. Department of Energy's epidemiologic research on worker health.

Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., is executive director of Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR), a national nonprofit organization working to protect public health through education and activism on issues such as nuclear disarmament, environmental protection and gun control. Musil has produced several award-winning radio and video documentaries and authored dozens of articles on military and foreign affairs. He has served as director or board member of the Central Committee for Conscientious Objectors, the American Friends Service Committee, SANE Education Fund, the Center for National Security Studies, and the Professionals' Coalition for Nuclear Arms Control.
