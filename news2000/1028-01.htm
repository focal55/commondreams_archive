Nader Endorses Universal Health Care Ballot Initiative
 
WASHINGTON - October 28 - Ralph Nader today announced his support of the Massachusetts Health Care Reform Ballot Initiative, Question 5.

Question 5 requires the Massachusetts legislature to enact universal health coverage by July 1, 2001, and implement it by July 1, 2002. The initiative would establish a broadly representative Health Council to advise the legislature on the specifics of a universal health care program. More than 110,000 Massachusetts citizens signed a petition to put the initiative on the ballot.

The Health Council would develop a plan for a health care system that provides barrier-free access to health care; assures patients’ freedom to choose health care providers and health care professionals’ freedom to act solely in the best interest of their patients; guarantees affordable coverage; preserves and increases the quality of care and encourages research; and simplifies paperwork.

Until the Council has determined that an appropriate health system is established, Question 5 would prohibit the conversion of non-profit HMOs, hospitals, and health insurance firms to for-profit status. It would also limit the amount HMOs could spend on overhead to 10% of premiums, and prohibit HMOs from offering physicians financial incentives to limit care.

The Vote for Health—Yes on 5 committee evolved from a movement of Massachusetts physicians and nurses who four years ago formed the Ad Hoc Committee to Defend Health Care in an effort to refocus the health care debate on the needs of patients. HMOs are spending more than $1 million per week on advertising and other efforts to defeat the initiative.

If passed, the ballot initiative would make Massachusetts the first state in the nation to achieve universal access to comprehensive, high-quality health care for all its residents.

Nader said the measure, which would ensure that at least 90% of every Massachusetts health care dollar is spent on patient care, instead of being diverted into paperwork and shareholder profits, is an important step in beginning to control unchecked excesses of the HMO industry.

“HMOs are destabilizing themselves by their own greed, by their manhandling of the professions in the medical and nursing areas, and their abuse of consumers. I think we are in a real transition period here that gives us a great opportunity to recast our health care system into a nonprofit mode and expand to universal health care,” Nader said.
