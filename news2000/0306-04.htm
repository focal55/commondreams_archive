Environmental And Taxpayer Groups Call On Administration And Congress To Halt Tainted Navigation Study, Cut Funding
 
WASHINGTON - March 6 - A coalition of environmental and taxpayer groups today called upon the Clinton Administration and Congress to halt a scandal-ridden study being drafted by the Army Corps of Engineers; and to cut all funding for the project. The coalition is made of: Sierra Club, National Audubon Society, Taxpayers for Common Sense, Izaak Walton League of America, National Wildlife Federation, Environmental Defense, Mississippi River Basin Alliance and Mississippi River Revival. The groups said that until a full and independent investigation is conducted, allowing the Corps to continue the ongoing planning to expand the upper Mississippi River locks and dams would be a huge waste of tax dollars.

"The Corps has lost all credibility in its efforts on the navigation study. Allowing them to continue to work on it is simply pouring good money after bad," said Carl Zichella, Sierra Club, Midwest regional staff director. "They treat tax dollars like monopoly money to grow their bureaucracy with."

On February 1, a long-term Corps employee, Don Sweeney, formally alleged that senior Corps officials deliberately distorted a feasibility study on extending seven locks on the Mississippi and Illinois Rivers to assure the Congress would appropriate up to $2 billion to build the project. The federal Office of Special Counsel referred Sweeney's complaint to Secretary of Defense William Cohen on February 28, saying, "there is a substantial likelihood that officials in the Corps have engaged in violations of law, rule or regulation and a gross waste of funds."

Both Tim Searchinger, staff counsel for Environmental Defense, and Steve Ellis, director of water resources for Taxpayers for Common Sense, argued that the $7 million Congress appropriated to the Corps for pre- engineering work on the proposed locks, on the condition that the project would be found "justified," should be returned to the federal treasury.

"Congress only authorized the Army Corps to spend millions on engineering plans for bigger locks if they were economically justified. Because the Corps in fact concluded that they were not justified, spending millions for engineering plans is a gross waste of taxpayer funds," Searchinger said.

"Not a penny of this money should have been spent by the Corps under the terms Congress required. This project is not justified in any sense, and it will be a long time before any decision can be made on it. Congress should rescind this funding immediately, before it is wasted on work that will never be needed," Ellis said.

Tom Adams a senior policy advisor with the National Audubon Society said the Corps was hurting itself by not addressing the scandal head-on.

"The senior officials at the Corps of Engineers need to realize that denial ain't just a river in Egypt. The allegations made in Dr. Sweeney's affidavit, and confirmed last week by Special Counsel Kaplan, makes it clear that the credibility and integrity of this study is damaged beyond repair. It is time for the flawed Corps' plan to meet a paper shredder. Congress must order the Corps to go back to the drawing board and deliver a plan that makes the Upper Mississippi a river that works and a working river," Adams said.

David Conrad, water resources specialist for the National Wildlife Federation reiterated the groups urging to Defense Secretary Cohen to meet with conservation and taxpayer organizations to discuss the institutional problems of the Corps and to help act to restore public confidence in the agency by halting the ongoing planning to expand the upper Mississippi River locks and dams. "While senior Corps leaders call for a full investigation, the Rock Island District staff has acted as if nothing is wrong," Conrad said. "This leaves all of us to wonder where the control lies in the Corps of Engineers."

Richard Moore, Midwest representative for the Izaak Walton League of America called on Cohen and the administration to protect the whistle-blower, Dr. Don Sweeney, whose testimony revealed the Corps' manipulation of the navigation study.

"Don Sweeney deserves our gratitude for potentially saving this country more than $1 billion. He played by the rules and did everything right. We have asked the Secretary of the Army to protect him from retribution and we have heard nothing in response. In fact, the only response we've heard has been the repeated denials of wrong-doing from the Rock Island District of the Corps," Moore said.

Jim Falvey, policy director for the Mississippi River Basin Alliance said that other Corps projects could also be affected by the scandal.

"Who's to say that the economic analysis for other projects, such as the destructive Yazoo Pump in Mississippi wasn't rigged too," Falvey asked. "Investigators of the Corps should consider this possibility. The same Mississippi Valley Division officials that demanded their economists `never take no for an answer' on the navigation study presided over other controversial projects. What else have they deliberately distorted."
