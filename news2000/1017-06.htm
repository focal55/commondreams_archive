Texans Tackle Bush's Environmental Record
Groups Highlight Importance of Environment in Presidential Race
 
ST LOUIS - October 17 - If his record in Texas is any indication, a George W. Bush White House would do little to address the environmental and health concerns facing communities across the nation. This was the assessment of three courageous women battling pollution in Texas, who were in St. Louis today highlighting the Republican presidential nominee's record of pollution as governor. The women were joined by Deb Callahan, president of the League of Conservation Voters, and Dr. Neil Carman of the Lone Star Chapter of the Sierra Club.

"I love my state of Texas," said LaNell Anderson, a grandmother who is working to highlight the environmental and health impacts caused by pollution in the Houston Ship Channel area. "But I am fearful of the prospect of a George W. Bush presidency. He has failed to protect Texas citizens. I live in a city that has the filthiest air in the nation. The state of Texas has violated the Clean Air Act every year that George Bush has been our governor. Americans deserve better."

Tamara Maschino lives in the community of Clear Lake, which is bordered by 52 chemical plants that make up the Bayport Chemical Complex. She is the founder of Clean Air Clear Lake, a grassroots environmental group, dedicated to addressing the health and environmental impacts caused by pollution from these plants. According to Maschino, there are entire families in the Clear Lake area that suffer from asthma and other health problems. "We asked Governor Bush for help and he's refused to do anything," Maschino said. "I want to have children someday, but I'm afraid to."

Chris Wilson, a former chemical engineer, is a stay-at-home mom and president of Lakeway Parents Concerned About Sewage Spray, a non-profit group founded after the Texas Natural Resource Conservation Commission decided to place an experimental wastewater disposal system next to her children's elementary school. According to Wilson, her group is working with Texas communities regarding wastewater permits and is currently drafting legislation that would change the Texas Environmental Code to require stronger wastewater treatment standards.

While Wilson, Anderson and Maschino come from different backgrounds, all three women share the common bond of working, to no avail, to get the Governor's office and Texas' environmental agency -- whose members are appointed by Bush -- to be more responsive to pollution concerns in the state. According to LCV's Callahan, voters who care about clean air and water in Missouri and across the nation have to ask themselves whether this legacy of pollution in Texas, is something they want for their families and communities.

"When it comes to protecting the environment, there is a contrast between Al Gore and George W. Bush" said Dr. Carman, a former inspector of industrial plants for the state of Texas. "People are hungry for the facts about their candidates. Before they head to the polls, voters want to know the positions of Al Gore and George Bush on clean water, clean air and protecting our wilderness areas."

A national poll conducted for LCV by Peter D. Hart Research Associates found that among Independent voters who believe clean air and water is an important issue, support for Bush disintegrates from 34 percent to 25 percent once they hear his record on the environment, while support for Gore increases from 30 percent to 36 percent. In addition, support for Gore among women voters increases from 48 percent to 52 percent when they are told about his record, while Bush support drops from 35 percent to 32 percent. The poll was conducted from Sept. 13-17 and has a margin of error of plus-or-minus 3.5 percent.

"The environment is at risk in this election and the stakes are real," Callahan said. "For this reason, LCV and the Sierra Club are working highlight the differences between the environmental records of Al Gore and George Bush. Voters who care about clean air and water and protecting their families from pollution have a real choice on Election Day."

The League of Conservation Voters is the political voice for the national environmental community. LCV was the first national conservation group to endorse Gore for president.
