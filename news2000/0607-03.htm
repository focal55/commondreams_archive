HRC Renews Calls For Hate Crime Legislation On The Two-Year Anniversary Of James Byrd Jr's Death
Dragging Death That Shocked Nation Highlights Need for Congress to Pass Common-Sense Hate Crime Legislation, Says HRC
 
WASHINGTON - June 7 - Today will mark the two-year anniversary of the murder of James Byrd Jr., an African American who was chained to the back of a truck and dragged to his death on a backwoods road in Jasper, Texas at the hands of neo-Nazis. In remembrance of James Byrd Jr. and others who were tragically murdered because of hatred, Congress should pass the Hate Crimes Prevention Act (HCPA) without delay, according to the Human Rights Campaign.

"It is shameful that after the high-profile murders of James Byrd Jr. and Matthew Shepard Congress has still not had the courage and wisdom to pass the Hate Crimes Prevention Act," said HRC Political Director Winnie Stachelberg. "We urge Congress to take action now on hate crime legislation in memory of James Byrd Jr., so his legacy can be one of helping to end hate violence in America."

Following James Byrd's death, the Byrd family emerged as one of the nation's most powerful voices fighting for all people, including gay and lesbian Americans, against hate and intolerance. James Byrd Jr.'s sister Louvon Harris and his nephew Darrell Verrett, who also serves as the Executive Director of the James Byrd Jr. Foundation, have been key advocates for both state and federal hate crime legislation. The family has spoken in support of the Hate Crimes Prevention Act at press conferences with HRC and appeared at Equality Rocks in April to promote their message of ending hate violence in America.

In July 1999 the Senate passed the Hate Crimes Prevention Act for the first time as an amendment to the Commerce, State, Justice appropriations bill. But in November 1999, the language was dropped in conference at the urging of the Republican leadership.

The HCPA would extend current federal hate crime protection - which covers race, religion, color and national origin -- to gender, sexual orientation and disability. HCPA would serve as a tool to help law enforcement by allowing federal assistance, when necessary, in the investigation and prosecutions of hate crimes. HCPA has broad support from notable law enforcement agencies and state and local leaders including 22 state attorneys general, the Police Foundation and the National Sheriff's Association. The Hate Crimes Prevention Act currently has 191 cosponsors in the House and 42 in the Senate and would pass if allowed to come up for a full House and Senate vote, according to HRC.

The Human Rights Campaign is the largest national lesbian and gay political organization, with members throughout the country. It effectively lobbies Congress, provides campaign support and educates the public to ensure that lesbian and gay Americans can be open, honest and safe at home, at work and in the community.
