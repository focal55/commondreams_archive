Wellstone Seeks to Strip Funding for Colombian Military, Transfer Money to Domestic Drug Treatment Programs
Blasts Clinton Administration's "Push into Southern Colombia" Plan, Citing Human Rights Abuses, History of Quagmires Like Nicaragua, El Salvador
 
WASHINGTON - June 21 - U.S. Senator Paul Wellstone today blasted the Clinton Administration's "Push into Southern Colombia" military plan, and offered an amendment to the Foreign Operations Appropriations Bill that would transfer $225 million from aid earmarked for the Colombian military into U.S. domestic drug treatment programs.

The Wellstone amendment, which failed by a vote of 89-11, would have transferred funds to the Substance Abuse Prevention and Treatment (SAPT) Block Grant, to provide desperately needed funds for state and local community-based programs and for drug treatment programs within correctional facilities. The amendment would have left intact assistance for the Colombian government and civil society, alternative development programs, judicial reform and human rights, but sought to remove, and transfer to more effective domestic use, the resources in the bill destined for the Colombian army's "Push into Southern Colombia."

"The decision to fund the Colombian army's push into Southern Colombia is an enormous policy shift, and a profound mistake. It represents a 7 to 1 shift in funding from the Colombian Police to the Army. General McCaffrey says the purpose of Plan Colombia is to help the Colombian army recover the southern part of the country, currently under guerilla control. But, honestly, if the purpose of this military aid is to stop drug-trafficking, shouldn't some of that aid target the North as well? Something strange is going on here," said Wellstone.

The bulk of funding for the Administration's Colombia Plan focuses on the supply side of the drug problem, without focusing on the severe emergency going on in our own country -- the emergency of drug addiction and the lack of treatment for those with this disease. Wellstone insists that any effort to fight the drug problem in our country also include treatment.

"Continuing to pursue our current Colombia counterinsurgency policy, cloaked under the veil of anti-narcotics efforts, risks drawing us into a terrible quagmire. History has repeatedly shown, especially in Latin America -- just think of Nicaragua, or El-Salvador – that the practical effect of the strategy now under consideration, is to militarize–to escalate--the conflict, not end it," Wellstone said.

The White House Office of National Drug Control Policy acknowledges that more than half the adults in immediate need of treatment cannot receive treatment; this means more than 2 million adults who need treatment right now are not getting it. Wellstone's amendment would have resulted in an increase in the state block grant that would enable state and local communities to immediately address these unmet needs, reduce the waiting lists, and enable people with addictions get the medical treatment they need.

In Minnesota, there are presently 21,277 people who requested treatment and were not able to receive it. Most cited the lack of funds to pay for treatment, or were put on waiting lists when they most needed treatment.

Funding for addiction treatment is cost-effective. A landmark Rand study of cocaine markets showed that, dollar for dollar, providing treatment to cocaine users is 10 times more effective than drug interdiction schemes, and 23 times more cost effective than eradicating coca at its source.

"Human rights organizations have detailed, abundant, and compelling evidence of continuing ties between the Colombian Army and paramilitary groups responsible for gross human rights violations. Human Rights Watch has collected evidence linking half of Colombia's eighteen brigade-level army units to paramilitary activity. In other words, military support for paramilitaries remains national in scope and includes areas where units receiving or scheduled to receive U.S. military aid operate," Wellstone said.
