Car Buyers Paying Thousands Of Dollars In Hidden Costs
Outdated Standards Add More Than 30% to Sticker Price
 
WASHINGTON - October 5 - Buyers of new automobiles will pay more than 30% more than the sticker price at the gas pump as a direct result of outdated miles-per-gallon standards, according to a new study released today by U.S. PIRG.

"Pumping Up the Price" documents how outdated Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards add up to 100,000 pounds in extra global warming pollution and an average of $5,700 in extra fuel costs to the cost of each new vehicle.

Studies have shown that auto manufacturers could build vehicles that average 45 miles per gallon. However, for the past six years, Congress has put a rider on the Department of Transportation's budget that prevents the agency from updating the standards that mandate average fuel efficiency. As a result, the average mileage of new passenger vehicles is at its lowest point since 1980, and falling.

"These costs won't be found listed at the car lot," said U.S. PIRG Global Warming Associate Katherine Abend. "Consumers shopping at dealerships rarely realize how much they are being shortchanged by outdated miles per gallon standards, but they're paying through the nose," she continued.

The U.S. PIRG report analyzes the additional monetary and environmental costs that low fuel efficiency standards add to each model year 2000 car and truck. According to the report, those buying the best-selling car, the Honda Accord, will pay more than $5,000 in additional fuel costs, and emit an extra 96,000 pounds of global warming pollution, over the lifetime of their vehicle. Those buying the best-selling SUV, the Ford Explorer, will pay an extra $7,100 and emit an extra 136,000 pounds of global warming pollution.

"By freezing miles-per gallon standards, Congress has in effect imposed a heavy tax on all new car buyers at the pump," said Abend. "We might as well just throw cash down the gas tank."

The report maintains that, by nearly doubling oil use for passenger vehicles, outdated standards increase air pollution and emissions of greenhouse gasses that cause global warming. Transportation accounts for a third of U.S. global warming pollution. Moreover, higher demand for oil leads to higher gas prices, which topped $2 per gallon in parts of the country this summer.

"The ten hottest years ever recorded have all occurred since 1980, and scientists have reached a virtual consensus that global warming is already happening. Upgrading fuel efficiency standards would cut global warming emissions that threaten catastrophic climate change, while reducing our dependence on foreign oil and saving drivers money," said Abend.

Among the findings of the report:

· Outdated fuel efficiency standards add more than 30% to the price of a model year 2000 automobile. For the average passenger vehicle, this means an increased cost of $5,700 in fuel expenses. This amounts to an average of more than $4,800 for cars and more than $6,600 for SUVs, minivans, and pickups. Overall, updating mileage standards would save drivers $80 billion a year at the gas pump.

· Updating fuel efficiency standards would annually save the country 1.3 billion barrels of oil, or 40% of U.S. imports of crude oil.

· Outdated fuel efficiency standards increase the average passenger vehicle's global warming emissions by more than 100,000 pounds of CO2 equivalent. The average car will emit more than 90,000 extra pounds, while the average SUV, minivan, or pickup will emit more than 125,000 extra pounds. Updating the standards would reduce total U.S. greenhouse gas emissions by more than 1.5 trillion pounds a year.

"Upgrading fuel efficiency standards would cut global warming emissions that threaten catastrophic climate change, reduce air pollution from refineries, prevent oil spills, reduce our dependence on foreign oil, and save drivers money," said Abend. "Congress should allow the Department of Transportation to set one standard of 45 miles per gallon for cars and light trucks. With gas prices on the rise, it is unacceptable that Congress keeps our cars inefficient," she concluded.

U.S. PIRG is the national lobby office for the State Public Interest Research Groups. State PIRGs are non-profit, non-partisan public interest advocacy organizations active across the country.
