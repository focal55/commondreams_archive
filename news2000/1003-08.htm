Public Opposes Debate Lock-Out
 
WASHINGTON - October 3 - Ralph Nader today called his exclusion from the presidential debates an “affront to democracy,” and cited a long list of Americans from all points in the political spectrum who have said that he should be given the chance to debate the major party candidates. This list includes: Senators Russ Feingold, Paul Wellstone and Barbara Boxer, former Senator Howard Baker, Representative Jesse Jackson Jr., Governors Jesse Ventura and Gary Johnson, former Governor Mario Cuomo, former third-party presidential candidates John Anderson and Barry Commoner, and media figures Brian Lamm, John McLaughlin, Don Imus, Phil Donahue, and William F. Buckley.

Polls have consistently shown support for having Nader and Pat Buchanan in the debates. A September 13 Zogby poll showed that 58.7 percent want Nader to participate in the presidential debates, while only 34.2 percent agree with the decision of the Commission on Presidential Debates and major party campaigns to exclude him. A FoxNews/Opinion Dynamics Poll from July showed that 64 percent of the American people want to see Ralph Nader and Pat Buchanan in the debates, a number that was duplicated in many informal polls, including an NBC News Today Show Poll. Numerous polls have also shown that the public disagrees with the 15 percent threshold set by the CPD for including candidates in the debates.

Additionally, many newspapers have editorialized in favor of including Ralph Nader in at least one presidential debate. These include the Washington Post, the Seattle Times, the Houston Chronicle, the L.A. Times, the San Francisco Chronicle, The (St. Paul) Pioneer Press, The Christian Science Monitor, and the Worcester Telegram & Gazette.

NARROW SCOPE OF PARTY-CONTROLLED DEBATES THWARTS DEMOCRACY Bush, Gore allowed to ignore important issues due to Nader lock out

BOSTON, MASS. – The first presidential debates at the University of Massachusetts at Boston will bring to light a democracy that has been weakened by the great concentration of corporate power over our society,

Ralph Nader said today.

“On a campus closed even to its own students, before an audience and moderator handpicked by the Democrats and Republicans, Al Gore and George W. Bush will discuss a narrow set of issues and avoid proposing serious ways to shift power from big business to workers, consumers, taxpayers and voters. Both men do not want to step on the toes of their major corporate paymasters,” Nader said.

Nader, the Green Party presidential candidate, has been barred from participation in the debates despite polls showing that he has the support of millions of voters and that the majority of Americans want to see him in the debates. Nader was excluded by the Commission on Presidential Debates, a private company created and controlled by the two major parties and funded by many of the same corporations – including beer, auto and tobacco firms – that contribute to both the parties.

“Commanding the money, the media and the access to both the ballots and debates, the Republican and Democratic parties have designed and enforced a closed system that largely shuts out significant new parties and old and new ways to strengthen our democracy,” Nader said. “It is the citizenry who will lose out Tuesday night as Mr. Bush and Mr. Gore are allowed to sidestep subject matters that are important to millions of Americans and the solutions that would improve their lives.”

Important issues that will be avoided by Bush and Gore at Tuesday’s debate include:

Universal, Accessible Health Insurance for All Americans Now

More than 45 million Americans lack basic health insurance, and one third of seniors have no prescription drug coverage. Nader promises to ensure single-payer, universal, accessible health care for all Americans now. He also proposes to negotiate patient-affordable prices of all prescription drugs developed with taxpayer money. However, in the interim there should be a liberal benefit for all elderly accompanied by government-negotiated prices so that they are affordable to the government that pays for these medicines just the way the Veterans Administration and the Department of Defense do now. Generic drug company competition should also be encouraged. Both Gore and Bush oppose controlling any prices of prescription drugs developed with taxpayer money. Gore supports only gradual steps on health care and Bush opposes universal health care.

Ending Corporate Welfare

Massive federal and state corporate welfare giveaways, through subsidies, handouts and bailouts for almost every major industry in our country, result in the loss of hundreds of billions annually.* Those billions could be used back in communities and neighborhoods to repair and expand schools, clinics, libraries, public transit systems, drinking water plants and many other public works in gross disrepair. These monies can also be used to abolish child poverty and fund universal health insurance. Nader proposes to end these corporate welfare giveaways, while Bush and Gore both support corporate giveaways and never raise the issue between them.

Empowering Working People

One third of U.S. workers make less than $10 per hour, hardly enough for even two workers to support a family in today’s “new economy;” and less than 10 percent of American workers in the private sector are unionized.

Nader advocates a repeal of the anti-labor Taft-Hartley Act, which is keeping tens of millions of low-wage workers from forming trade unions. He would renegotiate international trade agreements to fully protect labor rights and American jobs, and institute environmental and consumer-rights standards. Nader also advocates a living wage of $10 per hour, which will allow all working people to stay above poverty. Bush and Gore do not support a living wage or the repeal of the Taft-Hartley Act and are both strong supporters of NAFTA and the World Trade Organization opposed by labor.

Ending Cash Register Politics

Year after year, big business invests in politicians and political parties by giving them millions of dollars, and in return, those businesses get corporate welfare, weak law enforcement and tax breaks worth billions of dollars. The biggest single obstacle to honest, just government action – government of, by and for the people – is the corruption of special-interest money in our election campaigns. Nader accepts no corporate or any other kind of soft money or PAC contributions, and would end cash register politics with full public financing of public campaigns, believing public elections should be financed just like public libraries and parks. Bush and Gore, who both benefit from hundreds of millions in corporate soft money and PAC contributions, oppose public financing of elections.

Other important decisions that will be avoided by Bush and Gore on Tuesday include: media concentration; racism; renewable energy; criminal justice systems; environmental protections such as solar power and fuel-efficiency standards; consumer justice; weak enforcement against corporate crime, fraud and abuse; and the disastrous war on drugs, Nader said.

“Democracy is in a serious crisis when more than 94 million voters – more than half of all eligible voters – stay at home and turn their backs on the precious right to select the people who will lead the nation. Debates controlled by the two major parties about a narrow set of issues that do not present solutions to our most pressing problems further thwart voter interest and participation,” Nader said.

“Throwing away your vote is throwing it in the direction of the two major parties who have wasted democracy in this country.”

*See the new book by Ralph Nader, Cutting Corporate Welfare (Seven Stories Press, 2000)
