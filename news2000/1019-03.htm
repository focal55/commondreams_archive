Most Americans Want Arctic Refuge Protected from Oil Drilling, New Poll Shows
 
WASHINGTON - October 19 - A solid majority of Americans, 56 percent to 29 percent, say drilling in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge is not worth the risk it poses to the environment for what experts estimate would amount to just 180 days of the U.S. oil supply, according to a poll released today by The Wilderness Society and the Alaska Wilderness League.

After hearing balanced arguments on both sides of the issue a nearly equal majority (55 percent) say they support President Clinton declaring the coastal portion of the Arctic Refuge a national monument, which would prevent oil exploration and drilling there. Less than a quarter of those polled would strongly oppose such a move by the president.

"This poll demonstrates that the more people learn about the Arctic Refuge and the more they hear the arguments for and against, the stronger their support is for protecting the Arctic Refuge from oil exploration and drilling and for designating it a national monument," said Mark Mellman, president of the Mellman Group which conducted the survey. "Despite the alarm being sounded about energy prices, Americans do not want to risk oil drilling in the fragile Arctic Refuge."

The Arctic National Wildlife Refuge in northeastern Alaska has become a topic of intense debate in the last few months, as the oil industry and supporters have stepped up efforts to proceed with oil and gas development on the last five percent of Arctic coast currently unavailable to the industry. Government studies indicate that the amount of oil that could be economically recoverable might be enough to provide the country with a 180-day supply at current consumption rates.

"Drilling for oil in the Arctic Refuge makes no more sense than damming the Grand Canyon for its energy potential.," Allen E. Smith said, Alaska Regional Director of The Wilderness Society. "Some places are simply too special, and we need to pass them along to future generations just like they are. Even if we tear up every wilderness in America, we cannot change the reality that we have only three percent of the world's oil reserves and use 25 percent of the world's produced oil."

The Arctic Refuge is home to polar and grizzly bears, wolves, wolverines, and muskoxen, relics of the ice age. Millions of migratory birds nest and stage their migrations along the coastal portion north of the towering Brooks Range. A herd of caribou that numbers 129,000 travels to the coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge each spring to give birth. According to the US Fish and Wildlife Service, the 19.5-million-acre Arctic Refuge has more abundant and diverse wildlife than any conservation area in the circumpolar north.

Regardless of whether or how much oil the Arctic Refuge might hold, recent history has shown that new production would have no impact on oil prices or U.S. energy security. For example, when Alaska's much larger Prudhoe Bay oil field came on line in 1977, average U.S. gas prices doubled within four years. Experts say that prices are set by global supply and demand factors, not the presence or absence of an individual U.S. field. According to the U.S. Geological Survey mean estimates, the Arctic Refuge may hold 3.2 billion barrels of oil-far less oil than Prudhoe Bay-and the equivalent of less than six months' worth of U.S. oil consumption. Still, oil companies and their supporters have attempted to capitalize on recent gas and home heating oil prices to generate pressure to allow drilling rigs onto the last stretch of America's Arctic coast where they are prohibited.

Bipartisan congressional legislation that would permanently safeguard the coastal plain has more than 200 cosponsors, including Senate Finance Committee Chairman William Roth (R-DE) and Environment Committee Chairman Bob Smith (R-NH). However, Alaskan members of Congress chair the committees that have jurisdiction over such measures, and they have blocked all hearings and debate.

"The American people want the Arctic Refuge protected for future generations, not plundered for a speculative short-term fix of oil," said Adam Kolton of the Alaska Wilderness League. "If the oil lobbyists have a choke hold on congressional action, then President Clinton should act to declare the area a national monument."

The Mellman Group conducted the survey of 1000 likely voters from September 27 through October 8. The survey has a margin of error of +/- 3.1 percent.

Founded in 1935, The Wilderness Society works to protect America's wilderness and to develop a nationwide network of wild lands through public education, scientific analysis, and advocacy. Its goal is to ensure that future generations enjoy the clean air and water,beauty, wildlife, and opportunities for recreation and spiritual renewal provided by the nation's pristine forests, rivers, deserts, and mountains. For more information, visit our website at www.wilderness.org.

Founded in 1993, the Alaska Wilderness League is the only conservation group based in the nation's capitol that is dedicated full-time to the protection of federal lands in Alaska, including the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge and the Tongass National Forest. For more information go to www.alaskawild.org.
