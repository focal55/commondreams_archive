Smoke Signals: The Tobacco Industry’s New Fight on Capitol Hill
 
WASHINGTON - February 7 - Just when it seemed tobacco companies had used their clout to stub out any further federal regulation of their industry, President Clinton today is expected to propose increased taxes on the cost of cigarettes, as well as new fines related to the increasing numbers of underage smokers.

The proposal, set to be unveiled today as a part of the White House budget plan, would fine tobacco companies $3,000 a year for each smoker under the age of 18, renewing the long-standing debate over whether the industry should be held accountable for the millions of young Americans who take up smoking each year. The fines would take effect in 2004, if the rate of teen smokers isn’t cut in half by that time.

A similar proposal died in the Senate in 1998, along with measures that would have increased the federal excise tax on cigarettes another 55 cents. Clinton’s latest proposal asks for a 25-cent-a-pack increase in the cigarette tax, on top of a 5 cent tax that is set to take effect on Jan. 1, 2002. The White House plan also asks Congress to activate the already-approved 5 cent tax on Oct. 1 of this year, as opposed to 2002.

The proposals mark a potential setback for the tobacco industry, which has spent millions of dollars in recent years to lobby against various anti-smoking proposals moving through Congress. In 1999, the tobacco industry accounted for just over $2.1 million in soft money, PAC and individual contributions to federal parties and candidates, 82 percent to Republicans.

That’s a 45 percent decrease over contributions reported during the same period in the 1998 elections, when tobacco companies successfully killed anti-smoking legislation. Under a bill sponsored by Sen. John McCain, R-Ariz., the industry at the time would have paid more than $516 billion to settle smoking-related lawsuits in the states. Several state attorneys general later negotiated a settlement on their own, in which the tobacco industry agreed to limit its marketing practices, particularly to youth.

But tobacco companies still face a lawsuit filed by the federal government, which seeks billions of dollars for federal health care costs. Meanwhile, the Supreme Court continues to weigh a lawsuit that could determine whether the Food and Drug Administration should be given the right to regulate nicotine as a drug.

Much like the tobacco industry’s campaign contributions, lobbying expenditures for the industry’s top companies also decreased in 1999. Brown & Williamson, a subsidiary of British American Tobacco, reported $1.3 million in lobbying expenditures between January and June of ’99, a whopping 93 percent decrease over its $18.2 million lobbying budget during the first six months of 1998. Philip Morris, from January to June, reported $7.5 million in lobbying expenditures, a 48 percent decrease on spending during the first six months of 1998. RJ Reynolds Tobacco ($700,000) reported a 20 percent decrease, while UST Inc ($620,000), parent company of US Tobacco, reported 50 percent decrease in lobbying expenditures.
