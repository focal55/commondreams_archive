Sierra Club Applauds Gore Electricity Plans
 
WASHINGTON - June 27 - The Sierra Club today applauded Vice President Al Gore's call to clean up power plants to protect our families from global warming and air pollution. By instituting tough, comprehensive standards to cut emissions from outdated power plants, the United States could reduce greenhouse gas pollution, cut soot and smog, reduce excessive energy consumption, and help all Americans breathe easier.

"Vice President Gore's plan to clean up America's aging power plants could stop their smokestacks from belching so much global warming pollution and lung-burning soot and smog," said Carl Pope, Sierra Club's executive director. "On summer days, our children should be playing outside, not locked indoors because air pollution has worsened their asthma.

"Tough, comprehensive standards like those outlined by Vice President Gore will help Americans breathe healthier air and protect our climate," Pope added. "By committing $180 billion over 10 years to this effort, the Vice President has signaled that tackling the nation's global warming pollution and cutting air pollution are among his top priorities. In contrast, Texas Gov. George W. Bush's energy policy is to drill the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, which is as foolhardy as damming the Grand Canyon for hydroelectric power."

Pope welcomed today's power-plant announcement, and said he looks forward to the Vice President outlining his plan for improving the fuel economy of the nation's cars, sport-utility vehicles and other light trucks.

"We applaud the Vice President's opening salvo in the fight to reduce America's excessive energy consumption. We look forward to working with the Vice President to cut our gas guzzling with new modern technologies. Starting with raising Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards, our nation can end its dependence on foreign oil and save consumers millions at the gas pump while slashing pollution," Pope said.

"As we make the transition to cleaner power plants and to cars and light trucks that go farther on a gallon of gas, we must consider the people employed in those industries," Pope said. "We believe any solution can both clean up our environment and protect coal miners and autoworkers."
