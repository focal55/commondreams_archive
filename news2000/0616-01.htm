Public Overwhelmingly Wants Diesel Truck and Fuel Cleanup, According to New National Survey
 
WASHINGTON - June 16 - Nearly nine out of 10 people believe that big diesel trucks and buses should be required to use the best available pollution control technology, according to a new national public opinion survey.

The survey, conducted for the American Lung Association, Environmental Defense and the Clean Air Trust, also found that more than more than eight of 10 people believe up to four cents a gallon is a reasonable price to pay for cleaner diesel fuel that would significantly reduce pollution.

In addition, the survey found that nearly seven of 10 believe that cleaner diesel fuel and stricter diesel vehicle standards should be required within less than five years.

"It's clear from these results that the public overwhelmingly wants dirty diesel trucks and diesel fuel to be cleaned up," said Paul G. Billings, the American Lung Association's assistant vice president for government relations.

The public health and environmental groups released the survey results as the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency begins a series of hearings on a plan to require a sweeping cleanup of big diesel trucks and buses. Last month EPA proposed requiring that new trucks and buses be up to 95 percent cleaner than today's models.

To enable use of advanced technology pollution controls, the EPA also proposed requiring that oil companies slash 97 percent of the sulfur from diesel fuel. (Sulfur is a poison, much as lead was a poison to catalytic converters in the 1970s.)

The survey found that 87 percent of respondents believe that 18-wheeler trucks, buses and other big diesel vehicles should be required to use the best available pollution control technology even though it will cost the owners of these vehicles more money. Only 10 percent disagree.

"Most Americans can't attend these public hearings," said Vicki Patton, a senior attorney with Environmental Defense. "But these results clearly show they don't want diesel trucks and buses to get a free ride when it comes to public health. They want these vehicles cleaned up."

On the critical question of diesel fuel, 85 percent of survey respondents believe up to 4 cents a gallon is a reasonable price to pay for cleaner diesel fuel that would significantly reduce pollution. (The U.S. EPA estimates the cost of cleaner diesel fuel at 3-4 cents extra per gallon.) Fifty percent of respondents would find an increased price of up to 7 cents a gallon reasonable.(a)

EPA would phase in the stricter truck standards over the next seven to 10 years, but the public wants a quicker cleanup. Sixty-nine percent want cleaner diesel fuel and tighter truck pollution standards to be phased in within the next five years. Only six percent favor a stretchout of more than a decade.

EPA has estimated the new standards would cost the oil industry about $3.5 billion over the next six years. Over three-quarters of survey respondents agree the amount is just right (60 percent) or too little (18 percent). Only 13 percent believe EPA is asking the oil industry to spend too much.

"People know the oil companies are raking in huge profits, and they want a small portion of these profits spent to protect our health rather than simply going to stockholders or highly-paid corporate executives," said Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust. "ExxonMobil made enough in profits in the first three months of this year alone to virtually pay for the entire cleanup, while its chairman and CEO is making more than $25 million a year."

The nationwide survey of 1,000 adults was conducted June 1-4 by Lake Snell Perry & Associates. The margin of error is plus/minus 3 percent.

------ Note a: Thirty-five percent responded that cleaner diesel fuel that produces significantly less air pollution should cost only up to four cents a gallon. Another 15 percent believe cleaner fuel should cost only up to 7 cents more per gallon and 22 percent say only up to 10 cents more per gallon. Seven percent say up to 20 cents more per gallon, and six percent say more than 20 cents per gallon.
