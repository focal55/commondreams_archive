McCain Tapping Smaller Donors in Presidential Money Race
Among Democrats, Gore Leads Fundraising in 31 States
 
WASHINGTON - February 17 - Senator John McCain’s campaign for the White House shows a markedly different contribution pattern than that of the three other top-tier presidential candidates, with a much stronger reliance on small donations than either George W. Bush, Al Gore, or Bill Bradley, according to an analysis of campaign finance records by the non-partisan Center for Responsive Politics.

Thirty percent of McCain’s total dollars have come from individuals giving less than $200, the Center found – triple the proportion of any of the other three. Likewise, his reliance on donors who write checks for the maximum $1,000 amount is much lower than the others. While Bush and Gore have collected three-quarters of their dollars from donors giving the maximum (and Bill Bradley has drawn two-thirds), McCain has collected just 35% of his total dollars from $1,000 donors.

The figures reflect fundraising through the end of 1999. No reports are yet available on money coming in after the New Hampshire primary.

"McCain’s money pattern matches the campaign," said Larry Makinson, the Center’s executive director. "It’s much more an insurgent effort than an establishment campaign, and the numbers reflect that."

In industry-by-industry comparisons, George W. Bush leads in nearly every category – naturally enough, since he’s raised two-and-a-half times as much as his nearest rivals. But his lead is not universal across all industries and interest groups, and the exceptions are revealing.

Al Gore leads the fundraising from the entertainment industry (TV, movies & music) with $808,000, followed by fellow Democrat Bill Bradley with $617,000. Bush collected $577,000 and McCain drew $247,000. The entertainment industry has traditionally given most of its support to Democrats.

Gore also leads in contributions from lobbyists, collecting $592,000 to Bush’s $530,000. McCain drew $121,000 from lobbyists and their families; Bradley raised $97,000.

Bush had only a narrow lead over Gore in contributions from lawyers & law firms – $4.7 million to $4.4 million. Bradley collected $3.3 million; McCain drew $507,000. Most money from attorneys historically has gone to Democrats – though that tends to be the case more in congressional campaigns than at the presidential level.

Bush now also leads in money collected from casinos and gambling interests – $95,000 versus $69,000 for McCain, $25,000 for Gore and $8,500 for Bradley. Until the fourth quarter numbers became available, John McCain had led in dollars from the gambling industry.

Bill Bradley held a narrow lead in donors from educational institutions – $784,000 versus $739,000 that went to Bush, $601,000 to Gore, and $106,000 to McCain.

Bush did have wide leads over all the other candidates from a host of big-ticket industries in the financial sector. He drew $976,000 from commercial bankers, well ahead of the $360,000 collected by Bill Bradley. Gore got $186,000 from bankers, McCain got just $64,000.

Bush also led in contributions from the securities & investment industry, despite the fact that Bill Bradley has collected more from the biggest Wall Street investment houses – a legacy of his years representing New Jersey in the US Senate. Bush got just over $3 million from the industry as a whole, versus $2.4 million collected by Bradley.

In two areas – the automotive and oil industries – Bush holds a fundraising lead that is truly commanding. The Texas governor has collected $883,000 from the automotive industry – mostly from car dealers. That’s 11 times as much as Bill Bradley got, and the others got even less.

The balance of money from oil & gas industry – the dominant economic power in Texas, and an industry in which Bush worked before turning to politics – is even more dramatic. Donors within the industry have poured $1.3 million to the Bush campaign. That’s nearly 15 times as much as the $89,000 that runner-up Al Gore managed to collect. Bill Bradley got $62,000 from oil industry donors; John McCain got $31,000.

The organizations listed above did not contribute directly. Rather, the money came from the organizations’ officers and employees, and their immediate families. PAC contributions, if any, are also included. By law, corporations and labor unions may not contribute directly to federal candidates.

The top donor lists reflect one curious pattern in presidential fundraising: a number of firms – most notably, Wall Street investment houses – are giving to multiple candidates. Goldman, Sachs; Citigroup; and Morgan Stanley, Dean Witter showed up on the top 20 contributor lists of all four leading candidates. Media giant Time Warner and the accounting firm of Ernst & Young each made the top 20 donor list for three of the four main candidates. (Time Warner made the Top 20 list for everyone but Bush; Ernst & Young was on everyone’s top list except McCain.)

Geographic Money Patterns

On the Republican side, George Bush’s fundraising has been a coast-to-coast landslide over John McCain. Bush has raised the majority of Republican money in 49 of the 50 states. Only in Arizona has John McCain raised more.

On the Democratic side, it’s a very different story. Al Gore has carried most of the Sunbelt and the Great Plains. Bradley has raised more from the Northeast, most of the Midwest, the Pacific coast, Montana and Utah. Final score: 19 states for Bradley, 31 for Gore. Among Bradley’s 19 states, however, are California and New York – the two richest lodes of campaign cash in the nation. In overall fundraising, the two campaigns were even as 1999 ended. Each raised more than $27 million and as the year began, Bradley had more than $8 million in the bank, to just under $6 million for Gore.

The Center today also unveiled the first of a new series of state "money maps" that show where presidential contributions have come from in each of the states with upcoming primaries and caucuses. The maps highlight graphically the contrast between where most people live in each state and where the money comes from to candidates from each party.

The first set of money maps, released today, cover South Carolina, Michigan, Arizona, Virginia, Washington and North Dakota – all of which have presidential primaries in February. A second batch of states will be released prior to the March 7 Super Tuesday primaries.

The Center for Responsive Politics is an independent, non-profit non-partisan research organization that tracks money in American politics, and its effect on government policy.
