Greenpeace Activists Escalate Confrontation at BP Amoco's Arctic Oil Site
Five more arrested on second day of protest
 
BEAUFORT SEA, ALASKA - April 11 - For the second day running, Greenpeace activists attempted to stop BP Amoco’s controversial Northstar offshore oil project. Braving wind chills of minus 50 degrees F (-46C), activists towed a fiberglass dome with two others inside into the construction area. Meanwhile two activists made for Seal Island displaying banners reading "Stop BP’s Northstar, Save the Climate." As police and security guards attempted to remove the protestors from inside the hut, Alaska State Police, North Slope Borough Police and BP security personnel tried to block all video documentation of the event. Five Greenpeace activists are now in police custody.

Today’s events follow the arrest yesterday of four other activists who tried to stop the laying of a dangerous and untested subsea pipeline that will carry oil from the site six miles offshore. Greenpeace has maintained a protest camp near the Northstar site for the past six weeks.

BP Amoco’s Northstar project is the spearhead for oil development in the Beaufort Sea, which will lead to a sprawl of oilrigs across the Arctic Ocean. Greenpeace and other groups including Alaska Natives and responsible investment organizations are opposed to new oil development in the Arctic because of the dual threats of oil spills and global warming. Recent studies by NASA have confirmed that in the polar regions, global warming has already taken a significant toll as the ice pack melts eliminating habitat and hunting grounds for marine mammals such as polar bears and walrus. In the past two decades Arctic sea ice equivalent to an area the size of the state of Texas has melted away.

"BP and the State of Alaska can trample on free speech and freedom of the press in their own backyard, but Greenpeace will continue to oppose Northstar until BP Amoco shifts its resources to sustainable renewable energy" said Melanie Duchin climate campaigner at Greenpeace Ice Camp Sirius, on the Arctic Ocean. "It is irresponsible to continue to develop the Northstar project when we know that we can not afford to use even a quarter of known fossil fuel reserves if we are to avoid dangerous climate change."

On Thursday April 13th, BP Amoco will hold its Annual General Meeting for shareholders in London. At this meeting, shareholders will vote on a resolution which calls on the company to switch away from high-risk, environmentally harmful ventures like Northstar, and reinvest resources in solar and clean, renewable sources of energy. Although BP Amoco has aggressively promoted its solar division as proof it is concerned about global warming, the company actually spends over 100 times more on oil exploration and production.

"BP Amoco knows that oil fuels climate change and that clean, renewable energy is technologically available, but it is stalling widespread commercialization to the detriment of the climate and the Arctic environment," said Duchin.
