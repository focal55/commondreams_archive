'We must not allow the Indonesian government to continue to receive US military and economic assistance as long as it condones, if not fans, the terror taking place now in East Timor'

By US Senator Paul Wellstone

Floor Statement - US Senate
Wednesday September 8, 1999

     Mr. President, I rise today to condemn the brutal violence occurring at this very moment against the people of East Timor. I am outraged that the Indonesian government and army have done nothing to effectively stop the horrifying destruction and rampage across this land. The Indonesian government has not lived up to its commitment to maintain security and protect the people of East Timor. In fact, it appears that it is openly supporting the militia violence against the majority of East Timorese, who bravely cast their vote for independence. If the Indonesia government cannot or will not maintain the peace, I believe a strong international peace keeping mission is the best option. Further, I believe that if the Indonesian government does not immediately take steps to stop the carnage in East Timor, the United States must use any and all of its economic leverage with that government, until a safe and secure environment is restored.

     Last week in a referendum with nearly 100 percent turnout, East Timor's voters bravely went to the polls and overwhelmingly voted to support independence from Indonesia. In 1975, after Portugal withdrew from its colony of East Timor, Indonesia brutally invaded. Since then, more than 200,000 of East Timor's population have perished from massacres, disease and famine. Against this backdrop, against this terrible history, there was an historic vote. At long last on August 30, the East Timorese risked their lives go to the polls to vote for their future and to determine the fate of their homeland. Now that courageous and brave act is being systematically and deliberately undermined by the Indonesian military authorities.

     Rampaging militias, with the backing of the Indonesian military and police, are taking their revenge. As I speak this moment, East Timor is being set on fire, people are being shot and beheaded, their homes looted and destroyed, or being loaded like cattle onto trucks and driven to the border. Even Nobel Laureate Bishop Belo's home was raised, and the 6,000 frightened people who took refuge in his compound, forced to flee.

     What East Timor is suffering from is not chaos, but an organized rampage by the militias, as thousands of Indonesian troops stand by and even participate. The 15,000 Indonesian army and police officers in East Timor have done absolutely nothing to stop the terror, arrest the perpetrators, or protect the victims.

     Right after the results were announced on Saturday morning, militias immediately went on what appeared to be a planned and coordinated offensive across the territory. There have been extensive eyewitness accounts of army officers taking part in militia operations and of militia being backed by lines of soldiers in their attacks. Any East Timorese associated in any way with the UN Mission in East Timor, was targeted. Thousands of displaced people were attacked in their places of refuge, and some of them have been trucked to West Timor.

     The Indonesian army may be trying to teach a lesson not only to East Timorese but to the people in secessionist struggles in other parts of Indonesia. The lesson is: if you legitimately seek separation from Indonesia, even if support for separation is overwhelming, we will destroy you and no outside power will come to your aid.

     The Indonesian armed forces could stop the violence if it had the political will to do so. All of the attacks over the last week, indeed most of the attacks over the last six months, with few exceptions, have been initiated by militias that were created, armed, and supported by the Indonesian military. Local human rights organization, UN personnel, and other international agencies have identified Indonesian officers working directly with the militias in acts of violence. These names have been turned over to the appropriate Indonesian authorities, but no action of any kind whatsoever has been taken against them.

     Since the Indonesian government and army has the ability but not the will to stop the violence, we must use every appropriate means possible to press it to either stop the militia violence now or allow international peacekeepers in.

     Getting Jakarta's consent for an international peace-keeping force will require strong, immediate pressure from the United States, Australia and Japan, and other key donor states. America must take the lead and cut off all military aid and sales. I believe the Secretary of Treasury should instruct US executive directors to international financial institutions to oppose financial assistance to the Indonesian government. International financial institutions should withhold the balance of any undisbursed approved loans or other assistance to the Indonesian government. All but humanitarian loans and aid should cease.

The resumption of aid should be conditional on:

1) the ability of the UN to fully and safely resume its operations in East Timor, with full freedom for local and international staff to work and travel without abuse;

2) the ability of refugees to return home safely and the arrest of key militia leaders responsible for acts of violence;

3) steps taken by the Indonesian government to implement the results of the vote on East Timor's political status; and,

4) the Indonesian military conducting itself in a law abiding manner, consistent with international humanitarian and human rights law.

     For too long the Congress has allowed military and economic assistance to be awarded to the government of Indonesia with few conditions, despite its grim human rights record and its deplorable treatment of the people of East Timor. The time has now come for the Indonesian government to learn that the United States and the international community will not tolerate the violent suppression of the legitimate aspiration of the East Timorese. President Habibie and General Wiranto must stop the violence and must allow international peace keepers to enter East Timor.

     The Indonesian government promised the United Nations and the international community that it would ensure the safety and security of East Timorese before and after the referendum. If the Indonesian government cannot or will not keep this commitment, I believe we must support an international peace keeping presence in East Timor. I also hope the Senate will act quickly on the important legislation introduced by Senator Feingold yesterday. We must not allow the Indonesian government to continue to receive US military and economic assistance as long as it condones, if not fans, the terror taking place now in East Timor.
