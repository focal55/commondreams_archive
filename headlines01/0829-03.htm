Published on Wednesday, August 29, 2001 by Agence France Presse
Landmines Still Widely Used Despite Some Progress, Campaigners Say
 
GENEVA - As many as 15 governments and 30 rebel groups still use landmines in two dozen conflicts around the world, despite progress in curbing the weapons, the International Campaign to Ban Landmines reported Tuesday.

The group signaled some progress towards curbing production and use of a weapon that generally makes no distinction between combatants and civilians, and until recently was killing as many as one civilian every 15 minutes on average, according to one estimate.

ICBL spokeswoman Susan Walker told a press conference there had been a "dramatic drop in production, with the number of producers going from 55 to 14 in recent years."

She was commenting on implementation of a 1997 international convention banning the use, stockpiling and production of anti-personnel mines. The accord has been ratified to date by 118 countries.

Twenty-eight signatory states had completely destroyed their antipersonnel mine stockpiles, Walker reported.

There were also fewer new mine victim in Bosnia, Afghanistan, Kosovo and Croatia.

Central America, where several conflicts are raging, could become the first region in the world without landmines by the year 2004, Nicaraguan representative Cecilia Sanchez Reyes told journalists.

But the pictures was not all that rosy. "Antipersonnel mines are still used in two dozen conflicts by close to possibly 15 governments and 30 rebel groups," Walker said.

The international aid organizationSave The Children said in a 1999 report as many as 8,000 to 10,000 children a year were at that time being killed by stepping on hidden landmines.

Countless others are maimed, often losing legs.

An ICBL report presented Tuesday said 90 countries remained affected by landmines and new victims had been registered in as many as 70 countries this year alone.

Countries which have not signed the landmine ban treaty include the United States, Russia, China, India and Pakistan.

Asked to comment, Ambassador Steffen Kongstad of Norway who chaired the 2000 review session of signatory states, said: "There is an international norm established that is apparently felt by non signatories: that it is politically uncomfortable not to stick to the norm"

As examples of inhibitions among non-signatories he cited "the US moratorium on exports, indicating a willingness to respect certain parts of the convention." China was likewise not exporting landmines.

Ambassador Jean Lint of Belgium, co-chair of the convention's standing committee, said Chile would ratify soon and Algeria had announced it would ratify.

"A most important announcement was made in June by the Turkish and Greek governments which have decided to join the convention together at the same date," Lint reported.

Isabelle Daoust, legal adviser to the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC), outlined the scope of the third convention review session by signatories in Managua next month:

The session would "clarify certain interpretation issues that have started to arise from the treaty: the ICRC remains for example concerned with the sensitivity of certain vehicle mines which may act as anti-personnel mines," she warned.
