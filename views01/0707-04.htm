Published on Saturday, July 7, 2001
Independent Media
by Seth Sandronsky
 
What is independent media? You’re reading one example of it now. Many other examples were present at the Press Freedom event in San Francisco, sponsored by Project Censored in late April at San Francisco State University.

For the past 25 years, Project Censored, based at Sonoma State in California, has selected the top undercovered news stories in the U.S. mainstream media. In other words, the mainstream ran the stories once and dropped them, or the stories were ignored altogether. Either way, the public has been kept in the dark about important news.

Consider my friend Ray Cushing. His story about the federal government’s coverup of research that showed how smoking marijuana reduced tumors was selected by Project Censored as the 22nd most censored story of 2000.

Well, what partly makes for independence in the independent media is freedom from big advertisers. Ads from corporate America take up two-thirds of mainstream newspapers. (Think of all the trees that were cut down for Wal-Mart ads.)

But that’s just one cost of business for the mainstream media that sell their audiences to other businesses. In turn, the businesses pay the mainstream media outlets to advertise their products. This process brings returns on investments to mainstream media owners.

For mainstream media to criticize advertisers such as the auto industry would be to bite the hand that feeds it. Under capitalism, corporations don’t willingly cut their own throats.

Which leaves independent media free to cover news and views the mainstream media dismisses or downplays. One example is reports on political prisoners Linda Evans (recently freed) and Marilyn Buck. Other examples include the lethal effects of U.S. foreign policy on the people of Colombia, East Timor, El Salvador, Indonesia, Mexico, Nigeria, Puerto Rico and Serbia, a partial list to be sure. Mainstream coverage of the Middle East leads many Americans to think that Palestine is occupying Israel, when the opposite is true. Keeping the American people unaware of their government’s deadly foreign policy is a major achievement of the U.S. mainstream.

Activism and analysis around the struggles of the poor, the nonwhite, young and women in America has a home in independent media. This is urgent because the American work force is increasingly made up of women and people of color. Their experience living and working in a racist and sexist society should be at the center—not the margins—of independent media’s voice for social change. The current campaigns against racial profiling and prison building featured in independent media are positive steps in that direction. There is room for improvement.

Without question, independent media has many challenges. One is that it’s not quite awash in money, battling hostile corporate mergers and acquisitions. On one hand, there is no pressure from Ford and General Motors to weaken independent media’s contents. On the other, the independence from corporate ads means that the money to produce and distribute independent media must come from the general public. But the bottom line is that independent media is able to put news and views out that the mainstream can’t for business reasons, and more.

In Manufacturing Consent, Edward Herman and Noam Chomsky use case studies to detail how the mainstream media passes through and is shaped by five filters. According to Herman and Chomsky, there are five filters in the mainstream: sourcing, ownership, advertising, flak and anti-communist ideology. These filters make up their “propaganda model,” a framework for analyzing and understanding mainstream media’s performance.

Here’s more on one of Herman and Chomsky’s mainstream filters. Official sources. They are political representatives and business leaders with power and wealth. California Governor Gray Davis. President George W. Bush. Bill Gates. Mainstream news relies on them.

By contrast, independent media relies on unofficial sources. They don’t have power and wealth, just news and information about life as it’s lived by those with little or no access to the mainstream. Energy expert Daniel M. Berman. Prison writer Mumia Abu-Jamal. Author and professor Martha E. Gimenez.

People who create independent media understand that “who tells the story” counts, in the words of performance artist Keith Antar Mason at this year’s Project Censored Award Ceremony.

People are born curious about the world around them. Despite the shadow cast on them by the “corporate run and propaganda-run society” that Chomsky analyzes so well, people’s natural desire to learn and speak for themselves remains alive. The American people don’t choose to be propagandized. Independent media is a case in point.

At the recent Press Freedom event, I talked with a man from Vallejo, California about starting an independent paper there. Some 400,000 people live in Vallejo, he said, and they need independent news and views. He’s right.

I also met a woman from Detroit who co-produces a show called “Flush Rush” on the radio and Internet , thanks to funding by the United Auto Workers. Other independent media people on hand were the producers of Eat The State!, a community paper based in Seattle, “a forum for anti-authoritarian political opinion, research, and humor” .

There are, of course, many struggles in the independent media movement. Here’s one featured at the Press Freedom event.

The Democratic Media Legal Project (DMLP) is challenging corporate control of the media by the Big Six: AOL Time Warner, Disney-ABC, GE-NBC, Viacom-CBS-Westinghouse, Bertelsman and Murdoch’s News Corp-Fox. Henry Kroll heads up the San Francisco-based DMLP . He told me that the goal of the DMLP is to open a legal fight with a popular movement similar to the legal battle against racial segregation in alliance with the Civil Rights movement.

We weaken alone or strengthen together. You, the supporters of independent media, are us. And we are you. Onward.

Seth Sandronsky is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento’s progressive newspaper. ssandron@hotmail.com
