Published on Thursday, April 19, 2001 in the Madison Capital Times
New Trade Scheme Worse Than NAFTA
by John Nichols
 
Since the fall of 1993, when Congress passed the North American Free Trade Agreement, The Capital Times has stood in militant opposition to the "free trade" policies of mainstream Democrats and Republicans. We have objected, again and again, to trade deals that make the expansion of corporate profits a higher governmental priority than worker rights, environmental protection, safeguards for consumers and the preservation of family farming.

From a journalistic standpoint, it has been a lonely mission. U.S Rep. Marcy Kaptur, D-Ohio, the leading congressional critic of current trade policy, says she knows of only two daily newspapers that consistently editorialize against NAFTA. Similarly, when Congress debated permanent most-favored-nation trading status for China last year, all but a few newspapers sided with the corporate lobbyists who drive the "free trade" agenda.

Does it bother those of us who edit The Capital Times to stand in opposition not merely to bad government policies but also to bad media policies? Not at all. William T. Evjue founded this newspaper for precisely that purpose.

Evjue started The Capital Times in 1917 to defend U.S. Sen. Robert M. La Follette, who was under attack by major newspapers in Wisconsin and nationally for opposing U.S. entry into World War I. La Follette believed the United States was being pulled into that European war by arms merchants seeking fortunes at the cost of young lives. La Follette believed, as well, in Thomas Jefferson’s abiding principle for the United States: "Peace, commerce and honest friendship with all nations — entangling alliances with none."

The Capital Times, at its founding and to the present day, has followed the same Jeffersonian impulse that La Follette did. That impulse today informs our approach to the trade debates that will be the central struggles of George W. Bush’s presidency. On Saturday, Bush will travel to Quebec City to promote the development of a Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA), under which the United States would enter into the most entangling alliance in the nation’s history.

If the FTAA scheme succeeds, the United States would join other western hemisphere nations in a pact that would allow an unelected, unaccountable "trade resolution" panel to overrule the laws and regulations of the United States and other countries if they are judged to be barriers to free trade. Beyond the fact that such a structure is fundamentally undemocratic, it is, as well, entirely biased in favor of multinational corporations that operate on a "trade uber alles" ("trade over all") ethic.

Global Trade Watch’s Lori Wallach has referred to the FTAA as "NAFTA on steroids." And that’s a scary thought.

Since NAFTA went into effect in 1994, 23 complaints of systematic violations of workers’ rights have been filed under the agreement’s structures for resolving such matters. According to Human Rights Watch, "not one of the 23 complaints (has) so far resulted in sanctions against an alleged labor rights violator."

A surprise? Hardly.

NAFTA was not structured to protect workers, farmers, consumers or communities. And the FTAA will be even worse.

So The Capital Times will oppose the FTAA. Passionately.

That may put us out of sync with most of America’s increasingly monopolized and homogenized media. But that’s nothing new. In 1919, La Follette wrote to Evjue, "The enemies of democracy are entrenched in power in every community. They speak through presidents and cabinets. They rule in Congress. They are in the pulpits. They control the press."

In opposing the latest attempt to involve America in an entangling alliance designed to strangle the rights of workers, this newspaper will stand on the side of those who object and protest. That will place us outside the American media mainstream — exactly where Evjue would have wanted us.
