Death By “Free Trade”: The U.S. and Iraq
by Seth Sandronsky
 
When it comes to international trade relations, the planet’s most powerful man is taking the moral high ground. I mean President George W. Bush. In a June 26 New York Times article about the prospects for trade relations between developed and developing nations, the president mentioned free trade as a “moral imperative.” The context was talks following the November meeting in Qatar of the World Trade Organization, the unelected world body that governs the terms of global trade.

Meanwhile, the Bush administration is continuing the Clinton administration’s policy that prevents Iraq from trading freely with other nations. This has tragically hampered Iraq’s legal and moral right to self-determination.

Under the current U.N. sanctions against Iraq that began on August 6, 1990, the Bush administration is trying to end Iraq’s border trade, Lauren Cannon writes. She’s with Voices in the Wilderness, a Chicago-based group working to end the sanctions, having just returned from Iraq on a humanitarian delegation.

Supposedly, Iraqi revenue from border trade is funding weapons making. Based on this allegation, the Bush administration is preventing Iraq from raising funds to rebuild its infrastructure such as water treatment plants.

“Because of the direct targeting of the water treatment plants during the Gulf War (documented in the recently de-classified DIA papers), Iraq has a greatly reduced capacity to treat solid waste,” Cannon writes. “We learned from UNICEF on June 20 that they have tracked a six-fold increase in normal water pollution over this decade of sanctions, and that a full six million people are at risk in Iraq for lack of clean water.

“Dr. Carel DeRooy, Director of Health and Nutrition for UNICEF, stated that $1 billion is needed to repair the water treatment facilities in the central and south of Iraq (a conservative estimate by many accounts).”

Iraq is a nation of 23 million people. According to UNICEF, 500,000 Iraqi children would be alive today if 1990 conditions in Iraq had continued through 1998. The sanctions have claimed the lives of over one million innocent Iraqis, whose deaths would be more newsworthy in the U.S. if they had white skin.

The U.S., with over 12 times as many people as Iraq, is the world’s biggest economy. In addition, the U.S. is the leading importer of oil. The U.S. military presence in the Middle East is directly related to its oil dependence and car-centered economy.

For the U.S., trade relations are based in big part on its domination of the global oil market and competition with Japan and the European Union. Oil is the essential energy source for economic activity around the world. Energy sources such as wind and solar power are simply no alternative to oil, the International Energy Agency reported in 1998.

On one hand, Iraq has the world’s second largest estimated crude oil reserves after Saudi Arabia. On the other hand, keeping Iraq flat on its back with cruel economic sanctions neuters that nation’s ability to alter the U.S.-led balance of power in the region. That power over Japanese and European competitors is flowing from control over Middle East oil, “a stupendous source of strategic power, and one of the greatest material prizes in world history,” according to the U.S. State Department in 1945. Today, U.S. policymakers speak instead about the need for “stability” in the Middle East. Old wine in new bottles.

Currently, a British proposal to modify the sanctions against Iraq is being deliberated at the U.N. However the proposed change to so-called “smart sanctions” is more about spinning a new image of economic barbarism than offering concrete relief to the Iraqi people, according to Father Simon Harak. “Sanctions should be lifted, not smartened,” Cannon adds.

If his moral sense about free trade meant anything other than helping U.S. corporations gain market share and boost profits, Bush would allow Iraq to rebuild its infrastructure. To do this, Iraq needs to have normal economic relations with the rest of the world. Then, every fourth Iraqi child wouldn’t be underweight, with one in 10 being felled by disease and hunger. And Iraqi hospitals would be filled with basic medicine and equipment, as they were before the Gulf War, when Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein was a U.S. ally.

To mislead public opinion about U.S. foreign policy, Bush uses the term “free trade” in part to direct attention away from the barbaric reality of U.S. Iraq policy. While the president moralizes about the need for global “free trade,” his administration is working to prevent Iraq from trading freely.

Seth Sandronsky is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento’s progressive newspaper <ssandron@hotmail.com>
