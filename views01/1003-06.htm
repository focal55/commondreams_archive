Published on Wednesday, October 3, 2001
Fly the Flags
by Matthew Duss
 
The last several weeks have been an interesting lesson in semiotics, the language of symbols. What does the United States’ flag mean? Some voices on the American Left have been grumbling about the mass unfurling in the wake of the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks. Katha Pollitt writes in the Nation that she will not fly the U.S. flag because it “stands for jingoism and vengeance and war.” San Francisco Gate columnist Neva Chonin is uncomfortable with her community’s newfound patriotism, and suggests that “what's needed is a new symbol for those who want to express both national diversity and national solidarity, but not national chauvinism.”

As if intent on personifying that very spirit of chauvinism, the jingoists and chauvinists have been out in force. Right-wing hyperventilator Ann Coulter wrote of the terrorists, on Sept. 12, that the United States “should invade their countries, kill their leaders and convert them to Christianity.” She was not being ironic, although it’s difficult to see how one might convert a dead terrorist. Coulter is also apparently ignorant of the fact that, if comparing numbers of innocents slaughtered, Christianity beats Islam with a big stick. Rich Lowry of National Review writes that “if only Osama bin Laden and his 50 closest advisers and followers die in the next couple of weeks, President Bush will have failed in a great military and moral challenge of his presidency.” Why do I see Lowry licking his chops?

I have no problem with the flying of the flag, and the reason for this is that I never surrendered it to the likes of Coulter and Lowry, the militarists, the America-firsters, all those now predictably beating the war drums, as if a display of national military strength were a confirmation of their own potency. To many of them the flag is a symbol of power, of empire, and they cleave to it grossly and publicly, convinced of the absolute rightness of the American way. These are the fundamentalists of our own political culture; they are emblematic of our diversity just as are the Hasidic book-binder, the Sikh cab driver, the libertarian survivalist.

There are as many particular meanings to the American flag as there are Americans. To me the flag represents a set of ideas, goals to be aspired to, values to be upheld, a society to be created. Over the years, I have frequently been ashamed of my government, but never of my country. This is a distinction that many often fail to make. Some on the right consider any criticism of American foreign policy equal to urinating on Washington’s grave, while many on the left see a flag in a shop window as endorsement of the carpet bombing of civilians on Christmas.

When I see the flag, I often think of my grandparents and my infant father, steaming into New York harbor one morning in 1952 on a boat-load of refugees. They had escaped Soviet terror in the forties and spent several years in German camps. They were luckier than millions of others, and they knew it, and they would never let their children or grandchildren forget it. Every Thanksgiving, before the meal, my grandfather would get up and say the prayer. From the old school, he was not a man who betrayed much emotion, but the tears came to his eyes as he said this prayer, year after year, thanking God for bringing our family to this land where we could live and worship freely, where we did not have to fear our neighbors or our government. Now my grandfather is gone, and my grandmother gives this prayer, and someday it will be my turn, and I will give thanks without reservation and probably with tears, knowing that this ritual is repeated around tables in every American village, town, and city.

So, distasteful as I find such wing-tip-warrior swill as that dished by Coulter and Lowry, I wholeheartedly support their right to call for death even as I, and others, call for life. A bit more strained, I support the right of Pollitt and Chonin to reject the national symbol, however poorly reasoned I feel is their rejection. What we all want, in the end, after all, is for the people of Afghanistan to be able to do the same: to be able to strenuously and publicly disagree with each other and with their government. To me, this is what the flag embodies. As others are certain, so I am certain.
