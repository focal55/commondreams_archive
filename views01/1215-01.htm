Published on Saturday, December 15, 2001 by Common Dreams
Let Social Security Be
by Seth Sandronsky
 
Pay attention to President Bush’s Social Security Commission. It says that Social Security has big problems. One of the commission’s proposals is investing some of the Social Security Trust Fund in financial markets.

The commission claims that the Social Security system will come under increasing strain because of retiring baby boomers. They will swamp the system. Some in the corporate media agree.

“Reforms are needed because the program will go broke by 2037,” reported the USA Today last May 2 when the president named his Social Security Commission. But economist Doug Henwood has a different opinion on the Social Security bankruptcy crisis.

“Projections of Social Security’s bankruptcy are based on gloomy assumptions about economic growth--1.4 percent over the next 75 years,” according to Henwood. In his view, the factor to focus on for the long-term financing of Social Security is not the ratio of beneficiaries to workers, but the growth of Gross Domestic Product (GDP).

GDP growth “will determine how much money is available to pay retirees,” Henwood said. “The bankruptcy scenario is based on an assumption (by the Social Security system’s trustees) that GDP will grow at a rate seen only in depression decades.”

Given this dire assumption, it is unclear how financial markets could be a cure for what is supposed to ail Social Security down the road. If anything, a depression-like scenario would seem to be a reason for staying the current course.

Moreover, financial markets are unstable, hardly “rock-solid.” There could be a major difference in the payout for retirement, survivors' benefits or a disabling injury from Social Security funds invested in financial markets.

On a related note, Social Security funds invested in government bonds are stable as long as the U.S. government exists. Social Security has never missed a payment to retirees, survivors or those disabled and away from work. Social Security is stable also because it spreads risk just like Allstate, State Farm and other insurance companies.

By contrast, financial markets can crash and burn. Since summer 1997, financial markets have fallen in Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, South Korea and Russia.

Financial crises are common to capitalism. If U.S. financial markets tumble, Social Security Trust Funds invested in them could leave the American public holding the (empty) bag.

The recent Enron Corp. meltdown should serve as a warning to the American people on the perils of financial markets. They are the problem, not the solution, for Social Security.

Seth Sandronsky ( ssandron@hotmail.com ) is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento’s progressive newspaper.
