Published on Saturday, September 8, 2001 in the Boston Globe
Should Wall Street Be Open to Slavers?
by Charles Jacobs and Carey D'Avino
 
AMERICANS CANNOT buy Sudanese oil because it's illegal. The United States has identified Sudan as a terrorist nation because it uses profits from its oilfields to kill, starve, and enslave the nation's blacks. Christian and traditionalist African villagers are targeted because they resist being ruled by Khartoum's version of Islamic Law. Oil profits are enabling the regime to step up its bombings of hospitals, churches and schools, so Congress has rightly declared an economic embargo.

However, it's not illegal to buy stock in the oil companies that provide the money for Khartoum's holy war.

Indeed millions of Americans are inadvertently partners in Khartoum's onslaught because their pension funds and mutual funds contain shares of Canada's Talisman Energy, and PetroChina, which are Sudan's petro-partners.

In June, the House voted to close the loophole when it passed the Sudan Peace Act with the ''Bachus Amendment,'' which would evict Sudan's oil partners from our stock exchanges. The Congressional Black Caucus, and many church and human rights groups, support this move to prevent American money from financing what both Congress and the U.S. Holocaust Museum have warned is genocide.

But Wall Street is upset, and with the help of the Departments of State and Treasury, and now President Bush, has begun a campaign to keep our stock markets open to anyone. They say that preventing the free flow of capital would damage the free market economy; any exception is a ''political'' move that would create a slippery slope. Soon, they warn, any business out of political favor could be a target of capital market sanctions.

Alan Greenspan is an especially outspoken critic. Not only is he opposed to closing Wall Street to the partners of mass murderers, he also warned against imposing a more stringent level of disclosure on companies whose profits are made in association with ''rogue states.''

Why? Because, he predicted, firms would ''move financing from New York to London.'' But Talisman CEO, Jim Buckee, under intense fire for his Sudan oil project, proves Greenspan wrong. Buckee says he would leave Sudan before he would allow Talisman to be forced from Wall Street.

The other arguments against the Bachus Amendment are equally false. Blocking access to Wall Street to those who help commit human rights crimes simply comports with the law of nations - that well-defined set of traditional norms of international law, generally accepted among civilized nations, which outlaws only the most heinous offenses, specifically: genocide, slavery and torture. These are bright red lines, not slippery slopes, and they are based on principles applied every day by federal judges in this country as part of the federal common law.

No legitimate corporation has a business plan based on profiting from the crimes defined in the law of nations, so the Bachus Amendment would affect only a small number of rogue enterprises.

Vigorous law enforcement helps and does not hinder our capital markets. The United States already has the toughest securities, tax, and money-laundering laws in the world, yet the wealthiest foreign companies choose to comply in order to enter the world's largest financial market.

Capital market sanctions would hardly trigger mass migration to the foreign exchanges. American corporations that violate the law of nations overseas may be held responsible for their conduct under state and federal laws enacted specifically to address these heinous crimes such as the Torture Victim Protection Act. They would gain no relief from moving, say, to the London Market. As long as the Fortune 500 stay on Wall Street, the best and the brightest foreign companies will seek to belong to this most prestigious of financial clubs.

Genocide and slavery have been documented in Sudan by such authoritative human rights groups as Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch and the United Nations. They have made it clear that everyone who knows Sudan, knows that oil profits finance the onslaught. Today, the oil fields are being ''cleansed'' of Africans by bombing and slave raids.

The president's pastor, Franklin Graham, son of Billy Graham, built a hospital in Sudan. Khartoum's planes bombed it, using the Red Cross on the roof as their target. Bush, who has condemned Khartoum for what it does, evidently wants to allow their oil partners continued access to Wall Street. It was Franklin Graham who held the Bible and swore Bush in to the office of the presidency. Perhaps, when he said, ''Preserve, protect and defend this nation,'' he should have added, ''and its honor.''

Charles Jacobs is president of the American Anti-Slavery Group. Carey D'Avino is a member of the board of the American Anti-Slavery Group and an international human rights lawyer in New York.
