1st Ever: Forest Service Trying to Remove Old Growth Tree-sits
 
WILLAMETTE NATIONAL FOREST, OR - July 24 - Clashes between forest defenders and Forest Service law enforcement stepped up Thursday morning in the Willamette National Forest, following the beginning of old-growth logging and one arrest on Wednesday. The altercation is ongoing at this hour and expected to continue, as activists engage a variety of direct action tactics and Forest Service personnel make their first ever attempt to remove a tree-sit on National Forest land.

In addition to manning a number of tree-sits and dozens of traverses (maneuverable climb lines crossing between trees), forest defenders are now engaging in"cat and mouse", a tactic by which activists on the ground attempt to disrupt logging operations by putting themselves in the path of loggers and falling trees. This tactic, while often successful in stalling logging, led to the death of activist David Chain in California during the 1990's when a Chain was struck by a falling redwood when a logger willfully directed the felled tree toward him.

The Cascadia Forest Defenders have had a large encampment in the Straw Devil timber sale for several months. Despite well-documented populations of red tree voles and other species listed as threatened under Forest Service guidelines the entire sale is designated for clear-cutting. Also of serious concern is the fact that logging is commencing during a level III fire danger conditions, with a very real possibility that sparks from machinery could ignite wildfires.

In hope of stopping further logging operations, which are being conducted by Engels Investors, forest defenders will be continuing their campaign of grassroots outreach and non-violent direct action in the coming days and weeks. Forest defenders remain entrenched in many parts of the timber sale at this hour.

These actions come as the Bush administration prepares a number of sweeping assaults on existing environmental law, including the elimination of surveys for threatened "survey and manage" species under the Northwest Forest Plan. In response, forest defenders have initiated a regionally coordinated direct action campaign called "Cascadia Summer", which includes the operations at Straw Devil.

Help is desperately needed from the progressive community in the form of fiscal donations, legal support, and individuals in the region interested in taking part in or actively supporting those engaged in non-violent civil disobedience.
