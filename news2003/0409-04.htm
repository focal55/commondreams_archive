Bush Administration Proposes Rules Allowing Increased Opportunities to Degrade Sensitive Waters without Consequence;
Environmental Group Urges Public Outcry
 
ALBANY, NY - April 9 - The Bush Administration has published a proposal for public comment, which will cut the heart out of the Clean Water Act (CWA) by changing the definition of protected waters. Crucial waterbodies such as source springs, intermittent streams, headwaters, and so-called "isolated wetlands" instantly became at risk of losing federal protection. The proposed rule change includes guidance (ANPRM and Joint Memorandum, 1-10-03) to EPA and Army Corps staff to stop enforcing existing CWA provisions without prior headquarters approval. The public comment period is nearing its conclusion.

"Waterbodies rarely exist in isolation from other components of hydrological systems, and even if they do, these water bodies make valuable contributions to the water resources of communities and regions." Said Sarah Meyland, Executive Director for Citizens Campaign for the Environment (CCE).

The Administration’s attempt to redefine the term "waters of the U.S." severely reduces the number of water bodies that receive federal protection against filling, dredging and pollution. According to the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation there are numerous non-navigable streams and isolated wetlands in New York that are not covered under the state’s programs and depend upon Sections 404 and 401 of the CWA regulation for protection. Should the CWA jurisdictional scope be diminished as proposed, those waters would become vulnerable to alteration or degradation.

"These water bodies should not be excluded from vital federal safeguards," continued Meyland.

The CWA is a fundamental federal law enacted by Congress after Cleveland’s Cuyahoga River caught on fire and the Great Lake Erie was pronounced dead. The CWA provides the best protection for our nation’s waters against contamination. Signed into law in 1972 by Richard M. Nixon, the CWA has protected all surface waters of the United States for over thirty years and is responsible for greatly improved water quality. However, much of our nation’s waters have yet to achieve the CWA criteria of swimmable and fishable.

"Clean water is essential for life. By relaxing clean water regulations, the Bush Administration is sending the clear message that the protection of the nation’s water supply for drinking water, water-dependent industries, and recreation is not important." Said Bill Cooke, CCE Government Affairs Liaison.

"Water is our most important natural resource. With less federal protection for water bodies, the Bush Administration is risking the nation’s public and economic health in order to benefit polluters." Stated Sarah J. Meyland, CCE Executive Director.

CCE opposes the proposed changes and is actively encouraging the public to weigh in on this unprecedented weakening of the CWA.

"The people of New York and the rest of the country need to understand that redefining 'waters of U.S.' under the Bush Administration means wetlands critical to the health of our drinking water and our economy will lose legal protections against degradation under the CWA. The time has come for the public to demand protection against contamination and destruction of our water." Concluded Meyland.

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has extended the public comment period to April 16, 2003. For more information about the proposed Federal Rule change, visit the EPA’s web site at www.epa.gov/owow/wetlands/swanccnav.html.

Comments that were submitted to the EPA by CCE, additional information and tips on how to submit comments to the EPA are found at www.citizenscampaign.org.

Citizens Campaign for the Environment is an 80,000 member, not-for-profit, non-partisan advocacy organization working for the protection of public health and the natural environment.
