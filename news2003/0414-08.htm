Common Cause Welcomes New Guidelines On Gift Ban Rules, But Still Seeks To Reverse Change
 
WASHINGTON - April 14 - Common Cause welcomes the House Ethics Committee’s interpretive guidelines on recent changes to the gift ban rules, but believes the changes must still be reversed.

“Rep. Hefley and the Ethics Committee have written helpful and narrowing interpretations to these new rules, and we welcome that,” said Chellie Pingree, president of Common Cause. “But the weakened rules are still in place, so the underlying problem remains: Special interests have the opportunity to curry favor with Members of Congress and their staffs. These interpretations may be able to limit some of the damage caused by the rule changes, but we still need to have the gift ban rules restored.”

The House on Jan. 7, with no public notice and little debate, voted to roll back gift ban rules put in place under former House Speaker Newt Gingrich (R-GA) in 1995 to restore the public’s faith in the integrity of Congress.

One of the changes relaxes the $50 gift limit on food to congressional offices, while the other repeals the ban on reimbursement for charity fundraising event.

Hefley, chairman of the Ethics Committee, wrote in an advisory opinion dated April 11 that Members and staff must refuse food gifts, even within the dollar limits of the gift rule, if the person sending the food has a direct interest in the particular legislation or other official business on which staff is working at the time. “Acceptance of the food in that circumstance may implicate the illegal gratuities statute…” the opinion said. Hefley also makes clear that the food gifts must be disclosed.

Hefley’s panel also said it would scrutinize trips made under a loophole engineered by House Majority Leader Tom DeLay (R-TX) to encourage lawmakers to attend his own charity golf tournament, being held today and tomorrow in Key Largo, Florida. That change would allow special interests to pay for lawmakers’ travel and lodging to charity events.

Hefley states in his advisory opinion that special interests will not be permitted to earmark charitable donations to pay for Members’ expenses to charity events, such as golf tournaments, where they can get valuable face time with Members. Hefley also defines a “charity event” as one whose primary purpose is to raise money for charity.

Common Cause commends Mr. Hefley for taking steps in the right direction to address the problem, and will continue to work to build support in the House to reverse the gift ban changes.

The Ethics Committee advisory opinion can be viewed at http://www.house.gov/ethics/m_gift_rule_amendments.htm.
