Global Health Council Urges Congress to Invest in Children and Women for a Safe and Secure World
 
WASHINGTON - April 2 - Dr. Nils Daulaire, President and CEO of the Global Health Council, took an arsenal of low-tech and low-cost medical interventions to Capitol Hill today to demonstrate how U.S. funding on global health can save children's lives.

"There are five things that we can do with our federal funding that we know how to do, that are simple, inexpensive and highly effective, and that could save over 6 million lives a year," Daulaire said during a congressional hearing on fiscal year 2004 federal funding for foreign assistance. Daulaire told members of the House Foreign Operations Appropriations Subcommittee that the Global Health Council is requesting $620 million for international maternal and child health programs -- a one-third increase over existing funding.

"Eleven million children die unnecessarily around the world each year, and well over half of them could be saved at a cost of pennies per child. This is the best investment America could make in a safe and secure 21st century," he said.

Daulaire showed members of Congress the interventions used by public health workers around the world to fight childhood infectious disease and the lives each could save:

-- 10 generic antibiotic tablets (3 million lives saved) -- one vitamin A capsule (1 million) -- one oral rehydration packet (2 million) -- one insecticide-impregnated bednet (1 million) -- one pre-filled measles immunization syringe (1 million)

"Vitamin A is the simplest and cheapest intervention for the prevention of blindness and for providing children with immunities to a range of childhood ailments such as measles, malaria and diarrhea. One dose costs about two pennies, and children only need to get it once every six months. Vitamin A, by itself, could save up to a million lives a year if every child in the world got it. That is money well spent." Supplemental dosages of Vitamin A given to pregnant women reduce infant mortality and protect infants as well, Daulaire noted.

"The simple antibiotics that are used for treating childhood pneumonia costs less than 25 cents for a full one-week course of treatment and could save 75 percent of the three million who die from pneumonia each year. That, too, is money well spent. A 12-cents measles vaccines given once between 9 and 12 months of age could protect kids from measles for the rest of their lives -- and save 800,000 lives a year."

"At this critical point in our nation's history, we believe that it is time for the United States to reaffirm our commitment to the children, women and men struggling to survive in the developing world. Clearly, this Subcommittee understands the strong link between health, development and the growth of healthy societies," Daulaire said in outlining a Global Health Council "wish list" including funding for HIV/AIDS, maternal and child health, family planning, and infectious disease. "The appropriations process is one in which hard choices have to be made, and priorities fought for. This is an investment that works."
