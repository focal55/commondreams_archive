WildFire Watch: Bush Makes False Claim; Healthy Forest Initiative Does Not Make Protecting Communities a Priority
 
WASHINGTON - May 27 - Following is Volume II of WildFire Watch:

On May 20th, President Bush touted his so-called Healthy Forest Initiative and expressed his support for the Healthy Forest Restoration Act of 2003 (HR 1904), claiming the two proposals would reduce the risk of wildfires in our National Forests. Unfortunately, both fail to focus on protecting families, homes, and communities that are threatened by wildfires, and instead benefit the timber industry. In his remarks at the White House, President Bush made a number of misleading claims both about his Healthy Forest Initiative and the Healthy Forest Restoration Act (HR 1904). Here are the facts:

The Statement: "The Healthy Forest Initiative that I announced last summer is making American woodlands more safe, acre by acre."

The Facts: Although President Bush stressed his commitment to a responsible approach to protecting our forests, his approach is failing to protect both our communities and our forests.

-- Between FY 2001 and FY 2002, funding for Wildland Fire Management has decreased in both the Department of the Interior (DOI) and USDA/Forest Service. Under President Bush's leadership, the DOI's fire management budgets have been cut by nearly $300 million, while the Forest Services budgets were slashed by over $320 million. (FY2000-FY 2002 Wildland Fire Management Appropriations, Department of the Interior and Forest Service, http://www.fireplan.gov)

-- In addition, budget requests to fund the Department of Interior's Hazardous Fuel Reduction programs have seen absolutely no increases since FY 2002, when $186 million was requested. Not only have budget requests remained stagnant, but the Department of the Interior's budget for Hazardous Fuel Reduction was actually cut between FY 2001 and FY 2002 (USDA/Forest Service and Department of the Interior Bureau of Land Management Budget Justifications, FY 2001 to FY 2004; FY2000-FY 2002 Wildland Fire Management Appropriations, Department of the Interior and Forest Service, http://www.fireplan.gov)

-- Jerry Taylor, The CATO Institute's Director of Natural Resource Studies, responded to President Bush's false claim that his approach would protect forests, saying "The federal government reports that 70 million acres of federal lands need immediate thinning and another 140 million acres must be thinned soon. The president's plan to thin 25 million acres in the next 10 years will cost as much as $4 billion yet leave nearly 90 percent of those acres untreated. That will leave forest homes almost as defenseless as they are today." (Administration's Forest Plan Doomed to Fail, "Forests Initiative" Will Leave 90 Percent of Acres Vulnerable to Fires, 5/20/03; http://www.cato.org)

-- Taylor went on to say, "A recent Forest Service report estimates there are just 1.9 million high-risk acres with homes and other structures near federal lands. To defend homes and communities, we should treat those acres and fireproof the homes. That could be done in just one or two years at a tiny fraction of the cost of the president's plan." (Administration's Forest Plan Doomed to Fail, "Forests Initiative" Will Leave 90 Percent of Acres Vulnerable to Fires, 5/20/03; http://www.cato.org)

The Statement: "This bill sets priorities for forest management by authorizing work in the areas that are closest to rural communities, and work where there's the greatest risk for environmental damage."

The Facts: The Administration's forest policy fails to set clear priorities. In fact, the Healthy Forest Restoration Act provides little assistance to those families and communities most at-risk from wildfire and instead directly benefits the timber industry.

-- A January 2002 General Accounting Office (GAO) report notes that federal agencies have failed to identify and prioritize high-risk communities. "Therefore, it is not possible to determine if the $796 million appropriated for hazardous fuel reduction in fiscal years 2001 and 2002 was targeted to the communities and other areas at highest risk of severe wildfires. (General Accounting Office, "Severe Wildland Fires" GAO-02-259, page 4.January 2002.)

-- During the fiscal year 2002, just over a third of the acres where the Forest Service planned to reduce hazardous fuels were in areas in and around communities. In 2003, the agency's plan is for only 55 percent of acres mitigated to be in "wildland-urban" areas. (USDA Forest Service, FY 2003 President's Budget Overview," page B-13. February 4, 2002)

-- Forest Service Chief Dale Bosworth testified in June 2002 that the most effective means of reducing the risk of fires to western communities is through careful thinning of small diameter trees near homes and towns. (Dale Bosworth, Testimony before House Subcommittee on Forests and Forest Health, June 12, 2002)

-- Furthermore, the Forest Service's Fire Science Laboratory found that the most critical steps to protecting homes and lives are 1) to clear away fuels from within 100-200 feet of homes, and 2) to fireproof buildings. (USDA Forest Service, "Reducing the Wildfire Threat to Homes: Where and How Much?" Cohen, Jack D. 1999)

-- On May 14, 2003, the GAO released findings on the analysis of fuel reduction projects conducted under the National Fire Plan in fiscal years 2001 and 2002. Only 1.5 million of the 4.7 million acres treated were located within the wildland-urban interface (GAO-03-689R, page 3-4)

The Statement: "The Healthy Forest Restoration Act helps the people closest to the problem by allowing local citizens to help plan projects for nonprofit, for profit, and for stewardship groups."

The Facts: The Administration's forest policy actually undermines the right of public participation and gives agencies broad discretion to prioritize projects in remote areas of public land. The legislation calls for expedited procedures for fuel reduction at an unspecified distance from communities, allowing the Forest Service to log far from the homes and communities most threatened by fire, and does not specifically direct the Forest Service to do Fuels Reduction projects close to threatened homes.

Far from allowing increased local participation, the Healthy Forest Restoration Act of 2003 would seriously undermine citizens' ability to participate in the decision-making process and directly contradicts the Western Governors' Association's (WGA) strategy for consensus and community-based planning.

-- In fact, former Governor John Kitzhaber, of Oregon, wrote a letter to Reps McInnis and Walden, disputing their claim that the Healthy Forest Restoration Act is based on the strategy laid out in the Western Governor's Association 10-Year Comprehensive Study. Ktizhaber wrote, "I am very concerned that this legislation will be perceived by the public as a way to reduce or avoid legitimate public participation in and review of . . . important projects." (Letter from John A Kitzhaber, May 15, 2003, available at http://www.wildfirecentral.org)

In the last two years, the GAO has conducted two independent studies that demonstrate there is no evidence that wildfire prevention efforts are hampered by public involvement or the appeals process. The current GAO report is the third independent study that undermines the claims that public involvement hinders wildfire prevention.

-- The May 14, 2003 study from the GAO found that more than 95 percent of the 762 hazardous fuels reduction projects reviewed by the GAO -- covering some 4.7 million acres of federal forest lands -- were ready for implementation within the standard 90 day review period (724 out of 762). Less than three percent of protects were litigated. (GAO-03-689R, pages 1-5)

-- An August 31, 2001 GAO report demonstrated that over 99 percent of fuel reduction projects proposed by the Forest Service for fiscal years 2000-2001 went through without appeal, and none were litigated. The Forest Service proposed implementing 1,671 hazardous fuel reduction projects during that period. Of those, only 20 (or 1 percent) had been appealed by any interested party, including recreation groups, conservationists, industry interests or individuals. (General Accounting Office, "Fuel Reduction Projects" GAO-01-114R, August 31, 2001)

The Statement: "Sound science shows that we can prevent such fires by managing forests with controlled fire, cleaning out the underbrush and thinning the areas that are vulnerable to intense fires and insect attacks."

The Facts: The facts show that Bush's statement is misleading. The Administration's forest policy is not based on the best available "sound science" and will fail to control the threat of wildland fire. No single cause can explain the variety or number of wildfires that may occur. Therefore, solutions must be determined on a case-by-case basis. There is no simple, proven prescription for meeting the threat of wildfires and solutions require treatments adjusted to local needs.

-- The Forest Service's Fire Science Laboratory found that the most critical steps to protecting homes and lives are 1) to clear away fuels from within 100-200 feet of homes, and 2) to fireproof buildings. (USDA Forest Service, "Reducing the Wildfire Threat to Homes: Where and How Much?" Cohen, Jack D. 1999)

-- Forest Service Chief Dale Bosworth testified in June 2002 that the most effective means of reducing the risk of fires to western communities is through careful thinning of small diameter trees near homes and towns. (Dale Bosworth, Testimony before House Subcommittee on Forests and Forest Health, June 12, 2002)

-- An August, 2002 study by the Center for Biological Diversity revealed similar findings. The study found that protecting houses and communities from the threat of forest fire depends on the proper treatment of the Community Protection Zone -- the area directly adjacent to homes and communities. According to the study, the protection of houses depends entirely on treatment of the home ignition zone -- the house itself and the area within 60 meters of the home. (Center for Biological Diversity, The Community Protection Zone: Defending Houses and Communities from the Threat of Forest Fire, Brian Nowicki, August 2002)

-- Most importantly, the study showed that the largest community protection zone required under maximal conditions is less than 500 meters wide. However, most communities require treatment extending less than 400 meters from the house. (Center for Biological Diversity, The Community Protection Zone: Defending Houses and Communities from the Threat of Forest Fire, Brian Nowicki, August 2002)
