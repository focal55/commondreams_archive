Labor Network's Report Exposes Record of Misdeeds by U.S. Corporations Operating in Iraq
 
WASHINGTON - June 15 - US Labor Against the War, a network of labor organizations formed in opposition to the war in Iraq, announced today the release of a new report, 'The Corporate Invasion of Iraq Profiles of US Corporations Awarded Contracts in US/British Occupied Iraq.' The report profiles the labor, human rights, environmental, business and social records of eighteen U.S. corporations granted contracts to participate in the reconstruction of Iraq.

Bob Muehlenkamp, Co-Convenor of USLAW, depicted the corporations profiled in the report as a 'rogues gallery' of some of the most anti-democratic, anti-labor, socially irresponsible multinational corporations on the planet."Iraqi workers were overjoyed to be rid of the Hussein regime & but they also don't want to exchange Saddam's tyrannical rule for the rule of multinational corporations intent on privatizing their resources, and denying their democratic right to form independent trade unions. Iraqi workers need and deserve to know whom they will be dealing with," Muehlenkamp said.

'The Corporate Invasion of Iraq' exposes the anti-social record of these corporations, which together have been awarded contracts in Iraq worth billions of dollars many secured without competitive bidding. Among the businesses profiled is Halliburton Corporation, for which Vice President Dick Cheney was chief executive officer. Halliburton secured an initial no-bid contract worth $7 billion to rehabilitate Iraq's oil industry.While Cheney was at the helm, Halliburton subsidiaries sold $73 million worth of oil equipment and services to Iraq, in defiance of international sanctions. It continues to do business with Iran and, despite sanctions, continues its lucrative contracts with Libya.

Bechtel, another U.S. corporation profiled in the report, was granted a contract for reconstruction work in Iraq initially valued at $34.6 million but which could potentially be worth as much as $680 million. Bechtel worked in Iraq until Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. While Hussein was gassing the Kurds and Iranians, Bechtel was lobbying the Iraqi regime for a contract to build an oil pipeline and provided help in building a petrochemical plant that in 1991 was found by UN inspectors to be linked to Iraqs weapons of mass destruction program.Between 1996 and 2002, Bechtel was identified as one of the two dozen US corporations that supplied Iraq with one or more forms of banned weapons, military logistics and construction.

MCI-WorldCom, also named, was granted a $30 million contract to build a wireless network in Iraq, despite the fact that MCI is not a wireless carrier, has never before built such a network, and was found to have perpetrated the largest corporate securities fraud in US history, causing the bankruptcy of the company and a $175 billion loss in shareholder value, for which it will pay a $500 million penalty. MCI is described by the Communications Workers ofAmerica as one of the most union-hostile corporations in the telecommunications industry.

"As trade unionists, we know that central to any democracy must be fundamental trade union rights the freedom to assemble, to organize, to bargain collectively and to strike, if necessary, to protect and improve workers' standard of living", said Jerry Zero, Secretary-Treasure of Teamsters Local 705 in Chicago, second largest in the union, whose local isaffiliated with USLAW. "As Iraqis begin to rebuild their labor movement, they will confront not only USauthorities and their former Ba'athist managers, but also some of the most powerful multinational corporations in the world. This report serves as an important introductionto those employers," Zero said.

The report is being issued as the first step in an international campaign to assure that Iraqiworkers enjoy all the labor rights to assemble, organize, collectively bargain, and strike ifnecessary recognized by the International Labor Organization (ILO). Since the 1950s, the Iraq igovernment has ratified all of the major ILO conventions, which remain legally binding treatyobligations that the U.S. and British occupation forces and any emerging Iraqi regime areobliged to observe.

On June 15 in Geneva, Switzerland, Amy Newell, National Organizer of US Labor Against theWar, will present the report to an international labor rights conference sponsored by theInternational Liaison Committee held in conjunction with the annual meeting of the UNInternational Labor Organization. The report is being translated into Arabic and several other languages for national and international distribution.

Copies can be downloaded from the USLAW website or by writing to info@uslaboragainstwar.org.
