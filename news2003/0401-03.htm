Statement of Daniel Becker, Director of the Sierra Club's Global Warming and Energy Program, on the Bush Administration's Announcement on Light Truck Fuel Economy
 
WASHINGTON - April 1 - The Bush administration today announced plans to raise fuel economy standards for light trucks by less than automakers have already promised to do themselves: 1.5 miles per gallon by 2007. And because of a loophole in the law, the end result will be a tiny increase of just 0.3 mpg.

The loophole allows auto companies to get 1.2mpg credit toward fuel economy standards if they make vehicles that can run on both gasoline and ethanol. But since less than 200 of the nation's 178,000 gas stations serve ethanol, virtually none of these ethanol-capable or "dual fuel" vehicles ever see a drop of ethanol. According to a Bush administration report issued last year, extending this credit would waste at least 9 billion gallons of gas, since so few of these dual-fueled vehicles will use ethanol. Thus the Administration's real fuel economy increase comes out to a measly 0.3 mpg.

Now more than ever, it's wildly irresponsible for the Bush administration to be sentencing Americans to an oil dependent future. Our troops are risking their lives in the middle east for a war that is in part about oil, gas prices are skyrocketing, and global warming pollution is more of a problem than ever - so it's clear we need to reduce our oil consumption. But this cosmetic change will do virtually nothing to cut our oil use, and will be used by automakers as another excuse to avoid making real improvements.

We can do much better. Automakers have the technology to produce vehicles of all sizes that get significantly better gas mileage. By adding components like the integrated starter generator and the variable valve control engine, automakers could produce fleets that average 40 mpg, without compromising safety, size, or affordability. That would save 3 million barrels of oil a day - more than we currently import from the Persian Gulf, and could hope to find in the Arctic Refuge, combined.

With the Big 3 struggling to keep up with fast-moving foreign competition, the Bush administration's approach isn't even good for U.S. automakers. Rather than spurring them to become leaders in the growing market for fuel-efficient vehicles, it ensures that they'll keep lagging behind Toyota and Honda.

The automakers have shown that they won't improve fuel economy until required to do so by Washington. But last year, the Bush administration sided with the auto industry, opposing an attempt in Congress to increase fuel economy to 36 mpg. Now more than ever, if they're serious about improving our national security, it's time for the Bush administration to support real fuel economy increases.
