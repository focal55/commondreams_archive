Virginia Set to Execute Percy Walton Despite Questions of Mental Illness, Mental Retardation
 
WASHINGTON - May 23 - The National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty today called upon Virginia Gov. Mark Warner to commute the death sentence of Percy Walton, who is scheduled for execution Wednesday, May 28. Walton, a black man, is scheduled to be executed despite ample evidence of mental illness and mental retardation. In 1999, a psychiatrist, a neuropsychologist, and a neurologist assessed Walton's mental health, and found that he suffers from severe chronic schizophrenia.

While awaiting trial, Walton expressed his belief that he could not be seen if he had his eyes closed. He also claimed he was Jesus Christ and said that he looked forward to his execution because then he would be able to return to life and resurrect his dead family members. One court-appointed psychologist considered Walton "imminently dangerous to himself and others," and recommended that the state move him to a secure psychiatric hospital.

"The execution of Percy Walton would represent a gross violation of human rights," said Steven W. Hawkins, NCADP executive director. "Governor Warner should consider Walton's diminished moral culpability as a result of his mental illness."

Walton's attorneys have also brought forth evidence of mental retardation, and have cited his IQ at 66, well below the standard benchmark of 70. In 2002, the U.S. Supreme Court banned the execution of people with mental retardation in Atkins v. Virginia. Hawkins said Virginia now has a responsibility to evaluate inmates' mental retardation claims. "Virginia must review each and every mental retardation claim in light of the Atkins decision," he said.

Hawkins noted that in the past, Virginia governors have recognized the issue of diminished moral culpability and considered it as a factor in clemency decisions. In 1999, Virginia Gov. James Gilmore, a Republican, commuted Calvin Swann's death sentence on account of his schizophrenia. "The tragic history of this case is similar to Swann's, and Governor Warner should follow that precedent by using his executive authority to grant clemency to Percy Walton," Hawkins said.

As a child, Walton was neat, polite, and popular, but began struggling with his emotions and mental stability in his mid-teens. He stopped bathing, attending school, and playing sports, and began talking to himself.

In October 1997, Walton pled guilty to the murders of Jessie and Elizabeth Kendrick and Archie Moore in Danville. At his sentencing trial, his state-appointed lawyer did not offer any evidence of mental illness or mental retardation, despite the fact that Walton had repeatedly misidentified his family members, expressed his opinion that he would be released on bail, and referred to himself as a millionaire and a superhero.

Hawkins said NCADP is urging its membership to contact Gov. Warner through NCADP's new Legislative Action Center. To visit the Legislative Action Center, go to www.ncadp.org
