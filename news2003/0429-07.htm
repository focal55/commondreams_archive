State Lawmakers from Texas, North Carolina Named Clean Air 'Villians' of Month by Clean Air Trust
 
WASHINGTON - April 29 - Following is a statement by the Clean Air Trust:

Usually we present our "Clean Air Villain of the Month" award to some miscreant in our nation's capital. For there is usually no dearth of dirty-air skullduggery in Washington, D.C.

This month, however, we are casting our net wider. In an era when much power and many decisions are being delegated to those outside Washington, the potential for mischief also follows. We want to highlight two such instances, by awarding this dubious distinction as "villains" to Texas State Representative Dennis Bonnen and North Carolina State Representative Roger West.

Bonnen, a Republican from Angleton (south of Houston) is the influential chairman of the Texas House of Representatives Committee on Environmental Regulation. He caught our eye by introducing a resolution calling on the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality to adopt industry-backed Bush Administration changes that would weaken new source review requirements and allow smokestack polluters to emit more dangerous pollution.

In other words, Bonnen is siding with polluters against groups such as the American Lung Association. The lung association, numerous state attorneys general and environmental groups (including the Clean Air Trust) have gone to court to oppose this rollback of the Clean Air Act.

Bonnen's actions stand in sharp contrast to those of lawmakers in California (led by State Senator Byron Sher) who are seeking to enact a state law to ratify that state's ability to enforce the tougher (pre-rollback) clean air safeguards.

And even though he supports the federal government when it weakens clean air protections, Bonnen has shown he's ready to bash the feds if they dare do anything that would threaten polluters. For example, EPA's Dallas regional office recently wrote a letter objecting to proposed state legislation that would bar the state from using evidence from citizens in enforcement actions against polluters. Bonnen's reaction to what sounds like a reasonable communication? "I think this letter is just a glowing example of the overbearing EPA trying to dictate environmental policy in the state of Texas," Bonnen said, in comments published last week in the Brazosport Facts newspaper. "This letter in a way is very stupid, although they're arrogant and they don't care."

Our other villain, North Carolina State Representative Roger West, a Republican from Marble, N.C. (in the southwestern part of the state) is carrying water for industry in a different way. He has actually introduced legislation that would specifically eliminate the job of a state air quality enforcer. (He is also sponsoring legislation that would eliminate the jobs of state officials who enforce wetlands requirements.)

Even if the state legislature rejects this attempt to fire someone simply for doing his job, "This action is a pretty blatant act of intimidation," as a spokesman for the North Carolina Department of Environment and Natural Resources put it.

This apparently isn't the first time West has tried the bully-boy approach. The Raleigh News & Observer reported that West, a contractor, had previously contacted the state environment department in an apparent attempt to reduce dirty-air fines against a company run by friends of his.
