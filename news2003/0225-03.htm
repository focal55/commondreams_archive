Power Company Boss/Lobbyist Tom Kuhn Named Clean Air Villain of theMonth
Bush Crony Seeks to Weaken Toxic Mercury Standards
 
WASHINGTON - February 25 - The nonprofit Clean Air Trust today gave its "clean air villain of the month" award to Tom Kuhn, president of the Edison Electric Institute, the lobbying arm of the electric power industry.

Kuhn earned this dubious distinction by leading a lobbying blitz to head off tough controls on electric power plant emissions of toxic mercury, which has been linked to mental retardation and other neurological damage to children.

Electric power plants are the largest uncontrolled source of toxic mercury in the nation. The EPA is on track to set tough standards by next year, to take effect by the end of 2007.

That's where Kuhn comes in. His team of lobbyists is trying to eliminate those standards (known in the jargon as "maximum achievable control technology," which for comparable industries has meant 90 percent or more control) in favor of a much weaker plan put forward by President Bush, a college classmate of Kuhn at Yale.

"Mercury could be the poster child for the Bush Administration's air pollution plan," noted Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust. "It wants to delay controls on mercury and weaken them -- just to pander to the powerful electric power companies."

O'Donnell added: "Mercury will be to the Bush air pollution plan what arsenic was for the Bush drinking water plan. Both show just how far this administration is willing to go to reward their supporters and contributors in the mining and electric power industries."

And now power mogul Kuhn is reportedly trying to make the weak Bush plan even weaker -- by postponing any specific mercury controls until at least 2018. An Edison Electric Institute lobbyist recently told BNA's Daily Environment Report that the utility lobby would press for the President's dirty-air plan, but that it also wants "improvements" -- that is to say, no mercury reductions before 2018 beyond those that might be achieved coincidentally through the reduction of other pollutants.

There's no question Kuhn has clout when it comes to the White House. In addition to being a presidential crony, Kuhn was among the most prominent so-called "Pioneers" who raised at least $100,000 for the president's 2000 campaign.

Later Kuhn was permitted to vet some prospective candidates to head the EPA in the Bush Administration. (We don't believe the ultimate pick, Christie Whitman, was among those vetted.) The Wall Street Journal described the applicants as paying "courtesy calls" to Kuhn.

We expect that Kuhn will unleash his lobbying forces on Congress -- and follow up with ample campaign contributions -- to persuade them to press the power company agenda. Which is to do as little as possible to control a toxin that is poisoning children.

Power companies spent a whopping $19.4 million on campaign contributions during the 2001-2002 election cycle, according to the Center for Responsive Politics. But they spent even more -- an eye-popping $78 million -- in lobbying just during the year 2000, the most recent year for which complete figures are available, according to the Center.

The question here: who will ultimately win -- moneyman Tom Kuhn, or America's children?
