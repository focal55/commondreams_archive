Ford Protests Scheduled for 21 Cities on Saturday, June 7
 
SAN FRANCISCO - June 6 - Groups calling on Ford to help America reduce its dependence on oil will hold protests at Ford dealerships in 21 cities this Saturday, June 7. Meanwhile, the Sierra Club has launched a nation-wide advertising campaign timed to Ford's centennial.

"In the 1970s and '80s we lost market share - and good union jobs - to Japanese automakers because Ford executives refused to innovate." said RAN Executive Director Michael Brune. "Ford is once again on the verge of losing its leadership position. Bill Ford's broken promises are bad for American workers, bad for the environment, and bad for national security."

Ford Motor Co. is sponsoring a five-day Centennial blow-out in Dearborn, Michigan, June 12­16 officially titled "The Road is Ours." Ford has hired celebrities Beyoncé, Earth Wind and Fire, and Toby Keith. "We have always said we want the centennial to focus on the future," Ford spokeswoman Karen Hampton told Associated press on May 6.

The national effort is demanding that Ford increase its average fuel efficiency to 50 miles per gallon by 2010 and that, by 2020, it only produce zero-emission cars like those driven by hydrogen fuel cells.

Rainforest Action Network (RAN) and Global Exchange (GX), two national groups that were central in organizing the 1999 protests against the WTO in Seattle wrote in a letter on May 8th to CEO Bill Ford Jr. which said, "Right now, a patriotic American seeking to embrace energy independence by purchasing a high efficiency hybrid must turn to Japanese automakers."

GX and RAN argue that Ford has tried to position itself as an environmental leader while repeatedly breaking its commitments to produce cleaner and more efficient cars. The National Academy of Sciences concluded that current off-the-shelf technology could significantly boost fuel efficiency. Other studies have found that a jump to 40mpg is possible immediately, and that a jump to 60 mpg is achievable with full hybrid engines.

RAN and GX are known for their "market campaigns," which caused industry-wide changes to be led by Nike, Burger King, Home Depot, Citigroup, and Starbucks many other Fortune 500 companies.
