What's Wrong With This Picture? Seven of Eight Men Facing Serious Execution Dates in June are Black or Latino
 
WASHINGTON - June 2 - Seven of the eight people facing serious execution dates in June are African American or Latino, demonstrating unfair bias in the way the death penalty is applied, the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty said today.

Between June 5 and June 27, eight people face serious execution dates in the states of Indiana, Ohio, Oklahoma, Tennessee, Texas and Utah. (A ninth person, Troy Kell of Utah, also has an execution date pending but is expected to receive a stay.) Of the eight people with serious dates, six are African American, one is Latino and one is white.

The eight inmates facing execution, their scheduled dates and their ethnicity are as follows:

Kenneth Charm of Oklahoma, June 5. Charm is black. Kia Johnson of Texas, June 11. Johnson is black. Joseph Trueblood of Indiana, June 13. Trueblood is white. Earnest Martin of Ohio, June 18. Martin is black. Abu-Ali Abdur'Rahman of Tennessee, also June 18. Abdur'Rahman is black. Lewis Williams of Ohio, June 24. Williams is black. Jerome Campbell of Ohio, June 27. Campbell is black. Roberto Arguelles of Utah, June 27. Arguelles is Latino.

"The death penalty is biased in two ways when it comes to race," said Steven W. Hawkins, executive director of the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty. "First, blacks make up 43 percent of death row population, but only 13 percent of the U.S. population generally. Second, and more significantly, the race of the victim often determines who lives and who dies. Of those executed since 1976, 81 percent were convicted of killing white victims. 19 percent were convicted of killing minority victims."

Hawkins noted that a number of the executions scheduled in June dramatically portray problems with the way the death penalty is applied:

****Abdur'Rahman of Tennessee faces execution despite the fact that he is possibly innocent, received grossly ineffective legal representation at trial, was the victim of prosecutorial misconduct and may well be substantially mentally ill. In petitioning for a review of Abdur'Rahman's conviction and sentence before the U.S. Supreme Court, six former state and federal prosecutors in Tennessee wrote that the prosecutor's behavior "fell far short of the standards of our state court system and, indeed, below what we understand the federal constitutional minimum standards to be."

****Campbell of Ohio faces execution despite evidence of innocence. Campbell's innocence claim, which is supported by recent DNA testing, moved the Ohio Adult Parole Board to vote for clemency. Gov. Bob Taft will make the final decision, assuming that further appeals are unsuccessful.

****Arguelles of Utah faces execution, after waiving his appeals, despite serious questions regarding his mental competency. Given a choice between lethal injection and firing squad, Arguelles chose the firing squad and has demanded that his execution proceed without a hood being placed over his head. Arguelles has demonstrated wildly irrational behavior during his court appearances, and in 1998 attempted suicide.

Hawkins also pointed out that three additional people facing execution -- Charm of Oklahoma, and Martin and Williams of Ohio -- have strong claims of mental retardation. The U.S. Supreme Court has ruled that executing people with mental retardation is unconstitutional, but several states either have refused to implement guidelines to adhere to the Court's ruling or are denying the reality that there are people on death row with mental retardation.

"There are many reasons to oppose the death penalty," Hawkins said. "Innocent people are sent to death row. People with mental retardation and mental illness face death sentences, despite their lessened culpability. People with incompetent lawyers or unethical prosecutors get death. Bias permeates the death penalty system. The executions that are scheduled in June vividly demonstrate why it is time to abolish the death penalty."

Hawkins said NCADP is urging its membership to contact the governors and parole boards of Indiana, Ohio, Oklahoma, Texas, Tennessee and Utah and request that the death sentences be commuted. To contact these state officials through NCADP's new Legislative Action Center, please visit www.ncadp.org
