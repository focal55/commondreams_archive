FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE
APRIL 24, 2003
3:38 PM
 CONTACT:  Institute for Public Accuracy
 Sam Husseini, (202) 347-0020; or David Zupan, (541) 484-9167
* Korea * Hizbullah * Palestinian Prime Minister
 
WASHINGTON - April 24 -

  • BILL MESLER, b.mesler@verizon.net
    Mesler is a former editor at the Korea Economic Journal and writes on Korea for a number of outlets. He said today: "The apparent stall in talks between the United States and North Korea threatens to undermine the tremendous progress towards a peaceful solution on the Korean peninsula, progress that had netted South Korean President Kim Dae-Jung a Nobel Peace Prize. It's easy to cast all the blame at North Korea; but the truth is the Bush administration has never been happy with the historic 1994 agreement the Clinton administration had reached with the North Koreans that led to the dismantling of the North Korean nuclear weapons program. While we've heard a constant refrain of North Korean violations, most of which have later proved untrue or exaggerated, there has been little mention of the many U.S. violations that began as soon as the Bush administration took office, including the failure to complete the building of two light-water reactors and to move to a full normalization of the relationship. It is hard now to see how Pyongyang will give up the nuclear card so long as the administration refuses even to guarantee that it will not attack North Korea. This administration's hard-line position ... will harden the North Korean regime's attitude toward the outside world, denying the North Korean people the chance for a better life that peaceful engagement with the West -- and rapprochement with South Korea -- would ultimately bring...."

  • AS'AD ABUKHALIL, abukhali@toto.csustan.edu
    Author of the Historical Dictionary of Lebanon and Bin Laden, Islam and America's New “War on Terrorism," AbuKhalil is professor of political science at California State University at Stanislaus. He interviewed Hizbullah leader Sheikh Hassan Nasrallah last summer for two hours.

  • CHERYL RUBENBERG, mrubenberg@pol.net
    Author of the forthcoming The Palestinians in Search of a Just Peace and several other books on the conflict, including Palestinian Women, Rubenberg lived in the occupied territories from 1997 to 1999. She said today: "I don't think people in the U.S. generally understand the situation. Not only is Israel not committed to peace, it does a lot of things to block peace. The settlements, road building on occupied territory have made a real peace virtually impossible. The West Bank has been sliced up into some 64 cantons, so contrary to what Bush says, a 'viable state' has been gutted by Israel. It is no longer that Palestinian towns surround Israeli settlements; now, Israeli roads, settlements and infrastructure surround Palestinian towns. There is not only resentment among Palestinians against Israel, but Palestinians are also angry at the Palestinian Authority, which has been very corrupt. Mahmoud Abbas has a multimillion-dollar mansion in Gaza, one of the poorest places on earth. Clearly, his appointment as Prime Minister is the result of the Palestinian Authority capitulating to the U.S. and Israel, which are now choosing leaders for the Palestinians -- and all the while Bush is talking about democracy in the region. Abbas has hardly been a popular figure, pulling about 3 percent in opinion polls, as opposed to about 30 percent for Arafat -- though the Palestinians are skeptical of all leaders.... There is open talk of 'transfer' of Palestinians in the Israeli press -- that is ethnic cleansing of Palestinians. Even if that does not occur, the dire economic situation and the sheer difficulty of ordinary life forced on Palestinians by Israel are causing many of them to leave...."
