Public Interest Organizations from Across the Globe Urge President George W. Bush to Halt US Attempts to Derail Global Tobacco Treaty
 
BOSTON - May 14 - A week before the scheduled adoption of the Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC), public interest organizations from across the globe are urging President Bush to halt all US efforts to derail the treaty. Recent attempts by the Bush Administration to weaken the text of the Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC) have sparked sharp criticism. In a letter released today, 67 corporate accountability, public health, environmental, medical, consumer and faith-based organizations from every region of the world urge President Bush to instruct his administration not to interfere with the adoption of the final FCTC text at the World Health Assembly next week. Organizations that have signed onto the letter include: Infact, Center for International Environmental Law, Zambia Consumers Association, ASH UK, Consumers International Asia Pacific Office, the Turkish Medical Association and the Canadian Environmental Law Association.

"The Bush Administration is working down to the wire to derail the world’s first public health treaty. Pressing to re-open debate on a text that is supported by more than 170 countries is cowboy diplomacy at its worst. Across the globe people are urging President Bush to stop doing Philip Morris/Altria’s dirty work, and to instruct the US representatives to the World Health Assembly to join with the rest of the world in prioritizing global public health," says Kathryn Mulvey, Executive Director of the corporate accountability organization Infact.

The Bush Administration recently indicated to the World Health Organization that the United States will not sign the treaty unless governments re-open negotiations and allow each country to pick and choose which parts of the treaty will apply to them. There is concern among public health and corporate accountability organizations that the US tactic could undermine the near-consensus that has been reached and threaten the future of the FCTC process. A March editorial in the tobacco industry trade journal, Tobacco International, expressed optimism that the US would push to re-open the debate on reservations, and "unravel the whole process."

"With these outrageous attempts to weaken or derail the FCTC, President Bush would force other countries to smoke the transnationals’ coffin nails. He is exchanging the world citizens’ health for the tobacco companies’ license to continue killing in the future," says Dr. Hatai Chitanondh, President of the Thailand Health Promotion Institute and a member of the Thai delegation to the FCTC negotiations.

Signatories to today’s letter to President Bush point out that the implications of US efforts to subvert the FCTC extend beyond tobacco control. The letter states: "As public interest organizations across the United States and the globe, we are concerned that the United States has developed a pattern in recent history of negotiating environmental, human rights and other important multilateral treaties down to the lowest common denominator, and then failing to support them when they are completed. The Kyoto Protocol, Basel Convention on Hazardous Wastes, Convention on Biological Diversity, Convention on the Rights of the Child, Rome Statute on the International Criminal Court, and Landmine Ban Treaty provide a few examples of how the U.S. government has isolated itself from the global community on issues of enormous humanitarian and environmental consequence."

"The Bush Administration’s behavior in the FCTC negotiations has been disingenuous at best and, at worst, simply lawless," says Glenn Wiser, Staff Attorney for the Center for International Environmental Law. "The Administration has justified its advocacy for Big Tobacco with bogus legal arguments that would make a first-year law student blush. It’s time for this government to demonstrate that it cares more about the health, welfare, and security of people than about the profits of an industry that deals in addictive substances that kill."
