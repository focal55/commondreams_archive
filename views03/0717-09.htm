Published on Thursday, July 17, 2003 by CommonDreams.org
Is Bush Really Lying If He's Only Saying What People Tell Him To Say?
by Matt Peiken
 

When the President of the United States speaks, we assume the words coming from his mouth are his, or if not of his invention, at least of his adoption. We expect the president to take ownership of the speeches written for him, to have a concrete grasp of their content, that by speaking the words publicly, he endorses that content and holds himself accountable for the substance of what he's saying.

This expectation is all too reasonable when we consider the alternative, that we view the president as simply a mouthpiece and puppet for others inside and outside the White House who actually conceive, create and dictate policy. The suggestion is absurd and laughable, of course, so let's return to the premise that the president thinks and speaks for himself.

Until now, the White House under George W. Bush has gone to great lengths to convince the public this is so. The Bush administration expressed outrage in March 2002 when word leaked that it wasn't the president, but speechwriter David Frum, who coined the term "axis of evil" and fed it into the president's famed address. Soon after, Frum lost his job.

So it was astonishing, yet hardly surprising, to see the manner in which Bush and his administration handled the blowback from the revelation that, contrary to Bush's assertion in a State of the Union address leading up to war, Iraq never attempted to acquire uranium from Africa for a nuclear weapons program.

While higher-ups in the administration fretted publicly, and uniformly, how such bogus intelligence found its way into the president's speech, the president himself remained conspicuously mute and distanced from the issue. Only after CIA director George Tenet became the fall guy did the president speak at all about the controversy, and only then in a deflective expression of benevolence ("I've got confidence in George Tenet") and with a dismissive wave that he considers the matter closed (Did he ever acknowledge it as open?).

On Monday, Bush assessed the intelligence he received from the CIA as "darn good," which falls somewhere between "really good" and "sorta good" and, if we're to take the president at his word, indicates the intelligence certainly wasn't of the "extremely good" variety Americans would have demanded as a prerequisite for war.

While many inside and outside Congress are demanding a full investigation of what the president knew and when he knew it, Congress should instead probe whether the president ever knows what he's saying.

There are a number of books and Web sites dedicated to documenting the verbal mix-ups, miscues and muddled messages that fall from Bush's mouth like oats from a horse's maw. One of the most thorough and best-organized of the Web sites is dubyaspeak.com (Motto: "We record the damage"), where the president's oratory mistakes have piled so high, site organizers have divided them into more than a dozen categories (war, economy, education, himself, etc.).

Certainly, there's much to laugh at here. But as one combs through the site, revealing layer upon layer of befuddlement, the humor gives way to head-shaking disbelief and the quiet fright of a deepened understanding of this president's intellectual incapacity.

Along with dozens of simple grammatical gaffes are more serious contradictions, misleading and erroneous statements presented as truth and sentences so confusing they defy deciphering.

The president, who expressed ironclad certainty in the lead-up to war that Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction, on June 9 of this year indicated his certainty was far less than concrete when he said this in Washington, D.C.: "I am absolutely convinced, with time, we'll find out that they did have a weapons program." Had he said this in his State of the Union address, far fewer Americans would have supported the war.

Bush certainly had the welfare of American troops in Iraq close to his heart on July 2, when the cowboy unleashed this: "There are some who feel that the conditions are such that they can attack us there. My answer is 'Bring 'em on!' " The killing and wounding of American soldiers continues unabated.

Anyone wondering how half of Americans came to believe Saddam Hussein was connected with the September 11 terrorist attacks need only blame Bush for the perceived link.

First, there were statements such as this from the president, on Sept. 28, 2002, describing Saddam Hussein to an audience in Phoenix, Ariz., as "a man which hates -- who hates America, a man who loves to link up with al Qaeda, a man who is a true threat to America, to Israel, to anybody in the neighborhood."

In his prime time press conference of March 6, 2003, Bush addressed a question regarding the risks of going to war with Iraq in this way: "The price of doing nothing exceeds the price of taking action, if we have to. We'll do everything we can to minimize the loss of life. The price of the attacks on America, the cost of the attacks on America on September the 11th were enormous. They were significant."

People wondering about Bush's involvement in foreign policy won't find any comfort with this July 3 preview of his recent trip to Africa: "We'll be going to Goree Island, where I'll be giving a speech about race, race in the world, race as it relates to Africa and America. And we're in the process of writing it. I can't give you any highlights of the speech yet because I, frankly, haven't seen it."

Here's Bush's understanding of the economy, from an address in Canton, Ohio; April 24, 2003: "We had an emergency and a recession, which affected the revenue growth of the U.S. Treasury. I mean, the stock market went down.

Some of the pie-in-the-sky projections didn't make, and the investors said 'Oops.' The numbers weren't real. The investors said, 'Well, it looks like the days of everything is going up may end.' And so people started selling, and the markets went down. That affected the revenues coming into the U.S. Treasury. Recession -- negative growth means less revenues. And so, of course, we've got a deficit."

These are mere drops in the drool bucket, but they explain the defensive, protective instincts of this White House. When contained largely to local events that aren't broadcast beyond favorable soundbites, Bush's words are free to hit an audience with little scrutiny. People link and take home keywords such as "Saddam Hussein," "al Qaeda" and "recession," and suddenly the president is fighting terrorism and helping the economy.

Of course, the press, which is more likely to dissect the substance of whatever the president says, sees and hears far more run-around from White House spokesman Ari Fleischer than it will ever gain in direct Q&A with the president. This is no accident - everyone in the White House understands that the less face time Bush has with reporters, the better.

Focusing on whether the president knowingly presented false information in one piece of a State of the Union address unduly narrows the scope of the problem (after all, perhaps Bush meant to suggest Iraq sought geraniums, not uranium). We expect more from the President of the United States - not only in his full understanding of the words he speaks and messages he delivers, but in his acceptance of the responsibility for them.

While Congress should investigate the intelligence leading up to both September 11 and the war with Iraq, there appears little need to further investigate the intelligence at the top of our executive branch.

Matt Peiken is an arts and features staff writer at the St. Paul (Minn.) Pioneer Press. E-mail him at mpeiken@msn.com .
