Published on Monday, September 1, 2003 by the Denver Post
Texas 11 Fight for Democracy
Editorial
 

Two of the Texas 11 sought refuge last week in Colorado, of all places, a state mired in its own dirty fight over a midnight gerrymander.

They found the climate pleasing, but discovered the political air here stinks as much as it does in Texas.

The 11 Texas lawmakers, all Senate Democrats, fled the Lone Star State after Republican legislators tried to ram through a redistricting plan that would increase the number of Republicans sent to Washington from their state. Worse yet, Republicans tried suspending legislative rules to push their plan and stifled the voices of the Democrats and the thousands they represent.

So the senators fled to New Mexico. Without them, the Texas Senate didn't have a quorum and couldn't proceed. A second special session ended this past week without a single bill being passed, and the governor there has threatened to call a surprise third session. That way, he can send state troopers after the absconding legislators - if they dare tread deep into the heart of Texas.

It's beyond ridiculous.

The Republican power grab is a gross abuse of its majority status. The special sessions have been a monumental waste of taxpayer money. (They already had a $10 billion shortfall.)

And the redistricting effort, if it succeeds, will only further disenfranchise minorities who are being effectively silenced by being packed into their own districts.

The political backbiting has reached epic proportions. The Texas 11 have been fined $57,000 each, have been stripped of their Capitol parking privileges and had constituent mailings restricted. (Texas politicos even tried to get Homeland Security agents to chase down House Democrats who fled to Oklahoma during the regular session for the same reason.)

The Texans came to Colorado to voice solidarity for Democrats battling our state's own midnight gerrymander in the courts.

Lawmakers here in 2002 couldn't agree on how to remap the state's seven congressional districts, so a judge drew the lines. Because it was competitively mapped, it arguably drew the two best candidates in Bob Beauprez and Mike Feeley. But apparently Beauprez's narrow, 121-vote victory in the 7th Congressional District scared enough Republicans that, once they regained a slim majority in the state Senate, they pushed through a redistricting map that strengthens their hold on the 7th.

Democrats say it's part of a national movement, orchestrated by Karl Rove, President Bush's chief political adviser, to steal elections.

It does look orchestrated, and it definitely stinks - in Texas and Colorado.

"If the majority changes the rules when it needs to win, democracy loses," says Texas state Sen. Eliot Shapleigh.
