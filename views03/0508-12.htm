Of Star Trek and Our Founding Fathers
by Mike Bryan
 

The ideals and values expressed in the written words of our Founding Fathers continue to dominate our loftiest expectations of our nation. America represents democracy. We identify our nation with liberty, equality, truth, justice, civil rights, humanity, dignity, integrity, progressiveness, and freedom. We picture ourselves as a role model for other nations, a measure towards which they should strive, a trusted friend and partner.

Star Trek optimistically envisions the future of our planet as a time during which all people will unite and work harmoniously together. We will join peoples from other worlds in forming an egalitarian federation that obeys the prime directive of never interfering in the internal affairs of other nations and peoples, that strives for compassion and understanding, and that considers societies dominated by the desire for monetary gain and conquest as primitive.

President Bush offers America a totally different future.

Much of his vision derives from that of the Project for the New American Century: as the world's only superpower the United States should dominate the world's resources for economic, strategic, and political advantage. From that commanding position the US can build an empire unencumbered by the rules, regulations, and requirements of others. Domestically, business interests and the enrichment of America's most wealthy individuals supersede all other interests, needs, and rights.

Star Trek would likely portray the PNAC as a radical neoconservative fringe group with strong upper-level government connections and members bent on pillaging the riches of the world and subverting the interests of their government and its people for their own selfish goals and profit.

Mr. Bush now intends to export his vision of democracy to Afghanistan, Iraq, and the rest of the Middle East.

Shortly after terrorists slammed airplanes into the World Trade Center and the Pentagon, killing over three thousand people, Mr. Bush sought retribution from those responsible for attacking these symbols of capitalism and militarism. He claimed they attacked symbols of democracy and liberty. History may side with Mr. Bush: democracy and liberty just might equal capitalism imposed by military means.

While our Founding Fathers spoke and wrote eloquently, they also predominantly represented the interests of business and the land's most wealthy individuals. Historians argue they chose their lofty words to help recruit more soldiers and support from the lower classes of colonial society for the Revolution. They designed a government that excluded the native people, slaves, non-landowners, and women from voting and other rights.

Throughout our nation's history many other leaders have spoken great words, sometimes meaningfully, too often manipulatively, while government and business have predominantly worked hand in hand. Yet the historic preeminence of such practices does not necessarily validate their desirability or correctness.

In spite of whatever might have been the real intentions behind our Founding Fathers great words, many Americans truly believe in the ideals and values they expressed. Many also believe in those envisioned by Star Trek.

Before Mr. Bush progresses too far in imposing his concepts on the rest of the world, the people of this nation should determine whether or not they agree with his vision. A national forum should develop to openly explore, debate, and determine the position of the American people on the major issues that define our nation and chart our future. We should reassess America's goals, values, ideals, and mission.

Americans could choose whether they prefer a government run as a government or a government run as a business. In the process of reaching such a basic decision debates would likely surface over such topics as national health insurance, privatized water systems, ending mail delivery to those in outlying areas, and letting the poor starve and the weak die.

Americans could decide whether they want people or money to control their government, whether they want increased civil rights and liberties or increased government surveillance and restrictions, whether they want freedom of information or even greater media consolidation and greater corporate and government secrecy, whether they want more financial independence or more power and wealth to consolidate into fewer and fewer hands, and whether they want international relations conducted through building friendships and understanding among equals or as superiors solving problems militarily, bombing other nations into submission, and building an empire.

Some degree of urgency exists in creating such a forum since without it Mr. Bush's representation of our essence wins by default. Americans still have a choice: the future promised by our Founding Fathers and envisioned by Star Trek or the one proposed by Mr. Bush.

The world awaits our decision.
