War-Dazed
There are Other Things Going On... But They All Circle Back to the War
by Susan Estrich
 

I sat down this morning determined to write about something other than the war. After all, there are other things going on...

The Democrats have come up with a new schedule for the nominating season that, if history is any guide at all, is certain to produce exactly the opposite of what the conventional wisdom predicts. So, front-loading the system to favor the well-financed candidates is just as likely to give a boost to Howard Dean, the dark-horse who could pull off the better-than-expected showing in Iowa and New Hampshire, as it is to propel John Kerry, the early favorite, into locking up the nomination by March. Of course, how much the Democratic nomination is worth depends in part on the war.

I paid $30 to fill my car with gas yesterday, and I gave up my SUV years ago. And you're telling me this war isn't about oil...

The woman who Roman Polanski raped 25 years ago has shown up on the talk-show circuit, saying the best director nominee should be judged for his art, and half the people in Hollywood think the studio that made the movie is orchestrating the campaign, and the other half think his competitors are, with everyone denying any role. As the mother of a 13-year-old, which is how old the woman was when she says Polanski forced her to have sex, I find the whole thing appalling, but not as appalling as a war.

Bill Clinton and Bob Dole have signed up as television commentators, because they want to provide a "thoughtful" debate in 45 scripted seconds apiece on "60 Minutes." President Clinton's previous most memorable appearance on "60 Minutes" was during the New Hampshire primary, when he denied having had an affair with Gennifer Flowers. Don Hewitt, the Executive Producer of "60 Minutes," reportedly told the former president that he could be the next Edward R. Murrow. Murrow made his name during World War II. Will Clinton be the next Scud Stud? Dole was far more eager for the job -- 45 seconds with Clinton being far better than 30-second impotence ads. This would all be very funny, were anyone in the mood to laugh.

But it's hard to laugh these days, and it's hard to avoid thinking about the damn war. At my local synagogue, where I take my children to religious school, security has been enhanced. Like generations of Jewish children, my kids are always looking for excuses to skip, complaining of boredom, wanting to play instead of study. Usually, I jokingly tell them that it is part of Jewish tradition to complain, to be forced to attend, that someday they will force their own children.

Yesterday, we didn't joke. Colleen Rowley, one of Time's Women of the Year, the author of the now famous -- or infamous -- 9-11 memo about the inadequacy of the FBI's counterterrorism efforts, has written another memo, this one arguing that the country and the bureau are ill-prepared for the likely outbreak of domestic terrorism that a war against Iraq will provoke. I do not think I am alone in being far more terrified of attacks at home by terrorists provoked by an increasingly unpopular American war than I am of Saddam Hussein.

Supporters of the war, faced with increasing opposition at home and abroad, have taken to raising the decibel of the debate, accusing those of us at home who oppose this war of hating President Bush. I don't hate the president. I'm from the old school that believes it's not good for our politics to brand criticism as slander, disagreements as treason and opposition as hatred. I like Karl Rove, the president's top political adviser. I respect the presidency, and I have no reason to hate a man just because I didn't vote for him.

But make no mistake. Bush may not be listening, but that doesn't mean the opposition is irrelevant. This is becoming Bush's war, which means that if things go poorly, or if terrorism takes lives, or if there is no money left at the end for initiatives that people consider more important, there will be a political price to be paid. I don't hate him, but I am beginning to wonder about his competence and worry about his self-righteousness.

Imagine Bill Clinton were still president, and doing the same things. Imagine that he had lost the support of our allies; the European coalition had fallen apart; a nuclear North Korea was a fait accompli; the economy was stumbling; 200,000 soldiers were halfway around the world, hoping their chemical warfare suits don't have holes; anti-Americanism around the world was at a fevered pitch; and we were being told to buy duct tape.

Would the president's critics be as civil as I have been? They would be calling for his impeachment.

Susan Estrich is the Robert Kingsley Professor of Law and Political Science at the University of Southern California Law Center. She serves on the Board of Editorial Contributors for USA Today, and her recent works include: "Who Needs Feminism, Sex and Power?" (2000), "Getting Away With Murder: How Politics is Destroying the Criminal Justice System" (1998) and "Making the Case for Yourself: A Diet Book for Smart Women."
