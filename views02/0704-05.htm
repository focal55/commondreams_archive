Published on Thursday, July 4, 2002 in the Long Island, NY Newsday
Workers Are Big Losers in Corporate Scandals
by Marie Cocco
 

It's the workers, stupid.

Not the accountants. Not the corrupt corporate chieftains. Not even Martha Stewart. You can lock them all up and the bottom line - the bottom line you can trust, now and forever - is that the political fix will not trickle down.

The workers of WorldCom, of Enron, of Xerox, of whatever company is next up in headline news, cannot be made whole. No paycheck will arrive for laid-off workers because some congressional committee tinkers with accounting regulations. Nobody's retirement account is replenished with tough talk about corporate crooks. You can't pay for gas with that.

"The longer you've been here, the more you lost," said a clerk in the downtown Washington office of MCI, the giant long-distance company owned by the humongous conglomerate WorldCom, the latest icon of corporate corruption. She'd heard about someone at the WorldCom complex in suburban Virginia who'd lost $400,000 on the company's stock in his 401(k).

No one in her building seems to have been hit so hard, she said. But then, they are too scared to talk much, even to each other. They're barred altogether from talking to outsiders.

"They said not to talk, if you want another paycheck," said a legal secretary on a cigarette break.

This scandal is not quite so miserable as the Enron mess. There's no evidence corporate masters prevented employees from selling their WorldCom stock, even as the top guys cashed out. WorldCom did not require employees to hold its shares, chief executive John Sidgmore told reporters at a news conference Tuesday. So some did, some didn't.

But WorldCom is a New Economy company and that means it wants nothing to do with those quaint Old Economy customs, like guaranteed pensions for loyal long-timers. In 1999, the company reported $1.12 billion in assets in defined-contribution pension plans, according to Nelson Information, a financial reporting company.

These are funded by worker contributions. Pension researchers say America reached a milestone about two years ago: Workers now put more of their own money into pension funds and retirement savings plans than their employers do.

Most of the WorldCom money was in the 401(k) plan but about a third was in an employee stock-ownership plan, according to the 1999 report. That means WorldCom stock. It closed yesterday at 22 cents, down from its peak of $64 three years ago.

Three years is an interesting time frame. It happens to be the length of time the Bush administration believes employers should be allowed to force workers to hold company stock if, for example, the stock is awarded as the company "match" in a 401(k) account. This was a proposal the White House made after Enron exploded.

No one talks about Enron anymore. Kenny Boy has been upstaged by conniving acccountants everywhere. The tearful Enron workers who lost both their jobs and their life savings got their day before the cameras, but not their pension legislation.

The House passed a toothless measure that allows workers to get investment advice from firms picked by none other than the sponsors of their retirement plans - that is, their employers. The administration opposed efforts to limit the amount of company stock that can be held in 401(k)s. The Senate passed nothing, because of Republican objections and divisions among the Democrats controlling the chamber.

Next week, the Senate is to debate and probably pass a tough-on-accountants-and-corporate-crooks bill. Its chief sponsor, Sen. Paul Sarbanes (D-Md.), delivered the Democratic response to President George W. Bush's radio address last Saturday.

"Corporate wrongdoing is being felt not just at the boardroom table but at the kitchen table as well," Sarbanes said.

But it hurts more in the kitchen than in the conference room. One small way to ease the pain is for Congress to pass, and the president to sign, the pension protections both promised after Enron but still haven't delivered.
