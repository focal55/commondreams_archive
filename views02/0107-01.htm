Published on Monday, January 7, 2002 by Common Dreams
A Tale of Two Debts
by Seth Sandronsky
 
Private borrowing was the engine for the U.S. economy during the 1990s, as the decade ended with the federal budget in surplus. And the current recession will be a year old this March. What to do? We turn our attention to President Bush.

“We need a plan to lengthen unemployment benefits, we need a plan to shorten the recession,” the president said in his recent radio broadcast.

Some 1.2 million Americans have been fired during the past 10 months, with another million driven into part-time work, the Wall Street Journal recently reported, citing data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The official Dec. jobless rate was 5.8 percent, up from a revised Nov. rate of 5.6 percent.

The president faults Senate Democrats for blocking his economic recovery plan.

Democrat Tom Daschle, Senate majority leader, took a different view of Bush’s plan, while supporting his war in Afghanistan. For example, Daschle criticized the president’s $1.35 trillion income-tax cut for removing the federal government’s “flexibility.” His tax cut has worsened the recession and is leading to a projected federal deficit that will hinder an economic revival, Daschle added.

On one hand, federal deficit spending is supposed to be the wrong policy to help the economy grow. Presumably, public borrowing is a bad thing. Case in point is the Clinton administration’s success in sustaining growth and creating a federal budget surplus that emerged in 1997. Some claim that this improved the prospects for long-term interest rates.

On the other hand, the engine of the American economy last decade was private debt. Consumers and corporations doubled their debt in the 1990s. How? Lots of foreign money flowed into America, attracted partly by the high value of the dollar. Overseas financing enabled the U.S. economy to be the “consumer of the last resort,” thus keeping the global market system afloat.

Then America went into recession last March. National economies that relied on U.S. corporations and consumers buying their exports with borrowed money began to sink. As global economic activity led by America contracted, business investment began to decline. Consequently, payrolls shrank. U.S. workers had less income. Their ability to repay and take on more debt (the linchpin of expansion in the 1990s) also fell.

In the meantime, hope was high that monetary policy would maintain U.S. economic growth. But cutting the cost to borrow money has proven to be an inefficient form of economic stimulus, at best. In 2001, the Federal Reserve cut interest rates roughly once a month. Growth has not been forthcoming.

What of federal deficit spending to stimulate the U.S. economy? If not now, one wonders when absent monetary policy to stimulate a recovery in 2002?

Meanwhile, for millions of Americans seeing unemployment rise as part-time work grows and full-time jobs shrink, such questions take on a special urgency. Those out of a job before the recession began are increasingly off the political radar screen. What will it take for Democrats and Republicans to wake up and legislate as if the American people mattered?

Seth Sandronsky is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento’s progressive newspaper ssandron@hotmail.com
