The Color of Foreshadowing
by Seth Sandronsky
 

In the U.S., the attack on nonwhite people generally has foreshadowed the current assault on the general public’s civil rights. The concealment of this connection reveals its importance.

That is, skin-color has been a fault line in the class war waged by the few against the many in the U.S. This aggression has taken different forms in different places.

One recent form is the fraudulent Drug War. In brief, this war has been an exercise in state power (executive, legislative and judicial branches of government) against the nonwhite population in the U.S.

The Drug War has partly resulted in the incarceration of low-level illegal drug sellers and users, mainly people of color, with longer and longer prison sentences. Locking them up has dovetailed with the attack on living and working standards by the owners of the country during the past 25 years of slow/no economic growth.

Racial profiling was also a weapon of state power against people of color during the voting rights purge in the Florida 2000 elections. What goes around comes around.

Now, under the War on Terror, the white population in the U.S. is also the target of state power. In the name of security, their civil rights are at-risk.

Perhaps some with white skin believed, or never perhaps thought they could be profiled by the state. That was for the other woman or man.

Well, that’s not how the system works during crises. We live in such a time now.

During a crisis, the nation’s rulers swing state power with full force against those who dare to dissent. The FBI’s COINTELPRO campaigns to ruin and wreck the Civil Rights and anti-Vietnam War movements of the 1960s are cases in point.

Richard Cohen, whose work is distributed by the Washington Post Writers Group, recently criticized Attorney General John Ashcroft. Cohen continued: “Not since J. Edgar (Hoover, former FBI director) has Washington seen such a publicity hound.”

But using the cloud cover of national security to roll back the civil rights of U.S. citizens is hardly a matter of Ashcroft seeking the political spotlight. State power has been used against organized labor and the freedom movements of nonwhite people for decades.

For reasons of keeping the nation and the world safe for the market economy, the owners of the country have relied on the state to keep the U.S. working population under control. Too much freedom threatens the power, privilege and wealth of these rulers.

Accordingly, those who run the system have billions of reasons to maintain divisions within the diverse working-class. Some workers perpetuate these skin-color divisions, to their great disadvantage.

In fact, the blind spot of white workers in their relations with nonwhite workers in the U.S. is perhaps the major point of disunity. Throughout the nation's history, this division has served to weaken workers as a class.

But that weakness has changed and can change again. Two examples are agitation against slavery and for the eight-hour day after the Civil War, and mobilization against racism and for increased labor union strength during the 1930s.

Weakening the color line in U.S. society benefits the majority, a point obscured by the priesthood of politicians and pundits. The wielding of state power against those whose white-privilege previously shielded them follows from the state’s treatment of its nonwhite population.

Hush now? I think not.

Seth Sandronsky is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento's progressive newspaper. Email: ssandron@hotmail.com
