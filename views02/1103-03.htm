Published on Sunday, November 3, 2002 by the Toronto Star
West's Policies Miss the Mark
by Haroon Siddiqui
 

ELECTIONS IN TURKEY, Pakistan and Bahrain, and an electoral crisis in Israel — seemingly unrelated events — give a glimpse into our paradoxical relationship with the Muslim world. So do two other issues: Chechnya and American ethnic profiling.

Take the last first. Shame on Bob Runciman, Ontario's minister for public safety and security, for applauding the U.S. policy of fingerprinting and photographing Canadians born in selected Muslim nations upon their arrival in the United States. Good for Foreign Affairs Minister Bill Graham for forcing Washington to back off. Even legitimate American safety concerns cannot justify singling out those already given security clearances here before being granted citizenship.

In standing up for the principle that "a Canadian is a Canadian for all purposes," Graham has served a greater purpose: signalling that the West and its allies are at war with terrorists, not Muslims.

We cannot be prescribing democracy for Muslim nations while violating the basic rights of our own Muslim citizens. That's what the Bush administration has been doing, and paying the price in devalued moral currency.

Canada's stance will, in a small way, help assuage the universal Muslim grievance that they are victims of double standards, in lost rights and lost lives, from Chechnya (100,000 dead) and Kashmir (60,000 dead) to Palestine (35 years of occupation).

That sense of injustice partly explains willing recruits for Al Qaeda and other groups.

Beyond the war on terrorism, we need a political strategy, not merely a marketing campaign to soft sell American virtues, as the State Department is trying to do with its $255 million propaganda blitz, launched in Indonesia last week. Diffusing anti-Americanism needs real policies — helping to find political, not merely military, solutions to the bloody crises feeding Muslim despair, and being more consistent in our dealings with Muslims.

As much as we need to bring Russia within the Western orbit, and George W. Bush needs Russian support for his planned war on Iraq, neither should come at the expense of permitting Vladimir Putin to "wipe out" the secessionist Chechens. Even while allowing him room to deal with terrorists forcefully, Putin must be encouraged to abandon Stalinist ethnic cleansing and to participate in the French and Danish efforts at political negotiations.

There is even less chance of a military solution to the Israeli-Palestinian crisis. Labour leader Binyamin Ben-Eliezer said so in pulling out of Ariel Sharon's coalition government.

Sharon has been deploying force for 20 years, starting with the 1982 invasion of Lebanon to stop "PLO terrorism." He has brought nothing but misery to innocent Israelis and Palestinians alike, notwithstanding Yasser Arafat's bankrupt leadership and the rise in vile suicide bombings.

The collapse of the Likud-led government has reduced, not improved, the immediate prospect of a peaceful solution. Sharon faces a vote of non-confidence tomorrow. Should he lose, he must call an election, which he might win. If he survives the parliamentary test, he will do so with the support of rightist or religious parties even more hard line than he.

Such is the ebb and flow of electoral politics in Israel. But in Turkey, where voters go to the polls today, democracy will not be allowed to unfold as it should.

The mildly Islamic Justice party may win the most seats, but its popular leader, Tayyip Erdogan, has been barred from contesting a seat. He was once convicted and jailed for reading a protest poem in public. His party, too, may be banned, as was its predecessor, the Welfare party.

"Secular" Turkey prosecutes people of faith in politics. If church-going Bush, Tony Blair or Stockwell Day, or members of Israel's religious parties, were citizens of Turkey, they would be barred from public office for life.

Yet we hold up Turkey as a model of a "moderate" Muslim state. Ironically, it remains too Muslim for entry to the European Union!

The paradox is partly explained by the West's historic and contemporary reluctance to deal with political Islam. This led to crushing or diluting democracy through puppet regimes. The policy has fanned the very militancy it was designed to arrest.

In Pakistan, where Gen. Pervez Musharraf is emulating the Turkish model, Islamic parties had their best showing ever in an election after campaigning on nothing more concrete than anti-Americanism.

They had never won more than 16 per cent of the vote. Last month, they garnered 25 per cent.

In tiny Bahrain, home of the U.S. Fifth Fleet, the majority Shiites boycotted the recent election, protesting the impotency of the advisory parliament. As welcome as the vote was, it was not the democratic revolution the American media made it out to be.

None of this is to say that Muslims are blameless for failing to produce a single workable democratic model, failing to come to terms with pluralism, and failing to abandon some of the most barbaric practices against each other, especially women. Rather, it is to suggest that we, too, must rethink our approach if we hope for sanity to prevail.

Haroon Siddiqui is The Star's editorial page editor emeritus.
