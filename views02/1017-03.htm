Published on Thursday, October 17, 2002 by the Seattle Times
Asian Nations Don't Want to Live in Fear ... of US
by Floyd J. McKay
 

HONOLULU — A dozen young journalists from across Asia listened carefully to the words of President Bush as he addressed Americans on his need for congressional authority to go to war against Iraq. For most, it was their first visit to this country. The journalists are Jefferson Fellows at the East-West Center in Honolulu, beginning a month-long introduction to U.S. policies, politics and media.

Initial reactions were measured, in the polite manner of Asians — even journalists. A day later, they reflected on what they heard and what they knew of their own countries. The reflections are not encouraging for hawkish elements of American leadership.

No one was applauding, several were still noncommittal, but there was a strong air of concern.

An Indonesian reporter recalled Bush's words, "we will not live in fear," and suggested that others, too, had the right not to live in fear — and many are fearful of this proposed war against Iraq. Put simply, much of Asia is afraid of us, our rashness and our lack of long-term staying power.

Shaheen Salahuddin, an outspoken Pakistani television reporter and anchor, was most forceful. There will be no support in Pakistan, she said, and radical movements will be encouraged. Three days later, hard-line Muslim factions were victorious in the Pakistani parliamentary vote.

Salahuddin was critical of American abandonment of Afghanistan after the Russian pullout, citing that as an underlying factor in the rise of the Taliban. Afghan refugees, more than a million, are a destabilizing factor in Pakistan, she said, and many will stay because conditions even in the worst parts of Pakistan are better than those in Afghanistan.

Pakistan is one of two Muslim nations with reporters in the Jefferson program this year. The other is Indonesia, largest Muslim nation in the world.

Bambang Harymurti, editor-in-chief of Tempo magazine and one of Indonesia's most-respected journalists, was a guest of the program, and discussed the threat of a Muslim backlash in Indonesia. He made his comments before the bombing Saturday in Bali.

In respect to Iraq, Harymurti said a "quick and clean" campaign could be positive, if it brought regime change and the destruction in Iraq was minimal. But he said he feared, instead, a "messy, another Vietnam" type of action, and that would be a serious blow for moderate Muslim nations such as Indonesia.

Indonesia, he told the group, houses radical Islamists but they are a minority and not supported by most Indonesians. Help democracies develop in nations such as Indonesia, he pleaded: "It's better for outsiders to let us create our own (democratic leaders). ... Americans want instant democracy and you cannot create instant democracy."

Harymurti believes Indonesia must be taken more seriously by Western powers, and could be a test of whether democracy and Islam can co-exist. Indonesia has moved throughout history from Buddhism to Hinduism to Islam, changes coming through trade with nations holding those faiths, he noted. The result is a largely secular country in its politics and government, although 90 percent of its population is Muslim.

Although journalists from Pakistan and Indonesia were most outspoken, having the most at risk as the U.S. confronts an Islamic state, other Asian journalists were skeptical of the Bush initiative.

Chit Win Maung, a soft-spoken magazine editor from Myanmar (Burma), expressed concern that the United States has never started a war in the past, that it was against the ideals that others believe Americans have.

Others are still dealing with the trauma of conflicts more horrific than 9/11. A young Cambodian reporter and editor, Thet Sambath, described how the trials of Khmer Rouge leaders still split his nation, which lost 2 million people to the ravages of Pol Pot.

In much of Asia, noted Indonesian reporter Ardian Gesuri, serious economic problems are the priority of governments that would be hard-pressed to deal with them if an American attack on Iraq creates an Islamic backlash.

One of the functions of the Jefferson Fellowships, an East-West Center program for 35 years, is to broaden the horizons of both American and Asian-Pacific fellows. Asian journalists, close to the pulse of their nations, could do much to broaden the outlook of Americans who believe invading Iraq is a slam-dunk without other repercussions.

You won't convince Pakistan's Shaheen Salahuddin, Indonesia's Bambang Harymurti, Myanmar's Chit Win Maung or most of the other dozen journalists in Honolulu that such is the case. They have seen plenty of terrorism, plenty of violence, and they know better.

Floyd J. McKay, a journalism professor at Western Washington University, is a former Jefferson Fellow. He spoke last week at the 35th anniversary of the Jefferson Fellowships.
