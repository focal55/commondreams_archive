Published on Wednesday, January 16, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Right-Wing Death Squads
 United States Must Take Steps in Colombia Now to Keep Paramilitary Forces from Killing More Innocent People
by Frida Ghitis
 
THE VILLAGERS El of Salado know why thousands of people in Southern Colombia have been fleeing in terror. A few months ago, El Salado endured an ordeal that could soon be re-enacted on a much larger scale elsewhere in the country: a massacre of civilians by right-wing paramilitary forces, with the acquiescence of the country's military.

Peace talks between the government and leftist guerrillas are on the brink of collapse. A last-minute agreement Monday night -- just four hours before 12,000 troops backed by tanks were to roll into rebel territory -- provided a few more days to find a way out of the impasse, but prospects look dim.

The rebels now have six days to come up with a timetable for a cease-fire, or the president's order that they leave the Switzerland-size zone they were allowed to control will be reinstated.

If that happens, the rivers of blood that have flowed across Colombia will begin running faster and deeper with the blood of soldiers, rebels and innocent civilians.

Colombian military forces are poised for a major escalation of the civil war. Now is precisely the moment for Washington to tell Colombia, in absolutely unequivocal terms, that paramilitary fighters must not be allowed to target civilian populations in the rebel enclave or anywhere else.

In scores of towns throughout Colombia, the paramilitary's particular brand of horror has left scars that may never heal. On a Friday morning several months ago, a terrifying sight startled the people of El Salado: Hundreds of armed men marched into the center of town. One pulled out a list and started calling out names. In the next three days, those on the list would be tortured, beaten and stabbed.

The armed men identified themselves as members of Colombia's paramilitary force, the so-called United Self Defense of Colombia (AUC), a right-wing, anti- guerrilla vigilante force responsible for most, though not all, of the civilian deaths in the civil war. The AUC was started by ranchers and relatives of those killed by the country's leftist guerrillas. In El Salado, they accused the people on the list of helping left-wing guerrillas.

A few miles away, the official Colombian army had set up roadblocks, keeping villagers from escaping and human-rights monitors from entering the town.

In the first six months of 2001, the Colombian army reported nearly 100 separate massacres that left 568 civilians dead. The guerrillas are guilty of many of the murders, but most are committed by the paramilitary AUC, often with the complicity of the country's army. And it is the AUC whose actions the United States can prevent by demanding that government forces refuse to permit death-squads to do their horrific work.

The United States is the principal sponsor of Colombia's military. Colombia is the third-largest recipient of U.S. aid, after Israel and Egypt. The aid program is a controversial structure designed, in theory, to fight the drug trade.

Because the many parties to Colombia's conflict, principally the leftist guerrillas, receive funding from the drug cartels, the United States can claim to fight drugs while, in fact, supporting the government's war against a guerrilla insurgency that has gone on for decades.

With Black Hawk helicopters and American military advisers, the Colombian military is fighting two leftist armies: the Revolutionary armed forces of Colombia (FARC) and the smaller National Liberation Army or ELN. The fourth army in this complicated, many-sided war is the AUC.

During the past three years, the government of President Andres Pastrana has engaged in a peace process with the guerrillas, granting FARC full control of a section of the country as an incentive to negotiate.

If negotiations fail, as they very well may, the war in Colombia will undoubtedly get much worse. And there is every reason to believe the paramilitaries will punish the people of that region as accomplices of the guerrillas.

The United States, which has branded the FARC as a terrorist organization, no longer needs to justify its support of the Colombian military as part of its war on drugs.

In the new, post-Sept. 11 reality, the United States can act more bluntly. It will no doubt side firmly with Colombia's government in this war. But it must also stand with Colombia's people in protecting civilian lives.

Frida Ghitis, a native of Colombia, is an international journalist and author. Her latest book is ""The End of Revolution: A Changing World in the Age of Live television'' (Algora Publishing, 2001).
