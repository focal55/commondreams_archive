Published on Saturday, August 3, 2002 in the Boulder Daily Camera
Innocent People Died; No Apology From US
by Christopher Brauchli
 

Never apologize and never explain — it's a sign of weakness.
— Laurence Stallings,

"She Wore a Yellow Ribbon"
(screenplay.)

Who said: "The president views this as a heavy-handed action that is not consistent with dedication to peace. . . ." and what was he talking about?

Who said: "We have no regrets about going in after bad guys and there were some there," and what was he talking about?

The second statement was made by Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz on July 15 while talking about the July 1, United States air raid that killed 48 Afghan civilians and injured 117 more in several villages in Uruzgan province, Afghanistan. Among the dead were 25 people at a wedding celebration. The United States explained that the reason the wedding party had been attacked was because U.S. aircraft had come under sustained and hostile fire. The wedding guests, it was later learned, had been firing into the air, a Pashtun wedding tradition, when American planes struck.

A U.S. spokesman said the shooting was "not consistent" with a wedding. He did not say how many Afghan weddings he had attended. Donald Rumsfeld said "I read in the paper there was a wedding. I just don't know the facts." The Pentagon admitted a bomb was errant but refused to confirm that it hit the wedding party. The only people who seemed to be sure what happened were the wedding guests. They knew that 25 of their number were killed and by whom.

On July 6, Mr. Bush called President Karzai but but did not apologize for what happened. With his usual eloquence he said that he wanted to "express his sympathies to those whose loved ones lost their lives" and as a spokesman said, "he certainly did express the tragedy of the situation," a statement that while enormously sincere is somewhat vague. It was a slight improvement over what was reported to be his first reaction to the bombing when press secretary Ari Fleischer said the president expressed "deep condolences for the loss of innocent life no matter what the cause is determined to be" thus suggesting that in some administration minds, the cause was still not known, notwithstanding newspaper reports that gave some clues.

By July 16, Mr. Wolfowitz was willing to say that he deeply regretted the civilian deaths and promised a full investigation. As of this time not much more has been said nor has there been any indication that anything particularly worthwhile resulted from the bombing notwithstanding Mr. Wolfowitz' initial upbeat assessment that there were some bad guys there. If there were any bad guys there, no one has bothered to tell us who they were.

The first words in this column were spoken by the White House spokesman, Ari Fleischer. The president was not referring to the wedding party attack but to the Israeli bombing of Al Daraj, a densely packed neighborhood in Aza where an Israeli F-16 fighter jet dropped a one-ton laser-guided bomb. It killed Sheik Salah Shehada, the founder of the paramilitary wing of Hamas and a well-known terrorist. He was held in an Israeli prison for 12 years because of his Hamas activities and was released only a few weeks before the most recent uprising began in September 2000.

Shehada was not the only casualty. Among the other dead were nine children and five adults. A local hospital said more than 140 people had been injured, 15 of them seriously.

Prime Minister Ariel Sharon described the air strike as "one of our major successes" even though "we are always sorry about civilians that are hurt." Mr. Bush called it "heavy-handed" and said that the government of Sharon was aware that the apartment building was filled with children and adults. Fleischer criticized the Israeli action because of the loss of "innocent life," saying that "this heavy handed action does not contribute to peace."

According to reports in The New York Times, Mr. Bush was described as "visibly angry" at reports of the damage done when the bomb was dropped. One senior administration official was quoted as saying: "We had to show the Sharon government there are some redlines" on their action. "There was a lot of anger around here."

By evening of the day of the attack Israeli military officials were saying that the military had badly miscalculated the scale of the collateral damage.

There was a significant difference between the attack on the wedding party and the bombing of the building in Gaza City. The Israelis killed a number of civilians but in the process killed a well known terrorist. The Americans killed 25 wedding guests and as far as anyone knows, no terrorist.

Notwithstanding the success of the raid, the Israelis admitted they would not have undertaken it had they known of the unintended consequences. One senior military official said: "We wouldn't have done it if we knew what the consequences would be." There has been no such admission from the United States with respect to the wedding attack. Others have said it for us.

A United Nations investigation of the wedding attack found no corroboration that U.S. aircraft were fired upon from the ground. The Times of London reported that the U.N. draft report said the American military was attempting a cover-up by dragging its feet on its own investigation and said there were discrepancies in the American accounts of what happened. Colonel Roger King, spokesman for coalition forces at Bagram Air Base, admitted to discrepancies in the American accounts of what happened saying: "Early reporting is often sketchy, and sometimes it contains inaccuracies." He apparently got that right.

Ari Fleischer's words should have been referring to the attack on the wedding party. Mr. Wolfowitz' words should have been referring to the Israeli attack. They weren't, and that's no surprise. We don't acknowledge error. We don't have to because of who we are.

Christopher R. Brauchli is a Boulder, Colorado lawyer and writes a weekly column for the Knight Ridder news service.
