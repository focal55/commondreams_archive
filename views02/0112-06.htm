Published on Saturday, January 12, 2002 in The Indian Express (Bombay, India)
'Sept 11’s Lesson: Different Cultures Need to Start Dialogue, Not Conflict'
by Lord Bhikhu Parekh
 
The idea of multiculturalism still holds, says lord BHIKHU PAREKH

As a thinker, Lord Bhikhu Parekh has been unafraid to criticise attitudes in his country, India, and Britain, his adopted homeland. The last time the professor, one of the leading theorists on the philosophical underpinnings of multiculturalism, found himself in the thick of controversy was when the commission he chaired on the Future of Multi-ethnic Britain presented its report in October 2000. There was a huge row over the Report’s formulation that ‘Britishness’ has racial connotations, that it is often defined in terms that exclude ethnic minorities and the people of Scotland. The Professor at the London School of Economics was recently in New Delhi for a busy few days. Excerpts from a conversation with VANDITA MISHRA:

• 2001 was a troubling year, when the practice and possibilities of multiculturalism appeared to be severely challenged. There was September 11 in the US. In India, the year ended with December 13.

I WOULD distinguish between September 11 and December 13. December 13 was a local event, a group of people wanting to make use of Kashmir to destabilise not only Kashmir but also India. It was a pure terrorist act. What happened on September 11 was more than that.

At one level, it was an attempt to punish the US for what it appeared to have done in Palestinian territories, Iraq and so on. It was also a symbol of a larger clash between religious fundamentalism and modernity.

The question is what lessons can we draw from what happened on September 11. There is one that has been drawn by American liberals — that there is, as Samuel P. Huntington laid down, a clash of civilisations; that Christianity and Islam can never co-exist. And that the West must find ways of subduing Islamic societies. This, I believe, is the completely wrong lesson. Islam has been a part of western consciousness and vice versa for hundreds of years. To juxtapose these societies as wholly incompatible civilisations is totally fraudulent.

It also doesn’t take into account the fact that the Muslim population will constitute almost a quarter of the world population by the year 2025. How do you declare a war on a people of this magnitude? I would, therefore, draw the opposite lesson. That unless different civilisations enter into a dialogue and understand each other, none will survive and flourish.

The West would continue to fight Islam, become increasingly militarised and lose its liberal, democratic character. In order to fight September 11, the US has already bypassed its Constitution, its laws, locked up people and subjected them to torture. You might win the war, but only by losing all that you stand for. You have to find ways to engage each other in dialogue, to create spaces for, diversity. The world should not be shaped in the image of the US or the west; it should be a conversation between civilisations.

• Last year also saw an increase in racial violence in Britain. There was vicious rioting in June-July.

IN the last three weeks in Britain, there have been four reports on the riots that happened earlier in the year. These reports emphasise three things. One, different communities are leading parallel lives. Eighty per cent of those involved in the riots were Pakistani Muslims who live in areas where levels of poverty and unemployment are much higher than the national average.

Two, they go to different schools, so future citizens are growing up without sharing a common educational experience. Three, in this situation, it is easy for outside, racist elements to create misunderstandings and spark off explosions.

If you want communities to understand each other, only multiculturalism shows the way. Not a ghettoised view of multiculturalism, but an interactive one, which is the Indian way. It means that all cultures are part of my common inheritance, reaching out to each other while being rooted in our own culture.

A culturally footloose group of people are dangerous. They can be recruits for terrorism.

Multiculturalism must also go hand in hand with social justice. Philosophically, proponents of multiculturalism are criticised for being only interested in culture. My response to that is the politics of recognition must be integrated with the politics of redistribution.

• But hasn’t discrimination against Muslims festered as a separate question in western societies? The liberal establishment has often masked its prejudice behind posturing on female illiteracy in the Muslim world, or on female circumcision.

LIBERALS know what to do with racism. They can deal with injustice based on colour. They have been at the forefront of the anti-racist struggle in Britain, which has the most powerful legislation against racial discrimination.

But from the mid- to late 80s, partly because of the Salman Rushdie affair, a new phenomenon appeared. And Liberals panicked at Muslim militancy. Because they have always been uneasy with religion, and that includes Pandit Nehru in our country. Now, lo and behold, here were Muslims demanding certain rights based on religion.

The fact is people are being discriminated against, in terms of employment etc, not just on the basis of colour, but also religion. There is tremendous evidence to show discrimination against Muslims.

While the legal system is geared against race-based prejudice, what about religion-based prejudice? It is only now that the government has made some attempts to address this problem head on. There is a greater attempt among the people to understand Islam.

• In India, do you see the situation, post-December 13, triggering more intolerance? There are attempts by the VHP, for instance, to manoeuvre the current tensions between India and Pakistan to fuel the Ayodhya ‘‘movement’’.

THIS constant tendency to equate Pakistan with Muslims and Muslims with Pakistan is dangerous. It completely corrupts our understanding of our domestic situation. And it is also politically mad. If a small group of Muslims from outside the country could cause this degree of damage to India, how will you handle a large chunk of your population if they become profoundly alienated and dissatisfied? The Muslim community in India has been exemplary.

Our family life and cultural life would be infinitely poorer without its contribution. Although Muslims feel alienated from the national life, though they have been systematically discriminated against, they have been intensely loyal. How many Muslims in India demonstrated for the Taliban? How many went to fight with it? There was just that remark by the Shahi Imam that Shabana Azmi could tackle on her own. Why don’t you appreciate this and build on it? The VHP is being morally irresponsible and politically suicidal.

• You have written extensively on Gandhi. Is Gandhi’s way relevant in the present context?

There are important practical lessons from Gandhi’s life and work. At a time when public life is so corrupt, let us remember him as a man who only gave.

If Gandhi had been alive, what would he have said to Osama bin Laden? The day the terrorist attack took place, he would have said, you are going down the wrong way, terrorism is no answer. And once the Americans started bombing Afghanistan, he would have said to him — if you love these people, give yourself up for them. You will also get so much political capital out of it. Then the Americans will not dare to harm you.

Gandhi was this beautiful combination of high morality and shrewd calculations. The challenge is to combine morality with politics, idealism with realism. The old man did that.
