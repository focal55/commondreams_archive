Published on Sunday, January 6, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Dissent's a Dirty Word
by Stephanie Salter
 
IN NEARLY 20 years of speaking and writing about nonviolence, Rusty Nelson has grown accustomed to skepticism and challenge. These days, the climate for his message is about as open and hospitable as a bayonet.

"So many people are as anxious as John Ashcroft for everybody to just shut up and go along with what the president says," said the 57-year-old Vietnam veteran. "That's what's really scary about all of this."

Director of the Peace and Justice Action League of Spokane, Wash., Nelson recently was disinvited from speaking to the class of a public high school teacher I know in rural Washington.

Said my friend, who shall remain anonymous: "Both my principal and the superintendent said the topic of nonviolence was too controversial now, a real hot potato that would cause trouble. They said the only way the students would be allowed to hear him was if I also invited an extreme hawk to speak so it would be a 'balanced' presentation. I told them the kids already hear plenty from the other side -- everyday on TV, in the paper, from the White House. It didn't matter."

Like a lot of us, my friend knows she is not the only American who has declined to march in lockstep for the war on terrorism. But sometimes, she said, when she called to talk about Nelson, it sure feels that way.

For his part, Nelson was disappointed at the disinvitation, but not surprised. A Veteran's Day speech he gave in another public high school near the Idaho border ended with a faculty member -- a retired military officer -- calling him a liar and the school's principal sending letters of apology to the students' parents.

"I'd told them about another vet I knew, who came home from Vietnam and killed himself. I talked about the futility of war, the unused element of nonviolence, about how we passed on diplomacy," said Nelson. "I'd given a similar speech before at a Unitarian church. The reaction was a lot different."

Nelson's path to pacifism was slow and not without irony.

"My dad was a Navy chaplain, I was in R.O.T.C. in college in South Carolina and went to Vietnam with an Army mobile marine force," he said. "Some of that footage from 'Apocalypse Now' looks pretty familiar."

Vietnam itself caused no immediate epiphanies:

"I bought the Domino Theory, hook, line and sinker. Once I got there and saw the reality though, I remember thinking, 'Boy it would really be a waste if I got killed over here for this.' "

Now, "the bitterness I have over Vietnam is from not ever hearing about nonviolence before I went. It was like I didn't have all the cards. Nobody (in authority) ever challenged the rightness of what was being done; it was just what our country was doing."

Anti-war protesters, he said, "just seemed like strange people, far away from South Carolina or where I grew up in Georgia."

By the time Nelson and his wife Nancy moved to Spokane from her native Minnesota, both knew plenty about nonviolence. Joining a Mennonite Church community set them off in a direction that has defined their lives and 30-year marriage.

"I didn't consider myself a pacifist, it just made sense, politically and spiritually," said Nelson. "It was the right thing to do."

Now grandparents, thanks to their two adopted children (who are African American, unlike Nancy and Rusty), the Nelsons work part-time jobs to pay the bills and split duties at the Peace and Justice Action League.

Does the current national climate of intolerance for dissent cause Nelson to despair?

"I have my moments," he said. "The scary thing is, we haven't hit bottom yet. While I do feel the pendulum is going to swing back the other way -- I hear from too many kindred spirits out there who are courageous and articulate about the distortions of this war -- I just don't know how low we can go before we start rising."
