Published on Saturday, November 16, 2002 by the Boulder Daily Camera
International Court a Foreign Idea to Bush
by Christopher Brauchli
 

Mea culpa, mea culpa, mea maxima culpa.

The Missal,Confession of sins

The election made one thing absolutely clear — I have been barking up the wrong Bush. Election night demonstrated conclusively that George W. Bush is nothing less than a national hero. There will be no more carping about him and his crew from the likes of me. And as evidence of my conversion, what better place to start than to acknowledge the wisdom of Mr. Bush's continuing efforts to make sure that United States citizens are protected from the reach of the International Court of Criminal Justice.

The ICCJ is empowered to charge and try cases of genocide and other crimes against humanity. Upon becoming president Mr. Bush began a campaign to get the signatories to agree to exempt all Americans from the reach of the court. At the end of September the European Union agreed to exempt American military personnel and diplomats from its jurisdiction. That did not satisfy Mr. Bush. On Oct. 8, he dispatched a top State Department envoy to Europe to try to persuade several European countries to agree that NO Americans would be subject to the jurisdiction of the court.

Mr. Bush fears that American citizens brought before that court might end up being deprived of the kinds of constitutional protections of which Mr. Bush routinely deprives those he designates "enemy combatants." Mr. Bush, however, only applies that label to people truly deserving of it whereas foreigners less sensitive to those protections might apply it to people such as Mr. Rumsfeld or Mr. Cheney, especially if they were to do something like blow up a car in some country with whom we are not even at war. Such an action would, depending on the car's occupants, be entirely appropriate because of who we are, whereas it would be totally inappropriate were it done by someone else. And that brings me to a very important point.

In early November a missile fired by a U.S. Predator drone over Yemen blew up a car, killing its six occupants, one of whom was Abu Ali al-Harithi, the man believed to have been one of the planners of the attack on the USS Cole and a man linked, among other things, to the Oct. 7 bombing of the French oil tanker off the coast of Yemen. Mr. Rumsfeld delivered the administration eulogy for Mr. Harithi saying: "It would be a very good thing if he were out of business."

This was the first known use of lethal military force outside Afghanistan since the beginning of the war on terrorism. It was carried out under broad authority that Mr. Bush has given the CIA over the past year to pursue terror wherever it is found. The killing does not violate the ban that the United States has on assassinating political leaders because none of the six victims was regarded as a political leader.

Some people compared this targeted killing with the Israeli targeted killing of Palestinian figures it regards as leading terrorists that the Bush administration deplores. That comparison is not apt. State Department spokesman Richard Boucher, commented on that very point, saying: "If you look back at what we have said about targeted killings in the Israeli-Palestinian context, you will find that the reasons we have given do not necessarily apply in other circumstances."

Equally as illuminating as Mr. Boucher's comments were those of former Sen. Warren Rudman, who explained that in the war on terrorism there are no rules. The terrorists "have none and we have to take whatever risks you have to take to make them fear us." He went on to say that the United States may carry out attacks within other countries even without the cooperation or approval of those governments. He would, I am sure, be the first to explain that that is so because of who we are and that we would not tolerate another country's applying the same standard in deciding to blow up a vehicle filled with suspected terrorists in the United States.

A former Clinton administration foreign policy adviser said we must work with friends and allies when fighting a stateless enemy. He went on to say, however, that: "We have to make sure that the U.S. government does not become — in fact or perception — judge, jury and executioner around the world. If the technique is used indiscriminately, there will be a backlash."

The Israelis know what he means. In early November they killed Mohammad Abdel Al, a member of Islamic Jihad's military wing. Defending the attack, Israeli Transport Minister, Ephraim Sneh, using language mimicking Mr. Bush, said: "These are people who have set themselves a goal of destroying Israel." Abdul Al allegedly killed an Israeli soldier in a 1994 shooting attack, helped plot a 1995 suicide bombing that killed more than 20 Israelis and in February planted two roadside bombs in Gaza. Mr. Bush, who can distinguish between what he authorizes and what Mr. Sharon authorizes, thus has no difficulty criticizing the Israeli policy while employing it for his own purposes.

Under the new Bush plan it is more important than ever that United States citizens not be subject to the jurisdiction of some international court of criminal justice. There might come a day when some country would fail to understand the distinction between what Mr. Bush does and what Mr. Sharon does and charge U.S. citizens with war crimes. Given the purity of our motives and Mr. Bush's great popularity, that would be completely inappropriate. One can only hope that other countries see the error of their way and agree to exempt the United States from the reach of a court run by a bunch of foreigners.

Christopher Brauchli is a Boulder lawyer and and writes a weekly column for the Knight Ridder news service. He can be reached at brauchli1@attbi.com
