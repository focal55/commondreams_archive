Published on Monday, January 14, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Bush's Political Payoff
by Ruth Rosen
 
IN EVERY WAR, people suffer. Americans have proved that they are willing to sacrifice blood, money and time for their country. When asked, they have even gone shopping.

At the same time, these same folks expect their government to care about their rights and needs as workers.

That's why working women are gearing up to fight the Bush administration's proposal to eliminate the 10 regional offices of the U.S. Department of Labor's Women's Bureau.

The decision to close these regional offices is not about the budget. Only three people staff each office, which is geographically responsible for five to eight states. Cutting these 10 offices would save $3 million.

No, this is part of Bush's political payoff to his right-wing flank. In January 2001, the Heritage Foundation, a conservative think-tank, wrote the playbook for the Bush administration. They called it "A Budget for America." Among its many recommendations are the elimination of the Bureau of International Labor Affairs, the Women's Bureau and the Civil Rights Office. In other words, the hit list targeted labor, women and minorities.

Should you care? Yes, and here's why. Every working woman should understand the debt she owes to the Women's Bureau.

Created by Congress in 1920, the Women's Bureau has played a central role in representing and protecting the needs of wage-earning women for 81 years.

During the Great Depression, the bureau fought for the Fair Labor Standards Act. During World War II, it helped ease women's transition into the war industry. In the early 1960s, the Women's Bureau played a decisive role in establishing President Kennedy's Commission on the Status of Women and in shepherding the 1963 Equal Pay Act through Congress.

In the 1970s, the bureau fought for women's right to enter nontraditional occupations. During the 1980s and 1990s, the bureau campaigned for such family- friendly policies as the Family and Medical Leave Act, passed into law in 1993.

Conservatives would like us to believe that these regional offices are unnecessary and obsolete. But they're not. In recent years, the bureau's local offices have been educating women about their rights in the workplace, assisting employers with establishing child care and flexible hours, and encouraging employees to advance in high-tech careers. They have also worked closely with unions and the private sector and acted as a conduit of ideas to the national office.

Closing the regional offices of the Women's Bureau will just fulfill a campaign debt. It may also make the real goal -- closing down the Women's Bureau itself -- that much easier.

The fact is, we've still got a long way to go. In 1963, women made 59 cents for every dollar earned by men. By 1999 that number jumped to 72.2 cents, largely due to the Equal Pay Act of 1963. Clearly, working women have hardly achieved equality. We still earn less, more women than men still return home to a second shift, and working mothers still bear a disproportionate financial cost of raising the next generation.

Until that's changed, we still need the expertise and oversight of the U.S. Department of Labor's Women's Bureau.

Ruth Rosen is an editorial writer for The Chronicle.
