Constitution Project Welcomes Supreme Court's Restoration of Habeas Corpus for "Enemy Combatants"
 
WASHINGTON, DC - June 12 - Today the Constitution Project - a bipartisan think thank that builds consensus on controversial legal issues - warmly welcomed the Supreme Court's 5-4 decision in Boumediene v. Bush / al Odah v. United States, the consolidated case testing the government's authority under the Military Commissions Act (MCA) to deny so-called "enemy combatants" at Guantanamo the right to habeas corpus . Through the longstanding and fundamental right to habeas corpus, the detainees were seeking to ask a neutral federal judge to determine whether the government has the right to detain them. In its decision, the Court reversed a lower court ruling and found that detainees have a constitutional right to habeas corpus. Moreover, the Court found that the existing system of hearings established by the MCA and the Detainee Treatment Act is not an "adequate substitute" for the constitutional right.

"The Supreme Court's decision puts to rest the insidious notion that a president - like a monarch or dictator - has the unchecked power to lock people up and throw away the key on the president's say-so alone without regard for constitutional rights," said Virginia Sloan, president of the Constitution Project. "The decisive restoration of one of the great principles on which our country was founded should hearten all who seek the justice that is the hallmark of American courts. The Court's decision marks the end of a disgraceful period in 's history."

The Constitution Project previously organized two amicus curiae - or "friend of the court" - briefs urging the Court to find that the detainees are entitled to habeas: one on behalf of seventeen nongovernmental organizations, including Human Rights Watch, Human Rights First, the Rutherford Institute, and the Constitution Project, and the other on behalf of twenty former federal judges appointed by Presidents Johnson, Nixon, Ford, Carter, Reagan, George H.W. Bush, and Clinton. Last year, the Project's bipartisan Liberty and Security Committee and Coalition to Defend Checks and Balances issued a "Statement on Restoring Habeas Corpus Rights Eliminated by the Military Commissions Act." Signers include Mickey Edwards, former Member of Congress (R-OK); David Keene, chairman of the American Conservative Union; Harold Koh, Dean of Yale Law School and Assistant Secretary of State for Democracy, Human Rights and Labor in the Clinton administration; Alberto Mora, General Counsel to the U.S. Navy in the Clinton Administration; William S. Sessions, former federal judge and Director of the FBI in the Reagan and Bush administrations; and William H. Taft, IV, Deputy Secretary of Defense in the Reagan administration and senior legal advisor to the State Department in the George W. Bush administration.
