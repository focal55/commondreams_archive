Published on Sunday, July 6, 2003 by Reuters
As Bush Visits, Africa Resents Terror Hunt
by William Maclean
 

NAIROBI - At 2 a.m., baton-wielding police kick open the doors of a home in Kenya's Mombasa port, bludgeon its Muslim inhabitants and seize a terror suspect. Furious Muslim leaders complain of anti-Islamic prejudice.

In Malawi, U.S. agents and police grab five foreign Muslims suspected of plotting terror and whisk them overseas without a court appearance, triggering days of protests by local Muslims.

If President Bush expects sympathy from ordinary Africans for his global war against Osama bin Laden's al Qaeda group when he visits the continent this week, he will be disappointed.

Instead, anti-U.S. sentiment is deepening as governments hunt militants at the behest of Washington and Britain, raising fears for civil liberties, straining ties between Christians and Muslims and hurting old friendships with the West.

"Must we sing to their tune, as if we are still a colony?" wrote Kenyan Joseph Mutua in Nairobi's The People newspaper, referring to former colonial power Britain.

"The overt coercion used by the Kenyan government and other governments such as Malawi's is highly objectionable. They should use more subtle means," said community leader Mohammed Hydar of Mombasa's Muslim Civic and Education Trust.

"We don't want people taken to interrogation in unknown places where laws are abrogated. The police are shooting themselves in the foot, because we'll help (investigators) if the law is being followed and people are treated with dignity."

In Nigeria, analysts say a tough campaign against al Qaeda suspects could trigger a backlash in the already volatile Islamic North, where hundreds died in sectarian riots in Kano ignited by the start of the U.S. campaign in Afghanistan.

Matters have been made worse in East Africa by Western terror alerts and travel warnings that have hurt vital tourism. The move has encouraged many across the continent to blame the West more than Muslim militant bombers for exposing Africa to political violence stemming from disputes outside the continent.

"We are a target of terrorists mainly because of the global activities of Uncle Sam," said Kenyan columnist Mwenda Njoka, referring to Washington's perceived pro-Israel foreign policy.

"Remove the Palestinian problem and no Africans will care about bin Laden. He'll be left to fight on his own," said Hydar.

As rich countries tighten security after the Sept. 11, 2001, attacks on the United States, Africa's chaos, graft and poverty have made it a more congenial place for militants to operate and hide, analysts say.

"Kenya is helplessly exposed to any mediocre terrorist with evil intent," wrote Ambrose Murunga, a former detective who helped investigate the 1998 bombings of U.S. Embassies in east Africa.

"Our intelligence services have virtually no human intelligence sources within the established terrorist networks to alert us of impending strikes," he said, complaining that the police lacked high-tech gadgetry to monitor terror suspects.

OPPORTUNISM

Equally, experts say, there is no evidence that increasing numbers of African Muslims are flocking to join Osama bin Laden's Arab-led campaign against the West despite a deep sympathy with the Palestinian struggle against Israel.

"Terrorism in Africa by al Qaeda would be best categorized as opportunism, because Africa is chaotic and there are easy soft targets like hotels in Mombasa," said Alex Vines of Britain's Royal Institute for International Affairs.

Historical links to the Gulf make East Africa the main region south of the Sahara for Middle East-related terrorism.

In 1998 the bombings of the U.S. Embassies in Kenya and Tanzania killed 224 people. Three years later a U.S. court convicted four followers of bin Laden for the attacks.

November's bombing of an Israeli-owned hotel near Mombasa that killed 16 people was claimed by his al Qaeda group.

Bin Laden lived in Sudan in the early 1990s and Somalia may be a similar refuge for militants, although foreigners are likely to find it difficult to blend into its tightly knit clan system.

But Arabs lead al Qaeda and Africans, when they have been involved, have tended to be the foot soldiers, experts say.

"I don't see (a distinctive) African readiness to get involved in terrorism," said Jerrold Post, a former U.S. Central Intelligence Agency expert on the psychology of terrorism who testified at the trial of the men convicted of the 1998 blasts.

Militant Islam has found it difficult to penetrate West Africa, despite deep poverty and disaffection in mainly Muslim Senegal or Gambia. And any al Qaeda presence in West Africa is more to do with smuggling precious stones than finding recruits.
