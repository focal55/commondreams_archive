Published on Saturday, November 8, 2003 by the Capital Times / Wisconsin
Fighting Big Media
by Judith Davidoff
 

As hundreds of activists gathered in Madison for the first day of the National Conference of Media Reform, the message was clear: Nothing less than the future of democracy is at stake if the concentration of media outlets continues unchecked.

"I don't think there's a more important meeting taking place in America today," Michael Copps, a Federal Communications Commission member, told a packed audience in the Wisconsin Historical Society auditorium gathered to hear about the fight to overturn a recent ruling by the FCC relaxing media ownership rules.

Copps was one of two FCC members who voted against the new rules.

Copps later that night got a standing ovation as he revved up the crowd at the Orpheum Theatre before the kickoff for the "Tell Us the Truth" tour, a musical collaboration that will travel to 12 other cities to raise awareness about issues of media consolidation and globalization.

Friday night's lineup featured British singer-songwriter and political activist Billy Bragg; Tom Morello, guitarist for Rage Against the Machine and Audioslave; Lester Chambers, part of the '60s soul-rock group the Chambers Brothers; and Boots Riley, rapper with the Oakland hip-hop group the Coup.

More than 1,600 registrants signed up for the media reform conference, about 1,400 more than were anticipated when conference organizers thought up the idea for the gathering some 11 months ago, according to conference organizer Bob McChesney.

The crowd was so large, many could not fit in the Wisconsin Memorial Union's Great Hall for the opening session and welcome by Mayor Dave Cieslewicz and Lt. Gov. Barbara Lawton.

In his opening remarks, McChesney said the FCC ruling in June has alarmed -- and motivated -- activists across the nation.

"This issue has exploded on the national consciousness in a way no one expected," he said. He said the FCC ruling has become the No. 2 issue on Capitol Hill, just behind the war in Iraq.

McChesney and others throughout the day noted that progressives and conservatives have come together on this issue and are getting results. A letter, already signed by more than 200 members of the House of Representatives, asks that a vote be taken in Congress to overturn the FCC action of this past summer.

The rules were stayed in September by a federal appeals court, preventing them from going into effect.

In a session on media and propaganda during wartime, Amy Goodman, host of Democracy Now!, spoke about the Bush administration's efforts to control coverage of the Iraq conflict.

"Lies take lives," she said. "We must decide whether we'll be the sword or the shield."

At another session, Linda Foley, president of the Newspaper Guild, and John Sweeney, president of the AFL-CIO, talked about the importance of unionizing media workers so they have the freedom to produce unbiased stories without fear of punishment.

A talk by consumer activist and former presidential candidate Ralph Nader was rescheduled for 8 a.m. today in Room 3650 of the Humanities Building on the UW campus.

Marge Tully, a local social worker and self-described news junkie, said she was drawn to the conference because of her dissatisfaction with most mainstream news coverage, particularly the censored coverage on the war in Iraq.

She said the concentration of media power is alarming.

"It frightens me as a citizen of democracy," she said. "It closes off opportunities for thoughtful discussion."

Daithi Wolfe, a Madison teacher and one of the 220 volunteers for the conference, said the news stories produced by most mainstream news outlets reflect the interests of corporate America.

"The whole media is self-censoring," he said. "When there is money and power behind these decisions, you don't need a police state."
