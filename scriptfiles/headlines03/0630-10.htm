Published on Monday, June 30, 2003 by the New York Times
On-Line Fund-raising Stuns Rivals, Raises Dean to Top of Class
by Adam Nagourney
 

Former Vermont Gov. Howard Dean announced Sunday that he has raised close to $9 million this year, establishing himself as a top-tier candidate in the 2004 Democratic presidential field.

The figure stunned his rivals and transformed Dean from a maverick into a more traditional contender.

Much of the money was collected over the Internet, his aides said, leaving little doubt there are now ways to solicit contributions other than the telephone calls and elaborate fund-raisers that are the stock and trade for most mainstream candidates.

Dean's aides said he would report raising at least $6.2 million in the three-month period that ends at midnight, on top of $2.6 million he raised over the first three months of the year.

Dean announced the figure 36 hours before the filing period ends, timing the release for a slow-news Sunday afternoon.

"We are thrilled," said Dean's campaign manager, Joe Trippi. "Right now, our new goal is $6.5 million by midnight tomorrow."

The other campaigns said Sunday that they would wait until the fund-raising period was over before releasing their results.

"He'll beat everybody," Steve Elmendorf, a senior adviser to Rep. Dick Gephardt of Missouri, said of Dean.

Dean's strong showing seems certain to cause a problem for some members of Congress who are running for president -- in particular, Gephardt and Sen. Joseph Lieberman of Connecticut, who had weak financial showings in the first quarter.

Gephardt and Lieberman were looking to strong showings in the next report to erase any concerns among Democrats about their viability.

Several Democrats said that Dean's success posed a particular problem to Sen. John Kerry of Massachusetts, since the men have been competing for many of the same voters and since it could undercut Kerry's effort to present himself as the front-runner.

Kerry's campaign manager, Jim Jordan, disputed that analysis.

"If their fund-raising projections are accurate, then perhaps it makes it a two-candidate race," Jordan said. "If so, it makes it clarifying and helpful to us."

Dean's advisers said that as of a week ago, he had raised just $3.2 million. They said contributions had increased sharply over the past week because of his announcement speech Monday and the online vote by an organization called Moveon.org, which enlisted a lot of new supporters, many of whom made contributions.

Trippi said that Dean had been helped by his appearance on the NBC interview program "Meet the Press" on June 22.

Dean's appearance on that show, in which he was unable to answer some questions and appeared to change his position on some issues, drew widespread criticism among Democrats.

But his aides suggested that as far as Dean's supporters were concerned, it might have proved an old show-business nostrum: There is no such thing as bad publicity.

"My own theory of it is when Howard Dean says things like, 'I don't know the answer to that,' the echo chamber in Washington says, 'Oh, my God, he doesn't know the answer!'" Trippi said.

"But the guy at home says, 'Hey, someone who admits he doesn't know the answer. We haven't seen that before!'"
