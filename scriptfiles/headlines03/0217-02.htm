Published on Monday, February 17, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
'We're Here to Save Our Country'
Vietnam-era activists, religious leaders, celebrities preach peace
by Nanette Asimov
 

SAN FRANCISCO -- Most marchers were still blocks away from San Francisco's Civic Center Sunday and missed the passionate words of the activists and actors, rabbis and imams who spoke at the big rally.

Yet the speakers' message, driven by loudspeakers, carried across the city.

"We can stop this war!" actor Danny Glover told the thousands of peace marchers who had arrived at the rally by about 2 p.m., when he briefly took the stage in front of City Hall.

"This is the first time we have gathered in such numbers before bullets have been shot," Glover said, echoing the wonder of many that a global peace movement is emerging in advance of a war.


Hundreds of thousands of people crowd into Civic Center Plaza in San Francisco during an anti-war protest, February 16, 2003. Millions around the world marched in protests this weekend to voice their opposition against a threatened U.S.-led attack on Iraq. (Susan Ragan/Reuters)
As the rally grew larger by the hour, and drizzles gave way to sunshine, it was like the Vietnam protests all over again, with anti-war signs, folk music and guitars. On stage, a rendition of "Strangest Dream" got the crowd swaying and singing, as did a sweet version of "Let There Be Peace on Earth."

What differed from the protests of three and four decades earlier was the palpable fear that this time global annihilation is possible.

"(The government) announced this week that they would not rule out the use of 'usable nuclear weapons,' " said Hari Dillon, president of the Vanguard Foundation, the coalition of groups sponsoring the march.

The crowd booed loudly.

"The time has come when humankind has to put an end to war, or war will put an end to humanity," cried Dillon.

Sunday's rally, so entwined with the Middle East, sounded much like an interfaith service.

Rabbi Pamela Frydman Baugh of the Or Shalom Jewish Community led a prayer on behalf of all the world "and the ordinary people of Iraq."

"May they be free of violence," she prayed. "May they be free of war."

A Buddhist woman had the crowd do an "open-eyes meditation," instructing each person to "catch the eye of someone you don't know and smile to let them know peace is possible."

A woman in a green top and leopard print pants smiled at a gray-bearded man in a wheelchair, and suddenly thousands of strangers were looking at each other and smiling.

Sister Elizabeth Padilla sang "Blessings Upon Blessings," and Lien Finau of the United Methodist Church declared, "Peace is possible."

Suleiman Ghali of the Islamic Society of San Francisco told protesters: "War is not justified against anybody! We're all Americans, and we all belong here."

"Vive la France!" cried Wilson Riles, the former Oakland Council member, applauding the nation that is leading global opposition to the war.

David Harris, one of the nation's best-known Vietnam War protesters, told the crowd in a voice strained by age and activism, "We're here to save our country."

Sitting on the grass and listening, John Daniels and Toni Dertlieva of San Rafael said they hope it is true.

They had marched up from the Ferry Building and, in a sense, the less-than- fiery speeches they heard now were anticlimactic. The speakers were polite. They were warm and inclusive -- and even willing to bless President Bush in hopes he would change his mind.

"It's hard to feel as radical as this moment calls for because we're not in the middle of bloodshed yet," Daniels said.

Dertlieva shook her head. "We need much more," she said. "This is not going to stop the well-organized machine of the government."
