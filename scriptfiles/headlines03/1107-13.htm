Published on Friday, November 7, 2003 by the Guardian / UK
£100m in Aid Funds Diverted to Iraq
by Owen Bowcott and Grace Livingstone
 

The full extent of Britain's retreat from its overseas aid commitments was revealed yesterday as the Department for International Development (DfID) reshuffled finances to pay for the reconstruction of Iraq.

Established programmes in South America and eastern Europe will suffer most under the "reallocation" exercise, which will divert £100m over the next two years to projects in and around Baghdad.

More than 20 countries will be affected, with bilateral aid to some, including Peru, Honduras, Anguilla, Romania, Bulgaria, Croatia and Macedonia, being withdrawn completely.

Spending in countries as diverse as South Africa, Russia, China, Sri Lanka, Bolivia, Jamaica, Albania and Kosovo will be substantially reduced.

About a quarter of the money - £25m - will be extracted from next year's budget. The remainder will be taken from programmes scheduled for 2005-06.

The severe cutbacks in South America and eastern Europe, revealed by the Guardian last month, have triggered protests from aid agencies who claim they breach undertakings given by Tony Blair that money would not be redirected away from other programmes to pay for Iraq.

Yesterday the charities said they were disappointed at the outcome of the review.

Phil Bloomer, head of advocacy at Oxfam, said: "The idea that we should be reducing funds to countries such as South Africa, which is ravaged by Aids, or Bolivia, which is suffering political instability and where more than half the population live below the poverty line, does not fit in with development priorities."

Andrew Croggon, head of Christian Aid's South America division, said he was "shocked" by the cuts.

"Although these countries fall into the category of 'middle income', this should not obscure the fact that many are characterised by severe pockets of poverty and by large and growing inequalities," he said.

"In Honduras, one of the countries which will cease to receive funding from DfID in the near future, 44% of the population live on less than $2 [£1.20] a day."

Judith Turbyne, the head of Christian Aid's programme in the Caribbean and central America, said ending direct support for Honduras represented a reversal of aid policy.

"I'm very surprised at the withdrawal from Honduras," she said. "It's been a very positive engagement."

DfID opened an office in the country in 2001 in the aftermath of Hurricane Mitch. Now that office will close within two years.

The department admitted the cutbacks might involve redundancies. A spokesman said there would be "some staff implications".

Overall aid spending will increase over the next two years, but a departmental strategy means 90% of future bilateral aid will be targeted at low-income countries.

The war in Iraq has accelerated and deepened the necessary cutbacks to middle income countries.

Details of the "reallocations" were released in a written statement from the international development secretary, Hilary Benn, in the Commons yesterday: "These changes... will involve withdrawal from programmes earlier that we had previously decided."

Aid to Egypt would end and the planned programme in Jordan would be "rephased". Other countries that would lose funding include Serbia, Montenegro and Bosnia.

Programmes for the Palestinian Authority, Montserrat, St Helena and Nicaragua would be retained. There would be some compensatory increase in the amount of money given to aid agencies working in South America and middle income countries, Mr Benn added.

Extra resources could be found for Sri Lanka, a department spokesman said yesterday, provided the peace process moved forward significantly.
