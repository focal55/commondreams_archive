Published on Wednesday, December 10, 2003 by Reuters
U.N. Talks Permit GMO Forests Under Kyoto
by Alister Doyle
 

MILAN - A U.N. conference agreed on Tuesday to permit use of genetically modified trees in forests planted to offset global warming, clearing a final hurdle to the Kyoto protocol whose fate now hinges on Russia.

The 180 nations left it up to each country to assess "potential risks" of using genetically modified organisms (GMOs) under a set of rules promoting forest plantings in the Third World to soak up greenhouse gases emitted by rich nations.

The European Union had opposed inclusion of GMOs under Kyoto, fearing they can threaten native species. Nations like the United States or Argentina see benefits from GMOs as faster-growing and disease resistant.

"This was the last piece of this huge puzzle of Kyoto," Thelma Krug of Brazil, who led the talks, told Reuters. "Every part was difficult." The rules on forest plantings were formally adopted late on Tuesday by the December 1-12 conference.

The forest deal was the last to define the mechanisms of Kyoto. Remaining issues include a fund to help developing nations adapt to the feared impact of global warming, ranging from desertification to the melting of polar icecaps.

President Bush withdrew the United States, the biggest polluter, from the Kyoto accord in 2001, and the protocol will now only take effect if Russia backs it. Experts say Moscow is unlikely to decide until after the March presidential election.

All plants absorb carbon dioxide as they grow, making them storage "sinks" for carbon dioxide, a non-toxic, odorless gas blamed for blanketing the globe since the Industrial Revolution and now mainly emitted by power plants, factories and cars.

Environmentalists reckon that two trees, for instance, would be needed to absorb the carbon dioxide produced per passenger on a return airline flight from Milan to New York.

Under Kyoto, rich nations will be allowed to store up to one percent of their annual emissions of carbon dioxide in such forest sinks. Kyoto seeks to cut rich countries' emissions of carbon dioxide by 5.2 percent below 1990 levels by 2012.

OPPOSITION TO GMOs

Breaking the deadlock on GMOs, the text leaves it to each country to "evaluate, in accordance with their national laws, potential risks associated with the use of genetically modified organisms."

Greenpeace and the WWF environmental groups, who had opposed GMOs in Kyoto, said the forest rules were "two steps forward, one step back." They faulted failure to rule out plantations of single species forests like eucalyptus, acacia or pines.

"At least the agreement shows that multilateralism works despite the U.S. absence from Kyoto," said Jennifer Morgan, WWF climate change director. "Kyoto is moving forward."

Many environmentalists want to stifle emissions at source, by shifting from coal and gas to solar or wind power, rather than cleaning up emissions by trying to store carbon in forests.

Some developing nations, like China and Brazil, saw too many rules on forests as an infringement of sovereignty. Canada objected that rules, such as taking account of cultural and religious sites in deciding where to plant, were too complex.

The rules set in Tuesday's agreement even stipulate that trees must be at least 6-feet 7-inches tall and their canopies have to cover at least 10 percent of an area before they can count as a forestry project.

Kyoto will enter force only if it is agreed by nations responsible for 55 percent of emissions of carbon dioxide in rich states. It has reached 44 percent and needs Russia's 17 percent after the United States pulled out its 36 percent share.

Separately, the U.N. Environment Program, Greenpeace and the WWF launched a new climate symbol -- the earth pictured as a round candle with white wax dripping from a flame in the Arctic.
