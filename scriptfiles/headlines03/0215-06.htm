Published on Saturday, February 15, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Performers, Artists Bring Creativity to San Francisco Protest
by James Sullivan, Steven Winn
 

As the drama mounts over Iraq, San Francisco's Civic Center will once again become a theater of war protest on Sunday.

Singers, actors, poets and street performers will be on hand, adding color, melody, movement and humor to the serious business of anti-war demonstration.

Joan Baez and Bonnie Raitt are among the artists reprising their appearances at last month's anti-war rally in the Civic Center. Actor Danny Glover will serve as an emcee, with readings and speeches by the writer Alice Walker, Rep. Barbara Lee (D-Oakland), British Member of Parliament Jeremy Corbin and many others.


Cathrine Margerin, a volunteer with the political action group Art and Revolution, works on a sign for Sunday's peace rally. Chronicle photo by Mike Kepka
If the growing anti-war movement is drawing people from all walks of life, Sunday's organizers have made a concerted effort to ensure that the speakers and performers represent the broadest possible ethnic and cultural spectrum. Musicians of Latino, Native American, African American and Arabic descent will share the stage, as well as members of the Gay Men's Chorus and the Raging Grannies, a feisty group of senior-age Peninsula women who put anti-war lyrics to familiar songs.

They're not even all pacifists. "There are good things to fight for," said Boots Riley of the radical, Black Panther-style Oakland hip-hop group the Coup.

"Unfortunately, I don't think the United States government fights in the name of the people."

The right to protest, said San Francisco native Glover, star of such films as "The Color Purple" and "Lethal Weapon," "is the most important legacy of democracy. What good is it if you can't dissent?"

Despite his fame as an actor, Glover said he will attend this weekend's rallies (he also plans to appear at today's protest in New York) as a citizen like any other.

"We are expressing our will as 'we the people,' " he said, "not just a small, elite group. We the people demand accountability. We demand that our voice be heard."

Still, organizers are well aware that celebrity appearances help spread the message from the strictly political realm into the social.

"We try to infuse as much art and culture into the day as possible," said Maya Jones, a delegate to the rally's programming committee from Not In Our Name, which held a benefit concert last month at the Berkeley Community Theater with Ani DiFranco, Chuck D, Michael Franti and others.

"There's a burgeoning new section of people stepping forward because they relate to the artists and musicians," she said. "People respond to that, particularly young people."

Hari Dillon is president of the Vanguard Public Foundation, the activist organization that brought together the four co-sponsors of Sunday's event: Not In Our Name, Bay Area United Against War, United for Peace and Justice, and International ANSWER. For him, the cultural aspect of the rally is crucial, and not just as a distraction.

"Culture is much more than just entertainment," he said. "It speaks to the soul and the spirit. I don't think the artists see this as an entertainment role."

In addition to the artists confirmed for the main stage, the Civic Center and march route will feature dozens of street theater groups. Dancers bearing giant peace birds, drummers from the San Francisco Mime Troupe and dozens of coordinated poster images by the activist group Art and Revolution will give the parade a distinctive San Francisco temperment and flair.

"We want to bring a little noise, some energy and fun," said the Mime Troupe's Ed Holmes, who might wear his Dick Cheney costume. "Nobody feels we're actually going to stop the war doing this. But we've got to put our bodies out there."

Alan Moore, an organizer of the 500-member Musicians and Fine Artists for World Peace, plans to carry the 6-foot-wide Mylar butterly that fluttered over Market Street in last month's march. Historically, Moore said, the butterfly "is a powerful, archetypal healing logo. I don't believe in anti-war demonstrations. I believe in pro-peace demonstrations."

On a break from poster-making at the Mission District's Redstone Building, Art and Revolution's Alli Starr discussed the moves dancers and volunteers use to animate their painted quetzal birds (a peace emblem from Central America). As they turn, jump and twist, they create the illusion of flight.

The group hopes to have 50 or more images of Iraqi families, fashioned from bicycle boxes, deployed through the parade. "We Are Not Saddam," "We Are Not Al Qaeda," "War Destroys Families," some of the posters say.

"There's something really powerful about a unified image that is not machine-made," Starr said.

Randall Stuart, who is producing a 100-character reading of the ancient Greek anti-war play "Lysistrata" at the Berkeley Repertory Theatre on March 3, plans to march Sunday. For him, performers are naturals at such events: They have the art of persuasion.

"Artists are used to creating magic from nothing at all," he said, "then making it live and breathe to affect an audience." Stuart compared the impending war with the themes Aristophanes addressed: "This crisis is no less heart-rending than the one those women on the beach at Troy faced."

Laughter, in the face of warfare, may be just as essential. One of the Mime Troupe's pet parade chants is "No More Chanting."
