Published on Wednesday, March 19, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
A New Generation for Peace
Parents Hope Activism Helps Educate Children
by Peter Fimrite
 

They had talked the anti-war talk, but when the threat of an attack on Iraq seemed imminent, Alice Cannan and Phil Meymand decided their family would walk the walk.

The couple dressed their two children in homemade vests with painted peace signs and other anti-war slogans, left their home in Oakland's Rockridge district and joined the peace marches around the Bay Area.


Alice Cannan and her daughter Emily Meymand look at protest coverage in the Sunday paper. Chronicle photo by Kat Wade
Cannan, 39, was exhilarated. Her whole family was standing in righteous solidarity against what she was convinced would be an unjust war. Then, her 4- year-old daughter, Leila, tapped her.

"Mommy," she said, "is Saddam Hussein a boy or a girl?"

It was a comment that elicited some chuckles, but it also brought a little perspective to the anti-war movement. Unlike the 1960s student protests, war demonstrations today are, as often as not, family affairs.

Cannan and Meymand, with their 4- and 6-year-old daughters, are part of a growing "stroller brigade" that has created a new dynamic in the anti-war movement.

They are helping introduce political action to a new generation.

"I thought (the question) was funny, but it made me understand that you have to be careful about what you're saying to children because their interpretations can be so different," Cannan said. "We were going along thinking she understood things and then realized that she really didn't."

Cannan and Meymand are highly educated, upper middle-class professional parents with mostly liberal, democratic ideals. They oppose war in Iraq and have taken an active role in fighting the Bush administration's battle plans. The couple's newfound activism is a first for the Cannan-Meymand family. They have joined other couples and their children at several protest marches and intend to join the demonstrations once the war gets under way.

Meymand, 41, said the Bush administration's relationship with the United Nations was what prompted him and his wife to get involved.

"The U.S. is continually changing the definition of what is an acceptable outcome," Meymand said. "At first it was eliminate weapons of mass destruction, and now it's regime change. Saying, 'Hey, we don't like this government, so we're going to change it,' is a very dangerous precedent to set."

Meymand and Cannan came to the Bay Area in 1993 so Meymand could get a master's degree at UC Berkeley. He ended up getting a Ph.D. in civil and environmental engineering and now works as a geotechnical engineer for a large firm in Oakland.

Cannan, who has a master's degree in nursing from Boston College, is a women's health practitioner in Berkeley.

Both grew up in politically charged environments, Meymand in Massachusetts and Cannan in Pennsylvania.

Meymand's father was an Iranian naval officer who divorced and returned to Iran when Meymand was barely a year old. His mother, who was in the U.S. Navy, raised him alone.

Meymand said his father had been exiled to the United States after the Shah of Iran was deposed, but he never got to know him very well. And anyway, he said, his concern about the Middle East has nothing to do with his father. He said he had been interested in politics since prep school, eventually majoring in political science at Georgetown.

Cannan was the 10th of 13 siblings in a Catholic family. She was born in Lesotho, Africa, where her father, a long-haired, bearded history professor, taught school. The family eventually moved to a Philadelphia suburb, where her parents got involved in union and anti-war activities.

"They always joked that they were the first hippies," Cannan said. "I don't know if I ever went to a peace march during the Vietnam War, but there are pictures of my father at peace marches."

Cannan said her parents had instilled in her from an early age the notion that all people are special in their own way.

"Certainly that influenced me," she said. "I believe in open dialogue and understanding. I'm not sure I'm a pacifist. I just really don't believe in war."

Neither Cannan nor Meymand was actively opposed to the first Gulf War, but Meymand had misgivings.

"It was hypocritical for the U.S. to support and arm Saddam Hussein throughout the 1980s and never raise any objections to his comments about reclaiming lost territories and then, suddenly, declare war," he said. "I guess we had no options, but it's another example of all our mistakes coming home to roost."

Meymand said what he sees as foreign policy mistakes are still roosting, but the situation is different.

"Back then, we let things get out of control, and Saddam Hussein invaded another country," he said. "The difference now is that he has been contained, and I object to this notion of pre-emption. I don't think Hussein poses a danger to us or his neighbors."

But regardless of the outcome of the conflict, Meymand and Cannan hope their activism will teach their children valuable lessons that they will eventually use to make the world a better place.

Leila, at least, may actually know more about politics than spelling. "DON'T INVAD EIRAQ," reads the hand-painted paper bag vest she wears at rallies.
