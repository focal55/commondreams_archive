Published on Saturday, May 18, 2002 by CommonDreams.org
Market Rules and Welfare Reform
by Seth Sandronsky
 

Officially in the U.S., market rules are the cure for social problems. Take people living on welfare and in poverty.

The way for them “to move up the economic ladder” is to work for wages, noted Rep. John Boehner, R-Ohio. He supports a revision of the federal welfare program (now Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) signed by Democratic President Clinton in 1996 that ends in 2002.

House Republicans such as Boehner took their cue on revising TANF from President Bush. With little fanfare, House Republicans passed legislation on May 16 that would make TANF recipients labor longer (40 versus 30 hours per week).

As recent entrants in the job market, they would likely work for low pay. Market rules dictate that people with no or little history of paid work earn low wages.

These workers then get penalized with substandard nutrition and shelter. They are known as the so-called “working poor.”

We see here the role of the government. It is to enforce market rules on the politically powerless for the benefit of their employers.

The U.S. government is not shrinking in the age of “globalization.” Rather, government is increasing its enforcement power against the interests of the poorest people being pushed to participate more in the nation’s job market.

The proposed revision of welfare is a new phase in the class war waged by the few against the many to regulate wages downward. Flooding the job market with previously inactive workers will serve to moderate or even pull down the wage demands of active workers.

The latter know what time it is concerning the finding and keeping of a job that pays the bills. And that time is not the best for them now in the nation’s workplaces.

Officially, the U.S. job market has yet to show clear evidence of a recovery from last year’s no/slow growth, or recession. April’s official unemployment rate was 6.0 percent versus 5.7 percent in March.

Meanwhile, some employers have indicated that they will hire new workers at a more sluggish rate versus the previous year due to the “soft economy.” Consider the jobless rate, seasonally adjusted, for workers 16 through 19 years old, up 16.8 percent this April, a five-year high, reported The Wall Street Journal of May 15.

Moreover, a certain kind of economic prosperity creates social conditions of instability. In the U.S., the accumulation of capital by a tiny minority with the guiding hand of government has, in turn, helped make the lives of the nation’s poorest adults and their dependents more precarious.

They can in part thank the two parties of big business for federal welfare reform on Clinton's watch. This has paved the way for the current revision.

By mandating longer hours of wage-labor for TANF recipients, Congress and the president would force families to have less time together. This reduces parental oversight of children, a recipe to increase family stress and turmoil.

House Republican legislation that would lengthen the work week of TANF recipients will be subsequently considered by the Senate. Market rules and welfare reform march on.
