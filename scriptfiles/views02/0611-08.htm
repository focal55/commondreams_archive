Published on Tuesday, June 11, 2002 by CommonDreams.org
Leaving the ABM Treaty in the Dust
by Leah Wells
 

Two months ago, the Los Angeles Times exposed a U.S. plan for developing contingency plans to use nuclear weapons against Russia, China, Syria, Lybia, Iran, Iraq, and/or North Korea. This enlightening, albeit dangerous and revealing, policy demonstrates our motives of paving the road for a fast and furious arms race unlike any other. To make matters worse, the United States will unilaterally walk away from the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty this Thursday under the pretext that it is outdated and unneeded.

The ruthless pursuit to defend our country's borders, like deputizing law enforcement agents for expanding the Immigration and Naturalization Services, restricting access to information about the success of missile defense tests from Vandenburg Air Force Base in Lompoc, CA, or backing out of treaties like the ABM or withdrawing our signature from the International Criminal Court treaty, ultimately weakens our country because we missappropriate funds for potential military responses rather than creating a strong national infrastructure that cares for the needs of all its' citizens.

Leo Tolstoy, in his essay entitled "Patriotism or Peace" cites a passage by the Chinese philosopher Confucius: "In reply to a prince's question how to increase his army, in order to conquer a southern tribe which did not submit to him, Confucius replied: 'Destroy all thy army, and use the money, which thou art wasting now on the army, on the enlightenment of thy people and on the improvement of agriculture, and the southern tribe will drive away its prince and will submit to thy rule without war.'"

In response to our country's policies and to Confucius' advice, I have two questions: Is there more than one way to defend our country? Is there more than one way to show we love our country?

Seems like taking care of future generations is a good way both to defend our country and show we love it. When one-fourth of children in America live in poverty, our country is less secure and less stable. A healthy, well-educated and well-cared-for population will want to invest their time, energy and money in keeping America beautiful. A deprived, sickly and ill-informed population is actually a danger to our nation.

Future generations deserve a good education and foundation for making good decisions and getting good jobs. When we build 200 new prison cells every day, we demonstrate our priorities and expectations for our youth. Our President's educational plan claims it will "leave no child behind." Makes me wonder about those 969 missing foster children in Florida. We won't leave any kids behind - as long as we can find them!

We can create a safe country by protecting our environment as well. For example, in the proposed nuclear waste storage plan, radioactive material would be transported across most state lines. I wonder why, if the material is not safe enough where it is currently at the reactors, it would be sent in transit to Yucca Mountain, past homes and schools, past neighborhoods and communities, to be stored at a site which itself is situated on a dangerous earthquake fault. Ought we make safety for our land, water and air a priority, not to mention the fact that these moving nuclear transports could fall prey to terrorist attacks themselves!

We can defend our country by establishing alliances with other countries, especially those on the "terrible seven" list. Folks from the Peace Pilgrim Center, in a letter after the terrorist attacks on September 11, said that we still have the capacity to transform enemies into friends. Dr. King challenged us to do the same thing in his essay entitled "Loving Your Enemies." When we can transform an enemy into a friend, we have won a double victory.

Can we show love for our country demonstrating discipline? When a parent reprimands a child, hopefully we don't wonder if the parent hates the child. On the contrary, guiding a straying child by calling them back in line shows care and concern. Children with no boundaries often grow up to be unwieldy and difficult adults. Those who question our current defense policies do not hate the United States. We love it so much that we are willing to challenge its direction, make those in power justify why they feel that a military response is the best response.

Similarly, it is only smart to question such serious matters abandoning treaties. If someone hijacked our car, blindfolded us and told us to drive really fast, hopefully we would be concerned with our safety and mortality - and certainly wonder if the hijacker, acting alone, was sane. Our policy toward the ABM treaty is the same. We are unilaterally and dangerously blindfolding ourselves as we careen along a precarious path.

Perhaps we should heed Confucius' words. Or Dr. King's. Or buckle up - we're in for a wild ride.

Leah C. Wells serves as the Peace Education Coordinator for the Nuclear Age Peace Foundation. She may be contacted at education@napf.org
