Published on Wednesday, November 6, 2002 by the Boston Globe
A Political Winner's Curse
by Robert Kuttner
 

WHEN SOMEBODY bids too much at an auction or when a corporate raider overpays to acquire a company, this is known as the ''winner's curse.'' The too-eager winner would literally have been better off if somebody else had won.

To a great extent, the winners of yesterday's election will suffer from a political winner's curse. What they are facing is not pretty.

Start with the states. Democrats enjoyed some pick-ups of governorships. But the states have not been in such fiscal straits since the 1970s. All but a few states face huge deficits. This is less the result of past spending sprees than of economic slowdown and the reduction in federal aid.

Federal tax cutting and program reduction at the expense of aid to the states should have been a big election issue, but it wasn't. Republican gubernatorial candidates and incumbents were more loyal to their president and his ideology than to their own constituents. Most Democrats either didn't raise the issue or couldn't make it resonate.

Candidates idiotically outdid each other to promise no tax increases or to insist that previously unnoticed budget savings could be easily reaped so that services would not suffer.

Now comes the reckoning. Any candidate for governor who told the voters it is possible to solve the state fiscal crisis without serious cuts in services or an increase in state taxes was telling a whopper. So congratulations, winners. You now face the unenviable task of explaining to constituents why it's not really your fault that services are being slashed and in many cases taxes raised as well. But in fact, it's mostly not the governors' fault. Instead of hectoring each other, governors, consumers of state services, and taxpayers should all be marching on George W. Bush's White House.

In the 1970s, Republican presidents Richard Nixon and Gerald Ford came to the rescue of the states with revenue sharing. This was the ''New Federalism'' backed with actual resources. Bush, by contrast, talks a good federalist game. But he'd rather squander federal revenues on tax cuts for which he can claim credit and let the governors take the heat.

While yesterday's election was not really a referendum on him, the president faces heavy economic weather, too. The Federal Reserve's policy-setting committee meets today amid signs that the economy is continuing to weaken. Many analysts are calling for yet another cut in short-term interest rates, perhaps all the way from the current four-decade low of 1.75 percent to 1.25 percent.

The Fed is in a jam. If it does cut rates, it signals that the Fed agrees that things are pretty bleak. If the Fed fails to cut rates, it is confessing that it's out of tricks. The economy is on the edge of a double-dip recession. Business profits can't gain traction because the economy still suffers from all the over-investment of the 1990s.

With profits weak, business is laying off workers rather than hiring new ones. The layoffs, in turn, drag down consumer spending, which then reinforces the weak profits. To complete the cycle, the dismal profits serve to short circuit any durable stock market rally. Accounting scandals and lax regulation continue to sow doubts about whether even reported corporate profits are real.

The only thing keeping the economy afloat is those very low interest rates. Consumers may be pinched, but mortgages are a bargain; new car financing deals are great; and home equity loans are there to be tapped.

But this source of stimulus can go on only so long. Lower rates could mean a cheaper dollar, and perhaps inflation. Inflation invites higher rates. But with the economy so dependent on very cheap credit, any hike in rates would be a disaster. If, say, a war triggered even mild inflation and the Fed had to raise rates, things could get really nasty.

There is no easy cure for this condition. Eventually, the excess capacity of the 1990s will be worked off or written off. And eventually, some political party will again come to power actually believing in public investment. That would be good for those hard pressed states. It would also create more jobs. But with both parties tripping over each other to swear off serious public outlay, that day won't come soon. So the economy will remain soft for a while.

In the meantime, although Congress remains closely divided, it's a Republican administration setting the agenda. And a Republican president will be held accountable by voters two years from now.

Robert Kuttner is co-editor of The American Prospect. His column appears regularly in the Globe.
