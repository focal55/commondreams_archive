Published on Thursday, February 7, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
'Trust Us, We Know What We're Doing'
by Michael Graf and Matthew Orr
 
OF ALL the sins committed by the leaders of Enron, perhaps none is greater than the breach of trust between Enron's management personnel and its employees, who were encouraged to stay with the ship even as Ken Lay, Enron's CEO, was cutting loose the lifeboats.

If we look at Enron's demise as a capitalistic fable, two truisms come to light. First, people in charge don't necessarily have a clue as to the long- term consequences of their actions, which may inflict substantial injury on all persons involved.

Second, the average citizen will be the last to know what is going on, and likely to suffer the greatest injury.

"Trust us, we know what we are doing."

How many times was that phrase told to regulators, congressman, the White House and to Enron employees? How silly that phrase sounds today in light of Enron's collapse. Surely, we won't fall for that one again. Or will we?

One of the most disturbing aspects of the Enron debacle is the company's hand in the administration's energy policy. Vice President Dick Cheney has defended the secrecy of his meetings with Enron executives, citing a constitutional right to seek "good advice" in private. The message appears to be, "Don't worry about it, we know what we are doing. . . Trust us." Sound familiar?

Certainly, one can wonder how our congressional leadership could hand our nation's energy strategy to a group that has failed so miserably with its own affairs. Shouldn't we be a little skeptical when the same persons who were selling stocks while encouraging their own employees to buy tell us that there will be no permanent environmental degradation of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, the California Coast and other "public" lands from expanded oil and gas drilling?

The lack of public disclosure regarding the administration's energy policy is bad enough on the issue of development vs. preservation of pristine lands, but the stakes are actually much higher.

Just as Enron executives ignored warnings that reckless bookkeeping would bring disaster, so too the Enron-Cheney energy plan ignores an overwhelming consensus of scientists from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change warning of the dangerous effects of fossil fuels on world climate.

This warning was recently joined by more than 60 Nobel laureates, who in December co-signed a statement that begins: "The most profound danger to world peace in the coming years will stem from the legitimate demands of the world's dispossessed. Of these poor and disenfranchised, the majority live a marginal existence in equatorial climates. Global warming, not of their making but originating with the wealthy few, will affect their fragile ecologies the most. "

The House of Representatives has voted its faith in the Enron-Cheney energy plan even though the policy emphasizes fossil fuels over the development of alternative energies and conservation. Scientists and Nobel laureates, it would seem, are less trustworthy than energy executives.

Nor is this blind trust limited to Enron and energy policy. When a 500-page agreement giving the World Trade Organization the ability to overrule international conventions, acts, treaties and agreements in the name of "free trade" faced congressional approval, Ralph Nader offered $10,000 in the fall of 1994 to any charity chosen by a congressman who would sign an affidavit saying he had read the agreement and who could answer several questions about it.

Only one, Hank Brown, who was then a Republican senator from Colorado, accepted Nader's offer. Brown voted against the agreement, which passed Congress.

"In God We Trust" may be written on our money, but faith of late has been placed in the ideology that if we turn our government over to the highest bidder, our economy will flourish and so will we.

"Today, American opinion leaders seem generally convinced that democracy and the free market are simply identical," Thomas Frank wrote in his book, "One Market Under God."

Enron's fall may raise awareness of the limits of unregulated markets to provide for the general welfare. But don't count on it.

A noteworthy aspect of the Enron affair is that it was even noticed by the media and the public. In contrast to Enron's spectacular collapse, the fallout from many unregulated market-driven decisions are often more subtle, with much longer time lines.

As we increasingly place trust in private enterprise over government for leadership into the uncertain future, we can only hope that Enron does not become a metaphor for the demise of things far more important than a company built on smoke and mirrors.

Matthew Orr is an instructor in environmental sciences at the University of California at Berkeley. Michael Graf is an environmental attorney and instructor of land-use regulation at Golden Gate University School of Law.
