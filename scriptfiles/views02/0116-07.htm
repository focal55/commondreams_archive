Published on Wednesday, January 16, 2002 by Common Dreams
Can the Fed Rescue the U.S. Economy or Not?
by Seth Sandronsky
 
Can the Federal Reserve spur an economic recovery in the U.S.? This is no academic question. Officially, 8.3 million Americans are out of a job.

On Jan. 11 in San Francisco, Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan made a speech implying that cutting the short-term interest rate again may be a necessary move to get the nation’s economy back on track.

"I would emphasize that we continue to face significant risks in the near term," he said. Greenspan also noted frail corporate profitability and business investment, rising joblessness and weak consumer spending power.

On the day he spoke, the Ford Motor Co. announced that it would sharply cut production of cars and fire 35,000 workers worldwide. Zero-percent rate financing in late 2001 didn’t help the car maker maintain profitability. Could there be a lesson here about easy credit?

Perhaps the nation’s newspaper of record can shed some light on this. According to a Jan. 12 New York Times article, “Since last January, the Fed has lowered its benchmark federal funds target rate – which directly affects many small-business, credit card and automobile loans -by 4.75 percentage points, to 1.75 percent.”

Robert Manning, author of Credit Card Nation, , has a different view of what the Fed is (not) doing. “The Fed's traditional policy of reducing the discount rate to stimulate business borrowing and investment is incapable of addressing the new realities of the post-industrial, consumer driven economy,” he said.

The high value of the dollar has harmed U.S. industry by making its exports expensive on the world market. On a related note, the high dollar attracts funds from foreign lenders. This allows Americans to buy more than they sell, thanks to borrowed money from abroad.

Manning continued: “With the end of community banking and rise of financial services conglomerates, the banking industry is simply profiting from the lower cost of credit. They refuse to pass on the savings to consumers or small businesses in the form of lower interest rates. More strikingly, they have tightened their lending criteria and are in effect starving highly indebted households (simply forcing payment of double-digit interest rates) and not offering additional loans during a difficult period (like the crisis in Argentina).”

The credit system in America and Argentina is cut from similar cloth. During economic downturns, debtors in both countries who need money get milked like cows or denied needed loans.

What if Fed interest rate cuts don’t spur a U.S. economic recovery? Fiscal policy, anyone?

Seth Sandronsky is an editor with Because People Matter, Sacramento’s progressive newspaper ssandron@hotmail.com
