Published on Tuesday, October 1, 2002 by the Guardian/UK
A Law Unto Themselves
by Matthew Engel
 

We are approaching a big moment in American politics, but not for any of the reasons you might imagine, or are likely to have read about. Coming up is the first Monday in October, the start of the new term in the supreme court.

The nine justices met informally yesterday. Today or tomorrow they are expected to announce which cases they consider worthy of their winter-time attention. Do they fancy White v South Carolina, perhaps, in which Bill Clinton's legal pursuer, Ken Starr, is defending a tattooist, a practice banned under state law? Or Oakley v Wisconsin, which covers whether a state has the right to ban someone from having children as a condition of probation? They will definitely have to consider executions again, and California's patented three-strikes-and-you're-locked-up-for-25-years rule.

The judges will be sitting down as old colleagues, if not necessarily as friends. For the ninth successive year, the court's make-up is unchanged, an unprecedented run of stability and non-mortality. The tension is becoming overwhelming; sooner or later they have to start dying. And then all hell is going to break loose.

There are three prongs to the US government, remember. Because the executive (i.e. the president) dominates the attention of the addled media, the legislature and, especially, the judiciary get forgotten. Yet by far the most vituperative disputes between White House and Congress of the past year have concerned the appointment of judges. Republicans blame the Democrats for jeopardizing the judicial system by holding up appointments; Democrats say the other lot started it under Clinton, when the choices were less provocative. Actually, the arguments probably started in George Washington's administration, but they have rarely been this rancorous.

Supreme court vacancies come up infrequently, but the administration has to fill hundreds of more routine vacancies in other courts. The process is always overtly political. Still, the Bush administration, despite its own flawed mandate and all his pre-election talk of "compassionate conservatism", does seem peculiarly blatant in pushing its own partisans, sometimes extreme ones.

The Democrat-controlled Senate judiciary committee has held up many appointments and thrown out two, voting 10-nine on party lines. At least five more are in doubt. If the Democrats lose their Senate majority in the mid-term elections next month, all these nominations are likely to go through. And the gates will be open for Bush to make the supreme court appointment he wants. Stand by for someone (preferably, for political reasons, a Hispanic someone) combining the qualities of Judges Jeffreys, Roy Bean ("Hang 'em first, try 'em later") and Melford Stevenson.

The court is already split ideologically. This manifested itself most ignobly in December 2000 when, by an astonishing coincidence, the court's five Republican-leaning judges thought Florida's electoral laws required that George Bush should be declared as president, and the four Democratic-leaning judges thought they did not.

But the ideological divide exists on a more intellectual plain too, displayed in the split between two of the judges, Antonin Scalia and Stephen Breyer. Scalia is the leader of the rightwing in the court, a man whose reading of the constitution is rooted firmly in the 18th century. As he says: "The constitution that I interpret and apply is not living but dead."

It is not an unfamiliar view in a country of biblical fundamentalists; it is slightly surprising to find the judicial mindset that allowed the US to have first slavery, and then segregation, still at the very heart of government. Even Breyer, who talks of purpose and context when interpreting the law, is hardly an out-and-out liberal: he has usually voted to reject appeals against the death penalty, even if he does sound angst-ridden about it.

In any case, there is no doubt which way this argument is going. Scalia is possibly the next chief justice. If Bush stays in office until 2009, he is going to skew the court so far to the right it would take decades to unravel. Long after the president might have been forgotten, his appointees would be influencing American law.

Supreme court judges often thwart the intentions of their political patrons by turning out other than expected. Bush is likely to appoint people whose views will be so far off and up the wall that no mistake will be possible.

It could be enough to ensure a continuation of his worldview until Doomsday. It is not just a matter of tattooists in South Carolina. It will show itself in cases about capital punishment, corporate responsibility, gun control, you name it. Watch carefully.
