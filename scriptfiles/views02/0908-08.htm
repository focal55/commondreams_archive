Published on Sunday, September 8, 2002 by CommonDreams.org
My Rosh Hashana (Jewish New Year) Offering
by Rita Corriel
 

"On Rosh Hashana all is written and revealed, and on Yom Kippur the course of every life is sealed.....How many pass on and how many thrive, who shall live and who shall die.....Repentence, prayer and acts of justice can avert an 'evil decree'." The question is "How can acts of justice avert this 'evil decree'?"

I have been pondering this question in light of our post 911 world and mindset. A mindset that demoralizes me, offends me and quite frankly terrifies me more than any single act of terrorism. I see a world that appears to be spinning out of control at an alarming rate of acceleration. A world, that from my vantage point, is headed on a collision course with its own irrational fears and resulting hatreds and violence. I do not believe in "evil decrees", nor do I believe that the future is ever written in stone. However I do know that when certain patterns of behavior continue, unexamined and unchanged, they do indeed lead to some very predictable consequences. The handwriting is quite clearly, "on the wall". This year the High Holy Days hold a particularly profound and auspicious significance for all of us both personally and globally. There really is no difference. We are all in this together. And I am wondering how "acts of justice" can change the sweeping tide of current events and change the course of our collective future...

When I think of the word "justice" a mental image appears. I see an ancient Greek goddess holding high the scales of balance in her hand. And I am reminded that balance is the essential nature of peace. Therefore, it seems to me that any act of justice should inevitably lead to peace. So, in light of this time of new beginnings; this time of creating the world anew, I would ask us all to seriously consider the following questions... Will acts of destructive retaliation lead to peace? Will negation of the humanity of those whom we fear lead to peace? Will the destruction of homes and villages lead to peace? Will demonizing "the other" lead to peace? Will denying people their dignity and civil liberties lead to peace? Will constant threats of violence lead to peace? Will the constant repetition of hollow platitudes lead to peace? Will oppression lead to peace? Does the dictum "an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth" express the sort of justice that leads to peace in the twenty first century? Do two wrongs ever make a right?

I believe that on this Rosh Hashana the sound of the shofar blast must be heard as an urgent wake up call. A call for us to challenge ourselves to become spiritual grown ups. To behave as though there really is a Creative Source of our Being and our world. And as reflections of this Source we really do have the capacity to act responsibly. We can use our minds and hearts creatively to reconsider and reenvision what justice truly means; in far greater and much deeper ways than we have ever done before. The conclusion I have come to is this... If it does not reflect our highest values and our deepest compassion, if it does not renew our spirit and bring hope to our hearts, if it does not make us proud of our own humanity, if it does not create peace, than it is not justice... If this sounds naive or overly idealistic in today's world, I invite you to consider the concluding words of an official statement that was drafted last year by several of our Nobel laureates. "We have reached a time in our history when idealism is the only realism". Shalom.

Rita Corriel is a clinical psychologist and peace activist in Allentown, Pennsylvania. E-mail: rcorriel@fast.net
