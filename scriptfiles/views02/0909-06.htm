Published on Monday, September 9, 2002 by CommonDreams.org
Ignore the Distractions: Bush Means War
by Mike Zmolek
 

Only two days after President Bush's September 4th meeting with top Congressional officials, US forces patrolling the no-fly zones over Iraq stepped up their attacks on Iraqi military targets. This is happening just as reports surfaced that the largest US military buildup in Gulf region since the Gulf Conflict of 1991 was underway. The September 4th meeting itself was a watershed. Until that point at least two things had been ambiguous on the surface: whether the President would consult Congress on his war plans, and whether Bush was serious when he said he had not made a decision. With top Democrats indicating after the meeting that they could probably be persuaded to vote for war, the Bush strategy suddenly appears quite clear. A barrage of conflicting reports have been circulating in the media over the last six months, much of it known to be disinformation. And the media has been caught up in covering the various apparent splits over whether the US should 'go it alone' in invading Iraq (with no serious disagreement over whether or not attacking Iraq would, in itself, be a good idea). All of this served to distract attention away from any voices that oppose attacking Iraq at all. But at the same time, there has been a virtually unprecedented groundswell of action, organization and speaking out against the Bush administration's plans to attack Iraq. Tens of thousands of Americans have been organizing at the grassroots, meeting their congressmen and candidates, expressing deep concerns and strong opposition to a major attack on Iraq.

While almost overnight the Bush camp has gone from appearing divided and in disarray, it now appears virtually unanimous and confident that it can win over Congress and possibly a few allies at the UN to support a plan for war driven by a mix of hysteria and unsubstantiated allegations. While the Democrats are already indicating that they will not allow such a vote to take place before the election, the administration appears to be closing ranks in an attempt to rush a pro-war vote through Congress before it adjourns again in October, in hopes of making an end run around the growing opposition to war. The Republicans know that their Democratic opponents in the November elections will be wary about being seen weak on patriotism if they should dare to speak out against the war, even if the public is deeply divided and confused about the whole matter. It remains to be seen whether voters will be so distracted by foreign policy (normally a non-factor in elections) that they will lose sight of the stagnating and floundering US economy.

So if you don't want to see the US launch a massive assault against a country that was already 'bombed back into the stone age' eleven years ago, then you need to be spending every free moment you have writing letters, making phone calls, sending faxes and e-mails, voicing your opinion loud and clear on talk radio shows, in letters to the editor, any outlet you have to speak out. And take heart, all the facts are on your side.

First, there is simply no evidence of Iraqi government involvement in the attacks of September eleventh. Therefore, the Patriot Act does not provide for US military action against Iraq. Second, there is no evidence of Iraq having reconstituted its weapons of mass destruction programs. On September 7th, British Prime Minister Tony Blair met with President Bush and unveiled his long-awaited 'proof' that Iraq is reconstituting its programs. The evidence? Satellite photos showing construction at former weapons production sites. "What more evidence do we need?" Bush asked reporters. But the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) says that the photos do not reveal anything to arouse suspicions of renewed weapons programs in Iraq. And the White House staff have already admitted that Bush's claim that a 1998 IAEA study reported that Iraq would have nuclear weapons capability within six months was mistaken (the report actually "found no indication of Iraq having achieved its program goal of producing nuclear weapons..."). The fact that Bush and Blair's 'evidence' was discredited within 24 hours of its revelation means we should call on our leaders to be on guard and call for scrutiny, deliberation and caution before they commit young men to war based on specious allegations or suspiciously convenient evidence.

But the question of whether or not Iraq possesses such weapons distracts from the real issue: would Iraq use them against the US or its allies? Is historical memory so feint these days that we have forgotten about the Cold War and deterrence? With upwards of 40,000 Soviet nuclear missiles pointed at the US, not one was fired, and that has everything to do with the threat that the US posed to the USSR. Iraqi Foreign Minister Tariq Aziz is on record as saying that it would be suicide for Iraq to threaten the US with weapons of mass destruction.

Launching an all-out war on Iraq would distract this nation from the real danger our citizens face. That danger comes from groups like Al Qaeda that have openly declared their intent to take the lives of American citizens and who showed on September 11 just how seriously they are to be taken. The Iraqi government's military activities can be subjected to inspections, but Al Qaeda has the elements of surprise, anonymity and perhaps also patience (remember the WTC attack of 1993?) on their side. Chasing Al Qaeda operatives down in the hills of Afghanistan and confiscating arms caches may slow them down in the short run, but until the US reverses policies that brutalize whole populations in the region, every major military action it undertakes there will surely make recruiting all that much easier for these terrorists, and thus will surely make it more dangerous for Americans to travel abroad or even to stay at home. In the age of weapons of mass destruction, the risks of war for even the world's sole superpower are incalculable. That puts the highest of premiums on diplomacy. The US could have inspectors back in Iraq tomorrow if it wanted them; all it would have to do is lay out a clear timetable for the lifting of the cruel embargo on Iraq.

All the debate about whether or not the US has evidence to support its pretext for attacking Iraq also serves to distract from the question of what the US stands to gain from such an invasion. And virtually everybody knows that this question can be answered with one word: oil. If this were Western Australia, forget it, we would not even be talking about it. It is only a narrow range of interests that will be served by an invasion of Iraq.

Will the interests of Iraqis really be served? If Karzai's shaky puppet regime in Afghanistan is any indication, then clearly Iraqis can expect the US to expend tens of billions of dollars to bomb them, and only tens of millions to help rebuild a post-Saddam Iraq. Unlike Afghanistan, Iraq's population is concentrated in urban centers, where Iraq will no doubt locate its military units and where the US will no doubt concentrate its bombing. So the civilian death rates will likely be much higher and could easily rival the 75,000 to 150,000 Iraqi civilians that human rights groups estimated perished from the 1991 bombing.

And will the American public be served? Will we really be any safer? Will our troops come home sick as they did eleven years ago? And how many young men now in uniform will come back in body bags?

The National Network to End the War Against Iraq (NNEWAI) is asking everyone to call their senators and congressmen each Tuesday until the election to voice opposition to attacking Iraq. NNEWAI ('New Way') members will be joining other organizations on September 29th in Washington for a public forum and protest against plans to attack Iraq. The tentative plan is for an 'embassy march' to encourage other nations to hold fast to their opposition to the Bush war plan. On the following weekend, October 6th and 7th, local protests will be held across the US. And plans are underway for another, larger, anti-war demonstration at the steps of the Capitol in mid-October, before Congress adjourns.
