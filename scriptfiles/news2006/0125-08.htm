Campaign for America's Future Praises Newly Expanded Americans United
 

WASHINGTON - January 25 - Campaign for America's Future co-director Roger Hickey today praised the announcement of the newly expanded Americans United.

"Americans United to Protect Social Security - which successfully mobilized to stop Bush's Wall Street Social Security plan - grew directly out of our work to build a nationwide activist coalition to oppose Social Security privatization," said Hickey. "This new campaign, organized with a broader issue mandate, will build on and amplify the work of the Campaign for America's Future and many other groups around the issues of cleaning up corruption in government; health care; energy independence and the other 'kitchen table' economic issues Americans care about."

Americans United today released a new television ad about cleaning up corruption in the wake of the Jack Abramoff corruption scandal, reinforcing a targeted campaign by the Campaign for America's Future and the Public Campaign Action Fund in the communities of tainted members of Congress.

The Campaign for America's Future's "Corrupt Congress" project has targeted congressional corruption because big money payoffs, both legal and illegal, give undue weight to special interests in the halls of Congress. Payoffs produce legislation that undermines American's retirement security, hastens the loss of good jobs and prevents progress on health care, education and investment in a healthy economy.

"The disastrous Bush prescription drug benefit for seniors is a prime example of how special interest money creates bad policy that undermines security for average Americans," said Hickey. "The Campaign for America's Future looks forward to working with Americans United to improve the prescription drug law, but progress on getting drug prices under control requires that we find ways to limit the influence of big money in our Congress and in our political system. We will work for economic change - while cleaning up corruption in our democratic system that weakens the voice of average Americans."

Three Houston television stations recently began airing a targeted television ad that links embattled Rep. Tom DeLay, R- Texas, to corruption after considerable public pressure and negotiations. The stations' decision to air the spot marks a turning point in the controversy, which began when Rep. DeLay threatened to sue media outlets if they aired the earlier ad. The Campaign for America's Future and the Public Campaign Action Fund, the two public interest groups that joined forces to run the spots, vigorously fought back.

The groups unveiled $115,000 in new television, radio and billboard ads targeting Rep. DeLay and Rep. Bob Ney, R-Ohio, for their ties to corruption earlier this month, kicking off a yearlong campaign to clean up Congress. Speaker Dennis Hastert, R-Ill., asked Rep. Ney to leave his leadership post as chair of the House Administration Committee after the ads aired in southeastern Ohio.
