Center on Budget and Policy Priorities Conference Call: Would the Line-Item Veto Invite Abuse by the Executive Branch?
 

WASHINGTON - March 21 -

WHAT: Center on Budget and Policy Priorities Conference Call: Would the Line-Item Veto Invite Abuse by the Executive Branch?

WHEN: Thursday, March 23 at 12 p.m. (EST)

Register: http://www.cbpp.org/confcall.htm

WHO: Center staff:

-- Robert Greenstein, executive director

-- Richard Kogan, senior fellow

-- Jim Horney, senior fellow

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities will hold a conference call briefing Thursday, March 23 at 12 p.m. (EST) on the Administration's proposed line-item veto legislation. The line-item veto proposal is expected to be the centerpiece of budget legislation that the House Budget Committee could take up after the St. Patrick's Day recess and that the Senate will consider later this year.

In conjunction with the briefing, the Center will issue a new analysis which finds that the legislation is designed in ways that shift considerably more power to the Executive Branch than has been understood and that could invite abuse by the Executive. For example, if the President proposed to cancel funds appropriated for a program, Congress would have to vote on his proposal within 18 days, but if Congress turned down the proposal, the President could continue withholding the funds until 180 days had passed. If the fiscal year ended before the 180-day period did, the funds could expire, enabling the President to kill programs even if Congress had rejected his proposals to do so.

The legislation also would give the President the ability to press Members of Congress to support his budget and legislative priorities by threatening to cancel appropriation items that they favored if they did not support his priorities, and promising not to cancel their favored items if they did support him. As a result, the Congressional Budget Office has indicated the legislation could lead to increases, rather than decreases, in the deficit if a President uses the new power to gain support for tax cuts or spending increases he has proposed.

Center briefers will assess these and other aspects of the legislation.

After opening remarks, the panelists will take questions.
