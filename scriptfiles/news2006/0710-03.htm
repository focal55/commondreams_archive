Democracy 21 Calls for Investigations into Rep. Doolittle's Activities in Support of Abramoff's Clients
 

WASHINGTON - July 10 - In a letter sent today to the House Ethics Committee, Democracy 21 President Fred Wertheimer called for an investigation into whether Rep. John Doolittle (R-Calif.) violated House ethics rules by intervening in two case-specific matters pending at the Department of the Interior in support of lobbyist Jack Abramoff's tribal clients during the same period that a company owned by Rep. Doolittle's wife received payments from Abramoff's law firm, which inured to the personal financial benefit of Rep. Doolittle's family.

The Democracy 21 letter specifically noted the close proximity in time between the interventions at the Interior Department by Rep. Doolittle, which benefited Abramoff's clients, and the receipt by Rep. Doolittle's family of personal financial benefits from Abramoff's law firm.

The Democracy 21 letter also called on the Ethics Committee to investigate whether the services provided by the company of Julie Doolittle, Rep. Doolittle's wife, to Abramoff's law firm during the periods from August 2002 to January 2003, and from July 2003 to February 2004, were commensurate with the fees of some $67,000 paid by the law firm to Julie Doolittle's company during those periods.

Democracy 21 also today forwarded a copy of its House Ethics Committee letter to the Public Integrity Section of the Justice Department and called for an investigation of whether Doolittle's activities may have violated criminal statues.

Today's letter to the House Ethics Committee followed a previous letter sent by Democracy 21 to the Ethics Committee on June 23 that called for an investigation into other activities engaged in by Rep. Doolittle. These matters included:

Whether Rep. Doolittle violated House ethics rules, including the rules that prohibit a House member from converting any campaign funds under the member's control to personal use, through his arrangement with his wife's company whereby Doolittle's campaign committee and his leadership PAC paid more than $200,000 in percentage-based commission fees to his wife's company for fundraising services; and

Whether Rep. Doolittle's official actions to help provide businessman Brent Wilkes' company with $37 million in "earmarked" federal funds were undertaken in connection with, or created the appearance that they were undertaken in connection with, contributions made by Wilkes and his associates to Doolittle's campaign committee and leadership PAC, resulting in fundraising fees paid to his wife's company, in violation of House ethics rules.

"As set forth in our letters, the Doolittle case provides another clear test of whether the House Ethics Committee is going to take seriously its responsibilities to ensure that House Members comply with House ethics rules," Wertheimer stated.

"The Doolittle case also raises the important issue of whether the House Ethics Committee is going to allow Members to convert campaign funds from their campaign committees and leadership PACs to personal use, in violation of specific prohibitions in the House ethics rules, and thereby provide a license for wholesale abuse of these rules by House members," Wertheimer stated.

Copies of the letters sent today to the House Ethics Committee and the Justice Department and a copy of the June 23 letter to the House Ethics Committee are available at http://www.democracy21.org.
