Africa Action and 300 Religious Leaders Call for Renewed G8 Focus on Africa
Last Year's G8 Promises on Debt and HIV/AIDS Still Need Action, Darfur Genocide Must Also Feature on Summit Agenda
 

WASHINGTON - July 13 - As the Group of 8 (G8) wealthy nations launch their annual meeting in St. Petersburg, Russia over the coming weekend, Africa Action today released a new set of talking points on the G8 and Africa, urging new action from G8 leaders on urgent African priorities - from the continent's debt crisis, to the HIV/AIDS pandemic, to the ongoing genocide in Darfur. Also today, Africa Action released a letter to President Bush, signed by 300 religious leaders of diverse religious traditions, urging greater global engagement in support of African efforts to address the continent's challenges.

The letter to President Bush was circulated by Africa Action's Religious Action Network, an interfaith network of religious leaders and their communities committed to positively shaping U.S. Africa relations. The full text of the Religious Leader Letter to the G8 on HIV/AIDS and Debt is available on Africa Action's website here: http://www.africaaction.org/campaign_new/docs/2006G8SignOnFinal.pdf

Ann-Louise Colgan, Acting Co-Executive Director of Africa Action, said today, "Last year's G8 agenda rightly concentrated on Africa's challenges. But promises made last year - on debt cancellation and universal access to HIV/AIDS treatment - must not be forgotten now, and new action is required from the G8 on these urgent issues. The G8 represent a global elite, and as this year's summit begins, they must address the priorities of the global majority."

Marie Clarke Brill, Acting Co-Executive Director of Africa Action, said today, "Activists around the world have heard the G8 pledges towards Africa, and they are still waiting for the G8 to fully deliver on these promises. Hundreds of religious leaders have signed on to a letter to President Bush, calling on him to show leadership at the G8 summit to tackle the HIV/AIDS pandemic and to cancel 100% of Africa's debt. They represent a much larger population of people who refuse to allow the G8 to marginalize Africa's concerns."

This year's G8 summit will include discussion on three priorities: infectious disease, global energy security and education. While the explicit focus on Africa's challenges has disappeared from the agenda, the "Africa Action Talking Points on the G8 and Africa" - included below - demonstrate both that those challenges continue to require action and that Africa has a role to play in dealing with the concerns on this year's G8 agenda.

The G8 discussion of infectious disease will seek to address the global response to epidemic threats. The recent "Africa Action Statement on Avian Influenza and Africa," underscores how, in light of Africa's experience with HIV/AIDS and the risk of avian flu, a new G8 focus on strengthening Africa's health systems is required. This resource is available here: http://www.africaaction.org/newsroom/docs/avianflu0606.pdf

One week prior to the G8 launch, Africa Action released a statement on last year's G8 promises on Africa's debt, entitled "Africa Out of the Limelight: The Debt Crisis One Year After the Gleneagles G8." This document outlines the developments on this issue in the past year and emphasizes the pressing need for 100% debt cancellation. It is available here: http://www.africaaction.org/newsroom/docs/G8debtstatement06.pdf

Also today, Africa Action is publishing an op-ed entitled "The G8 & Africa: Reality Check" with the Foreign Policy Association, providing an analysis of the results of the 2005 G8 promises, as well as a glimpse of upcoming G8 priorities.
