Flower Power Round Two: Nationwide Protest for Women's Rights Proves Student Activism is Alive Again, Says Amnesty International
 

WASHINGTON - March 9 - So yesterday's protest fell on deaf ears, everyone's ignoring the peace tent on the quad, and there are more SUVs on campus than pages in Homer's Iliad.

Don't despair. Many students are ignoring their cynical political science professors, abandoning daydreams of the 1960s and joining a movement bent on proving that student activism is far from dead.

This year, more than 1,250 student groups will demand that the U.S. government demonstrate an unequivocal commitment to women's human rights. The activity will occur April 3-10, during Amnesty International USA's National Week of Student Action (NWSA), an annual week of heightened advocacy, which this year is aimed at convincing U.S. Senators to support the Treaty for the Rights of Women, officially named the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW). The action week will include protests, vigils, sit-ins and petition signing on college campuses nationwide. The week is open to all; Amnesty International membership is not required. Any groups or individuals interested in participating should visit: http://www.kintera.org/site/c.aeJLI0OCIrH/b.1144603/k.BD5A/Home.htm

"Youth activism has an unprecedented ability to affect change," said Njambi Good, AIUSA National Student and Youth Program manager. "From the protests of the U.S. civil rights movement to the pro-democracy demonstrations in Eastern Europe, students have successfully demanded that their voices be heard. This year, student activists are harnessing this power to put the protection of women's human rights and equality at the top of the U.S. agenda."

During AIUSA's 9th annual week of student advocacy, hundreds of students from Sitka High School in Alaska to the University of Georgia will invade student unions, cafeterias and hallways demanding Congressional action and calling for an end to discrimination against women. Several student groups plan to expand their usual advocacy repertoire by showing Iron Jawed Angels, a film that takes a candid look at the women's suffrage movement in the United States, while others intend to spread awareness by sponsoring a dialogue wall, a place where individuals can post stories of discrimination and abuse. Dozens more are planning teach-ins, vigils, speakers, rallies and other activities.

Judging by student interest in this year's campaign, Amnesty International expects that this year's NWSA will be more successful than the last. The 2005 NWSA focused on preserving the Sunset Provisions of the Patriot Act. Throughout the week, 421 groups, 26 other organizations and 197 individuals took action through teach-ins, discussions, speakers, vigils, rallies and other activities. Students at Ohio State stormed the university library, chanting "kill the Patriot Act" as they checked out books on Islamic fundamentalism and aviation. At New College in Sarasota, Fla., students passed a resolution on their campus opposing the Patriot Act. By the end of the week, activists had sent more than 4,300 letters and postcards, collected 5,300 signatures and made more than 500 calls to Congress and the White House.

The goal of this year's NWSA is to convince Senators to add their support of the Treaty for the Rights of Women to the Congressional Record. The treaty is the only comprehensive agreement to guarantee women's rights within the political, cultural, economic, social and domestic sphere. The treaty serves as a universal international standard for women's human rights and provides governments with a framework to combat gender inequality.

Although an endorsement from the Senate Foreign Relations Committee sent CEDAW to the entire Senate in 2002, Senators have yet to vote on the treaty. One hundred and eighty countries of the world's 192 have already ratified CEDAW -- including all industrialized countries except the United States.

"Millions of women continue to suffer from violence, discrimination and other abuse in societies that fail to protect their rights both in law and in practice," said Jumana Musa, AIUSA advocacy director for domestic human rights. "If the United States fails to take this treaty seriously, how can we expect the same from other countries with little respect for women's rights?"

Student activists are the backbone of AIUSA; the organization's 1,600 student groups make up more than 70 percent of its membership. In the past year, support from student activists played a pivotal role in freeing two prisoners of conscience, pressuring Congress to pass the anti-torture amendment and reauthorizing the Violence Against Women Act.
