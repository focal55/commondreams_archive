Beyond the Minimum Wage: Progressive State Leaders Promote Innovative New Policies
New Report Wide Variety of State Strategies to Augment Wages
 
HELENA, Montana - June 7 - All over America, people today are working longer hours for lower wages then they were twenty years ago. As the wages of American workers stagnate, state governments are eyeing new policy options to augment wages.  

The Progressive States Network recently compiled a range a new report of solutions considered around the country to improve workers’ earnings.

Across the country, states are increasing their minimum wages. These moves are critical, but they’re only a first step in making this economy work for the middle class again. The summary of state-level policies focuses broadly on three strategies: leveraging public dollars to promote higher wages, regulations targeting certain employers, and laws to strengthen employees’ ability to negotiate better wages.

“Policies to raise wages should be a linchpin of progressive leadership,” said Joel Barkin, Executive Director of the Progressive States Network. “Three decades ago, our national leadership began losing focus on such policies and the resultant collapse of wage standards has undermined support for progressive leaders during that time. State leaders have begun reversing that decline and we now have a range of policy options to help raise wages for working families and restore the American Dream so that the next generation will be better off than our own”

Among the policies highlighted in the report:

Leveraging Public Money

  • Idaho recently began requiring that businesses receiving “Workforce Development Training Funds” create jobs paying at least $12 per hour and benefits.
  • Denver and New Jersey now cover janitors and building service workers with their prevailing wage laws.
  • Maryland and California both recently saw passage of statewide living wage laws that would have required all government contractors to pay a living wage. Both proposals were met with veto stamps.
  • Airports in Oakland, San Francisco and Los Angeles all require that individuals leasing from the government pay living wages.

Regulating Targeted Employers

  • Santa Fe, NM has put in place a higher minimum wage ($8.50) for businesses with twenty-five or more employees. The law was challenged in courts, but upheld by New Mexico’s Supreme Court.
  • Berkeley, CA has established a higher minimum wage for a high-end tourist destination.
  • Emeryville, CA adopted a ballot issue requiring large hotels to pay a higher minimum wage ($9 an hour with an average of $11 an hour).
  • Chicago is considering an ordinance to require large retailers pay a minimum of $10 an hour in wages and $3 an hour in benefits.

Giving a Workers and Hand in Negotiations

  • New York’s legislature recently adopted a bill sponsored by Progressive States board member Adriano Espaillat that extended union protections to day care workers. The bill now awaits the Governor’s signature.
  • California, Washington, and Oregon extended union rights to home health care workers.
  • Colorado’s legislature passed a bill prohibiting employers from requiring employee attendance at religious and political meetings. Governor Owens vetoed the bill.
  • California, Colorado, and New Jersey have reclaimed malls as truly public spaces, requiring mall owners to give union organizers and others access to sidewalks, parking lots, and interior public spaces.
