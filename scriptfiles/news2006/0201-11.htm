The Best Idea Since Sliced Bread: Create Jobs, New Economy by Taxing Pollution to Support Sustainable Localized Energy Industries
 

WASHINGTON - February 1 - A Seattle-based program manager won first prize today in the Since Sliced Bread contest for his idea to create Sustainable Resource Industries. The unprecedented contest engaged tens of thousands of Americans who submitted bold new ideas to strengthen the economy and improve life for working men and women and their families. Peter Skidmore, a father of two, received a $100,000 check from the Service Employees International Union (SEIU) at a ceremony in Washington, D.C. and a commitment from the union to work to make his idea a reality.

Two runners-up in the Since Sliced Bread contest each received checks for $50,000. Leslie Hester, a 26-year old graduate student studying forestry in Raleigh, North Carolina, would reform public education by redistributing tax revenues for schools, controlling tuition at public universities, and raising teacher salaries. Filippo Menczer, a 40-year-old associate professor at the Indiana University in Bloomington, would tie the minimum wage to the cost of living.

Other finalists include a Provost Marshall in the U.S. Army who wants to create a Civil Works Corps, a grandmother who wants to teach children money management, a tutor who wants to separate health care from employment and create a special sales tax to fund national health insurance, and a divinity student who wants to establish a mortgage program to repair dilapidated houses. The contest attracted more than 22,000 policy ideas addressing jobs, health care, wages, retirement security and much more.

"Last night, we heard what the President thinks the country needs, and the Democratic response," said SEIU President Andy Stern. "We're here this morning to talk about what Americans think will improve their lives. Through the Since Sliced Bread contest, SEIU asked the public to tell us what will help the U.S. compete in the global economy and make work pay. The response was remarkable. We received more than 22,000 ideas, from people in every state and every congressional district. More than 40,000 people voted to choose the winners."

"It has been exciting to be part of such a big conversation about what matters -- that to me is the essence of a democratic society," Skidmore says. His idea involves imposing a "resource tax" on pollution, development, and fossil fuel to pay for development of renewable energy and environmental restoration. "Promoting sustainable localized energy industries (solar, wind, hydro, tidal, biofuels) will provide reliable, clean homegrown energy, exportable technologies, and bring energy jobs home," it says. "Funding widespread environmental restoration will expand existing industries (farming, recreation, tourism, and commercial fisheries) that are dependent on ecological services and will foster research, design and technology industries." Skidmore works for the Nature Conservancy, which did not endorse his idea.

"This contest accomplished something extraordinary," Stern added. "We got ideas from liberals and conservatives. College students and grandmothers joined the conversation. People who are unemployed and people with large incomes sent us ideas. People are hungry for this conversation. They have ideas, and they want to be heard."

"It's been wonderful to realize that public education is a priority for so many fellow Americans," Hester said. "I want to thank Since Sliced Bread for providing a unique forum for idea solicitation and sharing. It's been fun!"

Menczer says he considers himself lucky to have a good job, but knows that others do not. "I have learned a lot from others' comments about the issues surrounding my idea on the minimum wage. I am excited that the finalist ideas address important needs like sustainable resources, health care, and (in my case) the working poor."

A panel of leading economists, thinkers, businesspeople, labor leaders, activists and Wall Street investors selected the top 21 ideas to be voted on. The panel includes such notable names as former United States Senator Bill Bradley, former Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Carol Browner, former Secretary of the Army Louis E. Caldera, Teach for America Founder Wendy Kopp, CEO of Three Oaks LLC Ginger Lew, and Republican strategist Ed Rollins.

Stern said the winners will be honored in a full-page advertisement in USA Today tomorrow, Feb. 2. He encouraged people to go to http://www.sinceslicedbread.com and send their favorite idea to their Senators, Representative and Governor. Later this month, http://www.sinceslicedbread.com will invite visitors to sort the ideas by topic so they can be sent to lawmakers. "We received a wealth of smart, creative ideas in this contest," Stern added. "We are committed to sharing them with lawmakers and opinion leaders over the next several months."
