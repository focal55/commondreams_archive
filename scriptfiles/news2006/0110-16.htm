Center on Budget and Policy Priorities Conference Call Briefing on Recent Changes to Medicaid
 

WASHINGTON - January 10 -

WHAT: Conference Call Briefing

Last-Minute Changes Made in 2003 to Medicare Drug Benefit Causing Serious Unintended Problems; Will Recent Changes to Medicaid Do the Same?

WHEN: Thursday, January 12 at 12 p.m. (ET)

WHO:

-- Jude Walsh, Special Assistant to the Governor of Maine.

-- Jocelyn Guyer, Senior Program Director, Center for Children and Families, Georgetown University Health Policy Institute

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities will hold a conference call briefing Thursday, January 12, at 12 p.m. (ET) to examine factors causing many low-income beneficiaries to fall through the cracks as the new Medicare drug benefit is implemented. As the briefers will explain, these problems are rooted in aspects of the 2003 Medicare drug law that were designed behind closed doors at the very end of the legislative process and never received appropriate scrutiny before the votes on final passage. This process now appears to be repeating itself in the budget reconciliation bill, with the inclusion of several hastily designed, far-reaching Medicaid provisions in the recent budget reconciliation conference agreement that may have serious unintended consequences.

Since January 1, a number of states have had to intervene on an emergency basis to provide prescription drugs to low-income people who are not receiving the Medicare drug coverage to which they are entitled.

Under a change that Congress made to the Medicare drug legislation after it had passed both the House and Senate, these individuals -- low-income seniors and people with disabilities who qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid -- had their Medicaid drug coverage terminated on January 1 regardless of whether they were receiving drug coverage through Medicare at that point. Repeated assurances by senior Administration officials that these individuals would be shifted smoothly to the Medicare drug benefit have proved unfounded.

In a similar vein, congressional negotiators, operating behind closed doors, made several major last-minute Medicaid changes to the budget conference agreement now scheduled for a final House vote on February 1. These changes, which were not in the earlier versions of the legislation approved by the House or Senate, could have serious effects on low-income beneficiaries. For example, the conference agreement would allow states to impose co-payments of unlimited size on beneficiaries with incomes below the poverty line and also could cause poor children to lose coverage for important health services.

Like the last-minute changes to the Medicare drug bill, these changes appear not to have been fully thought through and rely on complex administrative procedures that may not prove workable.

After opening remarks, the panelists will take questions.
