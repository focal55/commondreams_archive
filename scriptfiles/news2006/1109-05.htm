Amnesty International Urges the Administration: 'Return to the Rule of Law'
 

WASHINGTON - November 9 - Larry Cox, Amnesty International USA's executive director, issued the following statement on the upcoming fifth anniversary of President Bush's Military Order authorizing military commissions and treatment of detainees in U.S. control, originally signed on Nov. 13, 2001:

"The president boldly asserted presidential powers that have now been rejected by the nation's highest court and led to failed policies regarding the detention and trial of suspected terrorists in U.S. custody. The executive order attempted to give the president ultimate authority to make all decisions without regard to U.S. and international law and amounted to a presidential coronation. President Bush granted himself the power to make the laws, execute the laws and try cases according to his will, with no outside review.

"The U.S. Supreme Court has rejected this assertion more than once. In June 2004 and June 2006, the court ruled that detainees in Guantanamo could access federal court, and that the president did not have unlimited powers to set up tribunals. In the face of the court's refusal to vest all powers of government in the executive branch, the administration went to Congress and asked members to endorse its reckless policies. In the aftermath of the Military Commissions Act, the battle has returned to court.

"Rather than obstinately pushing forward with the same failed policies, the administration should reverse course and return to an agenda that embraces human rights. We will never achieve true security without restoring the rule of law."
