ACORN Lending Study Says Minority Homeowners in California at Risk of 'Rate Shock'
 

SAN FRANCISCO - August 15 - leads the nation with the greatest disparity of high-cost home purchase loans to minorities, followed by San Francisco, and Los Angeles. About three fourths of all sub prime home loans are adjustable rate mortgages (ARMs), which, the study points out, will be much harder for borrowers to repay as interest rates rise. Five California cities are among the top 10 where homebuyers in neighborhoods were at least four times more likely to receive a high-cost loan than homebuyers in predominately white neighborhoods.

On Tuesday, August 15, the national community group ACORN will release "The Impending Rate Shock: A study of home mortgages in 130 American Cities."

Using data available under the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act, ACORN's study examines the extent of high-cost (sub prime) lending in 130 metropolitan areas and the disparities between borrowers of different race and income levels. The report will identify the metropolitan areas and demographic groups that are at a greatest risk of "rate shock" and a sharp increase in foreclosures.

National foreclosures are up by as much as 60 percent over a year ago, and experts expect the default-loan trends to continue.

Members of the media can obtain an embargoed copy of the study, including local data, beginning on Friday, August 11 by visiting http://www.acorn.org/reporters.
