Amnesty International USA Urges Clemency for Patrick Moody, Citing Mental Health Concerns
 

WASHINGTON - March 14 - Amnesty International USA (AIUSA) today urged clemency for Patrick Moody, a 39-year-old man who is scheduled to be executed in North Carolina at 2 a.m. on Friday, March 17.

Amnesty International, the world's largest grassroots human rights organization, has issued a worldwide call to action regarding Moody's case, mobilizing the public to send appeals to North Carolina Gov. Michael Easley on Moody's behalf. AIUSA Executive Director Dr. William F. Schulz has also written Gov. Easley, urging commutation of Moody's sentence.

Schulz said: "On behalf of the members of Amnesty International, I urge you to demonstrate compassion and grant clemency to Patrick Moody. As an international human rights organization, Amnesty International opposes the death penalty in all circumstances without reservation. While we have tremendous sympathy for the family and friends of Donnie Robbins, capital punishment only perpetuates the cycle of violence."

Moody has been diagnosed with an IQ that hovers around North Carolina's threshold for mental retardation. At his trial, clinical psychologist Dr. Jerry Wayne Noble diagnosed him with "attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, borderline intellectual functioning, alcohol dependence, a mixed personality disorder, child abuse syndrome, and physical problems resulting from psychological difficulties."

Since 2001, North Carolina has barred the execution of those with mental retardation and other states offer a precedent for commutation on mental impairment grounds. In July 2004, Indiana Gov. Joseph Kernan commuted the death sentence of Darnell Williams, whose IQ was comparable to Moody's. Gov. Kernan recognized that while Williams' IQ was slightly above the benchmark used to determine mental retardation (following the 2002 Atkins vs. Virginia decision, which barred the death penalty for people with mental retardation, but left it to the states to set the criteria for that condition), "it remains problematic to confidently place the solemn decision of life or death on a few percentage points."

AIUSA is also seriously concerned about the quality of Moody's legal representation at the trial stage. Moody's current attorneys allege that his trial counsel failed to properly investigate mitigating evidence such as his history of severe psychological problems and sustained child abuse. In contrast, co-defendant Wanda Robbins, the wife of the victim, received a life sentence for her role in the crime, even though Moody reportedly fully cooperated with the police investigation.

The circumstances of Moody's childhood were harrowing. He was subjected to severe physical abuse and neglect as a child at the hands of both his father and stepmother. His father beat him with a board and broke plates over his head in addition to locking him in his room for up to 18 hours without meals.

AIUSA urges Gov. Easley to heed the growing call for a death penalty moratorium in North Carolina. Nearly 1,100 resolutions calling for a moratorium have been passed statewide, prompted by concerns about the fairness and reliability of the death penalty. Some of the major North Carolina newspapers have also carried editorials supporting such a moratorium.

Local death penalty abolition activists plan several events on Thursday, March 16, including a daytime vigil from noon to 1 p.m. at the State Capitol, a prayer service at 7:30 p.m. at Pullen Memorial Baptist Church in Raleigh, a solemn procession at 8:30 p.m. from the church to the prison, and a nighttime vigil until the scheduled execution time of 2 a.m., Friday, March 17.

For more information on AIUSA's Program to Abolish the Death Penalty, please go to http://www.amnestyusa.org/abolish/.
