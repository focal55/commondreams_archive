Published on Tuesday, May 8, 2001 in the Independent / UK
Black Versus White on the White House Lawn
by Andrew Buncombe in Washington
 
It had been planned down to the last detail. They would mark up a pitch on the White House lawn, set up stands for the proud parents and invite two teams of youngsters to play a bit of baseball. Even the weather would not be a problem.

But the Bush administration's plan for an unbeatable public relations stunt came a little bit unstuck once the teams ran out to play: one was all-black, the other was all-white, except for one black player.

It had not been planned that the two teams chosen to play the first of an occasional series of "T-ball" games on the lawn should so dramatically illustrate Washington's racial divide. It just appears that no one stopped to think.

"I don't think it's a race issue at all," insisted Keith Jones, president of the predominantly black Satchel Paige league, from which one of the teams in Sunday's knock-up ­ the Memphis Red Sox ­ was drawn.

"Sunday's not about race. Sunday's about baseball," said Ann Kane, president of the predominantly white Capitol Little League, which provided the other team, the Rockies.

Karen Hughes, Mr Bush's adviser, also tried to play up the inclusive nature of the occasion. She said T-ball, a junior version of baseball, was chosen because of the President's love of the sport. In a previous incarnation, the President managed a Texas baseball team.

"You see that?" said one television cameraman as he looked into his lens. "It's black against white. What's that all about?" Other commentators thought the whole scene resembled something from the Fifties, with black faces lighting up when a black child made a good play, and white faces smiling when a good hit was struck by a white child.

In the end, the Red Sox beat the Rockies 8-5. No date has been set for the rematch ­ or for any other match.
