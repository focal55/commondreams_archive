Published on Saturday, June 9, 2001 in The Earth Times
'Coup of One' Can Affect UN As Well As Congress
by Tom Wicker
 
The ‘coup of one,’ as Trent Lott of Mississippi rather sourly described the Senate revolution forced by James Jeffords of Vermont when he became an Independent, could have large effect on the United Nations as well as on Congress.

That’s because of the downfall of the UN’s arch-critic in the Senate, the main force behind US arrears in dues to the world organization--Jesse Helms the elderly right-wing Republican from North Carolina. Helms will no longer be chairman of the Foreign Relations Committee. Joseph Biden of Delaware, not only a Democrat but a younger and far less conservative legislator than Helms, has assumed the chairmanship.

Biden will be less cantankerous than Helms surely would have been about UN matters, and also is likely--though a Democrat--to be more sympathetic than Helms to Bush’s ambassadorial appointments and other foreign policy actions.

Helms, moreover, is coming to the end of his fifth term and will be 81 years old if he seeks re-election in 2002--which, owing to age, infirmity and a greater-than-usual possibility of defeat, is not at all certain. Even if he should run and win, however, Senate Republican rules will not allow him to be the ranking member of the Foreign Relations Committee, if the Democrats, as expected, retain Senate control that year. That distinction would go to Richard Lugar, a moderate Republican from Indiana.

The Helms-Biden exchange is one of the two most important switches in committee chairmanships caused by Jeffords, when he became an Independent and handed control of the Senate to a 50-seat Democratic majority. It’s equally or perhaps more important that Jeffords’ Democratic colleague from Vermont, Patrick Leahy, now becomes chairman of the Judiciary Committee. Leahy will bring greater scrutiny to judgeship nominations by President Bush, including nominees for the Supreme Court.

In fact, the ‘coup of one’ set off by Jeffords is a sort of chain reaction that affects the Republicans in several important ways. The loss of the Senate also means that the importance of the slender Republican majority in the House will be diminished. Whatever legislation the House may approve still will have to pass a newly Democratic Senate before becoming law. So it may not be factual but it may be correct in practice to say that, owing to Jeffords, Bush and his party have lost effective control of Congress, not just real control of the Senate.

That being so, Dick Armey and Tom DeLay, the powerful Texas Republican conservatives who hold sway in the House, where Speaker Dennis Hastert of Illinois seems little more than a figurehead, have been shorn of some power. In the Senate, of course, such downgrading obviously has befallen both majority leader Trent Lott and Vice President Richard Cheney, whose constitutional authority to break ties had given the Republicans their only claim to majority status.

Because of the Republican party’s sharply reduced power in both houses, even Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld also faces a bleaker legislative outlook than he did only a few days ago. That could drastically affect one of Bush’s strongest campaign pledges, his promise to build and deploy a national missile defense even at the cost of withdrawal from the long-standing Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty.

So if six of the Republicans’ strongest conservative leaders--Lott, Cheney, Helms, Armey, DeLay and Rumsfeld--have lost standing and effectiveness, the entire right wing of the Republican party has been hit hard. Since it has also appeared since his inauguration that Bush was catering politically to that wing--if he is not actually controlled by it- the course of his Administration is bound to become more moderate. And the President’s actions and manner since the Jeffords thunder-clap seem to reflect that prospect.

There has been no national outcry against this seismic shift in the political terrain, and Vermont--my home state as well as Jeffords’s- seems mostly to celebrate rather than condemn his defection. Trent Lott’s complaint that the Democrats lack the ‘moral authority’ of an elected majority therefore seems more petulant than logical. What moral authority could be claimed by the former Republican ‘majority’--actually an even 50-50 split with the Democrats that reflected Republican losses in the 2000 elections?

What moral authority, for that matter, does even George W. Bush have, after losing the national popular vote and carrying a disputed electoral college only because of a one-vote victory in a basically conservative Supreme Court?

Lott and other Republicans would do well to leave ‘moral authority’ out of the question of who controls the Senate--or the Presidency. Seldom have there been more graphic lessons than those provided, first by the Supreme Court last November, next by James Jeffords this year, that American politics is a power game, not a morality play. Victory goes, not necessarily to virtue, but to him--more and more often to her--who has the votes.
