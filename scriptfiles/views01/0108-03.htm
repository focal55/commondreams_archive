Published on Monday, January 8, 2001 in the Chicago Tribune
Urban Dilemma
by Salim Muwakkil
 

The other side of the record incarceration rate that has made this country the world's leading jailer is the record number of inmates getting out of jail. Never before has America had to deal with so many ex-cons. This presents a problem of social reintegration; prisons these days are more concerned with punishment than rehabilitation, so former inmates are returning to society with fewer skills than ever.

For African-Americans, who are imprisoned disproportionately, the problems are multiplied. Not only are more black ex-inmates headed home, they generally return to the same inner-city neighborhoods where their concentrated numbers can easily overwhelm communities already crippled by a lack of jobs and resources. This is a formula for catastrophe but no one seems to be noticing.

Kublai Toure has been noticing. As executive director of an organization called Amer-I-Can, the 52-year-old Toure has been trying to intervene into this dismal cycle that wastes the lives of so many African-American youth. For much of his adult life he has tackled the problem in various ways. But in the last four years he has worked with so-called "at-risk" youth within the Cook County Juvenile Detention Center, through his group.

Amer-I-Can is the brainchild of Jim Brown, former National Football League player and movie actor. It is designed to teach inmates ways to empower themselves through training in life-management skills. The program is in operation in 13 states across the country, funded primarily by local agencies, and generally is well regarded. Toure has been involved with Amer-I-Can Illinois since 1991.

It now appears the group will lose its county funding because Toure made the mistake of hiring a counselor who allegedly was active in a West Side gang. Elbert "Pierre" Mahoney, who was slain in a gang-related murder on the West Side Sept. 29, was working for Amer-I-Can.

It's easy to understand Toure's dilemma. The record numbers of returning ex-cons have little in common with traditional black leadership. In fact, for cultural and economic reasons, civil-rights organizations and social-service agencies tend to alienate many former inmates. Many inmates respond only to a leadership they trust, that speaks their language.

But that language is threatening to many, and not just to "law-and-order" types, who reflexively belittle any attempt to rehabilitate wayward youth. Traditional black leaders also seem a bit threatened by the rough-hewn leadership emerging to deal with the byproducts of our incarceration epidemic. Toure candidly admits his mistake in hiring Mahoney without getting a go-ahead from his board of directors. But he also argues that the program shouldn't suffer because of one mistake. "I knew he was on the edge but he was really respected by the youth in the program," he said. "I caught him at the crossroad of his life and I thought he had crossed over already, but apparently he hadn't."

Many credit Toure, who also served eight years as a Chicago firefighter, for helping to convince warring gang factions or "nations" to put down their weapons and stop shooting at each other during the gang-truce movement of the early 1990s. "I got involved in all that diplomacy stuff after my stepson was killed in 1991 in a mistaken-identity attack," he explained. He credits the social consciousness triggered by leaders like Malcolm X and a late local leader named Al Hajj Lukman with forging his sense of commitment.

Toure is one of many leaders around the country who are stepping up to the plate to confront the growing crisis provoked by the increasingly invasive "corrections industrial complex." In October, the Sentencing Project, a criminal justice-reform and research group, reported that the U.S., with 690 prisoners per 100,000 population, has officially surpassed Russia as the world's leader in the rate of incarceration. From about 330,000 inmates in prison and jail in 1972, the inmate population grew to 2 million in 2000. "In comparison to other industrialized nations, the U.S. rate of incarceration is five to eight times that of Canada and most of western Europe," according to the Sentencing Project. While Americans don't seem too bothered by this global ignominy, other countries feel our pain. We got a flash of their concern when Stanley "Tookie" Williams, the incarcerated co-founder of the infamous Los Angeles Crips, was nominated for a 2001 Nobel Peace Prize. Williams is an inmate on San Quentin's Death Row who has written books and designed programs that offer hope to those trapped in criminal lifestyles.

Perhaps that jarring nomination will force Americans to look for leadership in new places. Toure certainly hopes so.

Salim Muwakkil is a senior editor at In These Times.
