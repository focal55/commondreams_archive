Published on Thursday, April 12, 2001 in the Madison Capital Times
'Trade Off' Captures Battle of Seattle
by John Nichols
 
Genius is largely a matter of being in the right place at the right time. And, of course, it helps if you bring a camera.

Case in point: Shaya Mercer.

Back in the summer of 1999, when "Seattle" was still just the name of a rainy town on the West Coast, Mercer scraped together enough money to produce a documentary on protests that were scheduled to coincide with the fall meeting of the World Trade Organization in that city. It was a risky bet, as demonstrations outside the esoteric sessions of esoteric international trade bodies had, up to that point, developed a reputation for being, er, esoteric. Not to mention small.

Mercer, a native of Seattle whose parental pedigree is equal parts longshoreman and earthmother, bet that her hometown would produce the protest heard 'round the world on Nov. 30, 1999. And she turned out to be right.

Tens of thousands of trade unionists, farmers, environmentalists, animal rights activists and students poured into the streets of the city — rerouting presidential motorcades, raising a ruckus and, ultimately, preventing the WTO from reaching a "Millennium Round agreement" that would have significantly broadened the shadowy trade organization’s ability to help multinational corporations avoid having to obey labor, environmental and human rights laws.

It was a historic moment in the struggle for democracy and justice, and Mercer was there with her camera and crew — capturing it all from start to finish. The result was "Trade Off," a brilliant behind-the-scenes exploration of the issues, the people and the events that made the name "Seattle" synonymous with the ever-expanding fight against corporate globalization. Awarded "best documentary" honors at the 2000 Seattle International Film Festival, "Trade Off" was selected for screening at the Human Rights Watch International Film Festival and it has earned plaudits from just about everyone who was on the right side of the barricades in Seattle. Says Jerry Mander, president of the International Forum on Globalization: "No film better captures the spirit, chaos, joy, power and meaning of the Battle of Seattle, than Shaya Mercer’s 'Trade Off.' "

"Trade Off" is coming to Madison this weekend, just in time for the next of the many battles after Seattle. Friday and Saturday screenings at 7 p.m. in Humanities Room 3650 on the University of Wisconsin campus, and a 7:30 p.m. Sunday showing at the WilMar Center, 953 Jenifer, are a perfect prelude to the April 20-22 "Summit of the Americas" in Quebec City, where President Bush will join leaders of other Western hemisphere nations to launch the push for a Free Trade Agreement of the Americas — "NAFTA on steroids." Outside the summit’s 10-foot-tall "security fence," tens of thousands of protesters will be busy bringing a little Seattle-style rabble-rousing to Canada.

Madison will see plenty of activism in support of the Quebec protests. In addition to the Friday, Saturday and Sunday screenings of "Trade Off," for instance, "This Is What Democracy Looks Like," an excellent Seattle documentary put together by Independent Media Center activists, will be shown at the Bartell Theatre at 7 and 9 p.m. Wednesday.
