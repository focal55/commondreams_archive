Published on Tuesday, March 6, 2001 in the San Francisco Chronicle
"Power of Choice"
 Breaking Bread With Reality
by Stephanie Salter
 
EACH YEAR when I attend the "Power of Choice" luncheon in San Francisco, I end up thinking the same thing: If only we could get all the anti- choice state and federal politicians in the country here for this.

No, not to be intimidated by so many abortion rights supporters in one place -- this year's luncheon drew 1,100 women and men and raised $500,000 for the National Abortion and Reproductive Rights Action League.

Rather, I want the legislators to sit at the luncheon tables and just listen.

To look women of all ages and ethnicities in the eye. To learn about our lives, our jobs, our families, our intelligence, our religious and spiritual practices, our principles.

How easy would it then be to do what anti-choice politicians repeatedly do when the issue is unwanted pregnancy: sponsor and vote for laws that presume women are too stupid, immoral, uninformed or in denial to decide what should happen inside their own wombs?

Could the so-called pro-life legislators still rob NARAL supporters and other pro-choice women of our humanity?

Could they assume we have lost or never possessed an ability to reason? Ignore our fundamental decency? Behave as though we lack morals or any sense of responsibility?

It is one thing to be dismissed and discounted by non-elected anti-abortion rights advocates. As private citizens in a democracy, they are entitled to their monolithic assumptions -- about the morality of millions of us pro- choice strangers and about the superiority of their own religious beliefs.

It is another thing entirely when the men and women who make laws for everyone do the discounting.

"You think that what someone does with her pregnancy should be dealt with at the personal level, not the political," said Diane Parish, a financial adviser and young mother who attended her first Power of Choice luncheon last Friday at the St. Francis Hotel. "You ask, 'Why are we still having to fight this?' You think it's an issue that should go away. But it's not going away."

Like all the attendees (who gave three standing ovations during the lunch), Parish said she was much moved by the speakers: former Secretary of State Madeleine Albright, NARAL President Kate Michelman and novelist Richard North Patterson, who described the many truths he learned about late-term abortion and opposition to parental consent laws while researching his best-seller, "Protect and Defend."

"He took on two hard issues and showed that there really is a strong moral foundation for both of them," said Parish.

Meanwhile, Albright explained the fallacy and danger behind George W. Bush's withdrawal of funds for international family planning organizations, and Michelman "showed what it's like to be living this fight, the personal involvement; she truly never rests on this issue."

But, when the meal was over, said Parish, she was inspired "almost as much by the crowd as by the speakers."

"It was tremendously exciting just to be in a room with 1,100 people, most of them women," she said. "It energized me and aroused my sense of urgency."

Maybe next year, a few anti-choice lawmakers will join Parish, me and the hundreds of other women. That is, if they have the appetite for breaking bread with reality.
