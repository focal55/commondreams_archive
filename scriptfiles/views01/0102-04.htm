Published on Tuesday, January 2, 2001 in the Chicago Tribune
Will the GOP Have a Future Without Wooing Minorities?
by Salim Muwakkil
 
Last summer's Republican convention was applauded and condemned for the large number of African-Americans showcased. Critics claimed it was a deceptive performance ("minstrel show" was a popular put-down) but others praised the GOP's attempt at racial outreach.

Whatever it was, the strategy failed to attract many black voters to the Republican ticket. Texas Gov. George W. Bush pulled just 9 percent of the black vote, the fewest garnered by a Republican since the second term of Ronald Reagan, a man loathed by most African-Americans. Even in Texas, where Bush was reputed to have crafted an effective interracial alliance, he polled merely 5 percent of the black vote.

Many Republicans strategists are wondering what went wrong. Why did African-Americans, particularly the black middle class, resist the Republican embrace so resolutely? And, since they did, why should the GOP continue to make gestures of racial outreach when its hand is consistently rejected?

The answer to that question is simple: It must. The GOP can never claim to be a national party unless it can attract a national constituency. Oh, it can gain control (as it has) over all three branches of the federal government, and it may use that brute political force to push its policies through. But in order to be truly representative, Republicans must earn--through policy, not symbolism-- the support of Americans across all spectra, especially the racial spectrum.

What's more, changing demographics make it clear that white Americans, not just white Republicans, will be a minority in this country in the next 50 years. If either party is to ensure long-term durability, it will have to gain a significant following among "minority" Americans. Unless the GOP mounts a serious challenge, the Democratic Party will be the party of the future, by default.

This means the party of Lincoln must stop exploiting white resentment to blacks' demands for civil rights, a la the "Southern strategy," and begin serious efforts to find solutions to our continuing racial impasse. The inclusive, "opportunity conservatism" pushed by 1996 GOP vice presidential candidate Jack Kemp, for example, was attractive to many in the black community and should have been absorbed as official Republican strategy. Instead, with the "Gingrich revolution," the GOP re-engaged the power of the white backlash to help fuel its political bandwagon.

Were that to change, the Democrats must then resist the temptation to exploit African-Americans' racial fears, a tactic that paid big dividends in this year's presidential election. Demonizing Bush was the low road, but it's a road well traveled by backlash strategists.

Bush's handlers understand this need for change; that's why the president-elect selected two black Americans as his first cabinet members, despite the black electorate's rejection of his candidacy. His choices of Condoleezza Rice and retired Gen. Colin Powell as national security adviser and secretary of state, respectively, stir both pride and anxiety within the African-American community. Pride, because the exceptionally qualified duo were chosen to fill slots not typically associated with black officials; Powell will be the third-highest-ranking official in the Executive Branch. Worry, because many African-American activists fear Republicans are trying to camouflage harmful policies with black faces, as with U.S. Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas.

When the first President Bush nominated Thomas for a spot on the U.S. Supreme Court, blacks by and large supported him; his race trumped his Republican pedigree. Today, however, there is a widespread consensus among blacks that Thomas has been a disaster for the interests of African-Americans. Racial symbolism is regarded a bit more warily these days.

Powell supports affirmative action and credits it for aiding his rise. In that, he differs from Thomas, who now wants to kick down the same ladder of affirmative action he climbed to his lofty perch. But in other ways, Powell's history is cause for even more concern among African-Americans than Thomas'. Powell had a major role in supporting right-wing terrorism in Guatemala, El Salvador and Nicaragua during the 1980s; in helping to design the Reagan administration's 1983 invasion of Grenada (an action by the Western hemisphere's largest nation against the hemisphere's smallest); in leading the 1989 invasion of Panama which killed hundreds of (mostly black) Panamanians, leaving thousands wounded and homeless. Rice has less to live down, but her connection to the GOP's foreign-policy brain trust makes many wary.

If those fears turn out to be baseless, the GOP may be on its way to becoming a truly national party.
