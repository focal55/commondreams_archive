Published on Saturday, January 13, 2001 in the Seattle Post-Intelligencer
Save the Steller Sea Lion
by Joshua Reichert and John Passacantando
 
In the waning days of his administration, President Clinton has been building a positive environmental legacy by protecting wilderness areas and creating new national monuments in the west. Depending on steps that his administration takes in the next dew days, however, he might leave an indelible black mark on that legacy by undermining one of the nation's most important environmental laws and paving the way for the continued decline and possible extinction of one of the world's most spectacular marine mammals.

At issue is the Steller sea lion, a magnificent creature that makes its home in the waters off Alaska. Weighing up to a ton and capable of diving up to 800 feet below the surface, these remarkable animals feed on a variety of sea life including Pacific cod, Atka mackerel, salmon, octopus and pollock. As recently as 1960, about 200,000 Stellers lived in the North Pacific, making their homes on the rocky islands in the Bering Sea and Gulf of Alaska. Over the last 4 decades, however, the sea lion population has plummeted by almost 90 percent. Today, there are fewer than 25,000 left.

The precipitous decline of the sea lion population over the last 40 years has coincided with a virtual explosion of commercial fishing in the region, first by foreign factory trawlers in the 1960s and 70s, and then by American vessels. While there are differing opinions regarding the cause of the sea lion's decline, the majority of marine biologists familiar with the problem attribute it to an inadequate food supply caused by over fishing. Quite simply, sea lions are no longer able to compete with commercial fishermen who are taking too many fish from those areas critical to their survival.

Although the Steller sea lion was listed as an endangered species in 1997, the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) was reluctant to restrict commercial fishing in those areas where sea lions forage for food until a federal judge, responding to a lawsuit brought by several environmental organizations including Greenpeace, ordered such restrictions in July of this year. Further support for such restrictions was provided by government scientists working for the NMFS who, on December 1, released a long awaited report which concluded that commercial fishing in the North Pacific is likely harming sea lions and must be restricted in order to insure their survival.

The judge's order, which temporarily prohibited trawl fishing in those areas deemed essential for the survival and recovery of endangered sea lions, caused a howl of protest from the commercial fishing industry in Alaska. It also prompted Alaska Senator Ted Stevens, the powerful head of the Senate Appropriations Committee, to attach a rider to this year's last-minute budget appropriations bill that would allow fishing to continue unabated in the sea lion's critical habitat, essentially waiving any and all restrictions required by the Endangered Species Act.

In the weeks preceding the final debate on the budget bill, environmentalists received repeated assurances from the White House that the president would not sign any bill that would waive the Endangered Species Act in the case of the Steller sea lion. However, on December 17, Senator Stevens and the White House agreed on a final budget bill that has the potential to do just that.

Under the terms of the agreement, the Secretary of Commerce is directed to amend the regulations governing Alaska's groundfish fishery by January 20, and is empowered to ease or eliminate restrictions on fishing that the National Marine Fisheries Service has determined are needed to protect the sea lion.

The agreement essentially postpones the decision on the fate of the sea lion to the final day of the Clinton presidency. It provides enough latitude for the president to direct that new regulations be issued that will protect the Steller sea lion and comply with the requirements of the Endangered Species Act. Conversely, such regulations can be written in a way that will condemn the sea lion to further declines and possible extinction. With the administration winding down, will the president and the secretary of commerce do the right thing? Or, in the commotion of leaving office, on a day when no one is paying attention to anything but the departure of one president and the arrival of another, will one of the last acts of .Clinton be to seal the fate of the sea lion, and in so doing set a precedent for future presidents to further undermine one of the nation's most cherished environmental laws? Let us hope he takes the nobler course.

Joshua Reichert directs the Environmental Program at the Pew Charitable Trusts. John Passacantando is executive editor of Greenpeace USA.
