Published on Saturday, April 21, 2001 on Common Dreams
Floods, Riots, and Other “Natural” Disasters
by Fran Shor
 
As the waters of the Mississippi River crest and engulf towns in Minnesota, Wisconsin, and Iowa, media attention highlights the heroic efforts of midwestern citizens to stem the flooding. With the mobilization of local, state, and federal resources, these overwhelmingly white residents are aided in their attempts to salvage something of their threatened situation.

Farther downstream from the banks of the Mississippi but on one of its tributaries, the Ohio River, another disaster has seemingly come to a close. In the aftermath of the murder by a Cincinnati policeman of an unarmed young African-American, Timothy Thomas, some black residents exploded in rage and “rioted” for several days. However, instead of sympathy being extended to those whose emotions overflowed the natural dikes of benign neglect, the “rioters” were denounced as violent and out-of-control thugs. There were no concerted efforts by federal, state, or local authorities to deal with the underlying causes; only the predictable condemnations by those in power and those blind to the righteous anger of black citizens of Cincinnati.

Part of the blindness to what African-Americans across the country are up against with police brutality, racial profiling, and continuing institutional discrimination and indifference inheres in the invisibility of race as a factor in public policy. Such invisibility feeds what is construed as natural and unnatural disasters and what can be done to alleviate the concomitant suffering.

The privileging of white citizens at the federal level has often hidden behind governmental policies on housing, public works, and relief programs. From the New Deal’s exclusion of black farm workers from Social Security to federal housing programs that reinforced restrictive covenants to highway construction that aided white flight and destroyed black neighborhoods, whites have benefited at the expense of the continuing disadvantaging of blacks. Even so-called affirmative action programs have had a greater impact on white woman than people of color. No wonder many African-Americans still feel that “if you’re white, you’re alright; if you’re black, get back!”

Hence, when it comes to the recent flooding, no one questions why people continue to live on flood plains, getting federal and state assistance to fend off a “natural” disaster like flooding. But what if the situation were reversed? What if the overwhelming number of people affected by the flooding were African-Americans? Would they be exempt from criticism of their stupidity for living on dangerous flood plains? Would they be excoriated for getting government handouts to help them pull themselves up by their water bootstraps?

And what if an overwhelming black police force was shooting down unarmed whites in city after city with no major change in policing practices? What if white males were constantly harassed by the police and security guards because of alleged suspicious behavior? What if the media persistently pathologized white music and white youths as potential dangerous predators who needed to be censored and controlled?

So, what is natural and unnatural in all of this? Are we destined as a nation to turn a blind eye to the lingering vicious repercussions of the invisibility of whiteness and the visibility of a blackness? How much longer do we ignore the sign of “no more flood, the fire next time” before we are all engulfed in a conflagration of our own making?

Fran Shor is a Professor at Wayne State University and a member of the Michigan Coalition on Human Rights. He can be reached at f.shor@wayne.edu.
