Published on Sunday, November 25, 2001 in the New York Times
Arab-Americans
 After Sept. 11, Complaints of Job Bias Mount
by Pam Belluck
 
The complaints are coming from all corners of the country and from people in all types of industries — from an airplane mechanic in Texas to a Wal-Mart greeter in Virginia.

Since Sept. 11, Arab-American groups say, hundreds of people have said they are experiencing discrimination in the workplace because of their nationality or religion.

Nearly 100 people have filed complaints with the Federal Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, and others have decided to sue over what they perceive as a backlash against people of Arab descent. Their advocates say that the number of complaints to the commission would be higher but that many Arabs and Arab-Americans are suspicious of the federal government.

Of the complaints that have been filed, Reginald Welch, a spokesman for the commission, said: "These people say they were singled out for mistreatment, they were harassed, or they were fired. Something happened on the job that they believe would not have happened if it were not for Sept. 11 and if they were not perceived to be of Middle Eastern descent."

But a look at a handful of complaints suggests the circumstances may be murkier than that. Companies forcefully deny any discrimination. Some said the crippled economy was the culprit, forcing them to lay off many people, not only those of Middle Eastern descent. Others said workers were fired because of behavior or performance problems.

In at least a couple of cases, it appears that tensions arising from the terrorist attacks might have led to an employee being fired, but the employees and employers tell different stories.

Fatmah Anabtawi of Island Park, N.Y., said she was fired from the Island Park Laundromat because she is a Muslim. Ms. Anabtawi, 44, a Jordanian-born American citizen, said the owner of the self-service laundromat told her on Sept. 14 that customers were scared of her.

"He said, `They are scared you want to put a bomb in my store,' " said Ms. Anabtawi, who worked part time at the laundry, washing, drying and folding.

The owner, John Weber, said that he fired Ms. Anabtawi because of comments she made after the Sept. 11 attacks that he said caused some customers to threaten to boycott the store.

"She said to me, `America had it coming,' " Mr. Weber said. "I said: `You know, Fatmah, not for nothing, but that comment offended me. I don't want you saying anything to any of our customers.' That week I got complaints from my customers, customers' husbands, local merchants. She's in my store, my place of business and she's constantly praising Saddam Hussein. She got fired because of her own actions."

In Ashburn, Va., Ossama Elkoshairi, 66, said discrimination had cost him his job as a greeter at a Wal-Mart in Fairfax. Mr. Elkoshairi said that he was teased and harassed by two employees who pointed at him and said, " `He did it,' " and that one held up a picture of Osama bin Laden.

Mr. Elkoshairi, an Egyptian-born American citizen, said that wanting to ensure the employees knew he condemned Mr. bin Laden, he replied that if Mr. bin Laden was responsible, he himself would slaughter him.

Several days later, Mr. Elkoshairi said, he was called into the manager's office and questioned for two hours by Wal-Mart officials and an F.B.I. agent about his behavior and views on Afghanistan and American bombing. He was then fired and, he said, told he was not permitted to enter any Wal-Mart or Sam's Club stores.

Tom Williams, a Wal-Mart spokesman, would not discuss the case, but said the termination had been for "inappropriate conduct."

In several other cases, companies said the firings of people of Arab descent were unrelated to Sept. 11.

Mamdouh Bayoumy, a 47-year-old Egyptian, has filed a complaint with the commission against an Amarillo, Tex., aircraft service company, where he had started work as a mechanic on Sept. 10. Mr. Bayoumy said he was fired on Sept. 17 and given no explanation, so he said he concluded that it was because of his nationality and religion.

Mike Manclark, president of the company, Leading Edge Aviation, disputes that.

"He was flat-out fired because he's a terrible mechanic," Mr. Manclark said. "He was awful. A task that should take an hour, this gentleman would take eight to do."

Mr. Manclark made a point of saying that one of his managers has a Middle Eastern background, and that Mr. Bayoumy was told why he was fired. "I don't care where he's from," Mr. Manclark said. "I don't care if he's purple if the guy can turn a wrench. He is using this event as a tool. He's trying to get money out of it."

Mr. Bayoumy has filed another complaint in a different case. He said a San Antonio recruitment office promised him a job on a Boeing contract, but then turned him away, saying that Boeing was not comfortable with his background.

The recruitment concern, On Site Companies, referred calls to Boeing. A Boeing spokesman, Paul Guse, would not comment on specific cases, but said the company has strict hiring procedures to avoid discrimination.

Leaders of Arab-American groups say some complaints involve treatment by co-workers or bosses.

"People who used to joke with them didn't return hellos," said Hodan Hassan, a civil rights coordinator for the Council on American- Islamic Relations, an Islamic advocacy group. "Some say e-mails would go unanswered. Co-workers have anti-Muslim and anti-Islam conversations that were loud enough for them to hear, saying things like all Muslims should be deported."

The advocates say they believe there is more discrimination than is being reported to the commission because of suspicion of the federal government among some Arabs and Arab-Americans. Adele Rapport, regional attorney for the commission in Detroit, said she believed that was why only nine complaints had been filed through her office, including that of a man who claimed a co- worker followed him everywhere, even to the bathroom.

Still, in several cases where employees complained of discriminatory firing, the companies blame the economy. Sofy Moussa, an Egyptian- American from Ashburn, Va., said she lost her job with the BDM Corporation., which provides logistics and training to the military, because of her ethnicity.

Tom Fintel, the chief executive of BDM, a subsidiary of TRW, said the contract Ms. Moussa was working on ended and virtually all the employees in her section were laid off.

Arsalaan Siddiqi, a 27-year-old Pakistani living in Herndon, Va., said his employer, Veres Consulting, reneged on a promise to sponsor him for a work visa, saying he was told "their patriotic duty demanded they hire an American."

John Burnham, a senior manager at Veres, which conducts surveys for nonprofit associations, said the reason was economic. He said companies can sponsor visa applicants only if they cannot find a qualified American, and the softening economy made that claim unsupportable.

"I wouldn't be surprised if he felt that way," Mr. Burnham said of Mr. Siddiqi. "Arsalaan's a good guy. I felt bad that this happened to him."
