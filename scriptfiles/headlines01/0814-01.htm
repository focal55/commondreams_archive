Published on Tuesday, August 14, 2001 in the New York Times
Prohibited Meat Entered U.S., a Report Finds
by Elizabeth Becker
 
WASHINGTON, Aug. 13 — At the height of the foot-and-mouth epidemic in Europe, at least three quarters of a million pounds of prohibited meat found its way into inspection warehouses in the United States, the Agriculture Department's inspector general reported.

The internal report said the department needed to be more vigilant if it was to continue keeping foot-and- mouth disease from breaking out in this country and infecting American livestock.

In limited spot checks last spring, agriculture inspectors found 756,000 pounds of prohibited meat in warehouses in port cities in New Jersey, Maryland, Texas and other states. The meat was not allowed to be distributed, and some of it was destroyed. The report did not indicate whether any of the meat had been contaminated by the disease. But the inspectors said the meat should never have crossed the border.

The inspectors found more holes in the system than they had expected. Compounding the problems, in this age of explosive increases in agricultural trade, the agencies that control animal and plant diseases still work without a central computer database, the report said. Many safety officials still record entries by hand into ledgers.

In his July 23 report, Roger C. Viadero, the inspector general, said the department needed "more stringent controls to ensure that meat products entering the United States were free" of foot-and-mouth disease.

Ann M. Veneman, the secretary of agriculture, said today that she had accepted the report's recommendations and was tightening inspection procedures.

"As soon as the report came out," Ms. Veneman said in an interview, "we immediately responded to make sure we're doing everything we can."

She faulted Congress, however, for refusing her request for $35 million in emergency aid to improve the inspection system, including creating the database.

Without an increase in financing to hire more staff members and track shipments, many health experts fear that other diseases besides foot-and- mouth disease will enter the country.

"We're relying on the same system of controls to prevent mad cow disease," said Caroline Smith DeWaal of the Center for Science in the Public Interest. "It's clear that with the increase in outbreaks of animal disease, the agency needs more money and more staffing to fulfill their mandate."

Her group filed a petition last week with the Agriculture Department asking for greater controls of meat products that could contain fragments of spinal cords, where mad cow disease is often found.

In Britain, more than 3.5 million animals have been slaughtered in an effort to isolate and eradicate the foot-and-mouth disease. Yet British farmers continue to report an average of three new cases of foot-and- mouth disease every day, a testament to the disease's ability to spread.

To prevent the disease from entering the United States and threatening the country's $50 billion meat industry, the Bush administration banned the import of meat and meat products from the European Union in March.

Safety and health inspectors were added at airports and border posts to prevent the prohibited meat from entering the country and infecting any of its 170 million cattle, sheep and pigs. All borders were put on heightened alert, and Ms. Veneman issued statements saying that the disease would not spread here.

So far, there have been no reports of the disease in the United States.

But the inspector general found near-miss instances where prohibited meat could have left the warehouses to be sold in American markets.

Nearly 260,000 pounds of prohibited meat was stored in an unsecure area in Elizabeth, N.J., the report said. In Texas, a shipment of 32,000 pounds of sausage casings made from prohibited sheep, beef and hogs was sent from the Port of Houston to San Antonio without authorization.

Mr. Viadero said the lack of coordination was caused by confusion about each agency's responsibility and the lack of modern equipment that would allow them to communicate with each other.

The report strongly recommended pushing ahead with a planned database to keep track of imports — the Port Information Network Operations, which could be in service in 2002.

A sophisticated computer program is required to control and account for the increasing volume of trade, the report said. More than 16 million trucks and 5 million large maritime containers crossed United States borders in 1999.

Since border officials are charged with stopping the illicit narcotics trade as well as monitoring compliance with many laws, some experts who have read the report said luck must have played a role in keeping out foot-and-mouth disease.

"It's pretty amazing they've succeeded so far," said Ken Cook, of the Environmental Working Group. "They're stuck with a human-intensive manual system that dates from the 1940's."
