Published on Tuesday, September 25, 2001 by Agence France Press
British Peace Groups Fight to Make Their Voice Heard
by Philip Smucker
 
LONDON - Britain's peace campaigners are struggling to make their voices heard above the din of demands for military reprisals against alleged terrorist mastermind Osama bin Laden.


An unidentified mother and daughter hold a white flower, as a symbol of peace at a Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament (CND) rally in Whitehall, London on Saturday, Sept. 22, 2001 to protest at a possible military strike against Afghanistan for harboring suspected terrorist Osama bin Laden. (AP Photo/PA, Fiona Hanson)
The horror evoked when hijackers crashed airliners into New York's World Trade Center, killing thousands, provoked a huge public appetite for retaliation against the Taliban regime in Afghanistan which has been harboring bin Laden.

A poll for The Observer showed that three-quarters of British men back "surgical" air strikes against countries knowingly harboring terrorist organizations, although the figure fell to 55 percent among women.

An ICM poll for the Guardian newspaper last week found 66 percent wanted military action, with just 14 percent opposed to strikes.

Faced with such results, peace groups are working hard to organize rallies and vigils to get their message across.

"It's been difficult having our voice heard," admits Nigel Chamberlain, a spokesman for veteran British peace campaigners CND (the Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament).

"It may be still a minority in terms of the public as a whole (who are opposed to western military strikes), but out there there's a general disquiet about retaliation and its implications that's set to grow."

On Saturday, up to 5,000 people held a peace vigil at government offices at Whitehall in central London, according to Chamberlain.

CND distributed 1,000 cards carrying the words "Stand shoulder to shoulder for peace and justice."

The message echoed Prime Minister Tony Blair's promise that Britain, the closest European ally of the United States, would stand shoulder to shoulder with it in its war against terrorism.

The high attendance at the rally reflected a "massive increase in feelings of fear and trepidation about western retaliation," according to Chris Nineham of Globalize Resistance, an umbrella group which includes anarchists, trade unionists, anti-globalization protesters and Greens.

Asked about polls showing that most Britons favor retaliation, Nineham said: "When you start asking about blanket bombing and British troops on the ground, you get a majority of people against it.

"Those polls are not a mandate for all-out war, which is what the western powers are talking about."

And he insisted: "We are beginning to get our message across. There's already a deep unease about what the results of a mass onslaught will be.

"Most people recognize that innocent people in Afghanistan will die, leading to increased bitterness against the West."

Michael Foot, former leader of Britain's Labour Party, is to speak at a meeting titled "US Crisis -- Peace Not War!" in north London on October 4.

He will address members of the Labour Party, CND, the Green Party and the Socialist Alliance about predicted US retaliatory strikes, which are thought likely to involve British forces.

Maddy Cooper, of the Socialist Alliance, who will chair the meeting, said: "We urgently need to make it clear to the Blair government that we want justice, not revenge.

"The meeting will give us an opportunity to start to organize locally against the drift to war."
