Published on Tuesday, May 29, 2001 by Earth Times News Service
Going Backwards
 World Bank To Fund ‘Risky’ Project Involving Shell Oil in Nigeria
 
The World Bank is set to approve next week a $15 million loan to a financial intermediary to provide subcontracting services to Shell Oil Corporation in the Niger Delta of Nigeria. Already the Institute for Policy Studies and Friends of the Earth have denounced the plan to allot hard currency to banks in the Niger Delta, who could then lend to Shell Oil -- a company notorious for oil spills which have ruined the lives of fisherman and farmers in the area. Internal documents from the International Finance Corporation (IFC) reveal that the IFC recognize this association with Shell as a “reputational risk” to the World Bank.

“This loan sets an alarming precedent for a number of reasons,” says Daphne Wysham, an energy policy analyst with the Institute for Policy Studies. “It is being prepared without any accountability to World Bank policies, and it will provide support to one of the wealthiest and most controversial corporations on the planet in a region where human rights abuses continue to be commonplace.”

Wysham continued by implicating other threats that are almost sure to result. “Rather than alleviating poverty among the people of the Delta, this project will simply continue the pattern of environmental devastation, corruption, human rights abuse and corporate subsidies that have plagued the region for decades,” she warned.

Additionally, deputy director for international programs with Friends of the Earth Carol Welch said, “This debacle reveals why we need serious scrutiny of all World Bank oil, gas and mining projects.”

Since 1993, consumer boycotts, human rights and environmental campaigns have branded Shell’s reputation. The transnational company was even forced to cease operations in the Ogoni region of the Niger Delta after widespread protests by the Ogoni people, who have been plagued by constant oil spills. The most recent Shell spill occurred on May 9 of this year, and the CIA has divulged that years of spills in the Niger Delta, which have yet to be cleaned up, amount to the equivalent of ten times the Alaskan Exxon Valdez oil spill.

Nonetheless, the IFC plans to approve the loan via a streamlined process wherein the project would not require an environmental impact assessment or adherence to any World Bank policy or guideline. The World Bank plans to launch a review of their oil, gas and mining sectors this fall. It is not clear whether projects such as this loan would be part of the review process.
