"You Have to Crawl before You can Walk"
National Missile Defense Test Fails Utterly
   WASHINGTON - July 11 - Saturday morning's test of the U.S. Anti-Ballistic Missile (ABM) system showed just how far from deployment the system really is. Failing on at least two separate counts, the test garnered nearly no useful information for the Pentagon because the booster did not separate from the interceptor, plunging both into the sea.

"This is fundamental technology to the U.S., and we cannot even get that right," argued John Isaacs, president of the Council for a Livable World. "After four decades of trying to develop a national missile defense, all the Pentagon has proved is how far it is from developing a workable technology. This failure should slow the program significantly."

"The Pentagon claimed the tests were simple (read "dumbed down") because the system ‘had to walk before it could run,'" claimed Luke Warren, analyst at the Council for a Livable World Education Fund. "After the last test, I think the more appropriate analogy should be that ‘you have to crawl before you can walk.'"

The test was important since it is the last test of the proposed system before President Clinton is scheduled to make a decision this fall whether or not to deploy the system. Moreover, the results of Saturday's test will play a large part in influencing the Pentagon's Deployment Readiness Review decision this summer. It would be absurd for the Pentagon to try to turn this failure to issue a recommendation to proceed with a national missile defense; it is now unlikely that President Clinton will commit to deployment.

However, the President may decide this fall to start construction on a radar station used in the final NMD system. Such an action would violate the 1972 ABM Treaty, which bans defenses of the entire country , thus likely causing negative reactions among our European allies as well as with Russia and China, undermining the prospect for further reductions of nuclear weapons.

At this point, only three of 19 scheduled tests of the Clinton plan have been completed, two of them failures and one only a partial success. As a result the debate on the merits of deploying an NMD system is purely academic.

"No car manufacturer would decide whether to build a new automobile based on only three of 19 tests," said John Isaacs. "No new computer system would be placed on the market with such a paucity of market data," Isaacs continued. "The bottom line is that national missile defense is still not ready for prime time."
