California Voters Pass Divisive Anti-Marriage Knight Initiative
Campaign Unified Community; Educational Efforts Will Ensure Future Success, says HRC
  

WASHINGTON - March 8 - The battle against California's anti-gay Proposition 22 - while unsuccessful -- has paved the way for gains in the future, the Human Rights Campaign said today.

"Today's defeat was painful, but the organizing and educational efforts that resulted from the campaign have laid a rock-solid foundation for our future," HRC Executive Director Elizabeth Birch said Tuesday. "Thanks to the extreme right wing, for the first time Californians became acquainted with countless gay and lesbian families and their needs. While the forces of intolerance were victorious today, by unnecessarily elevating this issue they may have ensured civil marriage rights for same-sex couples tomorrow."

California voters approved the ballot measure Tuesday by a vote of 61to 39 percent. Proposition 22 -- also known as the Knight Initiative for its sponsor, Republican state Sen. Pete Knight -- prevents California from recognizing same-sex marriages if they become legal and are performed in another state. Currently, no state recognizes same-sex marriages. While the loss is a temporary setback in efforts to attain civil marriage rights for same-sex couples, the battle to defeat the initiative united the gay and lesbian community in California and across the country, according to HRC.

"The 'No on Knight' campaign led by Mike Marshall did an outstanding job of mobilizing voters and educating the public despite being outspent nearly 2 to 1," said Birch. "We applaud their tremendous efforts and thank them for leading the fight for fairness. One day we will look back and realize that they blazed the trail that our community followed to recognition of civil marriage rights for same-sex couples."

Efforts to defeat the Knight Initiative were complicated by the fund-raising advantage of proponents, who spent $8 million, compared to $5.5 million spent by the opposition. Outspoken support from Mormon, Roman Catholic and evangelical Christian church leaders contributed to the lopsided fund-raising advantage.

However, other religious leaders opposed the initiative, including three Southern California bishops - Episcopal, Lutheran and Methodist - who issued a joint statement saying it could lead to discrimination.

HRC raised more than a half-million dollars to fight the Knight Initiative and activated its 40,000 California members to volunteer and vote. HRC leaders in key cities, including Los Angeles, New York and Dallas, hosted fundraisers for the "No on Knight" campaign.

The presidential hopefuls were divided on the initiative, with Democrats Bill Bradley and Vice President Al Gore opposing the measure, while Republican Sen. John McCain favored it. A regional chairwoman for Republican Gov. George W. Bush's campaign told the Associated Press that Bush was "not taking a position" on the initiative because it was a state issue. California Gov. Gray Davis opposed the Knight Initiative, as did many prominent California Republicans, including: Rep. Tom Campbell; Ward Connerly, regent, University of California; all Republican candidates for mayor of San Diego; San Diego Mayor Susan Golding; Los Angeles Mayor Richard Riordan; and Assemblyman Jim Cunneen. Additionally, Pete Knight's gay s on, David, opposed the initiative in a column he wrote for the Los Angeles Times.

The Human Rights Campaign is the largest national lesbian and gay political organization, with members throughout the country. It effectively lobbies Congress, provides campaign support and educates the public to ensure that lesbian and gay Americans can be open, honest and safe at home, at work and in the community.
