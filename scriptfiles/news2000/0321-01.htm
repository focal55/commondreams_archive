Advocates For DC Voting Rights Gearing Up For Battle Despite Federal Court Setback
  

WASHINGTON - March 21 - In a decision handed down yesterday, a three-judge federal panel ruled against District of Columbia residents who sought full and equal voting representation in the U.S. Congress. Currently, DC residents have only a non-voting delegate in the U.S. House and no representation at all in the U.S. Senate.

"The panel's decision refusing to grant DC residents rights full voting representation in Congress swims against the democratic tide of American history which has moved us progressively toward 'one-person, one-vote,'" Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger said. "But the decision will in no way stem that democratic tide. Like women in the early part of last century, African Americans in the 1960s, and even 18-year-olds in the 1970s, DC residents will continue the fight for voting rights, and win to full representation in Congress."

Common Cause is a member of the Coalition for DC Representation in Congress (DCVote), a broad coalition of individuals and organizations that is educating the public and Congress about the need for U.S. citizens living in DC to enjoy the same rights to voting representation as their fellow Americans living in the 50 states.

"While the panel's decision is a setback for District residents, it will be overcome by the rightness of our cause and the strength of our commitment to democracy. Common Cause will play a leadership role in this effort," Harshbarger said.
