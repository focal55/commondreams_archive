Oil Drilling in Arctic Refuge Equals National Energy Security - NOT!
  WASHINGTON - May 16 - Statement of Athan Manuel, PIRG Arctic Wilderness Campaign:

Drilling for oil in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge has nothing to do with National Energy Security. Senator Murkowski's bill (S. 2557), the so-called National Energy Security Act of 2000, is more about financial security for Big Oil than it is about National Energy Security.

Oil from the Arctic Refuge, where the U.S. Geological Survey estimates that there are 2.3 billion barrels of recoverable oil, would literally be a drop in the bucket. At current rates of consumption that is enough oil to last the U.S. for about six months. And any oil thought to be in the Refuge would take almost 10 years to reach U.S. consumers.

Contrast that with the oil field recently discovered in Kazakhstan. That field is thought to contain between 8 and 50 billion barrels of oil.

The new field in Kazakhstan points out the harsh reality about oil - most of it lies outside the United States. The U.S. will never drill its way to energy independence.

If Senator Murkowski really cared about energy independence he wouldn't have voted against raising our nation's corporate auto fuel economy standards. He also wouldn't have sponsored legislation that repealed a ban on the export of Alaskan crude oil in 1995. As a result, millions of barrels of crude are being diverted from California to China and other Asian countries.

In addition to plundering one of the last remaining pristine areas in this country, S. 2557 authorizes a host of poor energy policies while resurrecting expensive boondoggles that have already been rejected by the American public and Wall Street. S. 2557 is nothing more than a license to plunder public resources and prop up the failed nuclear industry coated with a thin veneer of energy efficiency and renewable energy provisions.

This bill would increase already outrageous taxpayer handouts to the oil and gas industry. It also resurrects the "coal liquefaction" program, a continuation of the failed "synfuels" program of the 1970's and it promotes nuclear spent fuel reprocessing. Finally, at a time when public safety is threatened by aging nuclear reactors around the country, this bill would further erode citizens' ability to participate in nuclear safety proceedings.

What we need is an energy policy that focuses more on energy efficiency and renewables than on ruining some of the last unspoiled areas left in the U.S. We have plenty of oil rigs -- and plenty of pollution from the burning of fossil fuels -- but there is only one Arctic National Wildlife Refuge.
