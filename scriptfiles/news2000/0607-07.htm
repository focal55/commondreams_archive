Controversial Colorado Water Project in Violation of Federal Law, Group Says
  WASHINGTON - June 7 - The proposed $300 million Animas-La Plata (ALP) water project violates federal law, stated Jill Lancelot, Legislative Director of Taxpayers for Common Sense, in testimony today before the Senate Indian Affairs Committee.

Lancelot charged that by not accounting for inevitable project cost overruns and inflationary costs, and by not identifying the cost local sponsors should repay for the project, ALP is in violation of federal reclamation law.

At a hearing today on S.2508, the Colorado Ute Settlements Act Amendments of 2000, Lancelot criticized the bill as “signing a blank check” for a wasteful water boondoggle that will harm the American taxpayer and the environment.

“The bill would leave federal taxpayers vulnerable for inflation and inevitable cost overruns for the project. “The country is littered with water projects that have far surpassed original cost estimates,” continued Lancelot.

“Animas-La Plata is one of last relics from the age of water behemoths,” said Lancelot. “The projects cost too much and does too little.”

First authorized by Congress in 1968, ALP became a vehicle to settle the Southern and Ute Mountain Ute tribes’ water rights in the mid 1980s.

“Although the current version is touted as an Indian-only project, a significant portion of the project’s benefits would go to non-Indian interests,” continued Lancelot. “The reason why ALP is being built is to prevent non-Indian interests from having their water rights potentially disrupted.”

Lancelot testified also that S. 2508 should, but does not deauthorize the original massive project. “The Clinton Administration has given repeated assurances that they will not support legislation that does not specifically deauthorize all of the remaining components of the old ALP project; S.2508 fails to do so.”

Lancelot also testified that the ALP project is a bad investment for taxpayers. A recent analysis by an independent economist showed that the return to taxpayers could be as little as 22 cents per dollar.  

“The project’s limited benefits come at a heavy cost to federal taxpayers. ALP’s current price tag is at least $300 million, but benefits would amount to as little as $85 million,” continued Lancelot.

The ongoing costs of the project would be substantial. Federal taxpayers would pay millions annually for operation and maintenance costs for ALP until the Tribes put all of their water to use - even though much of the water will not be used for 30-100 years.

Lancelot also pointed out that the basic need for the water is extremely questionable, “It is outrageous that federal taxpayers would subsidize golf courses, resort hotels, dude ranches or a coal-fired power plant.”

“TCS recognizes the commitment to honoring the water rights of the Ute tribes. We agree that the obligation to the tribes must be met, but, we also believe that there are less costly ways to accomplish that goal,” concluded Lancelot.
