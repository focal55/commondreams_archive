Feds Act To Protect Salmon And Steelhead Habitat On West Coast
  

SAN FRANCISCO - February 10 - After having been sued repeatedly by numerous environmental and fishing groups, the National Marine Fisheries Service moved Monday to protect thousands of square miles of western freshwater streams used by salmon and steelhead for spawning and rearing. The critical habitat designation, finalized on Monday, February 7, 2000, includes the reaches of all streams currently occupied by or accessible to salmon and steelhead in Idaho, Washington, Oregon, and California, and also includes lands adjacent to the streams necessary to control erosion and sediment deposition, ensure bank stability, and provide woody debris. In California, the designation covers much of the California coast, the San Francisco Bay-Sacramento/San Joaquin Delta, and the Sacramento and San Joaquin Rivers and their tributaries.

The designation is the culmination of years of litigation brought by Earthjustice Legal Defense Fund against NMFS first to compel the agency to list declining populations of west coast salmon and steelhead under the Endangered Species Act, and then to designate their critical habitat.

Critical habitat designation adds another layer of legal protection to these imperiled salmon and steelhead runs: Federal agencies must now "insure" not only that any action authorized, funded, or carried out by the agency does not "jeopardize the continued existence" of the fish, but also that such action will not result in the "destruction or adverse modification" of the designated critical habitat. Before taking any action that could either jeopardize salmon or steelhead or result in the destruction or adverse modification of their critical habitat, federal agencies must first consult with NMFS.

"We applaud NMFS for finally taking this action," said Earthjustice staff attorney Mike Sherwood, who represented many of the environmental and fishing groups in several lawsuits against NMFS. "It's a shame that we had to sue NMFS to get to this point, wasting time and resources that should have been used for salmon and steelhead recovery. But better late than never, and we hope NMFS will now aggressively take the steps necessary to recover these magnificent species." Sherwood noted, however, that the critical habitat designations do not go far enough because they omit portions of streams upstream from dams that block upstream migration, and also omit entirely the ocean habitat of these anadromous fish where they spend their adult lives. "There is more work to be done," said Sherwood. "We hope that NMFS will fill in the gaps and that further litigation will not be necessary."

Most west coast populations of steelhead (an anadromous form of rainbow trout) and coho, chinook, chum, and sockeye salmon have declined precipitously in the last decade largely because of the destruction and loss of their freshwater spawning and rearing habitat due to human activities such as the building of dams and water diversions for irrigated agriculture, grazing, and logging activities within the watersheds, and water pollution. The new critical habitat designation will require a hard, new look at those activities on federal lands with an eye to salmon and steelhead survival and recovery.
