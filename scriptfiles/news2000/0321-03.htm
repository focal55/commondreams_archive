New Report Details NRA's Shameful Legacy of Weakening Our Gun Laws
   WASHINGTON - March 21 - The following was released today by Handgun Control Inc.:

In the wake of the Columbine massacre and other recent shootings, the National Rifle Association has doggedly tried to deflect any discussion of new or stronger gun laws with a simple -- and simplistic -- response: "Enforce the laws already on the books." Arguing that the Clinton administration has failed to enforce existing laws, the NRA claims that more gun laws aren't needed.

Now, as Congress prepares to take up a package of legislation to improve the ability of federal law enforcement agencies to prosecute gun criminals, Handgun Control and the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence have released a new report demonstrating the basic hypocrisy of the NRA's enforcement mantra. The Enforcement Fable: How the NRA Prevented Enforcement of the Nation's Gun Laws details the NRA's systematic weakening of federal gun laws and how the NRA's actions have made law enforcement's job of catching criminals more difficult. The report considers three important components of our gun laws: The McClure-Volkmer Act, the Brady Law, and funding of the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms (ATF).

The NRA-sponsored Firearms Owners' Protection Act of 1986, also known as the McClure-Volkmer Act, is perhaps the most damning evidence of NRA hypocrisy on enforcement. With its passage, the NRA accomplished the following:

-- Allowed unlicensed individuals to sell their personal firearms as a "hobby," allowing for the sale of massive numbers of firearms to criminals and juveniles without background checks. This provision not only created a vast secondary market, but also opened up the "gun show loophole" which Congress is now charged with closing;

-- Facilitated the sale of guns to criminals and juveniles by turning gun shows into a booming business;

-- Allowed criminals to keep or regain their rights to own guns;

-- Severely restricted the ability of the ATF to conduct inspections of the business premises of federally licensed firearms dealers; and

-- Raised the burden of proof for violations of federal gun laws.

The seven-year battle to pass the Brady Bill and the NRA's ongoing attacks on the National Instant Check System show that the NRA's claim to support background checks is utterly specious. Namely:

-- Before the Brady Bill was passed, 32 states lacked a background check system. A criminal could walk into a gun store, sign the form stating he is not a prohibited purchaser and walk out with a gun. The form would simply get filed away, with no follow-through to make sure the purchaser's statements were accurate. The Brady Bill was designed to close this loophole by providing for a background check and waiting period, and the NRA worked tirelessly to defeat it;

-- Only when it realized that the Brady Bill was unstoppable did the NRA shift its efforts towards weakening the law as much as possible by attempting to push through an immediate reliance on an "instant check" system, a system that was not technically feasible at the time;

-- Even after embracing an "instant check" system, the NRA has continually sought to undermine the system's integrity and efficiency by preventing law enforcement from maintaining any records on the background checks it conducts;

-- Most telling is the NRA's continued opposition to background checks on all gun purchasers, including all gun show sales and private sales. If the NRA supports background checks, why do they want to keep this gaping loophole open in our laws?

Finally, it is no secret that the NRA has little love for federal law enforcement, particularly the ATF. NRA rhetoric combined with its campaign to financially cripple the ATF demonstrate the gun lobby's singleminded thoroughness in carrying out its extremist agenda: You don't just make the laws weak and difficult to enforce, you also debilitate the agency that has primary responsibilty for enforcing those laws.

"The Enforcement Fable provides a thorough account of the NRA's campaign to hobble the enforcement of our nation's gun laws," said Michael Barnes, president of Handgun Control and the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence. "This report makes it clear that the NRA's sole concern has been to make gun ownership and gun commerce as easy and widespread as possible -- all at the expense of public safety."

------ Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, was founded in 1974 by Dr. Mark Borinsky and N.T. "Pete" Shields, two victims of gun violence. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works with law enforcement, public health, religious, and community groups across the country to strengthen and protect federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban all guns. HCI has more than 400,000 members nationwide, making it the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. Handgun Control's sister organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, was founded in 1983 and is also chaired by Sarah Brady. It seeks to reduce gun violence through education, legal advocacy, research, and outreach to the entertainment community. The Center's national initiatives include violence prevention programs for youth, education programs on the risks associated with guns, legal representation for gun violence victims, reform of the gun industry, and research on the risks associated with guns and on the efficacy of gun control laws. More information about both HCI and CPHV can be found on our Web sites at www.handguncontrol.org and www.cphv.org.
