Hollywood, Lawyers Loom Large In Hillary's Senate Campaign
  

WASHINGTON - February 24 - Hillary Clinton's campaign for the U.S. Senate is getting a big boost from the entertainment industry, according to an analysis of her first campaign finance report by the non-partisan Center for Responsive Politics.

Clinton raised at least $437,000 from individuals working in movies, television and music - second only to the $667,000 she got from lawyers, a traditional Democratic constituency. Her biggest single contributor was the Walt Disney Co., whose executives, employees and families gave $56,550 to her campaign effort. Most of those dollars came from donors in Disney's Miramax Films division.

She also drew nearly $364,000 from the securities & investment industry. Her number two donor was the Wall Street investment firm of Goldman, Sachs & Co., whose executives and families gave $39,120.

The Center's analysis covers money raised by the Clinton Senate campaign during 1999, both directly and through proceeds from a joint Senate fundraiser. The First Lady raised nearly $8.1 million during that period - more than 61% of it coming from individual donors giving $1,000 or more.

Reflecting the nationwide interest in this year's New York Senate race, the First Lady's contributions flowed in from every corner of the nation. Just over 44% of the money she collected - some $2.7 million - came from New York state. The rest came from 45 other states and the District of Columbia.

The New York City metro area was by far her richest mother lode of contributions, providing over $2.2 million in donations of $200 or more. Her other leading metro areas were Washington, DC ($515,000); Los Angeles ($408,000); Boston ($236,000); and Chicago ($226,000).

Within the entertainment industry, Clinton's money came from both coasts. She drew $201,740 in entertainment/media money from New York, and just under $170,000 from California. The list of donors is studded with Hollywood stars - from Tom Cruise and Tom Hanks to Barbra Streisand, Cicely Tyson, and filmmakers Steven Spielberg and Martin Scorcese.

Big name designers - many of whom have also enlivened the First Lady's wardrobe over the years - also popped up on the donor list. Among them were Calvin Klein, Ralph Lauren and Oscar de la Renta.

Contributions by Clinton's Republican rival, New York Mayor Rudy Giuliani, were still not available in electronic format from the Federal Election Commission as of this morning, making analysis of his money all but impossible. Despite the fact that both the Clinton and Giuliani campaigns are using high-tech software to keep track of their contributions, both filed their reports only on paper. The FEC must then re-enter all contributions by hand at taxpayer expense, causing a substantial delay in the computerized availability of the data.

A complete analysis of the Giuliani contributions will be released by the Center next week, presuming the data is computerized by the FEC in time.
