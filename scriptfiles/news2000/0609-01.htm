Clean Air Trust Questions Actions of Oil Industry
  WASHINGTON - June 9 - The following was released today by Clean Air Trust:

The headlines of the past two weeks have seemed bizarre, to say the least: "U.S. Facing Dilemma over Super-Clean Gas...Chicago Drivers Pay $2/Gallon for Gasoline... Wall Street Sees Wide Refining Margins: Strong 2Q Company Profits."

What the heck is happening to gasoline prices in the heartland? Who stands to profit? Is the oil industry engaged in piracy at the pumps?

Here, in brief, is what we know. By June 1, oil companies were required to begin selling cleaner-burning ("reformulated") gasoline in certain high-smog parts of the nation - principally the Northeast, the Gulf Coast, and parts of the Midwest, including Chicago and Milwaukee. This is the second phase of the cleaner-burning gas program that began in 1995.

Almost as soon as the new fuel began flowing through the pipelines in May, prices began to inch up as the oil industry started whining about "supply problems." (There was a temporary supply shortage in St. Louis because of a pipeline problem. Because of that, EPA granted oil companies a temporary waiver from selling cleaner-burning gasoline in St. Louis. EPA denied similar waiver requests for Chicago and Milwaukee on the grounds that supplies are adequate.)

As we headed into Memorial Day, prices went up -- and generally have stayed up. (Footnote: When cleaner-gasoline programs of this sort begin, prices generally experience a brief bump and then settle down. EPA has projected the cleaner gasoline ultimately will cost a penny or two extra per gallon. Prior EPA projections of this sort have proven accurate or even an overstatement of costs.)

The interesting thing in this case is that cost of cleaner-burning gasoline is much higher in Chicago and Milwaukee than in the rest of the nation. Consider the following from the U.S. Department of Energy's Energy Information Administration:

On June 5, the price of self-serve regular cleaner-burning gasoline was about two cents a gallon higher than nearby conventional gasoline in the Gulf Coast. In the Northeast, the price spread was about seven or eight cents a gallon. But in the Midwest - that is, Chicago and Milwaukee -- the difference was a whopping 23 cents a gallon! Wisconsin lawmakers are suing EPA for the right to poison their constituents with dirtier gasoline, while the U.S. Justice Department is said to be launching an investigation.

Here are a few questions that ought to be answered in any probe:

-- First and foremost, the oil industry has known for more than five years that it would be selling the cleaner-burning gasoline by June 1. Why didn't the industry plan for known supply needs? Is this some sort of price-gouging scheme?

-- The biggest difference between the cleaner-burning gasoline sold in Milwaukee and Chicago is that it contains ethanol, rather than the MTBE used in the Northeast and the Gulf Coast. Are big ethanol producers (like Archer-Daniels-Midland) complicit in some scheme to drive up prices? (ADM, by the way, was fined this week by the European Commission for operating an alleged global price-fixing cartel.)

-- Recently the Clinton Administration and some members of Congress have proposed that future gasoline include a national "renewable" fuels component -- that is, ethanol, including an increasing percentage produced from biomass. The oil industry is bitterly opposed to a renewable fuels standard. Is the Midwestern price problem connected to an oil industry desire to discredit renewable fuels?

A few final factoids:

-- The American Petroleum Institute, the big oil industry trade association, has gone on record opposing waivers of the cleaner-burning gasoline requirements.

-- The respected publication Octane Week reported June 5 that "Wall Street is already predicting a good showing for the nation's oil companies" in the second quarter, and that "much of this quarter's performance stems from healthy refining margins."

In other words, some consumers may be upset, but the oil companies are doing just fine.
