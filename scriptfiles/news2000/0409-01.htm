American Express Board Member Target of NPA Action
   WASHINGTON - April 9 - The following was released today by National People's Action:

Daniel F. Ackerson, board member of American Express, received a visit today at his home, 1100 Mill Ridge Rd. in McLean, Virginia, from some 450 leaders of grassroots neighborhood groups concerned about the role of the corporation in financing predatory subprime mortgage lending.

American Express is one of the largest investors in First Alliance, a Minnesota based mortgage lender notorious for originating "ripoff" loans, say advocates. American Express made more than $89 million on First Alliance investors last year. The company has the power to stop First Alliance, which recently declared bankruptcy to avoid paying off on scads of lawsuits that have been filed throughout the country, from making more predatory loans. American Express and its shareholders also benefit from loans made by First Alliance, another predatory subprime mortgage lender.

A recent joint investigation of 20/20 and The New York Times explored the link between investment banking firms such as Lehman Brothers, part of the AmEx corporation, and Minnesota-based First Alliance. The company has helped First Alliance originate loans that lead borrowers into foreclosure or toward losing their homes due to high fees and high interest rates.

"We're asking Mr. Ackerson to be the person who convinces AmEx to put people before profits and stop Wall Street money from flowing to firms that make equity theft loans -- loans that benefit the lenders and brokers rather than the borrowers," says Gale Cincotta, national chairperson of National People's Action.

Predatory subprime mortgage lending is issue number one for grassroots activists from across the country who will be in Washington, D.C., Sunday, April 9 and Monday April 10.

Neighborhood groups are concerned about all facets of the refinance business. This starts with the mortgage brokers who target homeowners for refinance loans in order to collect juicy kickbacks from lenders -- as much as 10 percent or more of the principal of the loan in many individuals' cases. The lenders, too, must be held responsible for charging an appropriate level of interest and realistic fees. So too the Wall Street investment firms that put up capital to make these loans possible and which rake in profits on loans that set people up for foreclosure. Federal regulators and the secondary market also have roles to play in ensuring that subprime lending has no adverse effects on communities.

"Subprime lending is the Wild West of the mortgage market right now," says Cincotta. "Unless we regulate the interest rates and fees that subprime, high-interest-rate lenders can charge, more families are going to lose their homes and more communities in the city and suburbs will have abandoned buildings."

National People's Action is a 29-year-old coalition of 302 grassroots neighborhood groups based in 38 states.
