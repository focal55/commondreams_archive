Work/Family Conflicts Still Largely Absent from Entertainment
TV New Study Finds TV Offers Few Realistic Depictions of Working Families’ Balancing Acts
   WASHINGTON - February 9 - During the 1998-99 season, prime time network television offered almost no depictions of the kind of work/family conflicts that Americans face every day. A new study, commissioned by the National Partnership for Women & Families, concludes that prime time entertainment television rarely shows characters struggling to balance work and family responsibilities like child care, elder care and family leave. The study also found that TV portrays single male characters in workplace settings more often than married or female characters, and depicts men in positions of power much more often than women.

Television is a powerful cultural influence that affects our lives in many ways, said National Partnership President Judith L. Lichtman. By reflecting Americans’ experiences, TV can draw attention to the challenges we face, and foster awareness and change. Our study confirms that, like many workplaces, TV is still catching up to the challenges facing America’s working families today.

Prime Time @ Work examined situation comedies, dramas, and animated series on four broadcast networks that were among the top-rated 100 programs of the 1998-99 prime time season. The sample consisted of a constructed week of prime-time programming on the ABC, CBS, Fox, and NBC networks, compiled from a three-month sample period (March, April, May). Martha M. Lauzen, Ph.D., professor of communication at San Diego State University, led the team that analyzed a total of 719 adult characters on 59 prime time programs.

Work/family conflicts were depicted just eleven times on a total of ten programs in the sample, and were major story lines in just five instances. A mere one percent of characters experienced any work/family conflict a drop from three percent of characters who experienced such conflicts in a study commissioned by the National Partnership of the 1997-98 television season.

The study also found that:

Fully 99 percent of prime time TV characters never experienced work/family conflicts;

Non-parents and single and male adult characters were most likely to be shown at work. Three in five adult non-parents (61 percent) and just 38 percent of adult parents were shown in work settings;

Two in three single characters (68 percent) and just 42 percent of married characters were shown in work settings;

Female characters were more likely to be identified by their marital status, while male characters were more likely to be identified by their occupation;

Sixty-nine percent of adult male characters and 62 percent of adult female characters were shown in work settings; and

When major characters were examined, 41 percent of male characters and just 25 percent of female characters held powerful jobs.

Examples of work/family conflicts portrayed include:

The Norm Show: Danny’s father drops by the office and asks Danny to go to lunch. Danny can’t go because he has to work.

Nash Bridges: Joe attends a parent-teacher conference during work hours.

Party of Five: A power outage at the restaurant he manages makes Bailey late for sister Claudia’s 16th birthday party.

Profiler: Samantha Waters is at work when she receives a call from her daughter's school. Her daughter, Chloe, is missing. Samantha leaves work to look for her.

Prime Time @ Work is the second in a series of periodic studies commissioned by the National Partnership for Women & Families on work/family issues on prime time television. The 1998 study, Balancing Acts: Work/Family Issues on Prime Time Television, found that on television most adults were men, few mothers worked for pay, and almost no one was over age 50. The National Partnership is using this research to raise awareness about work/family issues, and to alert TV networks and producers to missed opportunities in portraying the experiences of working families.

The National Partnership for Women & Families is a nonprofit, nonpartisan organization that uses public education and advocacy to promote fairness in the workplace, quality health care, and policies that help women and men meet the dual demands of work and family.
