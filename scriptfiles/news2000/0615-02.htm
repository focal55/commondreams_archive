New Web Site to Focus on Cleanup of Diesel Trucks, Fuel
   WASHINGTON - June 15 - The following was released today by Clean Air Trust:

How much money does the chairman of Exxon-Mobil make? Are oil companies crying wolf when it comes to cleaning up dirty diesel fuel? What can average citizens do to clean up big, smelly trucks?

These questions and others will be answered by a new web site, http://www.cleanupdiesel.org, unveiled today by the Clean Air Trust Education Fund in conjunction with the American Lung Association.

"This site is designed to educate the public about the major health threat posed by diesel trucks and buses and diesel fuel," said Paul G. Billings, the Lung Association's assistant vice president for government relations. "It is also set up to allow the public to communicate directly with the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency -- and to tell EPA to clean up dirty diesel trucks and diesel fuel now."

The EPA recently proposed the most sweeping diesel cleanup in history, calling on new trucks and buses to be up to 95 percent cleaner than today's new diesel vehicles. To enable advanced emission controls to work, the EPA also proposed eliminating 97 percent of the sulfur from diesel fuel. (Sulfur is a poison, much as lead was a poison to catalytic converters in the 1970s.)

Billings noted that the oil industry is vehemently opposed to the cleanup plan. "Armed with the facts, however, citizens can use www.cleanupdiesel.org to counter the oil companies' scare tactics," Billings noted.

Next week the EPA begins hearings on the plan, which it hopes to make final by the end of this year. The agency is also accepting public comments until mid-August.

"This web site, www.cleanupdiesel.org, will allow the public to send comments to EPA with a simple click of the mouse," said Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust Education Fund. He noted www.cleanupdiesel.org also contains: fact sheets about diesel pollution; links to details of the EPA proposal as well as comments from President Clinton and EPA Administrator Carol Browner; the latest on oil industry profits; an analysis by state and local air pollution regulators of diesel-related cancer; and links to allied organizations including the Lung Association, the Natural Resources Defense Council, Clean Air Network, U.S. PIRG, Union of Concerned Scientists, and state agencies. The site will be continually updated with new information.
