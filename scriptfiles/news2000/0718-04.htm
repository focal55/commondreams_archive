Physicians Warn Washington Resident to Prepare for More Floods, Drought, Bad Ozone Days and Health Emergencies
  SEATTLE - July 18 - According to Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR), residents of Washington could experience a wide range of increased health risks as a result of climate change. These threats are outlined in "Death by Degrees: The Health Threats of Climate Change in Washington," a new 35-page report released today at a press briefing in Seattle. Press briefings are also scheduled tomorrow in Richland and Spokane.

Climate change is expected to bring more extreme weather events to Washington, a state that is already above average in the number of presidentially-declared weather disasters. More frequent and severe floods could cause injuries, spread illness through contaminated water and destroy property. Severe flooding in Washington in February 1996 killed three and caused an estimated $800 million in damages. The January 1997 landslide in Snohomish County brought down more than 100,000 cubic meters of earth, swept five cars of a passing freight train into Puget Sound and killed a family of four.

Warmer summer temperatures could worsen air pollution by increasing concentrations of ground-level ozone and other pollutants that exacerbate asthma and other respiratory diseases. Warmer ocean temperatures could increase bacterial contamination of shellfish. Longer summers could increase the incidence of Lyme disease and other insect-borne diseases. Earlier snowmelts and higher temperatures could also cause more summertime droughts, such as the drought of 1999, threatening agricultural production, compromising water quality and impairing hydroelectric power generation.

"We have just ended the hottest decade on record," said Dr. Robert K. Musil, executive director of PSR, a nonprofit organization representing 16,000 health professionals nationwide. "The death rate for asthma has tripled over the past 20 years. Decisive action is needed now to protect human health. The number one priority is to reduce the burning of fossil fuels, especially by coal-burning power plants and automobiles."

The report lays out specific opportunities for personal and political actions needed to achieve this goal. The report on Washington is the sixth report in a planned series of 18 state-specific reports in which PSR describes the effects that rising world temperatures may have on local communities. Free copies of the report are available to the public through the PSR national office, 202-898-0150, ext. 228 or online at www.psr.org/washington.
