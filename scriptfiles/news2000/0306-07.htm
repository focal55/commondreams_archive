Peoples' National Town Hall Meeting to Address Urgent Environmental Issues
   SACRAMENTO - March 6 - Today, a prestigious panel of environmental and social justice leaders will convene a national town hall meeting in Sacramento to highlight growing threats to the planet, the citizenry and our democracy.

Falling on the eve of the pivotal California presidential primary -- and following on the heels of the protests in Seattle against the anti-democratic World Trade Organization and its assault on workers, farmers and the environment -- The Peoples' National Town Hall Meeting will provide citizens with a forum to address the most pressing issues of our time. The Peoples' Forum is being organized to highlight issues of concern to millions of Americans not currently being addressed by the major presidential candidates.

Included among the issues to be addressed by the panel will be workers rights, environmental protection, campaign finance reform, food safety and basic human rights.

"America and the planet are at a crossraods," said Tom Weis of the National Forest Protection Alliance, one of the organizers of the event. "We can either continue down our current path of environmental and human destruction, or we can chart a new course predicated on democratic values, compassion, sustainability and equal rights for all."

Among the panelists will be David Brower, the dean of the American environmental movement, and 90-year-old great-grandmother Doris "Granny D" Haddock, who just completed a historic walk across the country crusading for campaign finance reform.

"The presidential candidates don't yet fully understand what happened in Seattle," explained Brower. "But they will. That was a turning point in our nation's history. We are embarked in a struggle to take back our democracy, one step at a time."

"Granny D" summarized the spirit of the event by saying, "In this generation, the fate of our natural environment, and of our democratic environment will be decided. Only great leadership, and great love, can get us through the times ahead. We must all take our part in this great drama. It is more than politics; it is a struggle of the soul, and it is exquisitely personal to each of us."
