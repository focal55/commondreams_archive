Congress Kills Effort to Let Polluters Off the Hook For Toxic Waste Cleanups
Sierra Club Asks People to Urge their Members to Vote Against Future Bills that Harm Health Standards
  WASHINGTON - September 27 - The Sierra Club today criticized Members of Congress who voted last night to weaken the standards that protect American communities from hazardous waste. Fortunately, the Congressional leadership failed in their effort to let selected businesses avoid paying to clean up their toxic pollution.

The Sierra Club is calling on people to urge their Representatives to stand up for the environment and vote against efforts to weaken pollution standards in the upcoming budget bills. Despite having 253 Members of Congress in favor of weakening the Superfund policy, the 161 Representatives opposed to this effort were able to reject the legislation because the bill, H.R 5171, required a two-thirds majority to pass.

"The Congressional leadership tried to open a loophole in the nation's toxic waste cleanup law and allow large polluters to escape the cost of cleaning up their toxic waste," said Ed Hopkins, Director of Sierra Club's Environmental Quality Program. "When industries threaten our families' health with toxic waste, taxpayers shouldn't be saddled with the clean-up costs -- Americans want the polluters to pay."

Creating a new loophole in the Superfund toxic waste law, the bill would have allowed thousands of businesses to escape responsibility for their pollution.

Additionally, twisting the nation's Superfund law in a dubious direction, the bill would have shifted the burden of proving toxic waste violations. Under the proposed change, the U.S. government would be forced to determine that a business was not exempt from the law because it shipped more than an allowable amount of weight. This reversal of U.S. law would place an enormous burden on government to investigate businesses' operations.

"By exempting some polluters from paying their fair costs of cleaning up toxic waste, this bill would have dumped the costs onto taxpayers or other businesses," said Hopkins. "And it would have eliminated an important incentive to curtail future pollution by removing the threat of enforcement."

Under the current federal Superfund cleanup program, the Environmental Protection Agency has established a program to reduce small-business liability for their contribution of waste to Superfund sites. According to the EPA, this program has given relief to more than 18,000 small businesses from the Superfund liability system to date.

The Sierra Club is asking people to call their Representatives and urge them to vote against other efforts to weaken toxic waste standards in the remaining budget bills.
