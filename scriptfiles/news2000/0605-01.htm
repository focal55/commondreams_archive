"Robin Hood Was Right"
Say Social Change Funding Activists
  

BOSTON - June 5 -

ROBIN HOOD WAS RIGHT
A Guide to Giving Your Money For Social Change
By Chuck Collins, Pam Rogers and Joan P. Garner

We’re hearing a lot about the “new philanthropy” of young high-tech entrepreneurs eager to use their newfound wealth for social good. The longest economic boom in American history--now nine years old--has fueled tremendous growth in the assets of private foundations and controversy over philanthropic priorities. Americans have just begun an unprecedented transfer of personal wealth, as older generations pass on to heirs an estimated $41 trillion to $136 trillion in wealth over the next half century.

At this historic juncture, a group of people who re-defined philanthropy two decades ago with the motto “Change, not Charity,” have written a provocative, story-packed guide showing how the “new philanthropists,” established foundations, and all kinds of donors can tackle the root causes of the problems we face, and not just treat the symptoms. ROBIN HOOD WAS RIGHT: A Guide to Giving Your Money for Social Change (W.W. Norton, March 2000, $27.95) makes a powerful case for channeling funds to fight the diseases of poverty, social injustice, environmental hazards and war with dramatic examples of successful efforts across the country.

As co-author Pam Rogers observed recently on CNN, “The payoff for social change giving is a better, more educated, safer community. Now who wouldn’t want that?” ROBIN HOOD WAS RIGHT co-authors Chuck Collins, Pam Rogers and Joan P. Garner have worked with foundations, organizations and individuals on the cutting edge of social change for many years. Collins was featured in the December 1999 issue of Fast Company magazine as one of “17 leaders, doers, and change makers--17 givers--who offer instruction and inspiration on giving back.”

The book is rooted in the experiences of the Funding Exchange and its network of fifteen community-based foundations across the United States. These visionary foundations provided the early money that helped important social change movements to grow and are nurturing new movements today--such as working to stop domestic violence before most people ever heard of a battered women’s shelter or working to end U.S. support for apartheid years before it was a popular cause, or providing crucial early support to anti-sweatshop and living wage campaigns that have evolved into the most important student-community-labor movement to emerge in decades.

Last year Americans gave more than $190 billion to charity. Only a small percentage of that generosity now goes to organizations working to make fundamental change. The authors of ROBIN HOOD propose a shift in that equation.

ROBIN HOOD WAS RIGHT is a unique how-to guide for giving, whether you are living paycheck to paycheck or a multimillionaire. The book is a nuts-and-bolts primer for taking control of your money, investing, understanding tax-wise giving, identifying effective organizations and making donations that are right for your wallet and your values.

“Robin Hood was right. Tipping the balance of resources to include more of humanity is an adventurous, thrilling, and worthwhile pursuit. Charity is good, but supporting and creating social change are about power…This book trumpets a vital message. No matter who we are, no matter how much money we have…we can bring change to the world around us.” --Alfre Woodard, from the Preface

About the Authors
Chuck Collins is the Co-Director of the Boston-based national organization United for a Fair Economy. He is the author of numerous books and articles and a frequent commentator on economic inequality, socially responsible investing and social change philanthropy. Joan P. Garner is the first Executive Director of the twelve-state Southern Partners Funds and lives in Atlanta. She believes that “whatever charges us emotionally is what motivates us to act.” Pam Rogers worked for 17 years with one of the oldest members of the Funding Exchange, the Haymarket People’s Fund in Boston. She has helped thousands of people put their money to work for their values across the United States.
