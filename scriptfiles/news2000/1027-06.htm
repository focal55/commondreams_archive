The Nation Magazine Urges Progressives to Vote for Gore or Nader
   NEW YORK - October 27 - The Nation Editors announce their editorial position on Election 2000 in "The Election and Beyond," in the Nov. 6 issue of The Nation.

"In another season, when our insurgent values have accumulated more momentum and self-confidence, we might see things differently," The Nation Editors write. "This time around, we believe the practical priority of keeping the Bush squad from winning power takes precedence, while we also urge that, if possible, progressives help Nader score a blow to the status quo."

The Editors urge progressives to vote for Al Gore in the states that are too close to call on Election Day. In states where either Gore or Bush has a commanding lead, they encourage voters to support Ralph Nader. They add, "In either case, the imperative is to end Republican control in Congress by electing Democrats, also vital to the prospects for progressive change." And, as an extension of "The Election and Beyond," The Nation has created an online poll watch, which aggregates state polling data daily to determine exactly which states are too close to call, available at www.thenation.com.

The resonance of Ralph Nader's voice has been one of the precious few bright spots of the campaign, far eclipsing Pat Buchanan's right-wing version of insurgency. If Nader draws enough voters to carry the Green Party over the 5 percent hurdle, that vehicle provides concrete opportunity.

However, The Nation Editors warn, there is greater urgency to preventing Bush from "capturing all three branches of government for the right-wing agenda." They argue, "The real argument for Gore is named Bush, and it's the most compelling case. The implications of a Bush victory are well understood," for the Supreme Court, reproductive freedom and public education, among other issues.

In urging progressives to support Gore, The Editors write, "The Democratic candidate is a smart and capable man who, at different points in his career, has displayed a forward-looking vision on great public problems."

"After the past two decades of loss and retreat," The Nation Editors explain "it takes nerve to sound so hopeful." Despite its disappointments, Election 2000 "might yet turn out to be the progressive moment-when we stopped backing up and started moving forward."

Established by abolitionists in 1865, The Nation is America's oldest weekly magazine and one of its premier journals of opinion.
