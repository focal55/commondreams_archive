Award-Winning Policy Watchdog Finds "Privatization" of Public Services Harms Communities
Southern citizens experience "Private Gain, Public Pain" in privatization efforts--new report could impact pending legislation in GA
  DURHAM, NC - February 9 - A highly respected research group, known for its analysis on civic issues, today released a study blasting the growing practice by states and local governments to "farm out" the delivery of critical social support services to private companies. The community-based report, as the first of its kind, studies the long-term social impact of privatization. "Rather than provide a quick fix, ‘privatization’ consistently inflicts harm across program areas," observes the Durham, NC-based Institute for Southern Studies.

Pointing to case studies of privatized public programs for healthcare, corrections, and support of poor children, the report, titled Private Gain, Public Pain, cites numerous examples where the intention of the contracts backfired calamitously onto an already strapped program and its most vulnerable participants. Since the private/public arrangement is disproportionately prevalent in the southern United States, the watchdog group focused on examples in Florida, Tennessee, and Mississippi.

Findings cited in the 32 page report include:

1. As corporations slash overhead to boost profit margins, poor quality services, fewer jobs with worse conditions, and loss of predicted cost savings occur. Meanwhile, problems in publicly funded programs continue and often are exacerbated.

2. To maximize profits, private providers often favor one group of clients or service recipients over others, limiting access by charging higher costs, or reducing the time and resources spent to serve each individual, resulting in a pattern of preferential treatment leaving the weakest most harmed.

3. By minimizing community input; shifting the focus away from community needs; and leaving communities uninformed, privatizing services degrades the public's ability to monitor services, be involved in decision-making, or otherwise hold an agency accountable.

"The results clearly indicate that members of a community are the true watchdogs in assuring that reforms take place," said ISS Research Director, Kim Diehl, one of the study’s authors. "Concerned citizens, community leaders, and public officials need to create better and more thoughtful alternatives for the improvement of public services," adds co-author Keith Ernst.

The report recommends:

  • Regulatory Oversight: Legislatively require procedures and criteria for performance review.

  • Set Standards: Require agencies to meet threshold criteria for cost savings, protections for the public and rights of workers. Require that companies demonstrate they have not violated environmental, labor, or other community standards

  • Community-based evaluation: Encourage public discussion, such as town meetings and an informed press to assess root causes of problems.

  • Increased community awareness: Educate the public and policymakers on problems by airing concerns via letters to editors, internet bulletin boards and contacting elected officials.

  • Target solutions: Identify and advocate for specific reforms.

  • Freedom of Information: Extend public disclosure requirements of government agencies to the corporations that take over public services.

The Institute for Southern Studies publishes the journal Southern Exposure, winner of the prestigious George Polk Award for courageous journalism, and of the National Magazine Award.
