Clinton's 2001 Budget Falls Short on Refugee Needs
   WASHINGTON - February 11 - In its FY 2001 budget, released Monday in Washington, the Clinton administration proposed a reduction in the number of refugee admissions to the United States and decreased assistance overseas in almost all regions of the world.

Responding to the budget announcement, Ralston Deffenbaugh, president of the Lutheran immigration and Refugee Service and chair of The Refugee Council USA, remarked, "In this era of unprecedented American prosperity, it is with deep regret that we find the Clinton administration proposing to reduce the numbers of those eligible for resettlement to the United States."

In the midst of America's unprecedented post-Cold War boom, a new global anarchy has spread, producing nearly 14 million refugees and more than 17 million internally displaced persons. Many of those driven from their homes will never be able to return; a small number of these are resettled annually in developed countries. The United States has long been the world leader in welcoming such refugees to its shores. But during the past eight years, refugee admissions have declined over 40 percent. Refugee agencies and congressional supporters have advocated returning admissions to their historic levels of more than 100,000 per year.

The juxtaposition of American affluence and spiraling global misery should spur the Administration to reassert U.S. leadership and re-energize its commitment to refugees and the displaced. Instead, a reduction in the number of admission signals a further retrenchment by our government. "The U.S. government can and should do better in meeting the world's grave humanitarian challenges," remarked Mark Franken, head of the U.S. Catholic Conference/Migration and Refugee Services. "This budget has us going in the wrong direction."

Last year Congress approved a reduction in the Migration and Refugee Assistance account. But that was in the context of a substantial supplemental bill that provided funds for refugees from Kosovo. This year there is no supplemental on the horizon. That means that once again the needs of millions of refugees living under harsh conditions worldwide will feel the effects of the funding shortfall.

In Europe, refugees in the Balkans continue to face daunting challenges of repatriation or permanent resettlement, and political tensions in Montenegro are cause for alarm. In Southeast Asia, secular violence and separatist movements will likely continue to produce refugees in Indonesia and Burma. In the Middle East, millions of refugees still cannot return home safely to Afghanistan, and progress toward permanent Arab-Israeli peace will ultimately require a solution to the region's refugee situation. In the Americas, intensified warfare in Colombia could produce sizeable new refugee populations, and Haiti remains politically fragile.

Africa poses the greatest need for improved refugee assistance funding. Twenty-two African countries have suffered armed conflict, serious social tensions or received significant new refugee populations in the past year. A murderous, almost 30-year war continues in Sudan, with 2 million dead and more than 4 million uprooted. Peacekeeping forces are being deployed in Sierra Leone, where more than 300,000 refugees and thousands of amputees await assistance and stability. A regional war threatens to re-ignite in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, where hundreds of thousands have already been displaced and pushed from their homes, many into neighboring countries. There are currently 10 million children orphaned by AIDS in Africa and 40 million are expected by the end of this decade.

"On a continent with 10 percent of the world's population, 50 percent of the world's wars, and an AIDS epidemic ravaging millions of lives, we simply must do better. This is only one vivid example of the many desperate refugee situations around the world which call for a more generous American humanitarian response," concluded Deffenbaugh.
