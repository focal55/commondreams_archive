Public Figures Sign Amnesty International USA Letter In Opposition to Imminent Execution of Gary Graham
   AUSTIN, TEXAS - June 19 - Bianca Jagger, member of Amnesty International USA's (AIUSA) Executive Director's Leadership Council, delivered a letter of support for Gary Graham to Governor George W. Bush's office this morning in an effort to further dialogue about Graham's case.

The letter, co-signed by AIUSA Executive Director Dr. William F. Schulz, implores the Governor and Gerald Garrett, Chairman of the Texas Board of Pardons & Paroles, to halt the execution of Gary Graham. Graham is scheduled for lethal injection this Thursday, June 22.

"Since 1973, 87 people have been released from death row following the emergence of evidence signifying their wrongful conviction," Jagger and Schulz wrote in the letter. "Contributing factors to these wrongful convictions include: inadequate legal representation, prosecutorial and police misconduct and the use of unreliable witnesses. Governor Bush, you stated that you were confident no innocent person had been executed in Texas. The case of Gary Graham raises grave concern that a possibly innocent person will be put to death." Numerous public figures added their names to the letter, including Reverend Jesse Jackson, Susan Sarandon and Tim Robbins, the Congressional Black Caucus, Edward Asner, former New York Governor Mario Cuomo and Mike Farrell.

The case of Gary Graham, also known as Shaka Sankofa, has received unprecedented media attention as officials, community leaders and members of the public learn of serious doubts about his guilt. Convicted of killing Bobby Lambert in 1981, Graham was denied the right to adequate legal representation at his trial and was convicted on the basis of one highly disputed eyewitness account. His original trial lawyer failed to present extensive exculpatory, forensic and ballistic evidence. Tests by the Houston Police Department showed that the bullets that killed Lambert did not match the gun found on Graham.

In addition to questions about Graham's guilt, Amnesty International is concerned about the execution of a juvenile offender. Graham was only 17 years of age at the time the crime was committed, making capital punishment a violation of international law. Only six countries have executed juvenile offenders in the last five years -- Nigeria, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Pakistan, the Democratic Republic of Congo and the US.

Twenty-one of the 47 executions that have occurred in the US this year took place in the state of Texas, which now accounts for 220 of the 645 prisoners put to death since the US resumed judicial killing in 1976.
