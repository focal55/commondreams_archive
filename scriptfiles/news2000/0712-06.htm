Wellstone: Missile Defense “Illusion” Made Clear by $100 Million Flop
Senator Would Require Realistic Testing, Independent Review, and Report to Congress
   WASHINGTON - July 12 - Saying last weekend’s failure of the National Missile Defense (NMD) system perfectly illustrates the folly of the U.S. military’s current plan, Senator Paul Wellstone today announced legislation requiring more realistic testing. Wellstone is co-sponsoring an amendment reconvening the independent Welch panel to evaluate the testing program, requiring more realistic testing, and requiring a report to Congress on the adequacy of the program.

“Today, national missile defense must be recognized for what it is, an illusion,” said Wellstone. “A decision to move forward with deployment of this system at this stage isn’t responsible policy making, it’s politics. A good test asks tough questions. This test doesn’t. It’s make believe. We need an honest testing of this system, so American taxpayers know if their money is being spent for a shield which will actually protect them and their families or is just a waste of money. A sensible national defense policy would make a more credible assessment of the threats against our nation, determine whether or not this system will be able to protect us from those threats, and decide what the effect of deploying the system will be on overall U.S. security interests.”

The Administration will soon decide whether to begin deploying NMD. This system is intended to defend the U.S. from limited attacks by intercontinental-range ballistic missiles armed with nuclear, chemical or biological weapons. To date, the Department of Defense has conducted three tests of the NMD system, with a test conducted last Saturday failing in every respect. One test initially characterized as a success was actually later revealed by Pentagon officials to have actually been a fluke. In that test, the interceptor hit its target only after a series of technical mistakes caused it to drift off course and initially picked up on a decoy balloon rather than the warhead.

Previously, the Administration requested that the Pentagon provide an estimate of whether a national missile defense system can be deployed in five years’ time. General Kadish, the head of the Pentagon’s ballistic-missile-defense program, has described the 2005 timetable as much faster than military planners would like. The recommendation of the Pentagon’s own Office of the Operational and Test Evaluation Program stated clearly that the deployment readiness review “is a strongly ‘schedule driven’ approach”, rather than one based on results. Just last month, the NMD independent review team headed by General Welch stated that current testing is limited to the point that it “impacts confidence in the validity of the system.” This same review team noted that advanced decoys are likely in the future and that the NMD program will need to develop a program to combat this “threat evolution.”

Wellstone also expressed concern that proceeding with a flawed NMD system at this time could seriously harm, rather than increase, overall U.S. national security. Russia has publicly stated that it would respond with “adequate measures” if the U.S. proceeds with deployment of NMD. Additionally, China has threatened to accelerate its own strategic arms build-up if the U.S. moves ahead with this system and has threathened to withdraw its promises to stop exporting dangerous defense technologies and will base its disarmament policies on the U.S. decision on NMD.

“Are we prepared to discard an arms control system that has kept the peace for over fifty years? Are we prepared to ignore the concerns of key allies like Germany, France and Japan? Are we prepared to undermine the deterrence capacity of other nuclear countries, including Russia and China? On the one hand, we have a proven system that places the assurances of retaliatory destruction within a framework of treaties. On the other hand, we have an unproven system that could provoke a new arms race. The stakes are too high for us to be foolish.”
