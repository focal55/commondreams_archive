Fourth of July Survey: Only 28 percent of Americans Believe Rich and Poor Have Equal Access to the Federal Courts
   NEW YORK - July 3 - A nationwide opinion poll of 1,014 households released for the Fourth of July holiday reveals that Americans view a number of their constitutionally guaranteed rights as being less than secure.

Most endangered? Equal access to the courts. According to the survey, fewer than 1 out of every 3 Americans (28 percent) believe that rich and poor people have equal access to the federal courts a right of access guaranteed by the U.S. Constitution.

"In this survey, Americans are saying it's a fact of life that they no longer have access to the federal judicial system," says Jeff Barge, a New York-based public relations consultant who commissioned the study. "Historians will look back and wonder why we allowed this right to be taken from us."

The random telephone poll of 1,015 American households was conducted by Opinion Research Corp. International. of Princeton, N.J. and has a margin of error of 3 percent.

Courts Get Failing Grade, But Other Rights Fare Better

While panning the poor job federal courts have done in guaranteeing equal access to the federal justice system, Americans feel much more sanguine about their other constitutional rights as they approach the first Fourth of July of the new millennium. Among other results:

-- 87 percent of those surveyed say that rich and poor have equal access to free expression of religion. But while 90 percent of those aged 65 and older felt this right was secure, only 80 percent of those aged 18-24 felt this to be true.

-- 83 percent feel that rich and poor have equal access to the right to vote. Little difference of opinion existed on this issue regardless of age, race or income.

-- 64 percent feel that rich and poor have equal access to the right to free speech. But only 58 percent of those in the 18-34 age group felt this to be the case, compared to 76 percent of those aged 65 and older.

-- 41 percent say they truly believe that rich and poor have equal access to the right to run for political office. But only 35 percent of those in the 18-34 age group feel this way, compared to 48 percent of those aged 55 and older. By race, 45 percent of Hispanics, 43 percent of whites and 32 percent of blacks felt equal access to run for political office exists.

-- 28 percent feel rich and poor have equal access to federal courts. By race, only 1 in 5 black Americans say they truly believe that rich and poor have equal access to the federal courts (20 percent), with only 28 percent of whites and 27 percent of Hispanics feeling that way. Among those aged 55-to-64 belief in equal access to the courts plummets to 23 percent, while a third of respondents aged 18-24 (33 percent) believe equal access exists.

Survey: Cost, Bias Deny Equal Judicial Access

According to the study, several factors appear to explain why the judicial system got the thumbs down when judged on the job it has done in protecting equal access to federal courts. Among the top factors is a perception of bias on the part of federal judges: 49 percent of those surveyed feel that federal judges are "at least occasionally biased" against the poor, while one out of three Americans (34 percent) feel that federal judges are "often" biased against the poor when making their decisions.

Many of those surveyed feel the judicial selection process has become politicized and biased. Forty- seven percent of those surveyed believe that federal judges are appointed nowadays because they have helped raise money for political parties or candidates; and 37 percent believe that most federal judges being appointed today are wealthy white people.

Forty six percent of those surveyed say that if they had a legal problem they would not have enough money to hire a lawyer. More than two out of every three Americans (68 percent) with annual incomes below $25,000 feel this way. But even the richest Americans feel locked out: One out of every three (34 percent) Americans with annual income exceeding $50,000 per year say they couldn't afford a lawyer if they needed one.

Overall, 80 percent of those surveyed believe that most people who bring lawsuits in federal courts without a lawyer will lose because the courts have become too complicated for the average person to understand.

View of Judicial System: Reality Based

Recent events in the news may account in part for the poor perception of the federal judiciary when it comes to protecting equal access. One of the highest profile judicial news stories in past year has been the decades-long refusal of Supreme Court justices to hire black or minority law clerks.

"Americans were stunned to see Supreme Court justices Souter and Rehnquist testify before Congress this year that it's impossible for them to find a single black law school graduate in the entire country qualified to work on their staffs," said Barge. "Someone has given the Supreme Court some very bad advice on this issue. This is not a problem of public relations, but of substantive discrimination."

Other factors influencing Americans' view of vanishing judicial equality include a study released in April 2000, "And Justice for Some," funded by several of the nation's leading foundations. The study says black youths convicted of drug offenses are 48 times more likely to be sentenced to juvenile prison as white youths. Overall, black youths with no record are six times as likely as whites to be imprisoned.

As to civil suits brought by the poor, a study by the Hastings School of Law in San Francisco of civil suits brought in federal court by poor people who can't afford attorneys showed that federal judges allow just one out of of 650 suits to go to trial -- or just 0.0015 percent. When Hastings School of Law asked the federal Judicial Conference for its assistance in determining the cause of this apparent bias, the assistance was refused. The Judicial Conference makes policy for the U.S. courts.
