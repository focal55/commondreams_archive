Estimated Million Americans Urge Protection of Wild Forests
  WASHINGTON - July 17 - The U.S. Forest Service asked the public for opinions on their proposal to protect the last wild areas of our National Forests, and American's weren't shy. With the comment period closing today, an estimated one million letters, emails and postcards are flooding the Forest Service offices. An unprecedented number of Americans have stood up to support remaining wild forests by urging a halt to logging and roadbuilding in roadless areas and calling for immediate protection for the Tongass National Forest in Alaska.

"In meetings across the nation, in letters and postcards and in newspaper editorials, Americans have sent President Clinton a very clear message-- fully protect our remaining wild forests from roads, logging and other destruction, and don't delay," said Debbie Sease, Sierra Club Legislative Director. "Americans want to save these treasured places where they love to hunt, hike, fish and camp. These unspoiled forests provide some of our nation's best wildlife habitat, and provide millions of Americans with clean drinking water."

Support for wild National Forests was abundant at hearings in places such as Atlanta, GA where 48 spoke in favor of greater protection, 18 spoke against; and Seattle, WA where 217 spoke in favor of greater protection, and only 2 spoke against. However, this support extended to smaller, rural communities as well, such as:

Hood River, OR: 45 spoke in favor of protecting roadless areas, only 3 spoke against the proposal. Lufkin, TX: 24 spoke for greater protection, 5 against in the home of the Texas Forestry Museum. Sitka, AK: 63 spoke for greater protection, 3 against. Seneca, WV: 33 spoke for greater protection, 6 against.

In addition to the outpouring at hearings, conservationists estimate that Americans have sent over one million letters, emails and postcards to the Forest Service. This unprecedented outpouring is nearly four times the previous record of 275,000, notched as part of the 1998 debate over organic food standards.

Editorial boards have joined the chorus:

"What little `roadless' area left - only about 12% of the state, a much smaller slice than most people think - must carry a huge responsibility for unspoiled hunting, clean water, wildlife and fish habitat, camping, hiking, spiritual renewal and other activities that require and demand distance from cars and snowmobiles and development."
--The Miles City Star (MT), February 28, 2000

"Should the nation's roadless forests remain open to future road construction to promote logging? The short-answer: No._The ban excludes the Tongass National Forest in Alaska-- a better solution would be to ban road building and to ban logging there as well."
--USA Today, June 26, 2000

"It makes sense to save the wilder parts of the National Forests from road construction_"
--The San Francisco Examiner, May 12, 2000

"The public should encourage the president to choose an alternative that includes protection of the Tongass as well as a ban on logging in unspoiled areas of public forest."
-- The Kansas City Star, May 14, 2000
