Supreme Court Orders New Review Of Alabama School Prayer Case
Americans United's Lynn Praises Court Action
   WASHINGTON - June 26 - The U.S. Supreme Court today ordered a lower federal court to reconsider its ruling permitting certain types of officially sanctioned "student-led" prayer in Alabama public schools.

Americans United for Separation of Church and State, which cosponsored the case along with the Alabama ACLU, hailed the court's action.

"Public schools are not Sunday schools, and students should not be compelled to participate in religious worship against their will," said the Rev. Barry W. Lynn, executive director of Americans United. "The federal appeals court clearly misconstrued the law, and we're glad the Supreme Court has intervened.

"This is a victory for church-state separation," Lynn continued. "We're confident that the days of coercive prayer in Alabama public schools are now numbered."

The case, Chandler v. Siegelman, was filed in 1996 by Michael Chandler and his son Jesse. The Chandlers objected to school-sanctioned Christian prayer, Bible distribution, religiously based student assemblies and other sectarian activities in DeKalb County public schools.

In the fall of 1997, U.S. District Judge Ira DeMent struck down the activities in the DeKalb schools, declaring that they violated church-state separation. However, portions of his ruling were later overturned by the U.S. 11th Circuit Court of Appeals and "student-led" religious activities were permitted.

Today the Supreme Court ordered the appellate court to take another look at its verdict. The high court said the Alabama ruling should be reconsidered in light of a decision June 19 in a separate case dealing with coercive "student-led" prayer at public school football games in Texas. The justices held that majority rule on prayer at Texas public schools is unconstitutional.

AU's Lynn said today's action by the Supreme Court in the Alabama case is a step in the right direction. "Promoting prayer, Bible reading and Christianity is not the job of the public schools," said Lynn. "We have churches for that."

Continued Lynn, "Public schools must serve students from many different religious backgrounds. To protect everyone's rights, it's important that the schools remain neutral on religious questions."

The high court's action in the Alabama case was another defeat for TV preacher Pat Robertson's American Center for Law and Justice (ACLJ). The ACLJ's Jay Sekulow helped defend the DeKalb County School District's prayer policies. (Sekulow also represented the losing side in the Texas football prayer case.)

Americans United is a religious liberty watchdog group based in Washington, D.C. Founded in 1947, the organization represents 60,000 members and allied houses of worship in all 50 states.
