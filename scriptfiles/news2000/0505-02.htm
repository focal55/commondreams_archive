The American Friends Service Committee Accepts Donations for Horn of Africa Relief
   PHILADELPHIA - May 5 - The American Friends Service Committee is accepting financial contributions for famine relief in the Horn of Africa. Millions of people are at risk of starvation in Ethiopia and the effects of this humanitarian crisis are already being felt in neighboring countries. An equally devastating famine has been reported in parts of Somalia, where wells and rivers have dried up and cholera is now spreading at an alarming rate by refugees migrating across the Ethiopian-Somali border.

United Nations and other international aid agencies, which have established feeding centers in parts of southern and eastern Ethiopia, report that approximately ten people--most of them children--die daily as families reach the feeding camps. Little is known about what is happening in the remote and inaccessible areas of Ethiopia and Somalia.

"The failure of rains so desperately needed has placed many communities at risk. There are all the indications of impending widespread famine throughout the region," said AFSC Somalia Program Director Abdikadir Hassan Mohamed.

Although the international community has responded with pledges for aid and food shipments to some regions, the scope of the famine is enormous. Many people from small villages are too weak to travel to the feeding centers. AFSC will provide relief and recovery assistance such as cholera medication, food and seeds for the next planting season to displaced populations.

Contributions marked Horn of Africa Relief can be sent to AFSC, 1501 Cherry St., Philadelphia, PA 19102 or by calling 1-888-588-2372.

AFSC began relief and development assistance in Somalia in 1982 in the wake of the Ogaden war and massive drought, in which more than one million people died and a quarter of the population became refugees. AFSC has stayed in Somalia--one of the world's poorest countries--throughout years of civil conflict, maintaining Somali staff and program activities in the country. The aim is to increase the socioeconomic security of ethnically and socially marginalized groups, who are particularly vulnerable in situations of conflict and political instability.

The American Friends Service Committee is a Quaker organization that includes people of various faiths who are committed to social justice, peace, and humanitarian service. Its work is based on the Quaker belief in the worth of every person and faith in the power of love to overcome violence and injustice.
