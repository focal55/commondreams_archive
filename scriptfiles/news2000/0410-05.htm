Sierra Club Announces Campaign to Block Trade Deal with China
  WASHINGTON - April 10 - Citing serious environmental concerns, the Sierra Club today announced opposition to permanent normal trade relations (NTR) with China. To persuade Congress to reject permanent NTR, the Sierra Club is launching a grassroots campaign in cooperation with the AFL-CIO.

"We are once again confronted by a trade agreement that fails to protect workers, their families and the environment," said Carl Pope, Executive Director of Sierra Club. "The United States must retain the leverage afforded by annual review of our commercial relationship with China in order to ensure fair trade, clean trade, and green trade. By fighting to improve U.S. trade policy toward China, the Sierra Club and AFL-CIO proudly continue our efforts working in unity to make trade clean, green and fair."

AFL-CIO President John Sweeney also praised the growing movement among environmentalists and labor to work together to improve U.S. trade policy.

"It's good news for working families that the Sierra Club is joining the campaign," said Sweeney. "We will be successful together because concerns about the impact of a corporate-driven global economy on our people and our planet are inseparable."

In the coming weeks, Sierra Club activists around the country will be mobilizing to pressure Congress to vote against permanent NTR. Some of these actions will be done jointly with the AFL-CIO. Additionally, the Sierra Club is sending a letter to members of Congress which reads, in part: "Regrettably, the WTO/permanent NTR package fails to address impacts on working families and the environment. Congress should reject permanent NTR with China as the minimum step toward preserving its leverage in the fight for socially just and environmentally sustainable trade policies."

Several concerns have led to the Sierra Club's opposition to permanent NTR for China:

* Human Rights and the Environment -- Chinese environmental activists are already targets of abuse. Such abuse could grow as China seeks competitive advantage in international markets. By rejecting permanent NTR, Congress could oppose human rights abuses through its annual review process.

* WTO Reform -- Given China's political system, it is likely to oppose democratic reform of the World Trade Organization. By rejecting permanent normal trade relations, Congress will retain leverage with China in pressing for WTO reforms.

* Endangered Species -- China's illegal trade in animal parts now threatens Asian tigers, African rhinos, and North American black bears. Increased trade flows under permanent NTR and entry into the WTO could increase traffic in contraband animal parts.
