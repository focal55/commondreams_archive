Wellstone Blasts GOP for Sinking $5 Billion Affordable Housing Measure
Despite Booming Economy, Millions Cannot Afford Housing: Republicans Unmoved
   WASHINGTON - July 14 - Responding to the housing crisis in Minnesota and throughout the nation, Senator Wellstone today co-sponsored an amendment to the Estate Tax Elimination Act that would commit $5 billion for the production of affordable housing. Republicans unfortunately defeated the measure by a vote of 52-45 while they pushed onward with huge tax cuts targeted exclusively to the top 2% of wealthiest Americans. In an attempt to increase and preserve the stock of housing affordable to millions of low-income families in the United States, the Wellstone amendment sought to provide funds to increase the production of affordable housing through state matching grants and HUD competitive grants.

“Housing is one of the most critical needs this nation faces, but Senate Republicans choose to ignore it. Instead, they fight on to help their powerful friends; the privileged and the special few,” Wellstone said. “Here in Washington we congratulate ourselves daily on the good economic times. Yet, in 1997, 5.4 million households with 12.3 million people paid more than one half of their income in rent or lived in seriously substandard housing. Who are these 12.3 million people? 1.5 million are elderly persons, 4.3 million are children and between 1.1 and 1.4 million are adults with disabilities. We must make a commitment to address the shortage of affordable housing in this country, and I will not give up.”

Over the past five years, more than $20 billion has either been rescinded or diverted from federal housing programs while the number of Americans who cannot afford decent housing continues to rise. A July 1998 study by the Family Housing Fund reported that there are 68,900 renters with incomes below $10,000 in Minneapolis-St. Paul and only 31,200 housing units with rents affordable to those families.

“In my state of Minnesota, a family must earn $11.54 an hour, 40 hours a week, 52 weeks out of the year to afford a fair market rent for a two-bedroom apartment. That’s more than double the minimum wage. In fact, to afford a two-bedroom apartment at minimum wage, families must work at least 88 hours a week. We can afford to do better. I know we can afford to solve this problem.”

The amendment, introduced by Senator John Kerry, would have placed $5 billion in a Trust Fund to be made available for state matching grants and to the United States Department of Housing and Urban Development to award competitive grants to intermediaries, such as large, national non-profit organizations, who will then be required to leverage private funds.
