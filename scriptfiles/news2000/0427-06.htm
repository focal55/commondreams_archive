Humane Society Lauds Babbitt, National Park Service for Ban on Recreational Snowmobiling
   WASHINGTON - April 27 - The Humane Society of the United States (HSUS) with more than 7.3 million members and constituents, commends U.S. Secretary of Interior Bruce Babbitt and the National Park Service for the decision announced today to ban the recreational use of snowmobiles in national parks.

"The national parks are too valuable to all Americans and to the wild creatures that inhabit them to allow them to be despoiled and degraded by the clamor and fumes of hordes of snowmobiles," said Paul G. Irwin, HSUS President and CEO. "We thank Secretary Babbitt and the National Park Service for their courageous and far-sighted decision and know that the parks will greatly benefit from this action."

The harmful impacts of smowmobiles on wildlife, air quality and the experience of park visitors are so well-demonstrated and so serious, that the prohibition is more than justifiable. In Yellowstone National Park alone, it is reported that the number of annual snowmobilers has risen dramatically from about 27,327 in the late 1970's to 78,765 in 1998 (source: The Citizens' Solution for Winter Access to Yellowstone). Inefficient 2-stroke engines on snowmobiles emit air pollutants at levels so high that they would be unhealthy and unacceptable even in the downtown areas of major cities, much less be tolerated in national parks.

The HSUS believes that if the National Park Service is to fulfil its legal mandate to "conserve the scenery and the natural and historic objects and the wildlife therein and to provide for the enjoyment of the same in such manner and by such means as will leave them unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations," then no other course of action is possible.

"The decision to ban recreational use of snowmobiles is critical toward protecting the natural environments of our national parks," said Dr. John Grandy, HSUS Senior Vice President of Wildlife Programs. "Without the noise and pollutants from the vehicles, wildlife can live in peace and visitors can enjoy the parks as they were meant to be experienced."
