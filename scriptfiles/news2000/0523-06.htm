Environmental Working Group Criticizes China for Toxic Exports to United States
  WASHINGTON - May 23 - A national environmental group criticized China today for permitting the export of a highly toxic material that has been used in feed and fertilizer in the United States, and demanded that Chinese exporters take back the material. So far, they have refused to do so.

According to internal government memos that were leaked to the Environmental Working Group (EWG), both federal and state authorities are searching nationwide for products that may have been made using the toxic, imported Chinese material. The contaminant is cadmium, a heavy metal known to cause cancer and other health problems. The cadmium was detected at extremely high levels in shipments of zinc sulfate from China, a material used to boost the nutritional value of animal feeds and farm and garden fertilizers.

"This is very dangerous material to anyone exposed to the product, from workers in the fertilizer and animal feed industry to children playing in a garden where it may have been used," said Todd Hettenbach, an analyst with EWG who has published a number of reports on toxic chemicals in fertilizers. "Cadmium can cause lung cancer and kidney disease, and the reported contamination levels in these Chinese shipments are very, very high," Hettenbach said.

"This stuff could turn a vegetable garden into a Superfund site," he added. "China must take this material back."

The unpublicized government investigation has found that "as much as 1.3 million pounds (of contaminated material) entered the U.S. at 10 different ports since the first contaminated shipment was detected in November, 1999," according to one Environmental Protection Agency memo. "With customs information we have identified 14 importers that have received shipments of suspect zinc sulfate."

The cadmium-laced zinc sulfate has turned up so far in California, Oregon, Washington and Mississippi. Chinese exporters have refused to take the material back.

EWG, an environmental research organization, has conducted a number of studies on the risks of toxic chemicals in both farm and garden fertilizer products.
