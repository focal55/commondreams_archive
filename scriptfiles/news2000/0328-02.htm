National Coalition Seeks Better Post Office Planning to Fight Sprawl
   WASHINGTON - March 28 - The American Planning Association is leading a coalition of national organizations in creating the Community Post Office Partnership Web site, located on the internet at www.stampoutsprawl.org.

With more than 40,000 facilities nationwide, the U.S. Postal Service is the most visible federal institution in America's communities. Yet, the Postal Service has chosen not to abide by local planning, citizen participation, zoning, land-use, historic preservation and environmental standards that apply to all other businesses.

The Web site was launched on Monday, March 27. A main objective of the site is to create increased pressure on Congress for action on H.R. 670 and S. 556, bills that would force the postal service to consult with the local citizens and comply with community regulations and plans when closing or relocating a post office. The bill has more than 230 co-sponsors in the House of Representatives, but no final action has yet been taken. Chief authors of the legislation, Rep. Blumenauer (D-OR) and Sen. Jim Jeffords (R-VT) and Sen. Max Baucus (D-MT) will call for action in June.

"Until today, communities have been beholden to the whims of the Postal Service," said Rep. Blumenauer, "As the Internet connects the growing national movement, this website will provide a huge boost for the Post Office Community Partnership Act."

All too often the Postal Service creates a blueprint for sprawl by closing existing locations in favor of new facilities on the edge of town. Post offices in small towns and city centers are vital to ensuring the continued vibrancy of downtown businesses and maintaining the unique character of the community. A 1993 study by the National Trust for Historic Preservation found that 80 percent of people who shopped downtown planned their trip around a visit to the post office.

The site offers case studies of communities affected by post office closings or relocations such as Tallahassee, FL; Portland, OR; Ball Ground, GA; Pittsboro, NC; Christiansburg, VA; and Livingston, MT. Other features include resources for citizens and communities struggling with this issue, interactive forms for sharing information about what's happening locally, direct links to e-mail Representatives and Senators, and online subscription for e-mail updates on this issue.

The Web site is part of a new approach to legislative advocacy -- the use of the Internet to focus on a key issue and pending legislation, while providing citizens with a direct link to information and lawmakers. Organizations joining APA in this effort include the National Trust for Historic Preservation, National League of Cities, International Downtown Association, and Preservation Action.

"Through the world wide web," said Rep. Blumenauer, "the magnitude of the problem and the scope of potential solutions to it will become clear. We will bring citizens directly into the democratic process, in numbers we could never have been able to reach before."

In addition to partner organizations, the post office legislation being promoted by the website has been endorsed by the Bike Federation of America, National Association of Counties, National Alliance of Preservation Commissions, National Association of Home Builders, National Association of Postmasters of the U.S., National Conference of State Historic Preservation Officers, Sierra Club and the U.S. Conference of Mayors.
