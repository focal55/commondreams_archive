Wellstone Leads Fight Against Quick China PNTR Vote in Senate
  

WASHINGTON - March 8 - On the day the President sent legislation to Congress to cement Permanent Normal Trade Relations (PNTR) status for China, Senator Paul Wellstone greeted the move with a warning to his Senate colleagues not to rush to judgment on a matter of such importance. Wellstone sent a letter to his colleagues arguing that the legislation granting PNTR for China is much too important to be rushed through the Senate. Wellstone cited a litany of concerns prompted by the prospect of granting PNTR status for China including, Chinas deteriorating record on human rights, a race to the bottom of labor and environmental standards, recent statements made by the Chinese government toward Taiwan, and an increase in our bilateral trade deficit. Proponents of granting China PNTR have suggested that a quick debate and Senate vote early this spring in favor of PNTR could give the legislation needed momentum going into a much tougher battle in the House of Representatives.

This debate will bring to the fore the central issues of our role in the global economy, said Wellstone. It ought not be treated lightly. There are a whole set of issues dealing with human rights and environmental and labor standards that had, until the recent events in Seattle, been off the radar screen in this country and the Congress. People in the country have real worries that this deal may hurt them more than it will help. What Seattle showed was that there is increasing concern in our country about the costs of globalization. More people see the importance of maintaining basic labor and environmental standards. For the U.S. Senate to give these topics short shrift would be irresponsible. These are the central economic issues of our time and we ought to have a full debate.

The letter, sent today by Wellstone and Senator Ernest Hollings (D-S.C.), notes a list of issues that raise troubling questions about whether or not it is wise to grant PNTR status to China. The letter states the following concerns: The U.S. can obtain most, if not all, the benefits of Chinas WTO accession without granting PNTR Chinas accession agreement will increase our bilateral trade deficit Chinas record on human rights has deteriorated Mainland China has made troubling threats against Taiwan Encouraging the export of more jobs to China would put downward pressure on global labor and environmental standards. The confidential WTO accession agreement with China has not been declassified and the public and news media have not had adequate opportunity to examine it closely.
