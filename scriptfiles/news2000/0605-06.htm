Physicians Group Disappointed as U.S., Russia Ignore Disarmament Agenda at Summit
   WASHINGTON - June 5 - Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR) today expressed deep disappointment at the lack of progress towards disarmament at the U.S.-Russia Summit in Moscow.

"Presidents Clinton and Putin had a great chance at this meeting to negotiate deep cuts in nuclear weapons," said Anne Gallivan, "They've blown that chance."

The two Presidents have agreed on two limited accords, one for the removal of 64 tons of weapons plutonium from stockpiles, and the second for the opening of a joint early warning center in Moscow to monitor global missile launches. These are necessary steps, but not new.

The early warning center was agreed in principle at the Clinton-Yeltsin Summit in Helsinki in 1997, and was originally due to be open by early 1999. The enter will help cover gaps in the decaying Russian early warning capability by monitoring global missile launches. However, this stop-gap solution is far less effective than simply removing all missiles from their current, highly dangerous status of hair-trigger alert.

"If the U.S. and Russia could simply agree to back away from the brink of nuclear disaster, reducing the alert status of their missiles and thereby the risk of accidental nuclear war, this new center would be irrelevant," said Kimberly Roberts, PSR program associate.

The agreement to remove plutonium from the stockpiles is a welcome move, but does little more than confirm implementation of previous policies. Furthermore, if the plutonium is to be converted into Mixed Oxide (MOX) fuel for nuclear reactors, it may contribute to proliferation danger by spreading plutonium around the globe -- even in this adulterated form it could potentially be used for a crude nuclear weapon.

At this Summit, the two Presidents completely ignored the agenda for progress in disarmament set forward at the Non-Proliferation Treaty Review Conference on May 19. Both the U.S. and Russia agreed there to undertake measures including:

-- Early entry into force and implementation of START II
-- Negotiation of START III
-- Strengthening the ABM Treaty
-- Early Entry into Force and Implementation of the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty
-- Concrete measures for reducing alert status of nuclear forces
-- Increased transparency by nuclear states
-- Unilateral nuclear cuts to complement treaty cuts

"It is deeply disappointing that a comprehensive disarmament agenda, agreed to by 187 nations including the U.S. and Russia only three weeks ago, has been swept under the rug, "said Dr. Peter Wilk, president of PSR. "The world expected the two Presidents to do their duty to make the world safer, and instead they chose to eat a nice dinner and ignore the risks of nuclear war."
