Virginia Supreme Court Hears Argument In Dispute Over State Aid For Pat Robertson's Regent University
Mega-Bucks TV Preacher Is Seeking Government Help For Religious School With Endowment Of Over $276 Million
   WASHINGTON - June 6 - The Virginia Supreme Court heard oral arguments today in a closely watched case that centers on whether the state can issue revenue bonds on behalf of TV preacher Pat Robertson's Regent University. On one side of the Virginia College Building Authority v. Barry Lynn, et al case are the multi-millionaire religious broadcaster and his state government allies; on the other side are advocates of church-state separation. The conflict is considered a major showdown over the line between religion and government.

Background

Regent University, a graduate-level university in Virginia Beach founded by Robertson in 1977, has applied for $55 million in revenue bonds from the Virginia College Building Authority. Although the state would not be liable in the event of a default, government issuance of the bonds would save Regent about $30 million in interest over the 30-year life of the loan.

Opponents charge that Regent is ineligible for the bonds because the school promotes its fundamentalist religious viewpoint in hiring, admissions and curriculum. (In legal terms, the school is "pervasively sectarian.")

Americans United for Separation of Church and State and the Virginia affiliate of the American Civil Liberties Union filed suit to block the bond issue. The lead plaintiff in the case is the Rev. Barry W. Lynn, executive director of Americans United and a Virginia resident.

Lynn is one of the nation's leading advocates of church-state separation and a long-time critic of the Religious Right's efforts to combine government and religion. A McLean, Va., resident, he is both an attorney and an ordained minister in the United Church of Christ.

Americans United Comment

"Pat Robertson is trying to force the citizens of Virginia to support his ministry," said AU Executive Director Lynn. "I am confident the state supreme court will not let that happen.

"Robertson and his religious school both have millions of dollars sitting in the bank," Lynn continued. "He's got some nerve asking the state for a handout."

Legal Issues

In addition to the U.S. Constitution prohibition on public aid to religion, several sections of the Virginia Constitution bar government support for sectarian schools.

Last July, Richmond Circuit Judge Randall G. Johnson ruled against the Regent bond request, holding that the school is thoroughly religious. Documents submitted to the court demonstrated that the school imposes religious requirements on faculty, requires prospective students to submit a clergy recommendation and states upfront that the school's "ultimate purpose is glorifying God and His Son Jesus Christ."

In a similar case from 1991, the Virginia Supreme Court ruled unanimously that industrial development bonds could not be granted to Liberty University, a fundamentalist Christian institution in Lynchburg founded by the Rev. Jerry Falwell. Americans United sponsored that case, Habel v. Industrial Development Authority of the City of Lynchburg.  The group argues that the facts in the Regent dispute are essentially the same.

Regent U.: Big Money, Big Plans

Robertson wants to use the bond money to pay for a new satellite campus in Alexandria, Va., and to fund the construction of a new events center and student housing at the main campus. The university offers 20 graduate degrees through its schools of education, law, government, business, communications, divinity and counseling, and Robertson has talked about using its graduates to bring his sectarian views into those professions.

Ironically, the school is hardly in need of state assistance. In 1997, Regent had an endowment of over $276 million, a huge sum that places the university in the top 100 of college endowments. Since the school has an enrollment of just over 1,400 on the main campus, that means an endowment of nearly $200,000 per student. (A large part of the endowment was received in 1997 when Robertson gave the school $147.5 million in proceeds from the sale to Rupert Murdoch of the Family Channel, a commercial spin-off of his tax-exempt Christian Broadcasting Network.)

Robertson's Power

In recent years, Robertson has greatly stepped up his political activism in Virginia. He has donated large sums to Republican campaigns in Virginia and has even taken the credit for the GOP takeover of the Virginia legislature. This case could be an interesting test of Robertson's influence in the state.
