President Clinton Nominates Choice Of McConnell, Lott To FEC
Mr. Smith Should Not Come To Washington, Reformers Say
  

WASHINGTON - February 16 - "Mr. Smith's writings make plain that his agenda as a commission member would be a further dismantling of reasonable campaign limits intended to curb the corrupting influence of big money rather than serious enforcement of current campaign finance laws." - The New York Times

President Clinton has nominated Bradley A. Smith, Professor of Law at Capital University in Ohio, to serve on the Federal Election Commission (FEC). Smith, the hand-picked choice of the Senate's most extreme opponents of campaign finance reform, Majority Leader Trent Lott (R-MS) and Senator Mitch McConnell (R-KY), has written extensively that the whole corpus of federal election law is unconstitutional and should be repealed. The FEC is the body charged with enforcing these laws.

The saga over the Smith nomination began in the summer of 1999, when Smith's name was first floated as a potential nominee. Common Cause, the Brennan Center for Justice, and Democracy 21 waged a campaign to put pressure on President Clinton not to forward Smith's name for the vacancy created by outgoing Commissioner Lee Ann Elliott. While historically the Commission is made up of three Democrats and three Republicans, there is precedent for the President to refuse to nominate the choice of the congressional leadership of the opposite party. In 1985, for instance, President Ronald Reagan refused to appoint Thomas Harris, the choice of congressional Democrats, to the FEC.

In July, press reports indicated that Senate Republicans had linked the Smith nomination to the nomination of Richard Holbrooke to be Ambassador to the United Nations, by placing a "hold" on Holbrooke. Although the hold was eventually dropped and Holbrooke was confirmed, President Clinton did not immediately advance the Smith nomination. Recent news reports indicate the Smith nomination was made in exchange for progress in confirming judicial appointments that had been held up in the Senate.

SMITH OPPOSES LAWS HE WOULD ENFORCE

Professor Smith has written that most of the nation's campaign finance laws are unconstitutional. In a 1997 Wall Street Journal op-ed entitled "Why Campaign Finance Reform Never Works," Smith wrote that "the most sensible reform is a simple one: repeal of the Federal Election Campaign Act." The Federal Election Campaign Act, or FECA, is the body of law which sets out the limits on political contributions enacted in the wake of the Watergate scandal. This law, Smith has written, is "profoundly undemocratic and profoundly at odds with the First Amendment."

Such sentiments, say reformers, make it unlikely that Smith would meet even the minimum qualifications for Commissioner as described in the statute, which cite "impartiality" as a basis for selecting Commissioners.

"Any member of a federal regulatory agency should, at a minimum, believe in the mission of that agency, the laws to be administered by the agency, and the constitutionality of those laws," reform groups wrote to President Clinton in June. "Mr. Smith is in no way 'impartial' about the campaign finance laws. He does not believe in them."

"At a time when the idea that we need to reduce the influence of big money in our elections and our government is gaining ground, President Clinton is nominating someone to the FEC who has complete and utter hostility to the laws he would be charged with enforcing," Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger said when the nomination appeared imminent.

Smith's views seem more extreme to reformers in light of the Supreme Court's recent decision in Nixon v. Shrink Missouri Government PAC, which upheld the constitutionality of spending limits that are at the core of FECA. The Court explicitly pointed to the corruption-preventing properties of contribution limits, warning against the "broader threat of politicians too compliant with the wishes of large contributors." But Smith does not even concede there is a problem, writing in the Georgetown Law Journal that "money's alleged corrupting effects are far from proven."

Smith told Roll Call that "the reason most" why he agreed to accept the nomination is to "present the case that there's another way to talk about reform than reform being equivalent to more regulation." That, according to Common Cause, is the wrong reason to accept a high-ranking position at an enforcement agency.

"The FEC isn't some kind of debating society," Harshbarger said. "This is an agency which is supposed to administer and enforce the laws already on the books."

REFORMERS PLEDGE TO FIGHT NOMINATION

Because of the controversy surrounding Smith's views, Smith's main proponent in the Senate, Senator McConnell, appears as though he will try to ram the nomination through the Rules Committee and on to the full Senate, according to Common Cause. Groups like Common Cause, Democracy 21, and the Brennan Center for Justice are pushing the Senate Rules Committee to hold full hearings on the nomination, and to allow outside groups to testify.

Although Senate Republicans will likely choose to avoid the scrutiny that full confirmation hearings would bring on Smith and his views, Smith himself has said he does want the opportunity to air his views in open hearings. "Obviously, I'm pleased, and I'm looking forward to the confirmation hearings," Smith told the Columbus Dispatch on February 10. "I've taken a lot of whacks, sort of unanswered in the press over the last year," Smith continued. "And since most of these whacks have come from people who have never read my stuff, have never seen me, and are just reprinting Common Cause press releases, I look forward to actually being able to appear as myself."

Common Cause has pledged to fight Smith's confirmation in the Senate. "Any Senator who claims to support campaign finance reform cannot support this disastrous nomination," Harshbarger said. "A vote for Brad Smith would be a vote to return to the dark days of Watergate, when there was zero regulation of campaign contributions - because Professor Smith wants to completely discard the laws in this area."

"We also do not believe that judicial nominations should be held hostage and linked with the Smith nomination," said Harshbarger.

A simple majority is required to confirm Presidential nominees in the Senate. Last year,52 Senators voted for the McCain- Feingold campaign finance reform bill - all 45 Democrats, and Republican Senators Susan Collins (ME), James Jeffords (VT), John McCain (AZ), Olympia Snowe (ME), Arlen Specter (PA), Fred Thompson (TN), and the late Senator John Chafee (RI). On a separate cloture vote on a slightly different version of the bill - a bill that only included a soft money ban - reformers picked up the votes of GOP Senators Sam Brownback (KS), Tim Hutchinson (AR), and William Roth (DE).

"Fifty-four current Senators are on record for strengthening our campaign finance laws," Harshbarger said. "A vote for Smith is even more than a vote against campaign finance reform. Voting for the Smith nomination would mean eviscerating even the most basic campaign finance laws already on the books."
