NY City Council to Save Endangered Forests
  NEW YORK - June 15 - Sierra Club Executive Director, Carl Pope, New York City Council Speaker, Peter Vallone and New York City Council Member, A. Gifford Miller announced today the introduction of landmark legislation into the City Council that will protect endangered forests worldwide. The bill, which would apply to all City projects, will curb the use of wood from globally threatened forests and provide incentives to purchase wood certified by the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) and wood alternatives. If passed, New York will be the first major city to shift to green timber.

"New York City is one of the largest municipal wood users in the country and much of this wood comes from the most rare and important ancient forests in the world," said Carl Pope, Sierra Club Executive Director. Currently, New York City uses large quantities of tropical rainforest timber in projects ranging from the Coney Island boardwalk, to park benches in City Parks. Temperate rainforest wood from the coast of British Columbia was used recently in the construction of a bikeway/walkway in Riverside Park.

With forests from the US Northeast to the temperate rainforests of Canada under siege from destructive logging practices, the Sierra Club and other environmental groups are increasingly looking to consumer power to save forests. In the wake of protests from the Sierra Club and other groups, Home Depot announced last August that it would stop purchasing old-growth and switch to FSC certified wood. New York City, with the largest municipal budget in the country, is the next step in this effort to harness the market to save forests.

"New Yorkers are shocked when they discover that many of the city's park benches, boardwalks and other structures are made from wood cut down from rainforests," said Council Member Gifford Miller. "When so many alternatives exist, there is simply no reason why New York City should be contributing to global deforestation," he continued. "This legislation puts New York City in the front of a growing consumer trend of using environmentally friendly building materials," he added.

"If Home Depot can do it, New York City can too," said Peter Vallone, Speaker of the New York City Council. "We have a real opportunity to set a national example, support the growing market for eco-certified wood and to get other cities to follow suit," he added. "And, with New York State recently certifying 700,000 acres of its state lands, we will be closing the loop and supporting forestry jobs right here in New York State," he said.

### The Sierra Club is the nation's oldest and largest grassroots environmental organization, with 17,000 members in New York City and over 600,000 members nationwide.
