Women's Issues Expert, Informed by Important New Poll, Available for Comment on Presidential Debates
  WASHINGTON - October 3 - Linda Tarr-Whelan, president of the Center for Policy Alternatives, is an active media source and has appeared and debated on numerous news programs, including ABC World News Tonight, CNN Financial News, and Fox News. She has been quoted in the New York Times, the Washington Post, the Boston Globe, Chicago Tribune, and media outlets across the country as a leading source on women, politics and the economy.

In the most in-depth and thorough analysis of all women and women voters in the 2000 presidential election season, the Center for Policy Alternatives and Lifetime Television released results last week from a national poll that describe a complex picture of American women's priorities and values, where feelings of economic optimism are tempered by unmet expectations of the possibilities of a good economy. A full copy of the report can be downloaded at www.stateaction.org. The Women's Voices report also includes 10 Key Points to Understand about Women in the 2000 Elections.

Highlights show:

-- Women see opportunity rather than instability in a time of economic change, but many women say the economic boom has not reached them.

-- Women urgently and consistently placed juggling time and family and equal pay and benefits as the most dominant economic concerns in their lives.

-- There has been a shift in women's support for the role government can play in partnering with them to find solutions for their concerns, with nearly 60 percent of women saying government can and should help.

-- Concern about moral values continues to top the list and women and men continue to believe by large margins that parents having more time with their children is the preferred solution.

"Control" and "flexibility" are women's watchwords for dealing with economic change regardless of their income, marital status, race, party or generation," said Linda Tarr-Whelan, president of the Center for Policy Alternatives. "Equal pay with benefits and flexibility to juggle family and work have been remarkably constant as women's top concerns over the last three election cycles even though the country has moved from a bad economy to a good economy. Women will determine the outcome of the election. How the candidates choose to listen and respond to women's voices with meaningful solutions will have important consequences."

Women's Voices 2000 found that class differences, now particularly driven by education and marital status, are emerging as a key determinant of a woman's economic outlook. This national research project, which began with a series of racially and economically diverse focus groups to listen to the voices of women and the national poll released last week was conducted by the bi-partisan and multi-cultural team of Celinda Lake, Linda DiVall, Linda Williams, and Anna Rivera. The poll consisted of a sample of 800 women of all races and ages and 400 men; with over-sampling of Latinas, African-American and Asian-American women. Women's Voices was first conducted in 1992 and is the only comprehensive initiative of women's views that has been done in subsequent presidential elections, this being the third.
