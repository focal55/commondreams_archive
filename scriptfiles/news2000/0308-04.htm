Investigation of Cruise Industry Good First Step, Says Center for Marine Conservation
   WASHINGTON - March 8 - Today the Center for Marine Conservation (CMC) applauded a survey of the cruise line industry by the General Accounting Office (GAO) for the House of Representatives' Committees on Commerce and Government Reform. The GAO undertook the study to look at the status of the cruise ship industry in light of recent illegal discharge violations.

"The GAO survey of the cruise line industry is just the first step to address the big problem confronting sensitive ocean and coastal resources from this fast-growing industry," said Roger Rufe, president of CMC. "Without additional funding for Coast Guard monitoring, removal of current MARPOL exemptions for select government vessels, and increased penalties for illegal discharge violations, our oceans are not being protected adequately."

Currently, the U.S. Coast Guard has the responsibility for enforcing compliance with the standards for MARPOL and the Clean Water Act. Unfortunately during the years that the GAO report covers, the industry grew by almost 50 percent and Congress significantly reduced the funding available to monitor compliance.

"The cruise industry has made significant improvements in how it handles ship-generated wastes," said Rufe. "But with inadequate funding for monitoring, how can we expect our Coast Guard to detect all the violations? We cannot depend on violations caught by chance -- that is unacceptable."

In addition to the increasingly difficult task of monitoring the cruise line industry for violations, MARPOL standards are not unilaterally applied to government vessels. The MARPOL exemptions to the U.S. Navy and other government vessels need to be lifted so that we can ensure all U.S. ships will not harm the marine environment and are held to the same high standards.

"Just because a ship belongs to the U.S. government does not mean that its discharges are less harmful than discharges from cruise ships," said Rufe. "We want zero tolerance for pollution from any ship -- the U.S. government should not be exempt."

The GAO report also failed to review current monetary penalties for MARPOL violations. Many fines are so low that it is sometimes more cost effective for ship owners to take their chances on being caught rather than making necessary changes in equipment and legally disposing waste on shore.

"The GAO tackled some very difficult issues related to the safety and environmental impact of the cruise line industry," concluded Rufe. "Now it's time to think about how to effectively monitor and regulate this largely unregulated industry, an industry that is growing by leaps and bounds."

The Center for Marine Conservation is the largest national nonprofit organization committed solely to protecting ocean environments and conserving the global abundance and diversity of marine life through science-based advocacy, research, and public education, as well as informed citizen participation. Headquartered in Washington, D.C., CMC has regional offices in California, Florida, and Virginia and field offices in Alaska, Maine, Santa Barbara and Santa Cruz, Calif., and the Florida Keys.
