San Diego Rights Groups Demand Explanation for Police Fatal Shootings
   SAN DIEGO - May 23 - Four of San Diego's leading civil rights organizations have asked Mayor Susan Golding to explain why San Diego was ranked fourth in the nation in fatal police shootings in a recent study of FBI statistics released by the Detroit Free Press on May 15. In a letter sent to Mayor Golding today, the heads of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP), Earl B. Gilliam Bar Association, and La Raza Lawyers urge the Mayor to direct the City Manager to explain the apparent contradiction between this recent report and the San Diego Police Department's own statements that San Diego has relatively few shootings as compared to other cities. However, according to the FBI statistics, San Diego's rate of fatal police shootings over the past decade exceeds that of both Los Angeles and New York, cities whose police practices have been severely criticized.

"This is a distinction none of us in San Diego welcome," said ACLU Executive Director Linda Hills. "The community is entitled to an honest assessment of how San Diego compares to other cities when it comes to police use of deadly force."

In February, 2000, the San Diego City Council held a special workshop following the fatal shooting of William Miller, a homeless man who brandished a stick at officers. At that workshop, police officials touted San Diego's ranking as second lowest in the nation for police shootings in the 1997-99 period.

The letter to Mayor Golding follows. The Detroit Free Press article is available at freep.com/news/statewire/sw12416_20000515.htm. A chart of the FBI statistics prepared by the Detroit Free Press is available on request via fax.

Mayor Susan Golding
City of San Diego
202 C St.
San Diego, CA 92101

Dear Mayor Golding:

We are writing to express concern about the fact that San Diego is ranked fourth in the nation in fatal police shootings. This fact recently came to light in a study of FBI statistics conducted by the Detroit Free Press, which is enclosed. According to this report, San Diego's rate of fatal police shootings exceeds that of both Los Angeles and New York, whose police practices have been severely criticized in recent months. This is a distinction that none of us in San Diego welcome.

On February 22, 2000, following the controversial police shooting of William Miller, the City Council held a special workshop to discuss the use of force by our police department. At that workshop, and in related media reports about our deadly force policy, the Police Department asserted that San Diego's record in this area was laudable. For example, an Assistant Chief stated at the workshop that we were second lowest in the nation for police shootings in 1997-99. These statements seem to be at odds with the information reported by the Detroit Free Press.

We urge you to direct the City Manager to explain this discrepancy immediately. While we are aware that a special task force is studying the police use of force issue generally, without an accurate starting point with respect to how San Diego compares to other cities, this discussion will be pointless.

As you may know, our organizations and the San Diego Police Department have worked together constructively as partners on a number of issues. For example, at our urging, former Chief Jerry Sanders directed SDPD to become the first law enforcement agency in the nation to collect racial data on traffic stops, in order to enable us to address the phenomenon of "Driving While Black or Brown." Chief Bejarano has continued that commitment to ensuring that SDPD does not engage in racial profiling in traffic stops. Earlier, the ACLU worked closely with then Captain Bejarano to ensure that events during the Republican National Convention were orderly, while also respecting the constitutional rights of demonstrators.

We are confident that we can look forward to a similar commitment to honesty and responsiveness by the City in the matter of police use of deadly force. We no doubt share the goal of being last in the nation in police shootings and first in the nation in public safety. If we can be of assistance as the City sorts out the discrepancy between the Chief's statistics and those reported by the Detroit Free Press, please do not hesitate to call on us.

Sincerely,

Linda Hills, ACLU
H. J. Sims, Earl B. Gilliam Bar Association
Alvin Gomez, La Raza Lawyers Association
Randa Trapp, NAACP
