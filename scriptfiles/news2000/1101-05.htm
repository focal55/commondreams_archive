Nader Criticizes Concentration In Agri-Business Sector
   MINNEAPOLIS, MN - November 1 - Farm policies during the Clinton-Gore Administration have been a bi-partisan disaster for farmers, Ralph Nader said today.

“Many hoped the Clinton Administration would be more farmer-friendly than the Reagan and Bush Administrations, but instead its policies have moved in the direction of replacing family farms as the main food production unit,” Nader said.

Nader said the results of the Freedom to Farm Act—pushed by a Republican Congress and signed by a Democratic President—has been historically low commodity prices, severe decline in farmers and farm income. “It has meant grand profits for grain merchandisers who make money on low priced purchases from farmers,” Nader said.

Nader said the Clinton-Gore Administration has “presided over a record number of mergers and acquisitions throughout the economy, much of it in the agricultural sector.”

“Sectors such as poultry, livestock, grain, flour milling and related areas have seen growing concentration of control exercised by a handful of firms like Cargill, ConAgra and Dupont/Pioneer, among others,” Nader said.

Nader said the “Freedom to Farm Act” was drafted by agribusiness interests and peddled on the false premise that only increased international trade was the only thing that was needed to make the agricultural sector prosper.

Nader said government policies have shown “little or no regard for farmers net profit, ecological consequences or rural community viability.”

“Big Agri-corporations tend to incorporate savings from low farm prices into profits rather than transferring the savings to consumers in the form of lower prices,” Nader said.

“If we want good resource managers, if we want food produced in an environmentally responsible way, if we want the land held in a diverse manner that provides the maximum amount of social, political and economic benefit, then we have to start pursuing public policy that puts the economic interests of family farmers ahead of the economic interests of the corporate giants that control so much of agriculture today,” Nader said.
