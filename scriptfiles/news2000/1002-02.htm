Clean Air, Water Hang in Supreme Court Balance
  WASHINGTON - October 2 - With the Supreme Court opening a new session, Americans are focusing on the impact Justices can have on environmental protections. For example, last year in the Laidlaw case, Justice Antonin Scalia -- named by Texas Gov. George W. Bush as his judicial role model -- opined that community groups do not have the right to sue a polluter for violating the laws that protect our families from poisons -- even when a Roebuck, South Carolina company had dumped mercury into a stream nearly 500 times.

"To the Sierra Club, the ability to force a polluting neighbor to clean up its act is the very essence of common sense and fair play. Citizen suits are a powerful weapon for protecting our families from toxic pollution, but that tool could vanish depending on who the next President appoints to the Supreme Court," Cox added. "Our next President will decide whether the Court continues to protect the laws that keep our air breathable and our water drinkable."

Cases before the Court this year will play a vital role in setting future environmental precedents. When the Supreme Court hears Browner v. American Trucking Associations on November 7, it has the potential to strike a blow against a crucial effort to reduce soot and smog. At issue is a long-dead legal doctrine regarding whether federal agencies will be allowed to use modern solutions to address the problems plaguing our increasingly complicated society.

"If the Supreme Court uses a long-dead legal doctrine to throw out our nation's health standards on soot and smog, it will doom Americans to dirtier air and worse lung problems," Cox said. "Americans are concerned that polluters could turn back a generation of progress we've made in cleaning up the air we breathe. If the Supreme Court overturns the standards that protect our families from soot and smog, then people with asthma and the more than 100 million Americans who breathe dirty air will suffer while the polluters profit."

"While the next President will only be in office four to eight years, his legacy on the Supreme Court could harm our environment and our communities for more than a generation," Cox concluded. "If the next President packs the Court with people like Bush's role model, Justice Scalia, then our clean air and clean water protections will suffer profound damage."
