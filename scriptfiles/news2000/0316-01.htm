As Oil Giant Forced to Sell Alaskan Assets, Greenpeace Warns BP Amoco Still Wields Too Much Power

   WASHINGTON - March 16 - Greenpeace warned today that despite BP Amoco's forced sale of ARCO's Alaskan assets, the multinational oil giant is still 'directing' Alaskan oil policy to the detriment of the environment and the public.

The sale of Arco's Alaskan assets to Philips Petroleum was announced earlier today in a joint BP Amoco - ARCO press conference in order to meet Federal Trade Commission requirements before the merger would be allowed to proceed. The FTC opposed the merger on the grounds that BP Amoco already wields excessive market power in Alaskan oil exploration and production.

"This market power means political power in Alaska and all the way to Washington DC. BP Amoco still controls over half of Alaskan North Slope oil production and will continue to call the shots," said Iain MacGill, energy policy analyst for Greenpeace. "The Alaskan oil industry may have a new name but it's still the same dirty game."

The intense lobbying of Governor Tony Knowles and the State's Congressional legislators in support of the merger has highlighted BP Amoco's Alaskan power base. At the national level, Senator Frank Murkowski's has introduced a bill this month that would open the National Arctic Wildlife Refuge to oil drilling. BP Amoco, the major beneficiary, has been lobbying for such a move for years.

The undue influence of BP Amoco in Alaska is also evident in the company's effort to thwart Greenpeace's campaigns against the company's Northstar offshore oil project now under construction in the Arctic Ocean. The project uses untested and untried pipeline technology, which the Army Corp of Engineers estimates will expose the fragile Arctic environment to a one in four chance of a major oil spill. BP Amoco recently failed a spill clean up test; nevertheless, the project has been permitted to proceed.

And late last week at the Northstar site, three Greenpeace activists holding a banner reading "Global Warming Starts Here" and standing peacefully outside the "No Trespassing Zone" were arrested by Alaska State Troopers who traveled to the scene in a BP Amoco bus and departed on a BP Amoco jet shortly afterwards. The activists, who were part of a renewable energy camp the international environmental organization has set up next to the Northstar site, were arrested just before a contingent of journalists arrived on the scene.
