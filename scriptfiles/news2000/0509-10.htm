90 Environmental Groups Demand Complete MTBE Ban and Protection of Clean Air Act Benefits
  SAN FRANCISCO - May 9 - The Bluewater Network announces today that a letter from 90 environmental groups has been sent to Congress to demand a complete ban on the use of the gasoline additive MTBE and all other ether-based oxygenates; payment of MTBE cleanup costs by the oil industry rather than taxpayers; and prevention of air quality backsliding through protecting the Clean Air Act's fuel oxygenate program or passing comparable renewable fuels legislation.

Since the passage of the Clean Air Act Amendments of 1990, which required areas with the worst ozone or smog problems to use two percent oxygen in gasoline, the oil industry sharply increased their use of the oxygen-bearing chemical methyl tertiary butyl ether (MTBE). While other oxygenates such as ethanol can be used to meet this requirement, the oil industry's choice to use MTBE in 85 percent of reformulated gasoline has led to widespread contamination of the nation's water supplies.

"Even the MTBE from a single overturned automobile can contaminate 75 million gallons of drinking water," said Russell Long, Executive Director of the Bluewater Network. "With extensive damage already, and cleanup costs projected in the billions, the only answer to this threat is a complete ban."

There are over a dozen bills now making their way through Congress that reduce or eliminate MTBE use. Many of these bills also give states the choice to opt out of the Clean Air Act's oxygen requirement, despite the fact that this federal requirement does not specify the use of any particular oxygenate, including MTBE. Ethanol is an alternative, renewable fuel oxygenate that provides air quality benefits like MTBE, but compared to MTBE or the toxic components of gasoline, poses very little threat to water quality.

"We can't stand by and allow the oil industry to use the MTBE problem as an excuse to gut important air quality protections," said Elisa Lynch, Clean Fuels Campaign Director for the Bluewater Network. "Bills that lift this key provision of the Clean Air Act will not only endanger public health and the environment, but open a Pandora's Box for other hostile changes to one of the nation's best environmental laws."

The overwhelming majority of evidence suggests that the Clean Air Act's oxygen requirement has substantially reduced air pollution from vehicle emissions. States that opt out of this requirement will suffer worsening air quality in the very same areas where MTBE has devastated drinking water supplies.

Preserving the oxygen content requirement or passing comparable renewable fuels legislation will also support the burgeoning biomass industry. Ethanol can be produced from biomass waste products such as rice straw, wood pulp, corn stover and other agricultural by-products. This provides a clean, renewable fuel that not only goes a long way towards protecting air and water quality, but combats global warming, reduces waste, and supports local economies and national energy security.

"Using locally-produced biomass ethanol is a win all around--for the environment and for the economy. In addition to cleaning up emissions from gasoline, it decreases environmental impacts from oil exploration and transportation, puts waste products to good use, is a self-sustaining national energy source, and supports local farm economies and communities," said Russell Long, Executive Director of the Bluewater Network.

The 90 groups signed onto the letter to Congress include a wide range of national and grassroots groups that look after the interests of the environment, public health and local communities. These include the Bluewater Network, Surfrider Foundation, Southern Organizing Committee, Washington Environmental Council, Water Keeper Alliance, New Jersey Environmental Lobby, Earth Island Institute, Ozone Action, and the Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition.

The 90 groups are also asking Congress to ban other ether-based fuel oxygenates--ethyl tertiary butyl ether (ETBE), tertiary amyl methyl ether (TAME) and diisopropyl ether (DIPE)--which are of equal concern for the nation's drinking water resources. These chemicals have physical characteristics very similar to MTBE, and are already in use by many refiners, despite the fact that they are essentially unstudied. It is known, however, that they have toxicity to animals that is similar to MTBE, and that they may be carcinogenic.

"We want to make sure we don't repeat the MTBE mistake. If we don't also ban all of the ether oxygenates, the MTBE ban could amount to a shell game on the part of the oil companies, not protection of water quality," said Brooke Coleman, spokesperson for the Bluewater Network.

Cleanup costs for MTBE contamination could run into the billions and take decades to accomplish. Since it was the industry's choice to use this dangerous chemical, environmental groups are also calling on Congress to pass legislation requiring that polluters, not taxpayers, pay to clean up MTBE water contamination.

Last month the American Chemical Society reported that a third of the nation's community water wells may already be contaminated with MTBE. MTBE--a possible carcinogen that causes water to smell and taste like turpentine--is extremely expensive and technically difficult to clean up once it gets into water.
