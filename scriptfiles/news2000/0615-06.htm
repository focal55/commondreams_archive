Humane Society of the U.S. Applauds Makah Whale Hunt Ruling
   WASHINGTON - June 15 - The Humane Society of the United States (HSUS), the nation's largest animal protection organization, today applauded a federal appeals court ruling that has, at least for the time being, stopped a controversial whale hunt by Washington State's Makah Indian Tribe.

A panel of the 9th Circuit Court of Appeals in San Francisco ruled 2-1 on Friday, June 9, that an environmental assessment done by the United States government was biased in favor of the hunt and set aside a lower court ruling that would have permitted the Makah to continue killing Pacific gray whales.

"The 9th Circuit Court's decision demonstrates that the entire process, from the creation of the federal government's environmental assessment to the claim that the International Whaling Commission (IWC) authorized this hunt, has been contrary to the law. The court has vindicated everything we have said about this case for more than two years," said Patricia Forkan, executive vice president for The Humane Society of the United States.

In its ruling, the Court of Appeals said that the government's environmental assessment was "demonstrably suspect" and the government was "predisposed to finding that the Makah whaling proposal would not significantly affect the environment." The case now goes back to the same lower court -- U.S. District Court in Tacoma, Washington, and the same judge -- U.S. District Judge Franklin Burgess -- who ruled originally in favor of the Makah and the federal government, for additional proceedings and a new environmental assessment (EA).

This raises new questions, says Forkan. She notes that the Appeals panel also asks in its ruling that, "Having already committed in writing to support the Makah's whaling proposal, can the Federal Defendants now be trusted to take the clear-eyed hard look at the whaling proposals consequences required by law, or will a new EA be a classic Wonderland case of first-the-verdict, then-the-trial?"

"While the Court of Appeals did not require the completion of a more thorough environmental review known as an environmental impact statement (EIS), it is clear that only an EIS will be sufficient to examine all the issues in a fair and comprehensive way. We urge the federal government, despite their prior actions, to act honorably and prepare just such a statement," said Forkan.

The Makah, a coastal Indian tribe who live near Neah Bay in northwest Washington State, claim whaling rights under an 1855 treaty with the U.S. government. The tribe had not hunted whales in more than 70 years until last May when, with the permission of the federal government, the Makah harpooned and killed a juvenile gray whale in the waters outside Neah Bay. A similar hunt organized this spring has not been successful.

Commercial whalers hunted the gray whale to near extinction by the 1920s. In recent years, gray whale stocks have rebounded, leading federal officials to remove them from the Endangered Species List in 1994. The gray whale remains threatened with populations worldwide estimated at only 23,000.
