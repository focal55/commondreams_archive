Taxpayers for Common Sense Praises Efforts to Reduce Corporate Welfare
   WASHINGTON - June 8 - A Washington-based budget watchdog group today praised efforts by Budget Committee Chairman John Kasich (R-OH) for holding a hearing on corporate welfare and efforts by Rep. Joseph Hoeffel (D-PA) to create a commission to reduce corporate welfare.

In testimony before the House Budget Committee, Taxpayers for Common Sense Legislative Director Jill Lancelot commended Chairman Kasich's and Rep. Hoeffel's efforts to refocus the spotlight on federal subsidies and tax breaks to corporate interests, which total at least $125 billion annually, according to published estimates.

"As Congress begins to tackle the annual spending bills, it is an apt time to bring to light corporate welfare abuses" said Lancelot.

Rep. Hoeffel's effort, the Corporate Welfare Commission Act of 1999, seeks to reduce corporate welfare by identifying and prioritizing needless or wasteful tax breaks and subsidies for elimination. The bill, H.R. 3221, would establish a bi-partisan commission charged with the task of making clear recommendations for cuts.

"This is the first step in slaying the corporate welfare dragon once and for all," continued Lancelot.

Lancelot noted that most of the recipients of corporate welfare are already industry leaders with little need for special handouts.

Lancelot also argued that the benefits to taxpayers are questionable.

Lancelot cited the Advanced Technology Program (ATP), which was created with the objective of furthering technological advancements by awarding grants for research and development to corporations.

"Though the program may have had a worthy objective, there is no proof that ATP subsidies are essential for encouraging investment in research and development," said Lancelot, adding that some subsidy recipients are major corporations, whose research and development programs would continue without government subsidies.

Lancelot argued that corporate welfare has also left taxpayers with unexpected costs. The 1872 Mining Law, for instance, entitles the mining industry to take free of charge gold, silver, platinum, and other precious metals on public lands.

But the costs for cleaning up abandoned mines are more than the allotted reclamation bonds provided by the mine owners. For example, a cleanup in Montana cost the government $180 million, but the responsible mining company only paid $60 million toward the cleanup effort, according to the Department of the Interior.

"Although Congress at various times has addressed the unfairness of corporate welfare, unfortunately, very little has been done to correct these abuses," concluded Lancelot. "A successful commission would maintain its integrity with the least amount of political influence on the decision making process."

H.R. 3221 may be debated later this summer.
