Oil Industry Can Afford Diesel Cleanup, Says Clean Air Trust
  WASHINGTON - July 25 - ExxonMobil's latest profit announcement "clearly demonstrates" that the oil industry can afford to clean up diesel fuel, the nonprofit Clean Air Trust noted today.

The Clean Air Trust also noted that its analysis suggests that major oil companies are on track to report more than $13 billion in collective profits for the second quarter of 2000.

The world's largest publicly traded oil company reported this morning that its second quarter earnings more than doubled as profits surged to $4.15 billion for the three-month period.

Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust, noted that ExxonMobil made more in profits in this quarter alone than the diesel fuel cleanup is expected to cost over the next six years. (The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency has proposed requiring oil companies to remove most of the sulfur from diesel fuel by mid-2006 to enable use of advanced pollution control devices on big diesel trucks and buses.)

"It is time for the oil industry to stop crying poor -- and to stop its opposition to the diesel cleanup," O'Donnell said.

O'Donnell said the new profit figures for ExxonMobil and other major oil companies also raise questions about the industry's contentions that cleaner-burning gasoline standards drove gasoline prices sky-high this year.

"Prices at the pump have plummeted recently even though the cleaner-burning gasoline standards remain in effect," O'Donnell noted. "It does raise anew the question -- was the industry price gouging?"
