Arizona Border Delegation for Peace and Justice to meet with Policy Makers and the Administration Regarding the Border Crisis in Arizona
   WASHINGTON - June 28 - A delegation from Tucson and Douglas/Bisbee, Arizona, sponsored by the American Friends Service Committee (AFSC) and the Coalición de Derechos Humanos (Human Rights Coalition) of Tucson, will be meeting with members of Congress and the administration concerning escalating vigilante activity in their communities along the U.S./Mexico border.

A Press Briefing has been called for Tuesday, June 27, 2000 in room SC-6 at 2:00 PM in the US Capitol Building to focus on growing violence at the Arizona border where it has been reported at least 30 incidents of local ranchers detaining and arresting undocumented border crossers at gunpoint. These activities have been bolstered by the support of anti-immigrant groups nationally, including those headed by David Duke and Patrick Buchanan. Meetings have been scheduled with the Immigration and Naturalization Service, the Department of Justice, the White House, the Congressional Hispanic Caucus and delegation members' congressional representatives.

The Arizona Border Delegation for Peace and Justice is a group of human rights activists, religious organizers, community members, and ranchers from the Douglas/Bisbee and Tucson areas. Accompanying the group is Maria Jimenez of the AFSC. Jimenez, director of AFSC's Immigration Law Enforcement Monitoring Project (ILEMPS), coordinates efforts to monitor human rights abuses along the US/Mexico border.

"The voices of the affected communities must be heard by policymakers," said Jimenez. "The recent surge of vigilantism by ranchers on the border stems from an 'out of sight, out of mind' strategy pursued by the U.S. government. The Justice Department needs to find a solution to the border problems that is acceptable to everyone," Jimenez added.

The roots of this crisis lie in the current generation of border control policies being pursued by the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS). These "blockade-style" enforcement strategies claiming to stem the flow of undocumented immigrants, have actually forced migrants to cross into ever more hostile and dangerous desert terrain, where they risk accidents, dehydration, and death. Meanwhile, as a direct result of the heightened presence of law enforcement, border communities have become divided and filled with hostility, suspicion, and violence. As Isabel Garcia, a delegation member, human rights activist and attorney based in Tuscon, Arizona, asks the question, "How many deaths will it take before we realize that our policy is at the root of the problem?"
