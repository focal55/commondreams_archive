Nader to Gore: Keep Your Promise on East Liverpool Incinerator
   EAST LIVERPOOLl, OH - September 27—Ralph Nader called on Vice-President Gore today to honor a promise he made to the people of the Ohio River Valley and western Pennsylvania eight years ago by shutting down the Von Roll Waste Technologies Industries incinerator.  The incinerator has both failed test burns and been criticized in a damning 1994 GAO report that Vice-President Gore himself requested.  “Mr. Gore,” Nader stated, “after seven years of double-talk and delays, the time has come to shut down this incinerator.  Workers should be given two years full severance pay by their negligent employer, WTI.”

The hazardous waste incinerator in East Liverpool, OH burns 60,000 tons of hazardous waste every year, making it one of the largest incinerators of its type in the world, despite the fact that its permit expired in 1995.  It is located in an area with a history of environmental contamination, on a flood plain, and less than 400 yards away from an elementary school, in violation of numerous state and federal environmental regulations.  Among the toxins that the incinerator releases into the air are dioxins, furans, and metals such as chromium, mercury, lead, and arsenic.  “Any incinerator that emits almost a pound of mercury into the air every day can’t be good for our children’s ability to learn,” Nader noted.

Campaigning for Vice-President in 1992, Al Gore called the incinerator, then in construction “unbelievable,” adding: “the Clinton-Gore administration is going to give you an environmental presidency to deal with these problems.  We’ll be on your side for a change.”  Today Nader commented: “this is just the sort of phony populist rhetoric that Mr.  Gore has tried to sell the American public in this election year.”

After the 1992 election, Gore called for a GAO report on the incinerator, arguing that: “a thorough investigation is urgently needed.  Too many questions remain unanswered about the impact of this incinerator and the process in which it was approved.”  Yet on January 8, 1993 despite promises from President Bush’s EPA Administrator William Reilly for a smooth transition, accommodation from the Bush administration on other environmental issues, and a meeting between Reilly and top Gore aide Katie McGinty, a permit for trial-burns was issued.  “Last week I wrote a letter to Al Gore asking him to disclose whether he asked the Bush EPA to postpone the approval of a test burn,” Nader told an audience today in East Liverpool. “He has not responded to this question.”

The trial-burns that were conducted in 1993 revealed that the incinerator released toxins in quantities greater than the already dangerously high levels allowed by the EPA.  Mercury and dioxin levels were far higher than expected, giving the Clinton Administration ample reason to deny approval of full commercial operation to the Von Roll WTI incinerator.  As the September 1994 GAO report that Gore himself requested noted, toxic emission standards were lowered by Gore’s own EPA in order to allow the incinerator to operate. “In each case [where toxic emissions exceeded allowable levels],” the report explains, “EPA responded to the trial burn results by changing the operating conditions under which the incinerator could continue to operate.”

“I am challenging Al Gore and the Clinton EPA, which Gore has overseen, to adhere to their own declared principles,” Nader stated.  “If Gore can’t stand up for the people against this outrageously dangerous polluter, should anyone believe he will ever fight for the people, and not the powerful?  He hasn’t even made good on his current election year promise to conduct a binding review of the EPA’s relationship with WTI that was due in May.”

“Al Gore knows how to talk the talk on environmentalism and public health,” Nader said, “but when it comes time to stand up to corporate power and get results, he won’t even attempt to walk the walk.”
