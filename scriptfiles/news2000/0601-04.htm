Bush Attacks Protections for Grand Canyon and Seqouias
Environmental Advisor Advocates "Auctioning Off All Public Lands"
 

WASHINGTON - June 1 - Texas Governor George W. Bush today attacked efforts to protect America's most spectacular landscapes and our majestic ancient forests, deriding a conservation measure created under President Theodore Roosevelt and touting a mismanaged Texas program. In a speech today in Lake Tahoe, Nevada, Governor Bush outlined a plan that would allow seemingly endless debate over unprotected land, while allowing his oil- and gas-industry allies to continue their destruction for decades.

"Many of America's favorite vacation spots remain unprotected and vulnerable to destruction, but Gov. Bush says he would delay protection," said Sierra Club Executive Director Carl Pope. "American families don't want more foot dragging -- they want our spectacular landscapes and ancient forests protected for their children's children to enjoy. Delay means destruction for many of America's wildlands. Governor Bush's policy would wrap protections in red tape while oil companies continue to drill our public lands and the timber industry ravages our National Forests. "

In his speech, Bush praised the Texas Landowner Incentive Program for protecting rare species. However, the Texas Parks & Wildlife Department does not allow private projects funded by the this program to be reviewed by experts on the species of concern. This lack of scientific review lead to the misallocation of $18,000 for an environmentally destructive project involving a species not eligible for funding through the program. The $18,000 was used to clear vegetation and channelize a stream near Davis Mountains State Park, with no intended benefit for endangered species.

Gov. Bush attacked President Clinton's recent designations of National Monuments, which have included the north rim of the Grand Canyon, California's Sequoia forests and the rocky islands on the California coast.

Roosevelt, the first President to create National Monuments, protected the Grand Canyon as a National Monument in 1908; Congress designated it as a National Park after more than a decade of debate. This century, each President but Nixon, Reagan and Bush has protected part of America's landscape as National Monuments.

"We enjoy the Grand Canyon's wonders today because President Teddy Roosevelt designated it as a National Monument nearly a century ago," Pope added. "If President Roosevelt hadn't acted quickly, but instead waited for Congress to finish its debate, the Grand Canyon might have been developed and destroyed decades ago."

On January 30, 2000, The Washington Post's Al Kamen reported that Bush environmental advisor Terry L. Anderson "recently published a `blueprint for auctioning off all public lands over 20 to 40 years,' saying that that would improve `environmental quality and economic efficiency.'" Anderson explicitly leaves open the possibility of auctioning off Grand Canyon National Park.

American families treasure our National Parks, National Monuments and National Forests as their favorite vacation spots, places to relax, hunt, hike, fish and gaze at the vistas. Texas, however, invests little in protecting its natural resources. The state ranks 43rd in per capita spending on fish and wildlife protections, 47th in per capita water-quality spending, and 48th in per capita spending on state parkland and acquisition.
