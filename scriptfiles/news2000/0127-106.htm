Congress Introduces Bill to Ban Shark Finning in All U.S. Waters
  WASHINGTON - January 27 - The Ocean Wildlife Campaign (OWC) -- a coalition of six national conservation groups working to protect sharks and other giant ocean fishes -- applauded Rep. Randy Cunningham (R-Calif.) and Chairman Jim Saxton (R-N.J.), Subcommittee on Fisheries Conservation, Wildlife and Oceans, as they introduced binding legislation intended to permanently ban the reckless practice of shark finning -- slicing off a shark's fins and discarding its carcass at sea -- in all U.S. waters. The bill further directs the Secretary of State to work toward a similar ban worldwide.

Cited by the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) as "wasteful of valuable shark resources and pos 1/8ing 3/8 a threat to attaining the conservation objectives of fishery management", the practice of finning was banned in federal waters of the U.S. Atlantic in 1993, but has expanded dramatically during this same period in portions of the U.S. Pacific. "Our hats are off to Mr. Cunningham and Mr. Saxton for attacking head-on an issue characterized by rampant waste, egregious inconsistencies with U.S. domestic and foreign policy, and inaction by regional fishery managers," noted Russell Dunn, Assistant Director, OWC.

Despite concerns expressed by conservationists, fishermen, scientists, the public and the Hawaii State legislature over the practice, the Western Pacific Regional Fishery Management Council (WESPAC) has steadfastly refused to act. The move by Cunningham and Saxton to secure a permanent legislative prohibition on shark finning follows a litany of warnings to halt the practice by the Clinton Administration and the successful adoption of H. Con. Res. 189 by the U.S. House of Representatives in November 1999, which expressed strong opposition to the irresponsible and damaging practice of shark finning.

The number of sharks killed in the Hawaiian longline fisheries has climbed from 2,289 in 1991 to 60,857 in 1998 -- a whopping 2,500 percent increase. "Over 98 percent of these sharks were killed for their fins to meet the huge demand for shark fin soup," explained Dr. David Wilmot, OWC Director. "And because shark fins comprise only one to five percent of the animal's bodyweight -- 95 to 99 percent of the shark is going to waste." NMFS and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric have pressured WESPAC to prohibit shark finning for months, but the council balked at agency pressure in June and October.

The United States has been a leading proponent of international shark conservation measures and the conspicuous lack of protection for sharks in the U.S. Central and Western Pacific represents a glaring gap in otherwise relatively sound U.S. shark policy, remarked Sonja Fordham, shark fisheries specialist for the Center for Marine Conservation, an OWC member group. "Our inability to address the egregious finning problem within our own waters threatens to undermine U.S. credibility in the international arena."

According to the OWC, the massive increase in shark landings coupled with poor information regarding the status and health of Pacific shark populations is cause for concern. Sharks' unique biological characteristics -- slow growth, late sexual maturity, and the production of few young -- make them particularly vulnerable to overfishing. This biological vulnerability and the unknown population status of many sharks species in the Pacific Ocean demand a level of precautionary and proactive management that is far above the current patchwork of state and federal shark regulations now in place in U.S. Pacific waters.
