Conservation Group Kicks-Off $6 Million Effort to Elevate the Environment in 2000 Elections
   WASHINGTON - April 13 - Promising to hold elected officials accountable on the environment, the bipartisan League of Conservation Voters (LCV) today announced a $6 million effort to inform the public about the environmental records of members of Congress and federal candidates.

With today's announcement, LCV launched the most aggressive, most bipartisan campaign program ever undertaken by an environmental organization.

According to LCV president Deb Callahan, the 2000 elections will see the emergence of the environmental issue as a top-tier election issue. LCV plans to build on heightened environmental attention generated from the 30th anniversary of Earth Day on April 22, and maintain that environmental momentum through Election Day on November 7.

"The time is right for environmental issues to move to the forefront of this year's campaigns," Callahan said. "The nation is enjoying relative peace and prosperity and voters can afford to focus on issues that pertain to their quality of life. Voters are beginning to understand that Congress has been chipping away at our environmental laws. No other issue more completely affects each American -- from the quality of the water we drink and air we breathe, to the health of our communities and the legacy we leave our children -- than the issue of environmental protection."

As part of its "Earth Day to Election Day" campaign strategy, the LCV Action Fund, LCV's political action committee, unveiled a new "Environmental Champions" campaign to promote the strong environmental records of select Republican and Democratic congressional candidates in tight races. U.S. Reps. Chris Shays (R-Conn.), Joseph Hoeffel (D-Pa.) and Jay Inslee (D-Wash.) were named the first "Environmental Champions" of 2000.

About being named an "Environmental Champion" Shays said, "During my service in Congress, I've tried to maintain a healthy respect for the world we inhabit and I've worked with my colleagues to fight attempts to rollback laws that protect our environment. I'm honored the League of Conservation Voters has recognized that effort. We were only given one Earth on which to live and if we do not cherish it we may very well lose it."

In 1998, Congressman Inslee witnessed the power of LCV's campaigns when his opponent, then Rep. Rick White (R-Wash.) was named to the Dirty Dozen and was ultimately defeated. Said Inslee, "I feel honored to be recognized today as a champion for the environment. I hope this event marks the beginning of a legacy of lawmakers who are expected to protect our natural environment, rather than singled out for their actions."

About Hoeffel's environmental record, Callahan said, "Congressman Hoeffel is an outstanding example of how effective and courageous a first-term member of Congress can be. He consistently votes to protect the environment and has numerous bills to preserve open space and farmland, combat the effects of suburban sprawl and protect taxpayers from paying the bill for environmentally harmful government subsidies."

By employing a variety of tactics and strategies, the LCV Action Fund will aggressively campaign to inform voters about the "Environmental Champions'" leadership and record. The "Environmental Champions" list will ultimately grow to about eight to ten candidates.

LCV is perhaps best known for its Dirty Dozen campaigns that target for defeat Congressional candidates with poor environmental records. Since 1996, LCV has campaigned against 25 Dirty Dozen candidates, helping to defeat 16. In 1999, the average LCV score of defeated Dirty Dozen designees was 19 percent, their replacements averaged 84 percent. The only candidate to be named to the Dirty Dozen so far in 2000 is U.S. Rep. Jim Traficant (D-Ohio). LCV ran television ads in March to publicize his votes against the environment and to highlight the strong environmental record of his primary challenger Bob Hagan. Other Dirty Dozen designees will be named in June and September. LCV will dedicate nearly $3 million to the 2000 Dirty Dozen campaign.

In addition to tough campaign programs, LCV also provides direct candidate support. Through the EarthList program, the LCV Action Fund generates contributions for the campaigns of strong environmentalists who are in competitive races. Reps. Jim Ramstad (R-Minn.), David Bonior (D-Mich.), Marge Roukema (R-N.J.), Sherwood Boehlert (R-N.Y.), Mark Udall (D-Colo.), Rush Holt (D-N.J.) and Sen. Jim Jeffords (R-Vt.) are currently named to the 2000 EarthList. Three more candidates will be added before October.

By October, LCV will endorse about 100 Republican and Democratic pro-environment candidates running for the U.S. House or U.S. Senate.

"This is the third election cycle we've run direct campaigns and we've learned that our presence in a race can make the difference," said Callahan. "Our board, our staff and our members are committed to supporting environmental friends, defeating environmental foes, and most importantly, giving citizens the resources to make informed environmental decisions on Election Day."

While LCV has not made a presidential endorsement at this time, the group did produce in-depth environmental profiles of each presidential candidate that are available over the Internet at http://www.lcv.org/presidential. LCV updates the information weekly with the most recent environmental news from the campaigns. Callahan predicts that environmental issues will rise to the top of the agenda for the presidential candidates as the race heat up.

The bipartisan League of Conservation Voters is the political voice for the environmental and conservation community. The League is a nonprofit organization dedicated to informing the public about the environmental records of federally elected officials. For each session of Congress, LCV produces the National Environmental Scorecard that rates the environmental voting records of U.S. Representatives and Senators. All campaign material and the Scorecards are available to the public through the LCV Web site, http://www.lcv.org.
