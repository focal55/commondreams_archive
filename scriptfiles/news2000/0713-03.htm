Brennan Center Files Complaint Citing New Low in Sham "Issue Ads"
   NEW YORK - July 13 - A television ad paid for by the Conservative Party of New York, featuring Senate candidate Rick Lazio, has been targeted in a complaint filed today by the Brennan Center for Justice at NYU School of Law. The Brennan Center, on behalf of New York's Green Party, has asked the Federal Election Commission to find that the Conservative Party violated federal election laws by spending "soft money" on an electioneering advertisement in support of Congressman Rick Lazio's Senate bid.

The Conservative Party claims that spending soft money on the Lazio advertisement is appropriate because it is an "issue ad," not an electioneering ad. But it is impossible to find the issue advocated in the ad, other than Congressman Lazio's fondness for Long Island's beaches and his status as a lifetime New Yorker. The Conservative Party Chairman defends the use of soft money by claiming that the ad is about "party building," and that the Party "could have used any New Yorker; we used Rick Lazio."

"I doubt the Conservative Party will be spending $60,000 to broadcast that you or I love New York's beaches," says Brennan Center President E. Joshua Rosenkranz. "Other parties have crossed the line on the legal definition of 'issue advocacy' by featuring candidates in ads that sprinkle in a couple of issues. But this is the first time we've ever seen a party ignore the line entirely by running a candidate-centered ad under the guise of issue advocacy without even the pretense of mentioning an issue."

The ad, titled "Snapshots," opens and closes with pictures of Congressman Lazio and his family. The accompanying text says: "They could be snapshots from any New Yorker's life. Or they could be from Rick Lazio's own photo album. Summers clamming in the tides of Long Island. Growing up helping in his dad's auto-parts store. He and his wife sending their daughters to local public schools. Now working for the values he's lived by: Protecting the environment, helping small business, improving education. Rick Lazio. Made in New York. A message from the Conservative Party, fighting for New York's values."

According to The New York Times, the ad was paid for by the Conservative Party with "soft money" (see "The Ad Campaign," New York Times, June 6, 2000). Through a brazen misinterpretation of "issue advocacy," the Conservative Party claims a license to use "soft money--money collected in unlimited amounts from corporations, unions, and wealthy individuals--to influence the outcome of New York's Senate race. By law, soft money is not to be used to support particular candidates.

The Conservative Party claims the expenditure is issue advertising because it contains no "express advocacy" (i.e. a direct appeal to "vote for" or "elect" candidate Lazio).

"It is time for the FEC to put an end to this kind of legal maneuvering and loophole exploitation. The FEC needs to tell political parties that if they want to support a candidacy they have to follow the same rules that apply to the candidates: no corporate or union donations, limits on what rich individuals can contribute, and full disclosure," says Statewide Media Coordinator for the Green Party, Mark Dunlea. "The Green Party follows the law because the American people deserve an open and fair electoral process, and we call upon other parties to do the same."

The Center's recently published study, Buying Time: Television Advertising in the 1998 Congressional Elections, demonstrates that in 1998 it was political parties rather than advocacy groups who were the main purveyors of sham "issue ads."
