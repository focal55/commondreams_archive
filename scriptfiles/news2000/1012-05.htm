Debate Reveals Fundamental Differences in Bush, Gore Environmental Policies
  WASHINGTON - October 12 - The following was released today by the League of Conservation Voters:

When it comes to environmental protection, few other political issues point to such clear-cut distinctions between Vice President Al Gore and Governor George W. Bush. This was the assessment of the League of Conservation Voters (LCV), following last night's second presidential debate in Winston-Salem, N.C.

"Last night's debate revealed the fundamental differences between Al Gore and George W. Bush when it comes to protecting the nation's air, water and land," said LCV President Deb Callahan. "Gore is advocating a proactive approach to curbing the harmful affects of global warming, protecting our air and water and preserving our natural treasures, while Bush's 'wait-until-it's-too-late' approach to environmental and public health protections could threaten 30 years of forward-thinking environmental progress."

During the debate, Gore asserted that Houston surpassed Los Angeles as the smoggiest city. In fact, according to the Environmental Protection Agency, Texas ranks number one in the nation for dumping carcinogens in the environment, number one in carbon dioxide emissions and number one in hazardous waste production. Texas also ranks 49th among states in the amount of money it spend per capita on its parks, a fact Bush attempted to sidestep during the debate.

Also during the debate, Bush attempted to take credit for Texas' electricity deregulation bill, which requires Texas' older, polluting power plants to reduce their emissions. According to the League, Bush supported a bill that was passed by the state Senate, incorporating his plan to encourage but not require industrial cleanup. However, the bill, along with one to open the state's electricity industry to competition, were bogged down in the state House by supporters of mandatory pollution requirements. In order to avoid defeat, the bill's House sponsor agreed to amendments opposed by Bush, requiring companies to clean up or face mandatory shut down. A last minute House-Senate conference produced a compromise that included penalties for grandfathered plants that don't install anti-pollution devices and meet current state and federal standards for air quality. The separate utility bill included a requirement that 100 grandfathered power plants reduce emissions by 50 percent by 2003.

"Polls consistently show that the majority of voters -- Republicans, Democrats and Independents -- alike support stronger environmental and public health protections," Callahan said. "Bush recognizes the political power of the environmental issue and is trying to 'greenwash' voters about his commitment to conservation and public health protections. However, only Gore has the proven record and commitment on this issue."

LCV is the political voice for the national environmental community. LCV was the first national environmental organization to endorse Gore for president.
