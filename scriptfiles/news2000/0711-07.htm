New Report Underscores Need for U.S. Investigation into Passive Response to Rwanda's Genocide of 1994
   WASHINGTON - July 11 - An investigation published last Friday by the Organization of African Unity (OAU) criticizes the U.S. government for a negligent response during the 1994 genocide in Rwanda and charges that U.S. officials inhibited action by the UN Security Council as the genocide unfolded.

The U.S. Committee for Refugees repeats its call for the U.S. government to undertake a long overdue investigation into its own failure to respond appropriately during the 1994 genocide in Rwanda that slaughtered a half-million to a million people. The OAU report is the most recent of several investigations that have excoriated U.S. officials and other world leaders for their inaction during Rwanda's massive killings.

The OAU report, prepared by an "International Panel of Eminent Personalities to Investigate the 1994 Genocide in Rwanda," concludes that the U.S. government "knew full well" about the genocide as it occurred but refused to respond because of the Clinton administration's "geopolitical indifference." The report characterizes the U.S. failure to respond as "inglorious," "exceptionally insensitive," and "ridiculous." The report charges that "no nation did more than the United States to undermine the effectiveness" of UN peacekeeping efforts in Rwanda. The OAU panel entitled its report, Rwanda: The Preventable Genocide.

The U.S. government has refused to investigate its own failings and how to avoid them in the future. President Bill Clinton belatedly apologized in 1998 for the U.S. government's failure to respond to Rwanda's genocide and stated that he "did not fully appreciate" the scope of the killings when they occurred. However, the OAU report joins other investigations in concluding that U.S. officials were well-informed about the bloodletting in Rwanda as it took place.

Remarks by U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright on July 9 on ABC television in reaction to the OAU report suggest that she disagreed with the U.S. policy of inaction during the genocide when she was U.S. Ambassador to the UN. Albright says that she was following orders.

"I followed instructions because I was an ambassador. But I screamed about the instructions that I got on this. I felt they were wrong, and I made that point. But I was an ambassador under instructions." -- Madeleine Albright, U.S. Secretary of State, July 9, 2000, discussing criticism of her role in the passive U.S. response to 1994 genocide in Rwanda

"What could I do? What could I do by myself? Nothing.... We were powerless to fix the situation.... What can you do?" -- Rwandan Hutu religious leader, reacting to recent criticism of his passive response to 1994 genocide in Rwanda

"I don't know how Madeleine Albright lives with it." -- Stephen Lewis, former Canadian ambassador to the UN and co-author of new OAU report, released July 7, criticizing international community's passive response during 1994 genocide in Rwanda

Albright's words again raise serious questions about who determined U.S. policy during the fateful days of the genocide, how U.S. decisions were made, what options received consideration, how crucial information circulated among policy makers, and how the President could claim to have been poorly informed as a genocide occurred in full view of the world.

The U.S. government stands virtually alone in refusing to conduct a transparent examination of its policy failures during the Rwandan genocide. The governments of Belgium and France have conducted inquiries into their own actions amid the genocide. In 1996, a multi-donor assessment team published a report, Lessons From the Rwanda Experience, which concluded that "the role of the United States was critical" in impeding an international response to the genocide. In 1999, the UN published a self-critical report assessing its negligence during the genocide and documenting steps taken by the U.S. government to weaken UN protection efforts during the massacres.

The U.S. Committee for Refugees has repeatedly called for an independent review of U.S. policies during the genocide. The OAU report, and the reaction of Secretary of State Albright, reinforce the need for such an investigation.

President Clinton's claim that he did not know about the genocide, and Albright's claim that she was just following orders, are contradictory, grossly inadequate, and beg for elaboration.

"Pass the buck" excuses are common to this day inside Rwanda among the perpetrators of genocide. Such excuses are unworthy of America's leading officials.
