Sen. Wellstone Calls for National Action on Poverty
   WASHINGTON - June 15 - The following was released today by the National Association of Community Action Agencies:

"Why, in this booming economy, do so many politicians, both Republicans and Democrats, not see that we must help those who need help -- the more than 14 million children who do not get enough to eat, who live in substandard housing and don't receive adequate education and health care?" asked Sen. Paul Wellstone (D-MN) at today's National Town Hall Meeting on Poverty. "And what about the millions of adults who work, but do not make enough to support themselves and their families?" he added.

Over 150 people from across the country gathered in Washington today to look at the who and why of poverty. These people have taken part in over 500 dialogues on poverty in their local communities and states to explore the public policy actions that need to be taken by state and federal political leaders to solve poverty-related problems.

"There is a disconnect between what the public and the politicians think about poverty and what it really is," according to Deepak Bhargava, Center for Community Change. "The voices missing from the national debate are those who have experienced poverty first-hand. Those are the people that national policymakers don't see," he added.

Wellstone and other meeting participants called for legislation that guarantees a living wage and provides health care and adequate housing to those who can't afford them.

More than 34 million people live below the official federal poverty level. And millions more are among the working poor who are employed in jobs that pay the minimum wage ($5.15 an hour) and offer few, if any, benefits such as health insurance or pensions. A full-time, year round worker making the minimum wage earns about $10,700 per year -- hardly enough to support an individual or a family.

The Town Hall Meeting is being sponsored by the National Association of Community Action Agencies (NACAA) as part of the Dialogue on Poverty 2000: Leading America to Community Action.
