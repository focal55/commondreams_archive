British Petroleum Offer Requires Caution
  

ANCHORAGE - February 28 - “Press reports about BP divesting all of ARCO’s Alaska assets sound encouraging but we really don’t know exactly what is on the table,” said AKPIRG Executive Director Jim Sykes. Sykes cautioned, “The deal could still be bad for Alaska if assets are sold to multiple parties, an inactive silent partner or a company that colludes with BP rather than competing.

There are still many scenarios where BP could eliminate effective competition, and recent history points in that direction. Sykes said, “On the other hand if all ARCO Alaska assets go to one single active competitor, substantial competition could be restored and Alaska might not be hurt by a BP-ARCO merger.”

AKPIRG has fought to preserve meaningful competition in Alaska since BP’s takeover of ARCO was announced. “This development adds credibility to our fight for a fair deal,” said Sykes. “From the beginning it’s been clear that the state failed to negotiate a good deal for Alaska in the ‘compact agreement’ and news of BP’s significant offer is proof of that failure.”

According to Sykes, it is essential that the new buyer be known and approved before a merger takes place. A determination needs to be made on the realistic level of significant competition to which the new owner will commit prior to approval. Severe enforceable penalties for noncompliance by BP and the new owner must also be part of any merger package.

Even if Alaska monopoly issues are resolved, there may be other antitrust problems regarding the West Coast and Cushing, Oklahoma interests. AKPIRG will monitor the situation closely and keep open the option of filing an amicus brief in the pending legal case of FTC v. BP and ARCO.
