America’s Editors Say: ‘Close is not enough’ for National Missile Defense
   WASHINGTON - January 31 - At 9:19 PM, January 18, a 'target" missile lifted off from Vandenberg Air Force Base in California. Ground based radars and an array of satellites tracked its trajectory. Some 20 minutes later, the national missile defense (NMD) prototype missile took off from Kwajalein Atoll to intercept the target, trying to destroy it in a "hit-to-kill" encounter. But six seconds before the anticipated impact, both heat-seeking infrared detectors critical to the interceptor's final maneuvers failed - in effect blinding the NMD missile and causing it to miss

Last week, Secretary of Defense William Cohen responded with Orwellian doublespeak, telling reporters that the NMD program is "on the right track." The interceptor and target were "within 100 feet" of each other, he said, and the flaw was one of "engineering and not science" and one he expects will be corrected.

But as football 's Tennessee Titans know, close is not enough. The Titans were within 3 feet of their goal - a tying score in the Superbowl - but they lost. In football, there are second chances; in a nuclear conflict, there aren 't second chances. As President Clinton 's scheduled decision on deployment of a limited national missile decision approaches, a growing number of newspaper editorials reflect the growing doubt and concern about the effectiveness and overall national security value of the system. Below are excerpts of several recent editorials:

Atlanta Journal Constitution, January 20, 2000: "Dubious missile shield"
"...an aggressor would have to be suicidal to fire a nuclear- or chemical-tipped missile at the United States, knowing full well the projectile would bear his country's return address in big, bold letters and that Washington would retaliate massively. A missile defense would be of no use against a nuclear device secreted into the hold of a freighter docking at a U.S. port or a suitcase bomb smuggled into the country. Sad to say, these frightful scenarios are more plausible than North Korea or Iran lobbing an ICBM in our direction. Can there be any doubt that U.S. security would be better served by the drastic reduction of Russian nuclear warheads ... than by the raising of a highly dubious missile shield?"

Business Week, January 20, 2000: "Star Wars Lite: Political Plus, Diplomatic Disaster?"
"Headlines screamed failure after a Jan. 18 test in which a U.S. rocket streaked into the sky from Kwajalein Atoll in the Pacific, only to miss its target, an incoming dummy warhead. [T]his whiff may not alter the schedule for a Presidential decision come summer on whether to deploy a $13 billion missile-defense shield against an attack by rogue states. Alas, the proposed system may not reach even this modest goal. A blue-ribbon defense panel recently noted that critical computer simulations are behind schedule. The panel recommended delaying the decision to deploy, advice the Jan. 18 test would seem to bolster. What's more, the diplomatic costs of pursuing this program are high. The White House probably won't listen, however. After all, Bill Clinton has always been a master of the domestic political game. It's the next President who will have to cope with whatever fallout occurs."

Omaha World Herald, January 22, 2000: "No Shame in Deferring"
"By late summer, Clinton is scheduled to decide whether to proceed. Barring some new development, it strike us as too hurried. Why not just keep testing, one step at a time, until the picture is clearer? Deciding not to decide is, in itself, a decision. It can be a completely legitimate one."

Chicago Tribune, January 20, 2000: "Hitting a Bullet with a Bullet"
"The Pentagon suffered another humiliating failure in the latest test of its troubled national missile defense system .... the test clearly demonstrated one thing It 's premature to order deployment of this $12.5 billion shield. It 's not ready. [D]eployment will be seen in some quarters as a provocation, and the decision will force the U.S. to conduct a diplomatic tap dance. ... [T]he outraged Russians have threatened to stall progress on arms control treaties, fearing the U.S. might expand this defense system .... If the U.S. proceeds to deploy, other nuclear powers, such as China, may well build more weapons in an effort to overwhelm it, spurring a nuclear arms race if India and Pakistan follow suit. That's a risk that might be worth taking when the technology for an anti-missile system is ready. But it's not. It's not ready yet."

The Cincinnati Post, January 20, 1999: "Hit-and-miss defense"
The Clinton administration should ignore the political pressure and shelve a decision on deploying a missile defense system until we have one that works. For $12.7 billion, the initial cost of deploying the system being tested, the country would be buying little more than a false sense of security. The missile defense is scheduled for one more test this spring, but even a success is hardly likely to be conclusive. Further research on a missile defense is certainly warranted, but plans for deployment are not.

Fort Worth Star-Telegram, January 20, 2000: "Close Doesn 't Count"
"The failure of an interceptor test wounds the anti-missile defense program, but not fatally. They missed. The failed test, however, does not provide conclusive proof that such a system cannot be made to work. But it is significant setback that should give President Clinton pause about his plan to approve the deployment of the system later this year. Deployment quite simply cannot proceed until the Defense Department demonstrates conclusively that a missile can be made to do better than coming close to intercepting another missile."

Los Angeles Times, January 20, 2000: "Close Isn 't Good Enough"
"Even though both Moscow and Beijing implicitly recognize that a U.S. system would not significantly affect the balance of power, both bitterly oppose its development. Some NATO allies too have objected .... The potential missile threat from rogue states can't be ignored. But that threat can be met only with a missile interceptor system that has been shown to work in all of its aspects. Rushing ahead with anything less would provide neither deterrence nor defense."

Newark Star-Ledger, January 20, 2000: "A deadline for Star Wars"
"President Clinton faces a self-imposed June deadline to decide whether to deploy a limited Star Wars system designed to knock down missiles from rogue nations like North Korea or Iran .... The idea has obvious appeal.

But the unfortunate fact is that we do not have the technology to pull off this trick. Even if tests scheduled for this spring are successful, it's clear that knocking missiles out of the sky is so difficult that a president facing a crisis could not be certain that the system would work.

Against that dubious benefit, weigh the concrete costs of deploying a system. The Pentagon says it would cost $12.5 billion, which means the bill will probably be at least twice that. More important, deployment would break the 1972 ABM treaty with the Russians, and our repeated attempts to convince them we mean no harm have all failed. They will respond, probably by refusing to cooperate with other disarmament initiatives. China has threatened to escalate its missile programs as well if we deploy. The result of our deployment would be more danger, not less."

Houston Chronicle, January 21, 2000: "Quick Trigger: Don 't rush into unworkable missile defense deployment"
"The Pentagon's second test of a prototype interceptor missed its target over the Pacific Ocean last Tuesday, capping a string of tests that have failed or been of questionable success. It is clear that a deployment decision would be premature. A vigorous research and development program should continue, but the rush to deployment is getting way ahead of the true technical feasibility, and that's both unwise and a waste of taxpayer monies. Internationally, it is feared deployment of such a system by the United States will trigger a new nuclear arms race. That's debatable, but at the very least the risks associated with it should not be run for a system that is not going to work."

The New York Times, January 21, 2000: "A Slower Path to Missile Defense"
"The failure of a prototype missile interceptor to hit its target in a crucial test this week makes it glaringly clear that the Pentagon has not yet perfected the technology needed for an effective national missile defense, even one of limited scope. President Clinton, who has said under political pressure that he will decide this summer whether to start building such a system, should defer his decision until a fuller testing record can be established.

Until reliable missile defenses can be mounted without wrecking arms control, Washington should continue to meet threats from North Korea and Iran the way it met missile threats from Russia during the cold war -- through America's capacity for devastating retaliation and efforts to restrain the spread of nuclear materials and missile technology."

Tulsa World, January 21, 2000: "Almost"
"Almost only counts in horseshoes and hand grenades. To be effective, an interceptor missile must make a direct hit on an incoming missile. In the vastness of space, that 's a pretty tall order. This latest test follows reports of another test in October that at first was said to be successful. It later turned out that it, too, failed. Should the United States continue its research and tests into such a system? Yes. After all, technology does change. But there is no need to rush into a costly and possibly unworkable system too soon."

USA Today, January 21, 2000: "Why Rush Missile Defense?" "Unfortunately, the administration also has set a late-summer deadline for a decision on whether to build the system, and that is foolish. The system isn't likely to be ready, and in any case, time is needed to address issues raised by our allies and by abrogating a key weapons treaty, which the U.S. has never done. The risk of a bad decision is high."

The Washington Post, January 22, 2000: "The Anti-Missile Clock" "President Clinton rhas promised to decide by this summer whether to move ahead with deployment of a national missile defense system. The promise was made mainly for political reasons; it was a way of buying time and maneuvering room on the issue a year ago. It has become increasingly clear, however, that by summer Mr. Clinton won't have sufficient information to make a reliable decision. Tests of the elaborate technology have been inconclusive and likely will remain so. The decision should be deferred; the administration should begin to lay the groundwork for deferral now. The issue is too important to be driven by election-year considerations. This is not the right way to measure which party is strongest on national defense, and the president should muster the courage to say so."

The Hartford Courant, January 26, 2000: "National Missile Defense Is a Waste" "The failure Jan. 18 of the Pentagon 's latest anti-missile test is not just a temporary setback but a red flag to question our nation 's spending priorities. Even the Pentagon 's own expert panel has admitted the program is riddled with waste and is on a headlong rush to failure. ' Sure it 's tempting to think we 've got the technical smarts to develop a system that will protect the U.S. from the incoming missiles of "rouge states".... But there 's precious little evidence that this plan will ever get past the idea stage, let alone work in wartime conditions.
