Summit concludes:
Arms Control Progress Will Wait for New President
   WASHINGTON - June 5 - The June 4 - 5 summit between U.S. President Clinton and Russian President Putin was hardly a disappointment: little was expected, and little was achieved. A few minor agreements were signed: the elimination of 34 tons of excess weapons plutonium and the establishment of a joint U.S./Russian missile detection center.

At one point, this summit might have been produced arms control agreements that would eliminate thousands of nuclear weapons in both countries. It might have been an opportunity to agree to take nuclear weapons off alert. It might have been an opportunity to set the stage for significant arms control progress in the next Administration. Unfortunately, the opportunity has passed for any of these significant deals to be signed and ratified.

One of the major failings of this summit was the inability of Clinton and Putin to sign a START III treaty. START III, originally aimed at reducing strategic nuclear weapons to 2,000 - 2,500 per side, could have gone as low as 1,000 - 1,500, as Russia advocates, but the Administration unfortunately said "no thank you," bowing to Pentagon resistance to deeper reductions.

The Administration's continued insistence of pursuing deployment of a national missile defense has served to unite the rest of the world against U.S. plans. Russians, Chinese, Germans, French and other leaders made clear their opposition to U.S deployment (although the President will not make a deployment decision until later this year).

An arms control stalemate exists, as a result of the American quest for an anti-ballistic missile system, driven by politics, not technology. Significant arms control progress will now rest in the hands of the next U.S. president.
