Speaking Tour Takes African Scholars and Activists to American Cities to Discuss U.S. Policy in Africa
   PHILADELPHIA - March 21 - A group of African scholars and activists will visit college campuses across the southeastern region of the U.S. to promote better understanding of issues facing African nations. Topics range from the floods in Mozambique to slavery in Mauritania and Sudan to economic development throughout the continent. The annual Africa Peace Tour, sponsored by the American Friends Service Committee, will be launched from Atlanta, GA on April 9th and conclude on April 15th in New Orleans, LA.

Tour organizers believe that in order to improve U.S. geopolitical and economic policies toward Africa, citizens of this country must be informed about current African issues. The tour takes African activists to various communities to increase U.S. citizens' understanding of the diverse cultures, languages, religions, economic and social structures on the African continent.

"While the pictures of the recent floods in Mozambique have captured the attention of U.S. citizens, the issues underlying Africa's problems with poverty, warfare, and economic debt are rarely discussed in the U.S. media," said Jerry Herman, coordinator of the Africa Program in AFSC's Peace Building Unit and the Africa Peace Tour. "During the Mozambican disaster, for instance, the question of why Mozambique lacked the capacity to even minimally cope with the floods, was never asked," Herman said.

The Africa Peace Tour brings Africans directly to U.S. audiences promoting personal contact, which gives local citizens access to the continent on an understandable, compelling, and deeply personal level. Ultimately, the Africa Peace Tour seeks to promote organized U.S. citizen action that will influence U.S. policies toward Africa.

Herman said that many concerns of Africans stopping the flow of arms in their communities, stemming the outflow of natural and human resources, preventing violence, and seeking an economic livelihood for all people are similar to concerns of U.S. citizens. "We must support African peoples' right to select their own governments, to demand responsible governance and to build societies free from outside economic and military pressures," Herman said.

The speakers, from varied political, academic and activist backgrounds, are from Southern Sudan, Mozambique, Liberia, Mauritania, Rwanda and Western Sahara.

Its work is based on the belief in the worth of every person and faith in the power of love to overcome violence and injustice.

Speakers will include :

Jose Caetano - long time Mozambican political activist and founding member of the Frelimo movement, Jose worked at the side of late President Samora Machel from the beginning of the colonial struggle until Machel's death in 1986. Jose has just returned from Mozambique and will speak about the recent floods.

Ibrahim Saar - well known Mauritanian poet/journalist who was imprisoned for 5 years for speaking out against the Mauritanian government on the issue of slavery.

Bakary Tandia - President of the Mauritanian Human Rights Committee, has been actively speaking out on the issue of slavery in Mauritania.

Salka Barca - a member of Western Sahara's POLISARIO, a political movement that has been preparing for a referendum on independence from Morocco.

Akuei Malwal - activist from Southern Sudan, involved with educating the U.S. public about the civil war in Sudan. Speaks on the issue of slavery in Sudan and raises issues about recent moves by the U.S. to dialogue with the Khartoum government.

Kalisa Remera - Rwandan refugee who was seriously wounded and lost his family and many friends during the 1994 genocide. He speaks about war and genocide in Africa's Great Lakes Region, especially targeting youth. A book will be released in October 2000 about Kalisa's experience.

Ezekiel Pajibo - activist from Liberia during the period of Samuel Doe. As president of the Liberian National Student's Union, was jailed and beaten for leading students to rebel against Doe's government. He was tried by a military tribunal and sentenced to die; his sentence was commuted hours before the execution. He currently works for the Africa Faith and Justice Network in Washington DC.
