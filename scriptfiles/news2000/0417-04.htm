PSR Welcomes DOE Plan for Compensating Sick Nuclear Workers, Will Scrutinize Implementation
   WASHINGTON - April 17 - The following was released today by Physicians for Social Responsbility (PSR):

Elements in the DOE plan for compensating sick nuclear workers include the offer of either $100,000 compensation or a comprehensive package including health care costs, lost wages and job training. However, it will be necessary to examine the plan in detail before it is possible to assess its merits and demerits fully.

In particular, PSR is concerned to ensure that:

-- The DOE promise that the burden of proof on the cause of illness should not fall on workers is actually kept;

-- Workers' families, and communities surrounding nuclear weapons facilities are also be compensated for illness or the loss of loved ones;

-- The plan is put into operation immediately. Workers, their families and others are suffering now, and need all the assistance they can get immediately. The $17 million allocated in FY2001 to fund the plan is clearly inadequate to allow this.

-- The new Occupational Illness Compensation Office considers all worker claims fairly and without bias. DOE has promised they will now be the workers advocate but have to act to prove this claim. Truly independent physicians should assess each case, and the burden of proof should be with the government to prove a case bogus.

PSR will remain vigilant. DOE has an appalling record in treatment of its workers over 50 years. This announcement of a major change in DOE policy will have to be verified in practice. Independent scrutiny of DOE actions in implementing the plan through the coming years will be essential. PSR, whose authoritative and ground-breaking publication Dead Reckoning first brought this terrible legacy of nuclear weapons production to public notice, is uniquely placed to scrutinize DOE and will continue to monitor activities closely, bringing any backsliding to public attention. PSR will also push for the extension of this scheme to all affected communities, and for recognition of the fundamental problem that nuclear weapons production is a threat to public health. PSR will continue its work to bring an end to nuclear weapons production, and for their early abolition.

"DOE plan is a step in the right direction," said Kimberly Roberts, PSR Program Associate. "Nuclear weapons workers, and their families need help urgently. Communities living around polluted DOE plants are not yet included in the plan. DOE has made a good start, it should work with Congress to get the job finished."

PSR is also concerned that DOE regards this issue as a past problem that can be solved once and for all. In fact, while nuclear weapons production continues, the pollution of workers, communities and the environment continues. It is unlikely that this plan can function for only 3-4 years as DOE envisage, and then be scaled down drastically.

"This is not a problem that will just go away," said Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., executive director of PSR. "The only cure for this sickness is prevention, which means the end of nuclear weapons production."
