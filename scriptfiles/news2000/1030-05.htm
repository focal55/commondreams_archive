National Interfaith Leader Calls on Political Candidates and Surrogates to Stop Partisan Campaigning in Houses of Worship
   WASHINGTON - October 30 - Calling on political candidates and their surrogates to refrain from using houses of worship as places to endorse candidates and partisan agendas, the Rev. Dr. C. Welton Gaddy, executive director of The Interfaith Alliance, issued the following statement:

“With only 8 days before Election Day, votes are getting more and more precious. But the urgency of campaigns can not be allowed to weaken our protection of religious integrity. In recent weeks, The Interfaith Alliance has issued a call to religious leaders across the country, asking them to reject partisan voter guides and reject candidates using their houses of worship as places to campaign. Political candidates violate the sanctity of religion by seeking votes from pulpits.

“Yesterday, President Clinton visited Alfred Street Baptist Church in Alexandria, VA and Shiloh Baptist Church in Washington, DC, and implicitly asked members of these congregations to support particular candidates and a specific political agenda. The President is not the only politician to speak in a house of worship during this campaign. Other candidates have made recent appearances in churches touting their partisan agendas. With the same vigor with which The Interfaith Alliance has denounced the distribution of partisan voter guides in houses of worship by organizations like the Christian Coalition, we are also calling on all political candidates to refrain from politicizing pulpits and imploring partisan votes from within worshipping congregations.

“The Interfaith Alliance has issued public calls to President Clinton, Governor Bush and Vice President Gore asking them not to use houses of worship across the country for partisan get-out-the-vote operations. As political candidates who have professed their faith openly to the American people, it is my hope that they understand the risks they pose to houses of worship that engage in this political activity.

“While I wholeheartedly encourage efforts of clergy to inform and to inspire their congregants to cast informed votes on Election Day, political endorsements, or even the appearance of a political endorsement, from the pulpit or any sacred desk threatens to compromise the sanctity of religion.

“The campaigns of this election cycle have contained an unprecedented amount of religious rhetoric with candidates making overt declarations about the importance of religious faith in their lives. People of faith will play a decisive role—perhaps even the decisive role—in the outcome of this election. I see that as a positive prospect because the core values that cut across our varied religious traditions are values that strengthen the moral fabric and political process of our nation. We need people of faith involved in electoral politics. Indeed, many of us see voting as a spiritual discipline. However, how people of faith vote—for whom people of faith vote—is a matter of individual choice.”

Earlier this fall, The Interfaith Alliance issued an “Election Year Guide for Houses of Worship,” which provides religious leaders with key legal and ethical guidelines for the roles they can and should play in this election year. To read it online, visit www.interfaithalliance.org.

Founded in 1994, The Interfaith Alliance (TIA) is a non-partisan clergy-led grassroots organization. Drawing on shared religious principles, TIA advances a mainstream faith-based agenda dedicated to promoting the positive and healing role of religion in public life and challenges those who manipulate religion to promote an extreme political agenda. With more than 120,000 members drawn from over 50 faith traditions, and a national network of activists in every state, TIA promotes civility, mutual respect and cooperation in our increasingly-diverse society.
