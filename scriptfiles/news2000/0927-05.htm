Joint Chiefs Bang Tin Cup Once Again
   WASHINGTON - September 27 - The Joint Chiefs of Staff testified today before the House and Senate Armed Services Committees repeating their call for increased defense spending. While Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff General Henry Shelton refused to give an overall figure, each of the individual service Chiefs suggested increases that total between $48 to $58 billion per year.

Several Senators including Sen. Max Cleland (D-GA) and Sen. Pat Roberts (R-KS) echoed a remark previously made by Marine Corps Commandant Gen. James Jones recommending defense spending comprise 4-4.5 percent of Gross Domestic Product (GDP).

Under either the above GDP proposal or the Chiefs' plan, by 2005 the Pentagon's budget would increase to $483 to $544 billion or 70 to 80 percent of all discretionary spending as estimated by the Office of Management and Budget. "This would be tantamount to a declaration of war on education and health care," said John Isaacs, president of the arms control organization Council for a Livable World. Defense spending currently comprises one half of all discretionary spending (funds which Congress has direct control over).

Each of the Chiefs claimed that without significant increases for procurement they will not be able to maintain forces capable of conducting two major theater wars nearly simultaneously. But this strategy has come under increasing attack as a means of artificially maintaining a Cold War sized and armed force, even though the U.S. faces very few significant military threats.

"That's the real problem," said Isaacs, "this outdated and unrealistic two war requirement allows the chiefs to claim there is a huge gap between current funding and what is needed. Eliminate that requirement and they will be hard pressed to tell the American people that the $310 billion dollars the Pentagon will get next year is simply not enough."

Despite the fact that all four services will reach their recruiting goals this year and funding for readiness accounts such as spare parts has increased dramatically, the Chiefs pointed to mounting readiness problems. Shelton acknowledged there will be an "increasing trend in current readiness", but questioned the ability to maintain readiness in the future without massive increases in modernization accounts. He claimed that the services have been forced to "rob Peter to pay Paul" by using procurement funds to pay for immediate readiness needs.

Congress and the Pentagon waste billions each year by funding Cold War era weapons such as the F-22 fighter aircraft when current proven technologies are cheaper and still the most advanced in the world. Sen. McCain (R-AZ) also estimates that the fiscal 2001 Defense Appropriations bill contains $7 billion in unneeded, unrequested pork, and the Pentagon claims it could save $3 billion a year if Congress would authorize two more rounds of base closures.

"Sounds to me like Congress is ‘robbing Peter to pay pork'," said Isaacs
