Survey Documents Causes And Problems Of Identity Theft
  

LOS ANGELES - May 1 - Identity theft victims spend two years or more removing an average of $18,000 in fraudulent charges from their credit reports, according to a new report based on a survey of recent identity theft victims.  The report, by CALPIRG and the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse documents the various ways that consumers become victims of the fast-growing crime and makes recommendations for state and federal legislative action.

“Unless new laws force banks, department stores and credit bureaus to clean up the identity theft mess, this crime is only going to get worse,” said Janine Benner, CALPIRG Consumer Associate and report co-author  “This study shows that nightmares you hear on the news are not just extreme cases  and that identity theft can happen to anyone.”

Key Findings of “Nowhere To Turn: Victims Speak Out On Identity Theft”: 

  • The average amount of time it took victims to resolve their cases was nearly 2 years (23 months). Victims who have not resolved their cases have been dealing with the problem for an average of 44 months.
  • The average total fraudulent charges on new and existing accounts of those surveyed was $18,000. Victims spent an average of 175 hours and $808 in out-of-pocket costs (not including lawyer’s fees) trying to fix their problem.
  • 76% of the respondents who contacted the police found them to be unhelpful; 60% found credit bureaus, banks and department stores unhelpful.
  • 45% of the reported cases involved the victims’ driver’s licenses.
  • 15% of the respondents noted that there was a criminal investigation or warrant for their arrest due to their identity theft.
In 1998, Congress enacted legislation criminalizing identity theft.  California has also criminalized identity theft and taken other actions, including strengthening address change verification requirements to make it harder for thieves to intercept mail and apply for credit at new addresses. By making identity theft a specific crime, Congress and the states have taken an important first step in fighting it.  Yet, much more needs to be done.  Industry statistics estimate that between 500,000 and 700,000 people will have their identities stolen this year.

“Clearly, credit industry reform is the key to curbing this crime,” said Beth Givens, Privacy Rights Clearinghouse director, “Further, easy access to the bits of information that compromise a consumer’s financial identity must be curtailed.”

Among the highlights of the CALPIRG/Privacy Rights Clearinghouse Identity Theft Platform are the following:

  • Give consumers free credit reports annually on request as six states already do (CO, GA, MA, MD, NJ, VT). Proposals by US Senator Dianne Feinstein and U.S. Rep. Lucille Roybal-Allard include this provision.
  • Provide victims, as well as consumers, with the right to block access to their credit reports. A proposal by State Senator Debra Bowen would do this.
  • Improve change of address verification, a common indicator of fraud. And limit uses of the Social Security number, the key identifier needed by identity thieves. See Sen. Bowen’s bill.
  • Streamline law enforcement investigation and victim assistance procedures. Assemblymember Robert Hertzberg has introduced a bill establishing pilot programs.
  • Close the “credit header” loophole that allows Social Security numbers to be sold on the information marketplace, including over the Internet. Senator Feinstein’s and other Congressional bills would include this provision.
