CRACK APPEARS IN U.S.-COLOMBIAN DRUG WAR Senators Kennedy and Leahy Question Colombian Aid Package
  

WASHINGTON - January 30 - In a broadside against the Clinton administration's $1.6 billion anti-drug aid package to Colombia, two influential senators voiced strong opposition to the plan weeks before it was announced. Senators Patrick Leahy (D-VT) and Edward M. Kennedy (D-MA) sent a letter to Secretary of State Madeleine K. Albright in December questioning the wisdom of pouring billions of additional tax dollars into the Colombian anti-narcotics sink hole. The letter, obtained by Arms Trade Insider, noted that "hundreds of millions of dollars in assistance to the Colombian security forces, has not reduced coca cultivation in Colombia, the flow of cocaine or heroin to the U.S. from Colombia, or the profits of drug traffickers." Despite the administration's belief that Congressional approval is a given, the senators' letter revealed a major crack in support for Colombian drug aid.

In a recent interview, Leahy went even further by voicing a long-held fear on Capitol Hill that the aid program is a "counter-insurgency policy in the name of a counter-drug policy." The terminology used by both Colombian President Andres Pastrana and the Clinton administration supports Leahy's view that this aid is earmarked to fight insurgents. During his Washington visit earlier this week, Pastrana tipped the administration's hand, remarking that "If [the rebels] are in the [drug] business, logically, [the aid] is going to be used against them." In Colombia, narco- traffickers are indistinguishable from insurgents.

It is misleading for the administration to suggest that U.S. military aid will be used only to stop the drug trade. In a White House fact sheet outlining the administration's aid plan, the first goal is to help "the Colombian Government push into coca-growing regions of southern Colombia, which are now dominated by insurgent guerrillas." Clearly, providing Colombia with 30 Blackhawk and 33 Huey transport helicopters, as well as radar, aircraft and airfield upgrades, will help them fight a war against insurgents, not just traffickers.

The Clinton administration's decision to provide more military aid to the Colombian armed forces is an escalation of U.S. involvement in Colombia's civil war. An erie parallel exists between U.S. involvement in Colombia today and U.S. involvement in Vietnam in the early 1960's. The U.S. already suffered its first casualties in Colombia last July, when five Army personnel were killed in a plane crash while providing intelligence and reconnaissance support for a combined U.S.-Colombian anti-drug mission. The question to ask is: how long will the U.S. continue to risk American lives and resources by militarizing the drug war in Colombia. Rather, the administration should follow Kennedy and Leahy's advice and "provide stronger support for diplomatic efforts to strengthen the peace process and thereby create the conditions necessary for a more effective counter-narcotics strategy, which should include alternative development programs."
