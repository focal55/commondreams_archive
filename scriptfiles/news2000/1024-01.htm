DC Area Consumers Pay More for Prescription Drugs While Pharmaceutical Profits Soar
   WASHINGTON - October 24 - According to a survey released today by the national consumer groups Public Citizen, U.S. PIRG and the Gray Panthers, consumers without prescription drug coverage are charged twice as much as the drug industry's most favored customers. The organizations were joined by residents of the Senior Citizens Counseling and Delivery Center.

The D.C. Metro survey of 33 randomly selected pharmacies compared the prices of the nine most commonly prescribed medications used by senior citizens, three of which are the most widely prescribed to all consumers. Overall, the average retail price charged to Medicare beneficiaries without drug coverage was $201 versus $102 for most favored customers like the Department of Veterans Affairs. Overcharges ranged from 74% for the cholesterol medication Lipitor to 221% for the ulcer medication Pepcid. The D.C. Metro results are similar to those of 12 other surveys that Public Citizen and local groups have conducted in ten other states.

"The study shows that if the Medicare program received the same price discount as the Veterans Department, the price of drugs to uninsured Medicare beneficiaries could be cut in half," said Frank Clemente, Director of Public Citizen's Congress Watch. "Such a price cut would make Medicare drug coverage much more affordable for taxpayers and substantially reduce out-of-pocket costs for beneficiaries."

"D.C. Metro seniors are at a dual disadvantage -- because they are uninsured they have to pay out of pocket and they are charged more than double what the insured pay," said Rachel Weintraub, Staff Attorney at U.S. PIRG. "Congress should act to make prescription drugs more affordable, and states should pass legislation allowing them to negotiate lower drug prices for all consumers," she said.

As many as 127,000 seniors in the D.C. metro area have no prescription drug coverage and thousands of others including the uninsured and people with disabilities have little or no coverage.

"D.C. metro area senior citizens demand a prescription drug plan that allows Medicare to negotiate the same price discounts with the drug industry that other federal agencies receive," said Tim Fuller, Executive Director of the Gray Panthers. "If Congress refuses to give Medicare such authority it should pass the Allen-Johnson bill, which would require drug makers to sell drugs to local pharmacies at the same price given to most favored customers - 40-50% off retail."

"I know that if I don't take my medicine- I will die. I have had to cut back on my medication to make them last longer," said D.C. resident Eleanor Freeman who lives on a $831 Social Security check each month. "My medicines cost at least $150 each month. I should have the security of knowing that I will be able to afford the medication I need as well as the food I must eat. I do not have that security now," she said.

There are steps that Congress can take to end the prescription drug debate. Passing a comprehensive Medicare drug benefit and granting the Medicare program the authority to negotiate substantial price reductions with the industry will not only help put an end to out of control drug prices, it also will help seniors and other consumers to make ends meet.

Public Citizen is a national membership organization of 150,000 members, including 1,000 in the D.C. metropolitan area. Ralph Nader founded it in 1971 to protect the rights of consumers.

U.S. PIRG is the national lobbying office for state-based Public Interest Research Groups across the country. The state PIRGs is an independent, non-profit, non-partisan public interest advocacy organization.

The Gray Panthers is a national organization of intergenerational activists dedicated to social and economic justice. Founded in 1970, the Gray Panthers today are active in local networks coast-to-coast, working for progressive change in communities and across the nation through civic engagement.
