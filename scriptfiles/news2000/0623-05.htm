Union of Concerned Scientists: Closing Fuel Efficiency Gaps Would Save Every SUV Driver $3,500
  WASHINGTON - June 23 - Soaring gas prices have unleashed a wave of dubious remedies, from eliminating clean air laws to increasing U.S. oil drilling.

But a better solution lies in reducing oil consumption by making Detroit's hottest product, the Sport Utility Vehicle, more fuel-efficient. If SUVs were as clean as the average car, oil consumption in the United States would be down 5 percent from a decade ago; instead it is up over 10 percent. And fuel use will continue to grow if the SUV craze continues.

"Repealing air quality laws or drilling in pristine wilderness to ease gas prices is like shooting yourself in the foot to cure a headache," said Jason Mark, transportation program co-director at the Union of Concerned Scientists. "Since SUVs are the leading cause of increased gasoline consumption, making these vehicles more efficient provides better long-term protection."

Current loopholes in federal fuel efficiency standards allow SUVs and other light trucks to burn more gasoline than cars. The loopholes leave consumers and the environment to bear the burden of gas-guzzling SUVs. On average, light trucks use 42 percent more fuel than cars to travel each mile, costing their drivers an extra $27 billion this year at the pump. Less efficient light trucks have meant an additional 240 million tons per year of global warming pollution entering our atmosphere.

But the technology exists to remedy these problems. A UCS report released last year, Greener SUVs, laid out the technological pathways to protect the planet and save drivers money. Stronger fuel economy standards would give automakers the incentive to use these technologies.

"If new trucks were as efficient as today's cars, consumers would save more than $3,500 over the life of the vehicle at today's prices," said Mark. "Automakers have the technologies to bridge the gas guzzling gap, but government and consumers need to pressure Detroit to install them."
