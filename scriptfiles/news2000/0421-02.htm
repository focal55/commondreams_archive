PSR Welcomes Russian Ratification Of Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, Calls on U.S. to Follow Suit
   WASHINGTON - April 21 - The following was released today by Physicians for Social Responsibility:

Physicians for Social Responsibility welcomes the ratification by the Russian Duma of the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT). The CTBT is a vital cornerstone of global efforts to prevent the proliferation of nuclear weapons and a crucial step on the road to their elimination.

PSR was founded with a campaign to end nuclear testing. Testing poses a grave threat to public health by releasing radioactive elements (such as Strontium-90), leading to the contamination of babies through their mother's milk. For 40 years, PSR has been a leader in the global struggle to end all nuclear testing.

The CTBT was negotiated in 1996 and approved by overwhelming vote in the UN General Assembly. 155 nations have now signed. 44 key states, including the US, must ratify the treaty before it can enter into force. The treaty bans all nuclear explosions, however small. It will be verified with a global monitoring regime that would increase global security.

Russia is the third nuclear power to ratify the Treaty, after the UK and France. Only the US and China, among the five acknowledged nuclear weapon states, have failed to ratify the Treaty. This ratification, along with ratification of START II by Russia and the beginning of START III negotiations between Russia and the US will be a major boost for the nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) Review Conference that starts in New York on April 24.

However, the U.S. will now come under strong international pressure to live up to its obligations and ratify the CTBT. The U.S. Senate notoriously voted against ratification of the CTBT in October 1999. The Senate's action undermined 40 years of US policy to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons. As a result, the U.S. will be isolated at the NPT, alongside nuclear holdouts India, Pakistan and Israel.

PSR, along with the international community of nations, regards the CTBT as vital. This treaty will prevent current nuclear weapon states from developing new generations of nuclear weapons. It will also discourage potential nuclear proliferators from developing weapons of mass destruction. Both are significant steps along the road to fulfilling the mission of the NPT: the abolition of nuclear weapons.

"The public health consequences of nuclear testing have been disastrous, so Russia must be congratulated for ratifying the CTBT," said Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., CEO and executive director of PSR. "U.S. Senators who defeated the Treaty last year should hang their heads in shame. The U.S. has forfeited its position as a global leader in the reduction of nuclear dangers. Instead, the Senate risks becoming a leader of rogue states in a nuclear weapons free-for-all."

Robert K. Musil Ph.D. is available for interview.
