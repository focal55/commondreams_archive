Proposal Aims to Keep Public in Dark on Chemical Disaster Potential In Their Neighborhood
   WASHINGTON - May 2 - Proposed federal regulations could prevent the public from learning about dangerous chemicals potentially being spilled or released in their own communities. Senator Frank Lautenberg (D-NJ), however, is working to support the public's right-to-know about chemical accidents, earning the Sierra Club's praise and thanks. The environmental organization is joining Lautenberg in calling on Congressional leaders for hearings and the release of chemical disaster information.

"The old adage `what you don't know can't hurt you' falls flat when it comes to chemical disasters," said Debbie Sease. "What you don't know can kill you. But if communities are well informed, they can ask local chemical facilities to clean up their acts and reduce their risk of a chemical disaster. To save lives, prevent pollution and protect property, Sierra Club insists that leaders in Congress defend the public's right to know about the potential chemical disasters threatening their neighborhoods."

In the 1990 Clean Air Act, Congress gave citizens the power to learn about potential chemical accidents in an effort to improve public safety and to encourage companies to reduce their hazards. The law requires facilities that handle extremely hazardous substances to report what could happen under scenarios for different chemical accidents.

The chemical industry and its Congressional supporters worked to rescind the public's right to know, arguing that terrorists attacks might be prevented by keeping the information secret. Anti-environmental members of Congress claimed that criminals might target their attacks by using the Internet to learn about chemical accident information. Now, the Administration has proposed regulations to narrow people's access to the information.

"The real problem isn't Internet access," said Sease. "The real problem isn't releasing the information, either, since terrorists don't need this information to find chemical plants. The real problem is that dangerous facilities threaten families with toxic disasters. If these facilities are a terrorist threat, the answer isn't to keep the public in the dark -- the answer is to reduce the amount of hazardous chemicals and improve site security."

Fortunately, some in Congress -- led by Senator Lautenberg -- are fighting to protect the public's right to know. "Sierra Club applauds Senator Frank Lautenberg's efforts to improve the public's health and safety protections. We join Senator Lautenberg in calling for hearings on site security and hazards," Sease said. "We're very anxious to see whether Congressional leaders will hear his message: that the public's right to know is best protected by improving facility security and reducing the risks of chemical accidents."

Facts about chemical accidents: Nearly 60,000 chemical accidents occur each year; more than 400 per year cause immediate death, injury, and/or evacuation. Toxic chemical accidents kill more than 250 people annually and release over 135 million pounds of toxic chemicals into the environment.
