Presidential Candidates Refuse to Meet with Former UNSCOM Weapons Inspector About Iraq Sanctions Policy; Two UN Officials Resign
   CONCORD, NH - February 17 - The following was released today by New Hampshire Peace Action:

Scott Ritter, the former UNSCOM weapons inspector and an authority on Iraq's military capabilities, continues to be ignored by presidential candidates who refuse to meet with him.

Meanwhile, in the last three days two UN officials, Hans von Sponeck, head of the "Oil for Food" Program, and Jutta Burghardt, head of the UN's World Food Program in Iraq, have resigned their posts in protest of the Iraq sanctions policy. In addition to Denis Halliday, the former head of the "Oil for Food" program, that makes four high ranking UN officials who have denounced the sanctions.

Candidates George W. Bush, John McCain, Al Gore, and Bill Bradley, have all been challenged to meet with Scott Ritter. The four were notified of Ritter's desire to speak with them at recent open forums around New Hampshire as well as through repeated phone calls and faxes to their campaign offices.

Ritter has challenged all politicians to answer this question: "If you want to displace Saddam, how many American soldiers are you willing to get killed? If your answer is zero, how many Iraqi kids are you willing to kill under the sanctions?" Ritter stated that the sanctions strengthen's Saddam's control over Iraq and are both strategically ineffective and morally reprehensible. Ritter stated, "I resent having the moral character of the U.S. stained in this manner."

Bradley's campaign promised to speak with Ritter over a week ago but has not followed through. Ritter did speak with McCain's policy aides and he was told they would advise the Senator of Ritter's positions and his desire for a meeting. However, McCain has so far refused to meet with Ritter and will not alter his position on the Iraq sanctions policy. Both Gore and Bush refuse to comment.

According to the UN, sanctions kill over 90,000 civilians each year and more than 5,000 children each month from disease and lack of proper medications that are banned under the "dual usage" program. After ten years, the sanctions have failed to remove Saddam while Iraqi civilians, mostly children, have continued to suffer and die.
