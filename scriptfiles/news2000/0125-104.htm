Brower will be available during part of the evening for exclusive media interviews.

Where: Center of New Hampshire Holiday Inn
700 Elm St.
The Board Room (Mezzanine Level)

Who:
David Brower is the founder and chairman of Earth Island
Institute. At 87, Brower is widely recognized as the senior statesman of the American environmental movement. Designated by The New York Times as the most effective conservation activist in the world, Brower has been involved in conservation battles since 1938. Brower served as executive director of the Sierra Club from 1952 to 1969. In 1969, he founded Friends of the Earth. In 1999, Brower became the founding co-chair of the Alliance for Sustainable Jobs and the Environment. The author of several books, Brower most recently wrote, "Let the Mountains Talk, Let the Rivers Run," a treatise for those who would save the Earth. He has led the fight to keep dams out of the Grand Canyon and loggers out of Olympic National Park; establish the National Wilderness Preservation System and add seven new regions to the National Park System. Brower has been nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize three times (1978, 1979 and 1998) for his work to protect the planet.

From Seattle to Des Moines to New Hampshire: The People's
Challenge and the People's Agenda

Round One, The Battle in Seattle,
Round Two, The People's Challenge, Des Moines, Iowa
Round Three, The People's Agenda, Manchester, New Hampshire

On Jan. 12, a grassroots alliance of more than 100 environmental, religious, farm and other organizations issued a "People's Challenge" to the presidential candidates in Des Moines, Iowa, at a National Environment, Labor and Farm Day of Action. Following on the heels of the protests in Seattle against the World Trade Organization and its assault on farmers, workers and the environment, some of these same groups and organizations are now carrying infusing the spirit of Seattle into the presidential campaign arena. The groups are demanding that the presidential candidates begin addressing America's growing farm, worker and environmental crisis. As the citizens' alliance grows, we will add other issues (e.g. campaign finance reform) to the "People's Agenda." A delegation of leaders from the various organizations traveled to the campaign offices of the major presidential candidates in Des Moines to deliver (and hand-delivered to candidates Bradley, McCain, Hatch, Forbes and Bauer) "The People's Challenge":

We challenged each and every candidate for president of the United States to articulate a plan on the following questions by no later than Wednesday, Jan. 19.

1) What is your plan for the management of our federal public lands?
2) What is your plan for guaranteeing workers the freedom to organize without retaliation from corporations, and to protect workers who strike from being permanently replaced?
3) What is your plan from protecting small family farms and other small businesses in this country?

Gore and Bradley's Iowa staff were cordial and engaging. Bush's staff were nervous and offered us home-baked cookies. Forbes' staff showed us the door. Despite promises by several of the candidates and their staff to respond, none did.

On Thursday morning, Jan. 27, we will send a delegation to engage the candidates themselves at local campaign stops in Manchester to discuss the People's Agenda. This will be followed by a press conference to share with the voters what we have learned about the candidates' positions on the issues that matter to millions of Americans.

Further details on Thursday's delegation visits and press conference will be released to the media on Wednesday.
