Outraged Environmental Groups Call on Clinton/Gore to Correct Risky Dogfish Shark Policies
   MONTEREY, CA - February 15 - The following was released today by the Center for Marine Conservation:

Conservationists gathered in California for an international shark conference expressed outrage at today's announcement that the secretary of commerce has bowed to pressure from Massachusetts politicians and is delaying a long-overdue management plan for over-fished dogfish sharks off the New England and Mid-Atlantic coasts. This decision, coupled with the secretary's continued failure to put an end to the wasteful practice of shark finning in the Western Pacific, has prompted a coalition of environmental groups to send a letter to President Clinton and Vice President Gore requesting that the administration address these urgent shark management issues.

"Through inaction and delay, the administration is allowing sharks in U.S. waters to be grossly over-fished and killed solely for their fins. These short-sighted, risky policies affect some of the ocean's most vulnerable animals," said Sonja Fordham, shark specialist for the Center for Marine Conservation, who serves on two federal advisory panels for dogfish and other sharks. "We are calling on the administration to put an end to this abuse of sharks and the process by which they are managed."

Sharks are among the most biologically vulnerable species over which the Department of Commerce has management responsibility. Their slow growth, late maturity, and small number of offspring leave them exceptionally vulnerable to over-fishing and slow to recover from depletion. At the same time, sharks, as top predators, are essential to maintaining the balance of life in the sea.

Off the New England and Mid-Atlantic coasts, spiny dogfish sharks are completely unprotected from an intense, selective fishery for mature females. Regional fishery management councils have spent years developing a plan to stop over-fishing and rebuild the depleted breeding stock within ten years, as mandated by the Sustainable Fisheries Act. After months of delay, the secretary of commerce finalized the plan last month, but has now further delayed implementation in response to political pressure from a handful of members of Congress from Massachusetts -- the top dogfishing state.

"The short-term economic interests of one state are now jeopardizing the health of the region's marine ecosystem as well as the conservation record of the nation," said National Audubon Society scientist Dr. Merry Camhi author of several reports examining shark management in state waters and member of the dogfish advisory panel.

"Delaying the dogfish shark plan could cause the collapse of the population and subverts the public process for conserving our nation's public marine resources," added Wildlife Conservation Society's Dr. Ellen Pikitch, who chairs the New England Fishery
Management Council's Scientific and Statistical Committee (SSC). More than a year ago, the SSC warned the Council that immediate restrictions on dogfish catch were needed to avoid serious and long-standing population decline.

Off Hawaii, shark finning, slicing off a shark's valuable fins and discarding the sometimes-live body at sea, has increased in recent years by more than 2000 percent. Finning is banned in the U.S. Atlantic, and Department of Commerce officials have repeatedly warned the Western Pacific Fishery Management Council that continuation of this wasteful practice will not be tolerated. Despite the council's refusal to act, the secretary of commerce has failed to follow through with a ban. In response, Reps. Cunningham (R-Calif.) and Saxton (R-N.J.) have introduced a bill to prohibit finning in all U.S. waters.

"Shark finning in the U.S. Western Pacific is an egregious waste of public resources and contributes to over-fishing of poorly understood shark populations," stated Russ Dunn of the Ocean Wildlife Campaign. "While we strongly support the efforts of Congress to halt finning, we are gravely concerned that the secretary of commerce has left such a glaring stewardship void."

The groups are calling on the administration to immediately enact the dogfish plan and to ban shark finning in all U.S. waters. These actions would improve consistency with U.S. shark policies in other regions of the country as well as internationally.

"In recent years, the United States has emerged as a leading proponent of several global initiatives aimed at conserving shark populations worldwide," added Andy Oliver, program officer with the World Wildlife Fund who was involved in the design of the 1999 United Nations International Plan of Action for Sharks. "Failure by this administration to address these glaring inconsistencies in U.S. shark policy undermines our nation's credibility and leadership role in the international fisheries arena."

Representatives from Environmental Defense, Natural Resources Defense Council, the World Conservation Union's Shark Specialist Group, and the Mote Marine Laboratory's Center for Shark Research, among others also signed the letter to the administration.
