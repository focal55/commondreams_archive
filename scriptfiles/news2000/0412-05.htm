Elephant Ivory, Whales, and Sea Turtles Stirring Debate at CITES Conference Being Held in Nairobi
  NAIROBI, KENYA - April 12 - As over 2,000 CITES delegates enter their third day of debate over proposals which will affect the future of several dozen of the world's threatened species, three high profile issues have come to dominate discussions. On this year's CITES agenda are proposals by four southern African countries to allow trade in elephant ivory, proposals by Norway and Japan to downlist minke and grey whales, and a proposal by Cuba to allow trade of hawksbill turtle to Japan.

Widely considered the world's most important wildlife agreement, the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) is the only global convention that regulates trade in endangered and threatened animals and plants. Meeting April 10-20 in Nairobi, Kenya, CITES delegates are currently debating over 60 proposals affecting species threatened by trade.

At this year's meeting, World Wildlife Fund will seek stronger protection measures for some of the worlds "most wanted" species. Specifically, WWF opposes the resumption of elephant ivory trade, as well any loosening of restrictions on the trade of hawksbill turtle products. Additionally, WWF opposes the proposed downlisting of minke and grey whales from Appendix I to Appendix II, a move which would undermine the work of the International Whaling Commission (IWC) and potentially open up large-scale commercial whaling. WWF's position statements can be accessed at www.worldwildlife.org/cites and www.panda.org.

Since coming into force in 1975, CITES has played a significant role in halting illegal and unsustainable wildlife trade. Each year, millions of animals and plants are shipped around the world to supply the demand for wildlife luxury and food products, traditional medicines, and exotic pets.
