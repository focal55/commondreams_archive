Published on Wednesday, July 6, 2005 by the Associated Press
Hundreds Protest in Pamplona Against Bullfighting
by Adam Karlin
 
Several hundred animal rights activists - some in their underwear - marched through the streets of Pamplona Tuesday to protest bullfighting on the eve of the Spanish city's wildly popular running of the bulls.

The centuries-old festival that draws thousands of daredevils from Spain and around the world kicks off in earnest on Thursday, when six bulls and six steers meant to keep them in a pack rumble along a 850-meter (930-yard) cobblestone course from a holding pen to the city's bullring, with human thrill-seekers running alongside them.

The runs are held every morning, with streets often moist and slippery with dew, for eight days.

The mad dashes and the raucous, non-stop street parties that accompany them were made internationally famous by Ernest Hemingway's novel "The Sun Also Rises."

The festival's ceremonial opening is Wednesday, when a small rocket fired from a town hall balcony signals the start of Spain's most famous festival, named for San Fermin, the patron saint of Pamplona.

Since record keeping began in 1924, 13 people have been killed during the runs. The last was a 22-year-old American, Matthew Tassio, who was gored to death in 1995. This year's edition will pay a special tribute to him to mark the 10-year anniversary.
