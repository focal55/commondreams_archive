Published on Wednesday, July 13, 2005 by Reuters
Was Iraq a Factor in UK Bombs? Lawmakers Wonder
by Mike Peacock
 

LONDON - British politicians of all shades have united since London's bomb attacks but some are beginning to break ranks, wondering whether Tony Blair's backing for war in Iraq may have been a factor. In the immediate aftermath of the attacks on London's transport network, which killed at least 52, only mavericks like firebrand George Galloway dared criticize the prime minister.

Galloway, thrown out of Blair's Labour party only to win a parliamentary seat as an independent, said the invasion of Iraq and "occupation" of Afghanistan had fed hatred and bitterness throughout the world's Islamic communities.

Blair was widely regarded to have acted swiftly and struck the right tone after the attacks, receiving plaudits from world leaders and political friends and foes at home.

But now Charles Kennedy, head of Britain's Liberal Democrats who always opposed the Iraq war, has explored the same theme.

"I am not here implying some causal link between Britain's involvement in Iraq and the terrible terrorist attacks in London last week," he said by way of qualification in a speech.

But he went on: "Those like President Bush and Tony Blair, who have sought to link Iraq with the so-called 'war on terror' can hardly be surprised when members of the public draw the same link when acts of terrorism occur here in the United Kingdom."

Blair's office reacted sharply.

"This kind of terrorism was active long before the Iraq war," Blair's spokesman said. "9/11 was in September 2001, not 2003."

The prime minister told parliament on Wednesday he would issue a list of terrorist attacks around the world since 1993.

"People are able to see this is a process that now stretches back many years. It encompasses many, many different countries," he said.

Last year's Madrid train bombs however were almost certainly because of Spanish troops based in Iraq.

"I would not be at all surprised in those young men's heads ... if part of the rationale behind what they were doing was to inflict punishment killings for British support of U.S. policy," said Dr Shane Brighton at the Royal United Services Institute.

NO BLAME FOR BLAIR

So far, opinion polls show few blame Blair's foreign policy ventures for the attacks on home soil, now revealed to have been carried out by British citizens.

His parliamentary majority was slashed in May elections largely because of disaffection with the war in Iraq but polls showed while the public may distrust him, they overwhelmingly want him as their leader in times of crisis.

Crucially for Blair, London's high profile mayor Ken Livingstone -- a fervent opponent of the Iraq war -- has blamed no one but the bombers for last Thursday's attacks.

Nonetheless, the consensus forged by the bombs will not last for ever.

A handful of Labour members have already said London was hit because of Britain's staunch support for the United States.

And Blair's Conservative opponents have called for an inquiry into the attacks, although on the broader point they are hamstrung by their support for the invasion of Iraq.

"When it is symbolically and politically safe to do, troublesome questions will surface ... about the reasons for the increased threat posed by terrorists," said Steve Richards of the Independent newspaper, a seasoned Blair-watcher.
