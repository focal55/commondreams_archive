Published on Thursday, May 12, 2005 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Tally of Civilian Deaths Depends on Who's Counting
Definitive estimates difficult to obtain
by Jack Epstein and Matthew B. Stannard
 

The reports come in with numbing regularity. Iraqi police blown up by suicide bombs. Government officials murdered in the street. Dozens of insurgents killed by U.S. forces. Shoppers killed when cars explode near busy markets. Grieving mourners killed by explosions at funerals for their loved ones.

In the past two weeks alone, about 400 Iraqis reportedly have died across the country. On Wednesday alone, five car bombs and a man with explosives strapped to his body killed at least 69 people in Baghdad and elsewhere.

But while the incidents and the numbers all start to blur together, they raise once again the hotly debated and problematic issue of Iraqi civilian deaths since the United States invaded in March 2003.

The estimates of ordinary Iraqis killed -- by insurgents, U.S. troops and Iraqi criminals -- vary greatly, from as low as 6,000 up to nearly 100, 000, depending on the methodology used.

Most experts say more precise estimates are complicated by numerous factors, including the Islamic custom of speedy burial, political pressures to inflate or deflate the count and the lack of effective government, especially in rural areas, that make tracking deaths difficult.

"I've also heard of people ... who fear reprisals and don't want names of their dead relatives identified," said Mark Jurgensmeyer, director of global and international studies at UC Santa Barbara.

"On the other side," he added, "numbers are inflated when deaths related to blood feuds or conflict over illegal economic activities are masked as 'terrorist attacks.' The real problem is that for large segments of Iraqi society, there is precious little civil order, so even reading a water meter or giving out a parking ticket is problematic, much less keeping track of violent deaths."

The Defense Department says that more than 1,600 U.S. troops have died since the war began. While it has announced insurgent casualties after major military actions such as the current campaign near the Syrian border, the U.S. military says it does not keep track of civilian deaths. "We don't do body counts," Gen. Tommy Franks, who commanded U.S. troops in both Afghanistan and Iraq, said in 2002.

'An invisible issue' in the U.S.

The Pentagon's stance infuriates activist Andrea Buffa, communications director for San Francisco-based Global Exchange, who visited Baghdad and Fallujah early last year.

"Everywhere I went, I saw people dressed in mourning," she said. "If Americans had any idea of the toll of war, of how many civilians are killed every day, public sentiment would be anti-war. It's a tragedy that it is an invisible issue in the United States."

In March, 23 health experts from five countries, including Dr. Thomas Hall, a UCSF epidemiologist, called for an independent probe on civilian casualties and criticized the U.S. and British governments for being "wholly irresponsible" for not counting such deaths.

"Monitoring casualties is a humanitarian imperative," said the statement. "Understanding the causes of death is a core public-health responsibility nationally and internationally."

Richard Kohn, a professor of military history at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, says that for the Bush administration, "it's more politically advantageous not to count and not to know." But he says he suspects the U.S. military keeps a general count of Iraqi casualties.

"It would be hard for me to believe the military doesn't keep some sense of that," he said. "You need to know how much havoc you're wreaking in a county if you're trying to accomplish something in that country."

Malcolm Muir Jr., a military historian at the Virginia Military Institute, recalls the U.S. military tallying body counts as a "measure of its success" in Vietnam.

But Kohn says that practice ended during the 1990-91 Persian Gulf War. "We were emphasizing the use of air power so much we were unwilling to admit there were significant collateral (civilian) casualties," he said. "The issue is ... how will that play in world opinion?"

Contested counts

Thomas Mockaitis, a history professor specializing in defense and arms control at DePaul University in Chicago, says counting civilian casualties can provide important data, especially in an air war, where the count could show whether the amount of firepower being used is disproportionate to the amount of force necessary to reach a given goal.

But Mockaitis says compiling an accurate tally of civilian deaths is complicated by the fact that in an insurgency, the difference between a combatant and a noncombatant can be clouded.

"By definition, insurgents operate within a sympathetic or at least a tolerant population," he said. "Who is a civilian and who isn't and what is collateral damage is really something that can be contested."

As an example, he pointed to the widely seen footage of a U.S. Marine shooting a wounded and apparently defenseless insurgent in a Fallujah mosque in November. Critics of the war decried the act as the murder of a helpless noncombatant, while supporters of the military called it a legitimate act of combat since insurgents sometimes booby-trap wounded or dead fighters. The military said there was insufficient evidence to charge the Marine with wrongdoing.

In October, the British medical journal Lancet estimated 98,000 Iraqis had died as a result of the invasion and occupation. The study was based on interviews with people in 988 households with 7,868 residents randomly selected across Iraq. Forty-six percent of the violent deaths were of children younger than 15, and 7 percent were women, the researchers reported. The findings were dismissed by the U.S and British governments as inaccurate.

The London-based Iraq Body Count, a Web site that tallies deaths reported by the media or by official bodies and does not include casualty estimates or projections, puts the death toll since March 2003 between 21,523 and 24,415 as of Wednesday.

Humanitarian dimension

The Brookings Institution's Iraq Index estimates between 11,690 and 12, 930 civilians have been killed by insurgents and U.S. military as of April 13. If deaths from crime are included, between 21,490 and 41,600 have been killed as of March 31, according to the Brookings index.

And the Iraqi government, which counts only civilians who have been killed by insurgents and criminal gangs, estimates 6,000 victims as of April 5.

"It speaks well of people who are wondering where we're going and what we're accomplishing," Mockaitis said of those pressuring Washington for an accurate accounting of civilian deaths. "There is a humanitarian dimension, that people are concerned not only about American deaths, but also the deaths of Iraqis -- and they should be."
