Published on Wednesday, October 19, 2005 by the Sydney Morning Herald / Australia
Saddam's Trial Fails to Focus on Atrocities
by Paul McGeough
 

Saddam Hussein is back in the box today, for what Iraqi prosecutors insist is the serious business of dealing with the former dictator's abuse of his people and his power.

Deep within the blast-walled and razor-wired perimeter of Baghdad's green zone, a marble-clad building that Saddam used as a store for gifts he received as Iraqi leader is the setting for a remarkable encounter between the accused and his accusers.

The names of the five judges will not be disclosed - it remains to be seen if their faces will be shown on TV - and witnesses will speak from behind screens. Much of the proceedings will take place behind bullet-proof glass.

But when details of the charges against Saddam, 68, and his seven co-accused are revealed for the first time, many will wonder how an obscure assassination attempt on the former president could become the reason for his actual death.

Instead of atrocities such as the gassing of the people of Halabja in 1988; the death and disappearance of thousands in Saddam's war against Iran and invasion of Kuwait; or his notorious Anfal campaign against the Kurds; all attention will be on the small mixed community of Dujail, 60 kilometres north of the capital.

The gunmen opened fire as Saddam's motorcade swept out of this mixed community on July 8, 1982, after a visit to rally support for the war with Iran.

But instead of speeding away, Saddam ordered his driver to back up and he made a second speech to the people of Dujail. Cameramen accompanying the president failed to capture the attack, but they did record his dire warning that a "small number" of traitors who were "agents of foreigners" [read Iranian-backed] would be rooted out.

In subsequent days, tanks, helicopters and soldiers on foot moved through, killing at least 143 and trucking away hundreds of others - only a handful of whom were heard of again.

Various reasons are given for Dujail being selected as the first case, ranging from a central role in the attempted assassination of members of the now victorious Shiites to a belief that the case against Saddam is easily proved. There are different schools of thought. Foreign observers worry that Saddam never put anything in writing; Iraqis close to the prosecution say documentary proof exists.

But perhaps more importantly, if Saddam is convicted on the comparatively tight circumstances of the Dujail case, it is enough to see him hang without having to proceed with any others in a raft of much bigger cases against him, which would run for years and devour limited resources.

The laws governing Saddam's trial set it apart from those faced by other tyrants of modern times.

Human rights groups have criticised the exclusion of foreign judges and the inability of the Iraqi authorities to commute a death sentence in the event of a guilty verdict.

Coupled with a legal insistence that proceedings based on other charges against an accused are not a reason to delay execution, Saddam would have to be hanged within 30 days of exhausting his right of appeal to a nine-member appeals chamber of the Supreme Iraqi Criminal Tribunal.

One of the most vocal critics of the process by which Saddam will be tried is a human rights group that worked hard to gather material against him, the New York-based Human Rights Watch.

But in a paper published this week the group identified the reasons for its anxiety about the fairness of the process: no requirement for guilt to be proved beyond reasonable doubt; the denial to the accused of the procedural advantages enjoyed by the prosecution; political interference in the court; and the insistence on carrying out the death penalty.

In a statement the group said: "In Iraq's fragile political climate the legitimacy of the court will be in question. To provide a measure of truth and justice for hundreds of thousands of victims of gross human rights violations in Iraq, fair trails are essential."

Saddam's legal team is expected to respond to the reading of the charges today with a demand that the hearing be deferred for as long as six months to allow it to prepare a complete defence.

In the meantime, it remains to be seen whether the sight of Saddam on trial will go any way to fulfilling a hope among Iraqi authorities that his humiliation will dissipate support for the insurgency.
