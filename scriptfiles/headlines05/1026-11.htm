Published on Wednesday, October 26, 2005 by Bloomberg News
Grand Jury in CIA Leak Ends Day With No Announcements
by Richard Keil and Cary O'Reilly
 

WASHINGTON - The federal grand jury hearing evidence in the leak of a CIA agent's identity adjourned for the day without making any announcement of action in the case.


Special Counsel Patrick Fitzgerald, right, leaves the federal courthouse Wednesday, Oct. 26, 2005 in Washington after meeting for three hours with the federal grand jury investigating the leak of a CIA officer's identity. The jury adjourned for the day without announcing any action. The two-year criminal probe has ensnared President Bush's top political adviser Karl Rove and Vice President Dick Cheney's chief of staff I. Lewis Libby. (AP Photo/Manuel Balce Ceneta)
U.S. Attorney Patrick Fitzgerald met with grand jurors for about three hours at the federal courthouse in Washington. He made no statement as he left and refused to respond when reporters asked if he is seeking an extension of the grand jury term, which expires in two days.

The grand jury was empanelled to investigate whether anyone in President George W. Bush's administration disclosed the identity of covert operative Valerie Plame after her husband, former ambassador Joseph Wilson, publicly questioned whether the administration twisted intelligence to make the case for invading Iraq.

Fitzgerald has been focusing on the actions of longtime Bush adviser Karl Rove and I. Lewis Libby, Vice President Dick Cheney's chief of staff, people familiar with the case have said.

White House spokesman Scott McClellan repeated past refusals to comment on the case. ``Everybody's focused on the priorities of the American people,'' McClellan told reporters today. He added, though, that White House aides ``are following developments in the news.''

The investigation may be nearing an end after two years.

`11th Hour'

``We are now in the 11th minute of the 11th hour,'' said criminal defense lawyer J. Bradley Bennett, who has no direct involvement in the case. ``All the indications are that we will still hear something by Friday.''

Bennett, a partner at the Baker Botts law firm in Washington, said Fitzgerald still has several options, including extending the grand jury, empanelling a new one or bringing indictments and filing them under seal.

Fitzgerald met with Thomas Hogan, chief judge for the federal district courts in Washington, for about 45 minutes around midday, said court spokesman Sheldon Snook. While Snook declined to say what they discussed, Fitzgerald would need Hogan's approval to extend the grand jury.

Federal Bureau of Investigation agents were conducting interviews in the case as late as two days ago, the Washington Post reported today. The agents sought out Plame's neighbors in Washington to ask whether they knew she worked at the Central Intelligence Agency. That suggests the prosecutor may be seeking to show Plame's employment was covert, the newspaper said.

The investigation also has ensnared Cheney. Libby's notes show he learned about Plame's CIA connection a month before she was publicly identified by syndicated columnist Robert Novak on July 14, 2003, the New York Times reported Oct. 24.

Questioning

Bush and Cheney have been questioned by investigators in sessions held in their offices. Rove has made four appearances before the grand jury and Libby has testified twice.

As many as two dozen current or former administration officials also have been questioned, either by investigators working for Fitzgerald or before the grand jury, according to lawyers familiar with the case and published reports. Among them were former White House spokesman Ari Fleischer and Bush adviser Karen Hughes. In addition, Fitzgerald has sought information from at least a half dozen reporters, including NBC's Tim Russert, Matthew Cooper of Time magazine and Judith Miller of the New York Times.

Triggering the Probe

The investigation was triggered when the CIA asked the Justice Department to look into the matter after Novak's column was published. A 1982 law makes it illegal for a person with access to classified information to knowingly reveal the identity of a covert agent. Fitzgerald, the U.S. attorney in Chicago, was appointed as special counsel in December 2003 to avoid potential conflicts of interest between political appointees in Justice and the White House.

Democrats sought to increase pressure on the Bush administration, which would face a disruption of its agenda if any White House aides are indicted.

Senator Charles Schumer, a New York Democrat, said he sent a letter to the White House yesterday calling on President George W. Bush to declare, before Fitzgerald announces any action, that he will suspend any aides who may be charged.

``I believe the president should set a standard should indictments be issued by prosecutor Fitzgerald,'' Schumer said. He said the White House has not responded to his suggestion.

Bush said in July he would fire any member of his administration who ``committed a crime.''
