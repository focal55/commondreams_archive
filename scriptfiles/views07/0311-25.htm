Published on Sunday, March 11, 2007 by Newsday
Libby Trial Revealed his Bosses' Mind-Set
by Les Payne
 
Just as voters ran ahead of Congress with their November ballot against the president's war in Iraq, the people stormed past the media and the White House on Wednesday in convicting I. Lewis Libby Jr. as a lying puppet for those who gave him voice.

The jury made history by delivering its four-count guilty verdict against the first chief of staff for a U.S. vice president ever to face trial.

Libby obstructed justice by lying to federal officials probing the news leak that revealed Valerie Plame as a CIA agent. Along the way, the prosecutor got one reporter jailed for 85 days and forced several others to spend what must have seemed just as long testifying on the stand.

The cozy relationship of elite Beltway reporters with their government sources got exposed at trial for the conflict of interest it too often is. These media gentry, some of whom earn millions in TV and book deals, seem more loyal to their sources than to the truth and the public's need to know.

Former New York Times reporter Judith Miller likely doesn't earn millions, but worked big-time with the warmongers, inside and outside of the Bush administration, in making the false case for the Iraq war.

Ironically, and, some say, justifiably, this same Judith Miller, after refusing to reveal Libby as a source for a tip she did not print, served more time in jail than Libby might ever experience for his conviction.

One needs look no further than the next-day editorial in The Washington Post to see that the Beltway media were not happy with Special Prosecutor Patrick J. Fitzgerald, or Plame's husband, Joseph C. Wilson, who fueled the court case. The Post wrote that Fitzgerald's pursuit of the "Washington scandal remarkable for its lack of substance," was executed under a "prescription for excess."

Fitzgerald did indeed pursue Libby's cover-up instead of the crime it cloaked. The bypassed misdeed this time was not a two-bit burglary, but a part of the foundation of the Iraq war.

The convicted chief of staff was "the fall guy" for his superiors who launched the war, according to Denis Collins, who spoke for the jury. "It was said a number of times: What are we doing with this guy here? Where's Rove, where are these other guys?" Where indeed?

The jurors dutifully deliberated with the case they had and not the case they might have wanted. Said Collins, "This was not a question of 'Who can we punish for going to Iraq?'"

Among the 11-member jury were a retired math teacher, a government lawyer, a former journalist, a Web architect, a retired postal worker, and, for good measure, an investment banker with a PhD in economics from Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

The scene the press paints of their 10-day deliberation was a clinic on sifting evidence and testimony in search of a verdict. "The arithmetic of it became obvious," said Collins who, verdict aside, seemed sympathetic to Libby.

Despite the troubling poleaxing of the press in this affair, the Libby trial provided a window into the mind-set of the White House that brazenly marched the nation off to war against the grain of incoming intelligence. The narrow beam of the "obstruction" case, however, prevented much of the material damaging to the White House war policy from entering the record.

As Libby prepares his appeal, the White House is likely preparing his pardon as a contingency. As with the 2000 presidential vote, and the troop escalation following the November vote, President George W. Bush will spit on the will of the people.

The conviction of Cheney's chief of staff will not likely move the cowardly vice president to do what's best for the country: Resign and move back to Wyoming with his foot up on a pillow.

Thus it is perhaps time for the legislative and judicial branches of government to reclaim the republic with the constitutional mechanism provided by the Founding Fathers.
