Published on Monday, February 5, 2007 by CommonDreams.org
Thinking About the Unthinkable
by Chris Edelson
 

It's been more than five years since the September 11 attacks. In many ways, daily life in the United States has returned to normal. Of course, we hear about the horrific toll in far-off places-Iraq, Afghanistan. We know that American soldiers, as well as vaguely identified "insurgents", and, of course, ordinary Iraqi and Afghan civilians, are dying every day. But in American cities, children go to school, people go to work and, thankfully, life goes on.

Last week, a bizarre ad campaign gone wrong reminded us that we still live life subject to a hair trigger. Authorities in Boston mistook electronic light boards advertising a cartoon TV show for bombs. Fortunately, there was no real danger in Boston. But the scare is a reminder that we may have to deal with a real threat in the future-and that we are still very much on edge.

I certainly and fervently hope there are no future attacks to deal with. It feels faintly blasphemous even to think, let alone write, about such things. However, it is important to consider this possibility during a moment of relative calm.

How would we react if there is another attack? Certainly, there would be sorrow, fear, outrage. How would our government react? It would likely depend on the circumstances, but there is reason to be concerned about a draconian crackdown on freedom. Whether it is making decisions about war abroad, issuing "signing statements" that purport to limit the meaning of laws passed by Congress, or authorizing warrantless surveillance in the face of the contrary authority of federal law, the Bush administration has made clear that it sees presidential power as nearly unbounded.

Everyone wants security. The question is-at what price? Can we trust this administration to respect the Constitution and the rule of law? Can we trust it to respond to a real enemy rather than initiating reprisals against exaggerated foes? Can we trust it to devise an effective response to an attack?

The answer suggested by Abu Ghraib, Guantanamo Bay, a war in Iraq launched on misinformation if not outright lies, and warrantless surveillance ordered in defiance of the FISA is troubling, to say the least.

President Bush has often said that "everything changed" after September 11. In fact, everything did not change. Most of us continue to lead lives that are not wildly different from our pre-9/11 lives. We can work, travel, go to school, speak, write, and question authority (most of the time anyways) freely. We still have a Constitution, providing (at least in theory) checks on the power of each branch of government.

No terrorist can take these things away from us. The terrorists cannot rewrite our Constitution or take away our freedom. They will never control our President, Congress, or courts.

I hope we will never have to see how our government responds to another attack. Unfortunately, circumstances tell us that it is only prudent to consider this possibility now, when there is at least a chance of rational discussion. Perhaps it is possible to figure out a way to guard against excessive, unwarranted crackdowns on freedom-while, of course, recognizing the need for legitimate, lawful action designed to respond to real threats.

Only we can take away our freedom. We must make sure that never happens. Let's start a conversation about these serious issues now, in a moment of relative calm and sanity.

Chris Edelson is a lawyer in Washington, D.C. who writes frequently about current political and legal issues. His writing has previously been published in The Philadelphia Inquirer and Metroland (Albany, NY) and at commondreams.org. He can be reached at chedelson@yahoo.com
