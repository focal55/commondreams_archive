Published on Tuesday, February 8, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Choosing Sides on Abortion: Next President To Have Big Say Through Courts
by Carla Marinucci
 

There may be no more treacherous land mine on the political horizon than abortion -- an issue with the potential to explode along every step of the 2000 presidential campaign.

From Iowa to New Hampshire and then on to South Carolina and California, the candidates have been questioned about where they stand regarding a woman's right to choose an abortion. And woe to those who try to sidestep the question, obscure their position or morph their words from one place to the next.

``In 1992, it was `the economy, stupid,' '' said Belle Taylor-McGhee, president of the California Abortion Rights Action League. ``Right now, it's `abortion, stupid.' ''

That is because the next president is expected to appoint three to five Supreme Court justices -- and thereby will wield enormous power to recast Roe vs. Wade, the landmark 1973 Supreme Court decision that provided for legal abortion.

And though it is rarely included among the top 10 concerns of voters, a candidate's position on abortion carries enormous weight, particularly with women, who comprise the crucial ``gender gap'' at the polls, political insiders say. Abortion has become a kind of litmus test in itself -- a good indicator of where a candidate stands on a variety of related social issues.

``The position on the right to choose basically speaks to the fundamental principle on how we value women in our society,'' said Taylor-McGhee. ``That includes health care, how she can raise her family with a fair wage and how she can provide education for her children

--because if you don't have the power to decide when the time is right for you to become a parent or not, you really can't decide anything else.''

GOP CANDIDATES OPPOSE ABORTION

Without exception, the Republican presidential candidates -- Texas Gov. George W. Bush, Arizona Sen. John McCain, publisher Steve Forbes and former Ambassador Alan Keyes -- are vehemently opposed to abortion. All have said they will support the repeal of the Roe vs. Wade decision if they are elected.

But Bush has taken heat for sidestepping abortion questions and has been pressed repeatedly for failing to promise he would appoint anti-abortion judges and select an anti-abortion running mate. He said recently that ``it should be up to each state legislature'' to decide what restrictions to place on abortion.

McCain has had to soften a statement -- made in an interview with The Chronicle last year -- in which he worried that an immediate repeal of Roe vs. Wade could send women back to illegal abortion mills. The senator argues that he always has been unquestionably anti-abortion and wants to see Roe vs. Wade repealed, but he has said the GOP ``must be an inclusionary party'' that respects all views on the issue.

Forbes has faced criticism that he has changed his political views on the subject -- becoming far more hard-line -- for political expediency, specifically to win the critical backing of the Christian right. And Keyes has made the outlawing of all abortions one of the central issues of his campaign.

Among the Democrats, Vice President Al Gore and former New Jersey Sen. Bill Bradley strongly support abortion rights.

But they, too, have been mired in controversy around the issue. In New Hampshire, Bradley openly questioned Gore's commitment on the abortion issue, citing a letter Gore wrote to a congressional constituent in 1987 in which he said abortion was ``arguably the taking of a human life.''

Asked to explain, Gore said, ``I used the word `arguably,' and yes, my position has changed. . . . I would not use that phrasing today.''

Bradley said revisiting Gore's old position is legitimate, because it raises questions about the vice president's consistency on the issue -- and his truthfulness.

The arguing about credentials does not change the bottom line, said Democratic political consultant Garry South: ``You can gussy it up, but you are either for a woman's right to choose -- or you are not.''

And on this issue, in California, South argues that recent political history -- and polling -- proves Republicans are vulnerable on the issue.

PRO-CHOICE CALIFORNIANS

Bob Dole, the GOP's 1996 presidential candidate, took repeated criticism for his anti-abortion position and lost the state -- particularly female voters -- handily to Bill Clinton. And Dan Lungren, the GOP's 1998 gubernatorial candidate, touted his anti-abortion views across the state and went down to the largest defeat in modern political history here, again with women voting in large numbers against him.

``Consistently, Californians are pro-choice, and by a very large margin,'' said pollster Mark Baldassare of the Public Policy Institute of California, whose surveys show ``7 out of 10 said government should not interfere with women's rights to abortion.''

But GOP presidential candidate Keyes, in a recent interview with The Chronicle, argued that Republicans can win on abortion -- if they stick to their convictions.

``All of the major victories that the Republican Party has won in the last 20 years have been on the strength of pro-life, conservative candidates,'' Keyes said. ``(Take) the '94 Congress, with a freshman class that was overwhelmingly pro-life, moral, conservative.

``These folks won because they had moral, conservative convictions. So I just think that the pundits fly in the face of real experience,'' he said. ``It's not true that pro-life people win all the time, but it is true that it has not been any kind of liability.''

And anti-abortion groups insist that more change is coming.

``As technology changes, hearts are changing, because the proof is in the pictures,'' said Mark Washburn, president of Capitol Resource Institute, a nonprofit, bipartisan family advocacy group in Sacramento. ``Years ago, we had discussions about viability (of the fetus) -- and it was 30 weeks. Now it's 25 weeks. . . . The other side could get away with calling it a blob of tissue, but it's hard to argue that when you get an (ultrasound) picture of a baby (in the first trimester).

``The younger generation is seeing it more clearly than some of their elders, even,'' Washburn said. ``The newest voters, 18 to 24 (years old), are more pro-life than ever. And as they start having children, it will swing even harder to pro-life.''

But as Californians prepare to go to the polls, advocates say they are prepared to organize and fight the issue to the end -- and that the presidential candidates know it.

``The reason George Bush has been running away from the issue, and John McCain has straddled it,'' said Taylor-McGhee, ``is that they know if you are rabidly opposed to a woman's right to choose here, you cannot win.''

ABORTION ISSUES

The next president is expected to appoint three to five judges to the U.S. Supreme Court, which could have a dramatic impact on laws regarding the availability of legal abortion. The president's bully pulpit brings with it enormous power to lobby for -- or veto -- key legislation related to reproductive rights, such as parental consent laws or a ban on late-term abortions. The president also wields huge financial clout: the power to approve or veto federal funding for programs such as teen education, family planning and contraception services for poor women. The Chronicle asked the candidates to comment on related issues:

--Constitutional Amendment Banning Abortion George W. Bush: Supports the goal of a ``Human Life'' constitutional amendment with exceptions for rape and incest and to save the life of the mother. Steve Forbes: Supports a constitutional amendment banning abortion except to save the life of the mother. Alan Keyes: Supports a constitutional amendment banning abortion. John McCain: Supports a constitutional amendment with exceptions for the life of the mother, rape and incest.

Bill Bradley: Opposes constitutional ban on abortion. Al Gore: Opposes constitutional ban.

--Federal Family Planning Funding George W. Bush: Opposes the use of federal funds to provide or advocate for abortion, but supports ``practical steps to reduce the number of abortions,'' such as helping women in crisis through maternity group homes, encouraging adoption, promoting abstinence education and passing laws requiring parental notification and waiting periods. Steve Forbes: Would prohibit the federal government from granting any public funds to groups that recommend or perform abortions in the United States or abroad. Alan Keyes: Opposes family planning funding. John McCain: Opposes the use of taxpayer money to perform, promote or support abortion on demand and opposes the use of U.S. dollars to fund international organizations that provide or facilitate abortion.

Bill Bradley: Supports federal funding for family planning, opposes restrictions on funds for international family planning programs and supports requiring health insurers to provide a full array of contraception options in their health plans. Al Gore: Supports increased funding for family planning services.

--Federal Clinic Access Law George W. Bush: Supports the free speech rights of citizens to peacefully and legally demonstrate; opposes any violent protest. Steve Forbes: Opposes violence at clinics, but opposes laws like the Freedom of Access to Clinic Entrances Act, which established a safety zone around women's health clinics, because it targets a specific group such as anti-abortion demonstrators for specific type of free speech. Alan Keyes: No response. John McCain: Wants to maintain open access to clinics and is concerned about acts of violence, but believes the federal law is wrong because it criminalizes peaceful demonstrations.

Bill Bradley: Supports laws prohibiting protesters from blocking access to reproductive health centers and strongly opposes any effort to weaken those laws. Al Gore: Supports federal access law. Clinton-Gore administration this year proposed $4.5 million in the federal budget to provide more security at health care facilities and established a task force to assess further security needs.

--Late-Term Abortion George W. Bush: Strongly opposes and would sign legislation to ban it. Steve Forbes: Urges a ban. Alan Keyes: Strongly opposes. John McCain: Strongly opposes; co-sponsored law to ban late-term abortions and has taken a leading role in the fight to overturn Clinton's veto of the legislation.

Bill Bradley: Opposes a ban on late-term abortions, unless it contains protections for the life and health of the mother. Al Gore: Opposes late-term abortions, except where necessary to protect the life or health of the mother.

--Parental Consent for Contraceptives and Abortion George W. Bush: Supports. Steve Forbes: Supports. Alan Keyes: Supports. John McCain: Supports. Original co-sponsor of the Child Custody Protection Act of 1999, making it a federal offense to transport a minor across state lines to obtain an abortion in order to avoid state parental consent laws.

Bill Bradley: Believes a minor should seek advice from parents or another adult whenever possible, but believes a legal requirement that applies to all cases is not appropriate. Al Gore: Opposes. Says that while teenagers should be encouraged to talk to their families, those who do not have a family they can talk to should not be penalized.

--Litmus Tests for Judges George W. Bush: Declines to specify whether his judges or vice president would be ``pro-life.'' Has promised to appoint ``strict constructionists'' who ``do not legislate from the bench.'' Steve Forbes: Promises to choose a running mate and appoint judges who share his views on abortion. Alan Keyes: Would choose people who share his view. John McCain: Opposes.

Bill Bradley: Would appoint judges who will uphold the Constitution and respect precedent, including the right to privacy. Al Gore: ``A Supreme Court appointment in a Gore administration would support a woman's right to choose.''
