Published on Friday, July 21, 2000 by Reuters
UN, Corporations Form New 'Global Rights Partnership'
by Evelyn Leopold
 
UNITED NATIONS - In an attempt to save trade liberalization from a backlash, the United Nations announced a new business partnership to promote decent labor, human rights and environmental standards abroad.

But no sooner was the list released of the first batch of 44 corporations on Thursday than some advocacy groups, such as Greenpeace International, wrote a letter saying a the United Nations should have instituted a tighter legal framework to oversee practices by multinational corporations.

The companies include Nike Inc., which in 1997 and 1998 was harshly criticized for having its sporting goods made in Asian sweatshops as well as Royal Dutch/Shell, which came under fire for practices in Nigeria. Both have sought to improve their image and practices.

Called the ``Global Compact'' the first meeting of business representatives, labor unions and human rights and conservation groups meet next Wednesday with Secretary-General Kofi Annan to say what they have done and what they intend to do to promote labor, human rights and environmental standards.

Other corporations include DaimlerChrysler AG, Unilever Plc, Deutsche Bank AG, BP Amoco Plc, Novartis AG, LM Ericsson as well as companies in India, Pakistan, Norway, Mexico, South Africa, Denmark, Thailand and Peru.

The concept was first floated in a January 1999 speech by Annan at the World Economic Forum, a weeklong annual meeting of elite business figures at Davos in the Swiss Alps.

Annan warned that market expansion and protection of private property was moving far faster than rules on social and environmental rights. This situation would trigger a backlash and lead to protectionism.

But after protests erupted on precisely these issues, Annan argued against any social rules in the World Trade Organization, which would be detrimental to developing nations, and instead offered one-stop shopping at the United Nations on international standards for multinational corporations.

Specifically, Annan called for honoring the right to collective bargaining and making sure hiring and firing policies did not discriminate on grounds of race, creed, gender or ethnic origins. He said firms should make sure underage children were not employed and use environmentally safe technology.

Asked whether the United Nations was giving cover to corporations that needed to improve their image, John Ruggie, an adviser to Annan, told a Thursday news conference there was no material gain in this for any company.

Ruggie said each company was going to post its statements and practices on a U.N. Web site (http://www.unglobalcompact.org) where they could be disputed by labor unions, environmental and human rights organizations included in the compact.

``This transparency this creates is a truly powerful force and the companies are all well aware of that fact and have taken it into consideration,'' he said.

``I would be very surprised if one of these companies attempted to do a snow job on us on this web site. The cost would be enormous and I think they are well aware of this.''

In addition to issuing statements on what they are doing to promote good business practices, the companies are encouraged to initiate or join projects in various nations.

Ruggie gave the example of Ericsson, the Swedish telecommunications company, which has offered uninterrupted communications equipment within 24 hours to areas hit by natural disasters.
