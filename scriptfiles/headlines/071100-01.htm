Published on Tuesday, July 11, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
With AIDS, `Rich or Poor' Means `Life or Death'
A bleak future predicted for impoverished nations
by Sabin Russell
 
Durban, South Africa -- At the International AIDS Conference in Geneva two years ago, they talked about ``The Gap'' between rich nations and poor, and the contrasting consequences it had on care and treatment of the sick.

Here, at the first of the group's meetings to be held on the African continent, they are calling it ``The Gulf.''

The gross discrepancies in the abilities of rich and poor nations to cope with the spread of HIV have become glaringly apparent at the 13th International AIDS Conference, where statistics released yesterday show that the average life expectancy in some African countries will soon drop to 33 years or less.

Moreover, the population in some nations in southern Africa will actually decline as a result of AIDS, according to the estimates of population experts.

Amid those gloomy projections, the burgeoning movement to bring less expensive AIDS drugs to Africa has come to dominate conversation here, from the lecture hall to the coffee counter.

Following the path blazed by Pfizer Inc. and Boehringer- Ingelheim this year, American pharmaceutical powerhouse Merck & Co. announced plans yesterday to contribute free AIDS drugs -- this time in a deal with Botswana. The $100 million cost will be split between the company and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation.

``This is going to make a world of difference for people living with HIV in Botswana, and that is something that cannot be ignored,'' said Merck spokesman Jeffrey Turchio.

Just before the opening of the conference, Boehringer-Ingelheim said it will provide free access to its antiviral drug nevirapine to HIV- positive pregnant women and their newborn children -- a regime shown to cut by more than half the number of babies born infected with HIV.

But the gestures may have been lost the growing clamor for wealthy industrialized nations to do much more to forestall the predicted deaths of nearly 25 million Africans.

South African High Court Justice Edwin Cameron, a former labor lawyer and a white anti-apartheid activist, set the tone at a morning lecture named for Dr. Jonathan Mann, the passionate founder of the World Health Organization's Global Program on AIDS, who was killed with his wife in a Swissair plane crash off Nova Scotia in 1998.

A gay man with AIDS, Cameron mesmerized the 10,000 delegates at the opening lecture as he declared himself ``the living embodiment of the inequity of drug availability and access in Africa.''

He described how, on the brink of death three years ago, he was brought back to health by four pills a day of antiretroviral drugs he could purchase, on his salary as a judge, for about $400 a month.

``Amidst the poverty of Africa, I stand before you because I able to purchase health and vigor,'' he said. ``I am here because I can afford to pay for life itself.''

Cameron left no doubt that he believes HIV is the cause of AIDS and that the costly drugs that quell replication of the virus are the key to his survival.

``I should be dead approximately now,'' he said. `Instead, I am more healthy, more vigorous, more energetic, and more full of purposeful joy than at any time in my life.''

Cameron went on to lacerate his government's response to the crisis. ``A government that in its commitment to human rights and democracy has been a shining example to Africa and the world has at almost every conceivable turn mismanaged the epidemic,'' he said.

The response to an epidemic that is now estimated to have infected 20 percent of South African adults, said Cameron, is ``a cacophony of task groups, workshops, committees, councils, policies, drafts proposals statements, and pledges.''

Turning his attention to drug companies, he chastised them for pledging to cut costs eight weeks ago but failing to follow through. The AIDS conference, he said, ``can demand urgent and immediate price reductions for resource-poor countries.'' He also called on drug companies to permit makers of generic drugs to set up shop in poor countries and make low-cost versions of the antiretroviral drugs, or let poor nations import them from low-cost producers abroad.

Nearly three-quarters of the 34 million people living with AIDS reside in sub-Saharan Africa, and as the death rate rises, the average life expectancy will fall sharply, said epidemiologist Karen Stanecki of the Census Bureau.

``It's hard to comprehend the amount of mortality we will see in those countries,'' she said.

She projected that, by 2010, life expectancy will be 29 in Botswana, 30 in Swaziland and 33 in Namibia and Zimbabwe. A recent report by the World Health Organization estimated that 35.8 percent of the population of Botswana is now HIV-positive. Without AIDS, life expectancy would be near 70.
