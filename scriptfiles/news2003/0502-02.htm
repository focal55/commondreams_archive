NCADP Commends Ohio Parole Board's Vote
Urges Gov. Taft To Grant Clemency To Jerome Campbell
  

WASHINGTON - May 2 - The Ohio Adult Parole Board today voted 8-2 to recommend clemency for Jerome Campbell, who faces a May 14 execution date, after new DNA evidence cast serious doubt on the state's case against him. The vote marked the first time since Ohio resumed executions in 1999 that the Ohio Parole Board has recommended clemency for a person on death row.

"Campbell's innocence claim is particularly strong, and his case reflects some of the key problems with the death penalty system in the United States," said Steven W. Hawkins, executive director of the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty."The Ohio Parole Board did the right thing and Gov. Taft should accept its recommendation and commute Campbell's sentence."

At trial, prosecutors presented a bloodstained shoe belonging to Campbell, attempting to show that he had tracked the victim's blood from the murder scene. DNA testing later indicated that the blood was in fact his own. "Campbell has never received a fair review of his conviction in light of DNA evidence that could exonerate him," Hawkins said.

The state's case has further crumbled under the weight of questions concerning the reliability of two witnesses who turned out to be jailhouse informants. At trial, prosecutors falsely stated that the two men - Ronys Clardy and Angelo Roseman - received no special consideration for testifying against Campbell. In its report to Gov. Taft, the Ohio Parole Board wrote that Campbell's attorneys"presented credible evidence for the majority members of thisboard to question any sustained confidence or reliability in the jury's recommendation."

Hawkins announced that NCADP is encouraging its members and supporters to urge Gov. Taft to grant clemency by visiting the NCADP's new Action Center. To take action in the Campbell case, please visit www.ncadp.org

Hawkins said that "executive clemency is the proper venue for preventing gross miscarriages of justice when state and federalcourts cannot or will not act to provide a remedy." Since 1976, 107 people have been released from death row in the United States due to actual innocence, and just this Tuesday, the Missouri Supreme Court overturned the conviction of Joseph Amrine, ordering the state to file new charges or release him."Judges and state leaders from across the nation are beginning to recognize the countless flaws in the death penalty system," said Hawkins. "The fact is, the institution of capital punishment will always risk the execution of the innocent, and any system that poses that danger is absolutely unacceptable."

The National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty was founded in 1976 and is the only fully-staffed national organization devoted specifically to abolishing the death penalty. NCADP is comprised of more than 100 local, state, national and international affiliates.
