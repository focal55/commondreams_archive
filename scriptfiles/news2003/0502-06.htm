Court Decision on Constitutionality of New Campaign Finance Law Upholds Important Portions of Law
   WASHINGTON - May 2 - Following is an "On the Docket" report on Campaign Finance and the Courts from Democracy 21 President Fred Wertheimer:

-- Court Decision on Constitutionality of New Campaign Finance Law Upholds Important Portions of the Law, Declares Other Parts of the Law Unconstitutional

-- Supporters Confident Full Law Will Be Upheld in Supreme Court

"We are pleased that today's split decision by a three-judge District Court panel upheld major portions of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (BCRA), including major portions of the soft money ban," said Democracy 21 President Fred Wertheimer, a spokesman for and member of the legal team representing the congressional sponsors of the law.

"The decision, however, also creates serious loopholes in the soft money ban and this portion of the decision simply does not make sense in light of past Supreme Court rulings and the overwhelming record in this case. We are confident that the full soft money ban will be upheld by the Supreme Court," Wertheimer said.

"We are also pleased that the court has recognized that Congress has the authority to address the problem of campaign ads run by outside groups in the guise of issue ads," Wertheimer said.

"I am confident that the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act meets constitutional standards and I am eager to present the case to the Supreme Court," said former U.S. Solicitor General Seth Waxman, one of the attorneys who argued the case before the three-judge panel on behalf of the congressional sponsors.

"BCRA represents the culmination of years of study by Congress of a clear and present threat to the very vitality of faith in our precious system of democratic governance," according to Waxman. "It bespeaks legislative care in balancing, on the one hand, the requirements of the First Amendment with, on the other hand, the absolute imperative to reclaim the integrity of our system."

The decision by the District Court panel "recognizes that Congress has the power to address the corrupting influence of big money on our political system," stated Roger Witten, lead counsel for the legal team representing the congressional sponsors, who joined in defending the law before the District Court panel. "Portions of the court's decision, however, ignore the judgment of a bipartisan majority of Congress regarding the extent of the problem with large contributions and fail to take account of a legislative and court record that powerfully demonstrates that the need for the comprehensive loophole closing approach contained in the soft money ban," Witten said.

Defenders of the BCRA are expected to move quickly to ask the Supreme Court to review those portions of the District Court decision that strike down the law. They are also expected to make a quick decision about asking the Supreme Court to stay the portions of the District Court decision that declared sections of the BCRA unconstitutional.
