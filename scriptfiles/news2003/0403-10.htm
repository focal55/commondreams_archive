Span Hits the Road for Beginning of Poverty Tour;
Anti-poverty official to stay overnight in impoverished American cities
  WASHINGTON - April 3 - From April 6-9, Derrick Span, national president of the Community Action Partnership, will stay overnight in two of America's poorest communities as a part of his A Conversation with America National Poverty Tour. The trips will allow him to get a personal perspective of the problems faced everyday by the nation's poor and see what the Partnership's member Community Action Agencies (CAAs) are doing to address and eliminate these problems. Span's first stop will be in Cleveland, Mississippi, where he will stay with a single mother and her four children. He will participate in the family's daily activities, including attending Head Start classes with the youngest daughter and going to work at a hospice with the mother. Span will also visit the school where the mother became a certified nursing assistant. In addition to spending time with the family, Span will tour impoverished areas in Bolivar County, Mississippi, and meet with families. A special highlight of the tour will be stops at apartment complexes and Head Start Centers managed by the Bolivar County Community Action Agency. Span then heads to Rio Grande City, Texas, where he will stay with a mason, his wife, and their extended family. His day will begin early as he heads to work with the father, followed by a tour of the "colonias," un-zoned, semi-rural communities. Span will also visit programs operated by the Community Action Council of South Texas, including health clinics; an adult day care center; and Youthbuild, a GED and vocational skills training program.

The Partnership chose to visit these two communities because they are two of the most impoverished areas in the United States, according to recent US Census Bureau figures. Cleveland, Mississippi, located in the Mississippi Delta, grapples with a lack of affordable housing and transportation, teen pregnancy, and unemployment. Rio Grande City is located in Starr County, Texas, argued to be the poorest county in America. There is a high demand for healthcare, and affordable housing and adult education opportunities are limited. Water quality is a concern because many of the colonias are without wastewater systems.

These overnight visits represent an unprecedented endeavor for the Partnership. "We want to make poverty the focus of our nation because this issue has been ignored for far too long," said Span. "We feel these visits will help us reveal the faces of poverty and advocate for national policies that will help low-income people change their lives."

Key aspects of the visits will be captured on video and shown at various functions. "We wanted to document the challenges poor people face and put it into perspective for the general public," noted Span. The footage will also spotlight successful CAA programs and services. "The Bolivar County CAA and CACST are perfect examples of how CAAs are working to transform communities," said Span. "Bolivar County is running Head Start programs to help low-income children get an early opportunity to change their lives. And CACST's Youthbuild gives troubled teens a second chance."

Span warned that CAAs will need additional resources to continue providing new and innovative programs for their communities. "It is critical that our CAAs have access to as many resources as possible. We hope these visits will open up more avenues for CAAs to tap into additional funding resources and greater opportunities." Based in Washington, DC, the Community Action Partnership is the national association representing the interests of the 1,000 Community Action Agencies (CAAs) organized to change people's lives, embody the spirit of hope, improve communities, and make America a better place to live. CAAs care about the entire community and are dedicated to helping people help themselves and each other. For more information about the Partnership and CAAs, visit the association's website at www.communityactionpartnership.com .
