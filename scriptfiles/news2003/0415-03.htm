American Lung Association Welcomes EPA Diesel Proposal;
Will Work for Rapid Implementation
  WASHINGTON - April 15 - The American Lung Association today welcomed a new Environmental Protection Agency proposal to clean up pollution from heavy equipment and other non-road diesel engines, as well as require cleaner diesel fuel.

"This EPA proposal, when finalized and fully implemented, will dramatically reduce air pollution from these diesel engines and protect public health, " said John L. Kirkwood, President and CEO of the American Lung Association.

In 2000, EPA finalized rules that require the significant clean up of heavy-duty trucks and buses and highway diesel fuel. Today's proposed rule, when fully implemented, will provide additional dramatic reductions of particulate matter and nitrogen oxides from the non-road sector. This rule will help protect seniors, children and people with lung diseases including asthma, who are among the most vulnerable to the health effects caused by air pollution. Particulate air pollution has been linked to thousands of premature deaths and diesel exhaust has been classified as a likely human carcinogen by the Environmental Protection Agency. The EPA proposal will help reduce the health threat posed by diesels.

"This proposal must not be used by those who want to weaken or reopen the 2000 rules regulating heavy-duty trucks and buses and highway diesel fuel," said Kirkwood.

"The American Lung Association will educate the public about the substantial benefits of cleaning up diesel engines and the need to maximize the pollution reductions from this rulemaking," said Kirkwood. "Through our public comments, we will work to convince EPA to clean up all diesel engines including marine engines and locomotives."

Today, with Environmental Defense, the American Lung Association released a new report Closing the Diesel Divide, Protecting Public Health From Diesel Air Pollution to spotlight the magnitude of the impact of diesel air pollution. The EPA proposal is a major part of the solution. The report can be read at http://www.lungusa.org
