Consumers Call for Assisted Living Protection; Groups Urge Governor, Senate and Assembly to Make Law, Not More Legislation
  ALBANY, NY - April 15 - AARP, StateWide Senior Action Council, Nursing Home Community Coalition and the Coalition of NYS Alzheimer's Association call upon the Governor, Senate and Assembly to pass an Assisted Living law, which provides state oversight of assisted living facilities. With the release of the "5 Principles for Assisted Living Protection," the senior's advocacy groups specifically outline what must be present in law to protect all residents of assisted living facilities in New York.

"Our 2.5 million AARP members in New York want a law this session that protects assisted living residents by requiring Department of Health oversight," said Lois Aronstein, New York State Director for AARP. "In addition, the 5 principles we've outlined assure for some long overdue protections that need to be present in law. The time to legislate is over; we need an assisted living law that protects and we need it now."

"The Coalition of NYS Alzheimer's Association Chapters is pleased to join with our numerous partners committed to passage of critical legislation regarding Assisted Living Facilities operating in our state," said Marvin LeRoy, director, Coalition of NYS Alzheimer's Association. "This emerging residential care option for our more senior members of society -- especially those touched by dementia -- very much requires universal definitions and standards of operation and care to guide their work. With legislation addressing these concerns now on the books in over forty states, the New York State legislature needs to bring this debate to closure and pass legislative language in the current session."

Assisted living is the fastest growing type of senior housing in the country. In 1998 alone, assisted living facilities accounted for over 75 percent of all new senior housing, costing older New Yorkers anywhere from $1,500 to $6,500 to reside in these facilities. Currently, many assisted living facilities operate in New York State with no state oversight, are not required to provide consumer information and disclosures, and have no uniform admission and discharge policy. The position of AARP and our coalition partners is that providing no state oversight for assisted living continues to put a vulnerable population at risk.

"The growth of assisted living facilities in our state is a welcome alternative for older persons", said Mike Burgess, executive director for the StateWide Senior Action Council. "However, New York State must establish regulations which protect residents and set conditions for operating in the State."

Cynthia Rudder, executive director of the Nursing Home Community Coalition of New York State stated that, "Many assisted living residents are frail and are getting frailer. An NHCC study found that many residents in unlicensed facilities need help with basic life activities -- taking medications, bathing, dressing, etc. Many used assistive devices, were cognitively impaired and received home care. In addition, some of these residences reported what seemed to be inappropriate care. These vulnerable residents need protection. Government has a responsibility to set minimum standards, conduct surprise inspections and apply sanctions when appropriate."

The "5 Principles for Assisted Living Protection" that need to be incorporated into an assisted living law are:

-- A clear definition of assisted living -- A uniform admission and discharge policy -- The requirement for consumer information and disclosures -- Department of Health oversight of facilities -- "Residents Bill of Rights" must be posted and handed out to all residents
