Anniversary of Brown v. Board of Education Serves as Reminder of Continuing Education Gap, Says American Counseling Association
  ALEXANDRIA, VA - May 15 - The American Counseling Association (ACA) today expressed concern regarding the continuing gap in educational opportunities between children and youth attending low-income and wealthy school districts. On May 17th, 1954, the Supreme Court issued a unanimous decision in Brown v. Board of Education declaring it a violation of the 14th Amendment of the Constitution to separate children in public schools for no other reason than race.

Although schools are no longer "separate" in terms of race, they remain blatantly and shamefully unequal in terms of support. Examples abound. In Pennsylvania, an average of $6,000 is spent on each inner-city Philadelphia student - in teacher salaries, computers, texts and other supplies - compared to $16,000 for each student just a few miles away in Bucks County. At Santa Ana High School in Orange County, California, the ratio of students to guidance counselors is more than 700 to 1. In 1999, only 19 percent of the school's graduates fulfilled the requirements to get into a four-year state college, compared with 39 percent countywide. Just as the gap between higher income and low income Americans is widening, so is the gap between the educational opportunities available to our children. This gap must be addressed if we want our children and youth to reach their potential, and if we are to truly create a society in which everyone has a chance to succeed.

On the occasion of the 49th anniversary of Brown v. Board of Education, ACA is encouraging Congress and the President to take up and pass the "Student Bill of Rights Act", H.R. 236, introduced by Rep. Chaka Fattah (D-PA). Representative Fattah's legislation would hold states accountable-at the risk of loss of federal education dollars-for providing adequate access to elementary and secondary students to seven essential elements for learning, including access to instruction from highly-qualified teachers in small class sizes, and access to the services of professional school counselors.

"All students, not just those born to the right parents and living in the right school districts, deserve a quality education," stated Richard Yep, Executive Director of ACA. "Part of this includes having access to a professional school counselor, and we commend Representative Fattah for both his leadership in shining a light on the disparity between schools within our education system and for his recognition of the importance of school counseling services." Yep stated that ACA intends to continue working with Representative Fattah in an effort to gain passage of H.R. 236 before the 50th anniversary of Brown v. Board of Education.

Founded in 1952, and with over 50,000 members, ACA is the nation's largest non-profit organization representing the counseling profession, including professional school, mental health, rehabilitation, career and other counselors. All 50 states have established specific professional standards for school counselors requiring graduate coursework.
