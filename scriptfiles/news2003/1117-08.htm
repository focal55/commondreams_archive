NAFTA is 10
   WASHINGTON - November 17 - ADA President Emeritus Jim Jontz Speak Out Against FTAA.

"Ten years ago today, the U.S. House voted by a narrow margin to approve the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA). This week, tens of thousands of citizens are in Miami, Florida, to raise their collective voice against the expansion of NAFTA into the proposed Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA).

The workers, farmers, environmentalists, religious leaders, students, retirees, and community activists who are here in Miami are not against trade. We're not against trade agreements. We are against agreements that are so heavy weighted, as NAFTA is, toward the interests of investors and corporations at the expense of everyone else.

What exactly did NAFTA do? Indeed, it took down tariffs on various products traded among the signatory countries, but it did much more. NAFTA is fundamentally an investor's protection agreement, a collection of provisions to facilitate the flow of capital to where wages are lower and environmental protections weaker. NAFTA obligated the Mexican government to make changes in their intellectual property law, financial system, and currency rules, all to benefit investors. And NAFTA included strong protection against expropriation, even including protection for multinational companies against regulation that was "tantamount to expropriation" in its impact, allowing companies to file suit against governments to enforce these protections (Chapter 11).

The results have been rather predictable: written to facilitate the flow of capital into Mexico, NAFTA has done just that. But absent protections in NAFTA for labor and the environment, workers in Mexico haven't received their share of benefits; they haven't become middle class consumers of U.S. products, and the jobs that were supposed to be created in the U.S. as a result don't exist. Instead, workers and the environment in both countries have suffered. Low priced grain flowing into Mexico from the U.S. has pushed campesinos off the land and into the labor markets -- in both Mexico and the U.S. This is not the "win-win" we were promised.

Today the Bush administration seeks to extend NAFTA through the hemisphere (except Cuba), and also to expand the coverage of the agreement beyond manufacturing and agriculture to the remainder of the economy. Under the FTAA, multinational corporations would be given new rights to invest offshore in "services" -- including education, health care, the public sector, postal delivery, construction, transportation, even water supply. These "services" provisions would further undermine the interests or workers, consumers, and the environment here and abroad.

When NAFTA passed the House of Representatives ten years ago, lawmakers had serious reservations. In fact, the Congress would not have approved NAFTA absent the efforts of President Clinton. The Clinton Administration took extraordinary steps, even promising lawmakers help on public works projects in their districts, to find the votes they needed. As Bob Wages, then President of the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers (OCAW) said, "Clinton couldn't sell NAFTA, so he bought it."

There is a lesson for us today. The President of the United States has enormous clout regarding the rules of international trade and the Congress.

The agenda the Bush administration is pushing this week in Miami would lock into international law big-time benefits for multinational corporations at the expense of us all. We can, and must, do better.

The good news is that, given the timetable for negotiating the FTAA, there's little chance it will be done by time George W. Bush leaves office in 2005.

As the nine Democratic Presidential candidates crisscross the nation looking for support, we must secure their commitment now to oppose the FTAA and the remainder of the Bush trade agenda. The candidates need to make clear that, when elected, they won't put a few "bells and whistles" on the Bush FTAA and give it their blessing.

Democrats running for President cannot tell us that they support workers, the environment, public services, human rights, and family farmers, and then have any hesitation about how they stand on the FTAA. That's a painful lesson from ten years of NAFTA."

Jim Jontz was the director of the Citizens Trade Campaign which led the fight against NAFTA in 1993, and today is President Emeritus of Americans for Democratic Action.
