New Regulations Attempt to Control Illegal Fishing for Chilean Sea Bass
Good First Step, but Chefs Still Encouraged to Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass in U.S.
  WASHINGTON - May 1 - Today the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) announced new rules aimed at, but falling short of, stopping the import of illegally caught Chilean Sea Bass (toothfish) into the United States. By deciding to take action with these new regulations that strengthen U.S. law, NMFS has acknowledged the seriousness of the problem of illegal toothfish entering the United States.

The new regulations ban the import of toothfish from two areas in the waters surrounding Antarctica that have long been recognized by the international governmental body that regulates the trade of Chilean Sea Bass, the Convention on the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR), as "laundering points" for pirate fishermen who illegally catch fish inside CCAMLR's waters and then claim to have caught them in areas where it is scientifically unlikely that populations exist to support these claims. The new regulations also require that NMFS give pre-approval to all shipments of toothfish over 4,400 pounds prior to their landing at port.

"While we applaud NMFS's acknowledgement of the problem and their effort to stop the import of illegal toothfish into the U.S., it will fall far short of stopping the flood of illegal Chilean Sea Bass that winds up on dinner plates all across the country, which is why we are still encouraging chefs to stop serving this fish until there are safeguards in place to ensure that all the toothfish that enters this country has been legally caught," said Andrea Kavanagh, campaign manager for the National Environmental Trust's Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass campaign.

Despite estimates that conservatively put illegal catches of toothfish of at least 50 percent of the total annual worldwide catch, CCAMLR last fall authorized a significant increase in the total allowable catch in many areas. This pirate catch also results in the hooking and drowning of tens of thousands of seabirds. Over the past six years, at least 700,000 seabirds, including 144,000 albatrosses and 378,000 white-chinned petrels have been killed in this manner. These loss rates are entirely unsustainable and several of these species are now highly endangered.

"Unless the U.S. prohibits the import of catches claimed as being caught in unregulated high seas areas, NMFS will still not be able to prevent illegal toothfish from entering the U.S. even if it is accompanied by a validated catch-document," said Karen Sack, Fisheries Coordinator with The Antarctica Project. "The pirate fishermen will just start claiming that their fish were caught in other high seas areas that are not regulated by the United States, like areas 41, 47 and 87."

To view the NMFS release: http://www.nmfs.noaa.gov/press_releases/CSB.pdf

Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass is a campaign organized by National Environmental Trust and The Antarctica Project with more than 1000 chefs in the United States that have pledged to stop serving Chilean Sea Bass until proper regulations are in place to ensure the commercial sustainability of the species. The next city of chefs who will announce their intentions to stop serving Chilean Sea Bass will be Miami, FL in mid-May.
