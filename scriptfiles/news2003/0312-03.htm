Doctors of Internal Medicine Examine Dangerous Health Risks for Uninsured;
Conditions, Diseases Worsen Because of Delays Seeking Care
  PHILADELPHIA - March 12 - "A lack of health insurance is not just an inconvenience, but a serious health risk," said Sara Walker, MD, MACP, president of the American College of Physicians - American Society of Internal Medicine, during a health fair at Temple University Children's Medical Center in Philadelphia, PA.

The ACP-ASIM has produced a series of reports confirming the relationship between a lack of insurance and poorer medical outcomes. The evidence collected by the College confirms that the uninsured are more likely to delay seeking care and more likely to experience poorer medical outcomes.

As a result, uninsured Americans are more likely to: require avoidable hospitalizations and emergency care, suffer adverse health outcomes, and experience a generally higher mortality rate.

"The latest available data from the U.S. Census Bureau indicates that 41.2 million people are without health insurance protection," said Dr. Walker. "Another recent study shows that 75 million Americans were without health insurance sometime during the past year-many for months at a time. The plight of the uninsured persists despite the fact that nearly 80 percent are part of a working family."

"We know what the problem is-too many working Americans and their families have no health insurance and they live sicker and die younger as a result," said Dr. Walker. "We need solutions-practical approaches that build upon what works with our current health insurance system, while fixing what does not."

Dr. Walker presented a plan developed by the ACP-ASIM to ensure all Americans have health insurance.

"Instead of using the heavy-hand of government to dictate how people will get coverage, the College's plan relies instead on economic incentives for states and small employers to offer coverage," said Dr. Walker. "Instead of trying out some untested scheme on the American people, our plan builds upon existing programs that we know will work."

Legislation based on the College's plan will soon be introduced in Congress by Senator Jeff Bingaman. Under the plan, all Americans will be covered by an affordable health insurance plan by the end of this decade. The highlights of the proposal are as follows:

1. Coverage will be expanded in steps, starting with the poor- and near-poor and then later to all uninsured Americans.

2. States will be given strong financial incentives to cover more people through existing public programs -without imposing a new economic burden or unfunded mandates on economically strapped states. They would also be given more flexibility to establish their own new programs for the uninsured, backed up with dedicated federal funds.

3. Those who are not eligible for coverage under state programs will receive tax credits to help them buy coverage. They may buy coverage from a new group purchasing program modeled after the highly successful Federal Employees Health Benefits Program. Or, they could use their credits to buy individual insurance coverage or to help pay for the costs of employer-sponsored coverage.

4. The group purchasing program, based on the Federal Employees Health Benefits Program, will give eligible tax credit recipients the same choices of health plans, providers, and benefits that are now available to members of Congress, White House employees, and their families. At least two of the plans offered to them will have to include essential benefits as recommended by an expert Commission and approved by Congress.

5. Small employers will also be permitted to buy their health insurance from the same group purchasing program.

Dr. Walker urged Congress to enact legislation to assure that all Americans have access to affordable and sensible health insurance system by the end of this decade.

"As Americans, we should expect no less," she said.

The American College of Physicians-American Society of Internal Medicine is the nation's largest medical specialty organization and the second largest physician group. Membership encompasses more than 115,000 internal medicine physicians and medical students.
