Second Connecticut Foster Care Panic Will Devastate Children, National Advocacy Group Warns
  ALEXANDRIA, VA - May 23 - Governor John Rowland's response to the latest child abuse tragedy in Connecticut shows he has learned nothing from the harm he caused to children when he set off a foster-care panic eight years ago, a national child advocacy group said Friday.

Responding to the death of Al-Lex Daniels, Rowland demanded that child welfare workers rush to tear even more children from their parents -- setting off the second foster- care panic since he took office.

The State Senate will only compound the tragedy if it passes "sibling confiscation" legislation already approved by the House of Representatives, according to Richard Wexler, executive director of the National Coalition for Child Protection Reform.

"The most likely consequence of the Governor's foolish rhetoric and a sibling confiscation law can be summed up in three words: More dead children," Wexler said.

In 1995, after the death of Emily Hernandez, Governor Rowland, then newly in office, "confused child removal with child safety," Wexler said. "Then, as now, he demanded the removal of huge numbers of additional children in the name of 'erring on the side of child safety.' But experience across the nation and right in Connecticut shows that those are the most dangerous words in child welfare - because they make children less safe."

Wexler says Rowland's approach backfired. "The foster care population skyrocketed. But instead of making children safer, child abuse fatalities actually increased.

"That's because the real reason children 'known to the system' sometimes die is that underprepared, undertrained caseworkers are desperately overwhelmed. So they make bad mistakes in both directions. They leave some children in dangerous homes even as they take many more from homes that are safe or could be made safe with the right kinds of help.

"Governor Rowland's foster-care panic overwhelmed workers even more. So they made more mistakes, and more children wound up dead."

Wexler said that since Connecticut is a small state, some might argue that the increase in fatalities after Rowland's first panic might be coincidence. "But exactly the same thing happened in much larger jurisdictions," Wexler said.

"Cases like the Emily Hernandez case set off foster care panics in Illinois in 1993, New York City in 1996 and Florida in 1999. In all three cases, huge increases in the number of children taken from their parents were followed by increases in child abuse fatalities.

"There is one crucial difference between these other places and Connecticut," Wexler said. "The other places all learned from their mistakes. Illinois and New York City reversed course and embraced safe, proven programs for keeping families together. As a result, their foster care populations plummeted. At the same time, child safety improved. Florida is now promising to do the same.

"In contrast, Governor Rowland has chosen to react to the latest tragedy by repeating his mistakes. This time he has no excuse for the harm his approach will do to children," Wexler said.

Wexler said children will be harmed in many ways. "Some will be harmed by the devastating emotional trauma of being needlessly removed from everyone loving and familiar. Others will be harmed when they are abused in foster care itself. The more a foster care system is overloaded, the more dangerous it becomes, as workers are tempted to overcrowd foster homes and lower standards for foster parents. And still other children, truly in danger in their own homes, will be overlooked because workers will be too overwhelmed to find them."

Wexler said the legislature will compound the damage if it passes a bill to require that, if DCF takes away one child, it must take all the child's siblings.

"There are conditions in a home, often due to a family's poverty, that may be dangerous to a two-year-old but not a 17-year-old," Wexler said. "While the best solution would be to improve the living conditions for everyone, at a minimum there is no need to remove the child for whom there is no danger," Wexler said.
