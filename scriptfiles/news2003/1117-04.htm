Energy Bill Not a Comprehensive Energy Policy
  

WASHINGTON - November 17 - Remarks of William H. Meadows:

"The sponsors of this Energy Bill have been calling it an "Energy Policy".

There is no doubt that America would be better off if we had a comprehensive energy policy. A true energy policy would chart a new energy future, take us in a new direction, and learn from the mistakes in the past to solve the real challenges we face today. But make no mistake about it: this bill doesn't qualify as an energy policy. Not even close.

It doesn't do anything to wean us from our dependence on oil.

It doesn't do anything to make cars go further on a gallon of gas.

And it makes oil and gas production "Job Number One" for our most pristine and important remaining wild public lands across the Rocky Mountain West, and Alaska, even though those lands cannot begin to produce enough to change our dependence on foreign oil.

Yes, it spares, for now, the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge from a similar fate. For that we are grateful. But we are not fooling ourselves. If the energy conferees who cobbled together this grab-bag of party favors for the oil, gas, coal, and nuclear industries thought they could get away with it, the Arctic Refuge would be in there too.

This bill is ample evidence that the kind of closed-door, back-room process that has typified this conference invariably leads to legislation that benefits private special interests, and thumbs its nose at the public interest.

A poll released by Zogby International last week found 55 percent of likely voters, once you describe this bill to them, think it would be better if Congress didn't pass this bill at all. It's time for Congress to listen to the people, and toss this bill to in the recycling bin."
