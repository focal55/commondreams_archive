Cleanup Slowdown: How Under-Funding the Superfund Program Harms Communities Across America
   WASHINGTON - August 1 - Twenty-five years after Love Canal, the toxic waste catastrophe that prompted passage of the Superfund law, the Bush administration is slowing down cleanups and keeping the public in the dark about which Superfund sites will receive cleanup funds this year.

On August 7th, U.S. PIRG will release Cleanup Slowdown: How Under-Funding the Superfund Program Harms Communities Across America detailing the potential local impacts of the Bush administration's dangerous policies.

U.S. PIRG documents toxic waste sites in 48 states that may be subject to a delayed cleanup or less stringent EPA oversight of cleanup activities conducted by polluters. The longer these sites remain polluted, the greater the threat to the health of neighboring communities.

The Bush administration's funding announcement last month showed that only 10 out of 20 new projects will receive funding this year. In addition, the administration gave no information about funding for the hundreds of sites around the country with ongoing cleanup work. U.S. PIRG examined funding information for FY02 and publicly available data on Superfund sites around the country to predict potentially affected sites.

The report includes state-by-state site information including contaminants of concern. Among the states with the most Superfund sites potentially affected by a lack of funding are California, Florida, Illinois, Massachusetts, Michigan, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Texas, and Washington.
