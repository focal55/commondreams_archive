Should Boeing be Debarred from Federal Contracts? Background Info
   WASHINGTON - May 8 - According to reports this week, the Air Force is conducting an administrative inquiry to determine if Boeing should be suspended or debarred from contracting with the federal government (see http://www.msnbc.com/news/909389.asp). On Monday, it was revealed that two federal investigations concern allegations that Boeing obtained proprietary information from Lockheed Martin in bidding for defense contracts.

Last year, the Project On Government Oversight (POGO) completed an investigation into the federal government’s suspension and debarment system and compiled extensive information about Boeing’s civil and criminal violations, and unethical conduct.

According to POGO’s report, over the past decade (1990-2002) “Boeing committed 36 violations and alleged violations and paid approximately $358 million in fines/penalties, restitution, and settlements.” The allegations included concern procurement fraud, violations of the Arms Export Control Act, defective pricing on contracts, and overcharging the government.

Despite these cases, Boeing was not suspended or debarred during the period POGO analyzed. In fact, only one of the 43 top contractors in POGO’s study was suspended – the General Electric Aircraft Division – and then only for five days. Current policies prescribe that taxpayer dollars only be awarded to “responsible” contractors that have a satisfactory record of “integrity and business ethics.” Unfortunately, POGO’s investigation confirmed that this is not always the case.
