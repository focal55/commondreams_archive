African-American Leaders Host Town Hall Meeting at Howard University School of Law on Affirmative Action
  WASHINGTON - March 12 - News Advisory:

Days before the U.S. Supreme Court hears the most significant affirmative action cases since Regents v. the University of California v. Bakke in 1978, leading African-American civil rights advocates, scholars and news media executives will meet in a historic town hall meeting to discuss Affirmative Action and the U.S. civil rights agenda. The Town Hall Meeting will take place on Friday, March 28 at Howard University's School of Law.

Speakers include Harvard University Professor Charles Ogletree; Professor Ron Walters, African-American Leadership Institute, University of Maryland; Wade Henderson, Executive Director, Leadership Conference on Civil Rights; Elaine Jones, Director-Counsel, NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund, Inc.; and George Curry, Syndicated Columnist.

WHAT: Affirmative Action Town Hall Meeting with Leaders of the African-American Community

WHERE: Howard University School of Law, Van Ness Campus 2900 Van Ness Street, N.W. Washington, D.C.

WHEN: Friday, March 28, 6 p.m. - 8 p.m.

WHO: Co-sponsors include Howard University, Americans for a Fair Chance, American Constitution Society (ACS), African-American Leadership Institute, Howard Law School Clinical Program, and ACS Howard University Chapter.

WHY: On April 1, the Supreme Court will hear Gratz v. Bollinger and Grutter v. Bollinger, cases that challenge the constitutionality of the affirmative action admissions policies at the University of Michigan. On the eve of the oral arguments in one of the most important legal battles in civil rights history, African-American leaders will speak out about Affirmative Action, race relations and the future of integration in the United States. Today's leadership will return to the historic Howard Law School, where, half a century ago, Thurgood Marshall and other civil rights lawyers prepared for Supreme Court oral arguments in Brown v. Board of Education, the landmark case that outlawed state-sponsored segregation in education.
