Conservation Groups Label Corps' Missouri River Assessment Dead on Arrival
  

WASHINGTON - November 17 - Conservation groups blasted a "new" Army Corps of Engineers biological assessment on the Missouri River released today as a failed effort and warned the document would lead to continued legal wrangling over the Missouri River's future.

The Corps sent the biological assessment to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, as the document will serve as the basis for consultation between the two agencies over endangered and threatened species concerns on the nation's longest river.

"This biological assessment is old news and is dead on arrival," said Chad Smith, Director of American Rivers' Nebraska Field Office. "It contradicts basic river science, fails to include the biological steps necessary to improve the river's health, and is simply a 'business as usual' poke in the eye from the Corps."

According to the assessment, the Corps does not believe restoration of more natural flows on the Missouri will benefit the river's ecology or specific endangered and threatened species on the river.

Flow restoration, namely modestly higher flows once every third spring and lower flows every summer, have been recommended by the Fish and Wildlife Service as a key step necessary to prevent species extinction on the Missouri.

The recommended flow changes are supported strongly by the National Academy of Science and all of the state fish and wildlife management agencies from the Missouri River basin states. Last summer, a federal judge ordered the Corps to implement lower summer flows to comply with the Fish and Wildlife Service's recommendations contained in their 2000 Biological Opinion.

The assessment will be reviewed by the Fish and Wildlife Service, which will then develop a new biological opinion on Missouri River dam operations. Last week, the Department of Interior announced members of a team that will write the new biological opinion by December 15.

"The Corps is trying to pretend that the patient can recover even though her heart has stopped beating," said Smith. "The science is clear: this river needs CPR."
