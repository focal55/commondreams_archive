Ruling Could Strike More Than 100 Death Sentences
  

WASHINGTON - September 2 - A ruling issued today by the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals could result in nearly 150 death sentences being invalidated, the latest result of the U.S. Supreme Court's 2002 determination that juries, not judges, must decide between a sentence of death or life in prison.

The Court's 8-3 vote is believed to mean that anyone sentenced to death in Arizona, Idaho and Montana under those states' old death sentencing statutes faces commutation to life. The same reasoning that led to the ruling eventually could apply to Colorado and Nebraska, two states that are not within the Ninth Circuit's jurisdiction, and perhaps could one day be extended to other states such as Alabama, Delaware and Florida, where in the past judges have overruled jurors' recommendation for life in prison and instead sentenced defendants to death.

"Today's ruling is landmark," said Brian Roberts, interim executive director of the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty."The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals has upheld the bedrock principle that juries and only juries may decide an issue as important as life or death. We are cautiously optimistic that either the U.S. Supreme Court will decline to accept an appeal of this decision or, if it does, that it will affirm the simple constitutional logic underlying it."

At issue prior to the Ninth Circuit's ruling was whether the Supreme Court's 2002 Ring decision applied to all death row inmates in Arizona and other states with similar death-sentencing statutes, or whether it applied only to a small number of people who were early in their appeals process.

Writing for the majority, Circuit Judge Sidney R. Thomas wrote,"By deciding that judges are not constitutionally permitted to decide whether defendants are eligible for the death penalty, the Supreme Court altered the fundamental bedrock principles applicable to capital murder trials."

Of the three states believed to be directly impacted by today's decision, Arizona has the largest death row population, at 126. Idaho has 17 people on death row and Montana has five.
