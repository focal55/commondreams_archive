Rights Group Urges Independent Verification on U.S. Weapons Inspection in Iraq
Center for Constitutional Rights Says Iraqi Scientist should be turned Over to UN Inspectors
   NEW YORK - April 21 - The United States must invite other nations to cooperate in the inspection of possible weapons of mass destruction (WMD) in Iraq, according to the leading human rights group, the Center for Constitutional Rights (CCR). The group also raised concern that U.S. and British forces might try and fabricate evidence of WMD'S to justify their rational for war, a concern amplified by recent reports that the an American military team has located a scientist who they claim has led them to materials that can be used as the building blocks for illegal weapons.

"No significant evidence of weapons of mass destruction has been discovered despite the fact that this was the major reason the U.S. gave for attacking Iraq," said CCR Executive Director Ron Daniels, adding that if the U.S. had intelligence before the war about these weapons, it would be reasonable to assume they would find them quickly.

"The search for and verification of weapons of mass destruction must be independently verified by United Nations weapons inspectors and their destruction carried in accordance with the Chemical Weapons Convention of 1993," Daniels asserted.

CCR President Michael Ratner said: "While the U.S. acted virtually unilaterally in attacking Iraq, it is now time to reassert the authority of international law and global institutions. We cannot permit the United States to operate in times of peace as they have in times of war." Regarding the Iraqi scientist who allegedly has led U.S. military inspectors to the site of buried weapons, Ratner added: "The U.S. must turn this man over to the United Nations and their experts who can make an independent assessment of him and the information he claims to possess."

Daniels pointed out that the world has a right to be cynical about U.S. claims concerning weapons of mass destruction in Iraq. He cited, as an example, a revelation by nuclear inspectors that documents offered by the United States and Britain as evidence Iraq tried to buy uranium from Niger between 1999 and 2001 had been forged. He said the forgeries demonstrated the importance of having an independent eye review all information on this issue.
