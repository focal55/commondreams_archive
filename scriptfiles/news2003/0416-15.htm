Western Washington University Associated Students Rejects Boise Bid; Commits To 100% Post Consumer Paper
   BELLINGHAM, WA - April 16 - The Associated Students of Western Washington University set a policy this week that requires all A.S. offices and organizations to use 100% post consumer paper. The A.S. Board of Directors also passed a resolution endorsing the switch, and encouraging other University departments to follow their lead.

After months of research by a team of students and administrative staff, it was determined that the cost of switching to 100% post consumer paper is minuscule, and outweighed by the long-term benefits of the switch. The paper contract will be awarded to New Leaf Paper or Badger Paper, both environmentally responsible companies and industry leaders in post consumer content paper.

Bids for 100% post consumer paper were offered by Georgia Pacific and Boise Office Solutions, but were rejected due to both companies’ socially and environmentally irresponsible business tactics. “We believe that the A.S. has taken a bold step by, not only switching to tree free paper, but also pursuing the most socially and environmentally responsible companies in the paper industry,” said Trey Avery, head of Students for Environmentally Responsible Business, the student organization that lead the paper campaign at Western. “I don’t want my tuition dollars supporting a company that is involved in the logging of old-growth and endangered forests around the world. Any school or company that does business with Boise is in the business of forest destruction.”

Western is joining a long list of companies and institutions that have canceled their contracts with Boise as a result of the environmental problems plaguing the company. Kinko’s, Levi Strauss, L.L. Bean, Patagonia, the University of Texas at Austin and the University of Notre Dame are just a few of Boise’s former customers that severed business ties with the logging giant for its refusal to stop the destructive practice of selling products made from endangered, old growth forests. Boise actively logs old growth forests throughout the U.S., and distributes wood products from endangered old growth forests around the world, including pristine forests in British Columbia. Boise has also come under increasingly intense public criticism for failing to join more than 400 companies, including Home Depot, Lowe’s and Kinko’s in a pledge to stop selling products made from endangered, old growth forests.

Widespread public support exists for old growth forest preservation. According to the Los Angeles Times, 9 out of 10 Americans favor preserving our remaining wilderness, and a Yankelovich poll shows that nearly two-thirds of Americans support old growth forest protection. Most tellingly, the Los Angeles Times reported last April that 70 percent of consumers in traditional timber regions such as Oregon and Washington favor an end to old growth logging.

Despite this, Boise is leading the charge against the popular U.S. Roadless policy, a measure that would protect 58.5 million acres of America’s public wilderness areas and is considered the most significant forest conservation measure in the last 100 years. Boise is also a top logger of forests on U.S. public lands and is one of America’s largest distributors of wood products from endangered, old growth forests throughout Indonesia, Canada, Chile, and Russia.

More than three-quarters of the world's old growth forests have already been logged or degraded, much within the past three decades. In the U.S., less than five percent of original forests remain.

“This is a major victory, but just the beginning of the larger campaign at Western,” said Avery. The coalition of campus environmental groups, which includes Students for Environmentally Responsible Business, Western Environmental Watch, and the A.S. Environmental Center are now asking the rest of the University to switch to tree-free paper.
