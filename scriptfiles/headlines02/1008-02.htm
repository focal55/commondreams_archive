Published on Tuesday, October 8, 2002 in the Guardian/UK
Ten Die as Israeli Helicopter Fires on Palestinian Crowd
by Chris McGreal in Khan Yunis, Gaza Strip
 

The worst came just as the residents of Al-Katiba thought they were safe. A little before 4am yesterday, hundreds of Palestinians spilled on to the streets to check on their neighbors and vent their anger as dozens of Israeli tanks withdrew after a grueling three-hour raid on the Gaza strip.

Ghada al-Farra watched from her balcony as her husband and 10-year-old son Fawzi joined the throng.

"I was holding my daughter in my arms looking over the balcony," she said. "I saw something in the sky, fire in the sky. I was about to shout to my husband but in a fraction of a second there was an explosion. Everything was smashed. My son was lying there crying 'Papa, I don't want to die'."

Fawzi survived with shrapnel wounds to a leg and both arms. But at least 10 people did die, including a 15-year-old boy and a middle-aged woman, and about 130 were wounded after an Israeli helicopter fired a missile into the crowd of several hundred people.

Two of the dead and 22 of the injured were from a single family, the al-Astals.

More bloodshed followed. Altogether 14 people died in the Israeli raid on the Khan Yunis district of southern Gaza, a Hamas stronghold which includes Al-Katiba. Another was killed and three were injured when the Israeli army opened fire on the hospital where many of the wounded from the night's attacks were taken.

The Israeli military claimed that someone "in the vicinity" had fired mortars at a nearby Jewish settlement.

And then Palestinians began killing Palestinians. Later in the morning, the head of Gaza's riot police, Colonel Rajeh Abu Lehiya, was abducted and shot, apparently in revenge for his role in the killing of three students during an anti-US demonstration a year ago. A brother of one of the students killed by the riot police, Raed Akel, claimed responsibility for the killing.

The colonel's murder provoked a bout of fighting between the Palestinian police and Hamas that left at least two more people dead.

The Israeli army said it launched the raid on Khan Yunis shortly after midnight to "disrupt Hamas activities in the area". In Al-Katiba, residents say they were forced to lie on the floors of their homes for more than two hours under Israeli guard, and forbidden even to go to the toilet.

Brigadier General Israel Ziff, the army commander in the area, said he gave orders to minimize civilian casualties but his men were forced to fight their way out of the area under heavy Palestinian attack and the helicopter was called in to protect his forces.

"They fired a lot and threw grenades. There was a battle there. The helicopter aimed at this armed group and hit them," he said.

Palestinians do not deny there were men with guns in the crowd but claim the only shooting was in the air in celebration at the Israeli withdrawal, and the lack of Israeli casualties proves the army was not under attack.

Whatever the truth, the helicopter fired a missile that exploded a few feet above the ground in front of Fatima al-Astal's home. Her 17-year-old son, Ahmad, was killed. His 10-year-old sister, Yusra, and adult brother, Mahmoud, were hit by shrapnel. Yusra was injured in the leg. A 15-year-old cousin was lying across the hospital ward from her. Both were in great pain.

"There were helicopters flying all night so we weren't surprised to hear one," said Mahmoud al-Astal after a doctor treated shrapnel wounds across his neck and back. "Suddenly there was this explosion. No one expected it or we would have run for cover. We would not have wanted the children there."

At Naser hospital, Haider al-Kedra, a surgeon, said a flood of casualties descended shortly after he heard the missile explode.

"We heard a huge explosion. Minutes later all these casualties appeared. They came three or four to an ambulance, in taxis, in private cars. We had to clear the gynecological unit to make room," he said.

Of the 101 admitted to the hospital, nearly one-third needed emergency surgery. Among the injured was a 70-year-old woman and, the hospital authorities say, about a dozen children.

At funerals a few hours later for several of the victims - "martyrs" as they are invariably called by the Palestinians - the green flags of Hamas and white banners of the al-Aqsa suicide bombers were on prominent display.

Hamas responded to the attack on the heart of its volatile stronghold by vowing to avenge the deaths of civilians "by the killing of civilians."

"The response for today's massacre will come in the shape of escalating resistance and strikes everywhere in the land of Palestine," Mahmoud al-Zahar, a senior member of Hamas, said. Another Hamas leader, Abed al Aziz Rantisi, called on supporters to "kill every Zionist".
