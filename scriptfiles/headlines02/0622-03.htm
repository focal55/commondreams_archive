Published on Saturday, June 22, 2002 in the Boston Globe
Massachusetts Politics
 Reich Supports Gay Right to Marry
by Joanna Weiss
 
In a move that is attracting national attention, gubernatorial candidate Robert Reich has announced that he supports the right of gays and lesbians to marry.

The announcement, first reported earlier this week in Bay Windows and InNewsweekly, two Boston gay newspapers, sets the former US labor secretary apart from his major-party opponents. The four other Democratic candidates support Vermont-style civil unions, which provide most of the legal benefits of marriage but are not recognized by other states. Republican Mitt Romney opposes civil unions, but supports domestic partnership laws that would give gay couples access to health benefits and survivorship rights.

Green Party candidate Jill Stein and Libertarian candidate Carla Howell also support marriage for gays.

''I became convinced that it was really a matter of leadership, and that it was a civil rights issue, and that it was important to make a stand,'' Reich said yesterday in an interview with the Globe.

Reich said gay marriage would be more far-reaching than the civil unions that are now law in Vermont. Gay couples married under Massachusetts law could sue more strongly for recognition in states that do not recognize civil unions, he said.

The announcement is gaining attention among gay and lesbian political activists around the country. Gay rights groups estimate that gays make up 5 percent of the electorate, but are an increasingly visible constituency and source of campaign contributions.

The Reich move also underscores the perceived clout of gay voters in the Massachusetts Democratic primary. Earlier this week, Senate President Thomas Birmingham, also a gubernatorial candidate, pleased gay rights leaders when he blocked a vote on a ballot initiative that would bar gay marriage, preventing its advancement. Birmingham adamantly opposes the ballot question.

But support of gay marriage is a rare position. Gay rights advocates yesterday could not name a prominent national politician who shares Reich's position. And while only Vermont recognizes civil unions, and Hawaii and California offer more limited benefit rights for gay couples, at least 36 states have passed bills outlawing gay marriage.

''He deserves enormous credit for taking such a principled position,'' said David Smith, a spokesman for the Washington, D.C.-based Human Rights Campaign. ''This is a refreshing change.''

Reich does not have a blueprint for working a marriage bill through Massachusetts, where the Legislature has been reluctant to support more modest gay rights measures, such as domestic partnership health benefits for gay partners of state employees.

But Reich says that he is confident that, by educating the public, he will be able to convince state residents to support gay marriage, and to see it, as he does, as a civil rights issue. The Legislature will respond to a groundswell of support, he said.

''It's a matter of leadership,'' he said. ''Many people have not focused on the issue. They don't fully understand it.''

Reich's decision to support gay marriages surprised even some members of his staff. Until now, he has said he supported civil unions, but feared that gay marriage would cause a backlash that could jeopardize gay rights.

But Reich said he changed his mind shortly before he spoke at the Boston Pride gay and lesbian festival earlier this month, after talking to supporters - particularly his campaign volunteer Corey Johnson, a young gay man from the North Shore who made national news when he came out to his high school football team.

Gay rights advocates say the topic is still provoking tough battles. In Vermont, civil unions came about only after a lengthy and confrontational process. It began with a 1999 ruling from the state Supreme Court, ordering the Legislature to create a remedy that gave gay and lesbian relationships legal status. After fiery debate, legislators produced the civil union law in 2000.

The state elections that followed were widely seen as a backlash: the state House of Representatives changed hands and became Republican-controlled, while Democrats barely retained control of the state Senate.

In Massachusetts, debates on gay rights have long been contentious. James Lafferty, spokesman for Massachusetts Citizens for Marriage, the group that proposed the ballot initiative, said he believes voters here remain opposed to gay marriage. ''It's cultural. It's very religious,'' he said. ''It's not like some sort of tax policy where you can change a few numbers and everyone smiles and goes along. It's very passionate.''

His group seeks to put the gay marriage ban before voters in 2004, and won a legal victory this month when the state's highest court said the question could go forward. It requires the support of 25 percent of legislators in two subsequent sessions to reach the ballot.

And while some local gay rights advocates said they sense that views on the issue are evolving within the public and the State House, they were cautious about the chances for gay marriage anytime soon, especially with a social conservative, Thomas Finneran, presiding as speaker of the House.

''People are really grappling with it,'' said Gary Buseck, executive director of Gay and Lesbian Advocates and Defenders in Boston. ''To get it through the Legislature is maybe a long shot.''

Reich said the issue would be one of his top priorities, but offered no timetable for its achievement. The former labor secretary had a front-row seat for a similar battle in 1993, when President Clinton fought to allow openly gay soldiers in the military. Clinton eventually implemented a controversial ''don't ask, don't tell'' policy, and observers said he lost political capital.

Reich said he could avoid the same problems by sticking to a clear, if ambitious, goal rather than seeking a compromise like civil unions.

''Sometimes, more confusion is sowed by politicians who don't take clear stands - or want to, in a way, satisfy everyone without educating anyone about an issue,'' he said.
