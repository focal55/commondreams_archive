Published on Friday, September 20, 2002 by the BBC
Explosions Rock Arafat HQ
 

Israeli forces besieging Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat have been blowing up buildings at his West Bank headquarters, as the number killed in the latest suicide bombing rose to six.


The Israeli army blows up a building in Palestinian President Yasser Arafat's Ramallah compound Sept. 20, 2002. Israel forces kept Yasser Arafat under siege in his West Bank presidential compound and sent tanks deep into the Gaza Strip after a Palestinian suicide bomber killed five people on an Israeli bus. Photo by Ammar Awad/Reuters
A huge pall of smoke rose from the compound in Ramallah as a series of explosions rocked the area.

Latest reports say an armoured bulldozer is digging a trench around Mr Arafat's office building.

One of the demolished buildings is thought to be directly behind where Mr Arafat is currently located.

But the Associated Press quotes one of his aides as saying he remains in good spirits.

The army has blown up or bulldozed more than 10 structures in the compound, already battered by previous incursions that followed Palestinian attacks in Israel, Reuters news agency said.

There has been no immediate comment from the Israeli military, whose tanks have been surrounding the compound since late on Thursday.


The (Israeli) policy of siege, starvation and military incursions... is in total contravention, not only of signed agreements, but also of international law and humanitarian norms

Saudi Foreign Minister Saud al Faisal
The BBC's Barbara Plett in Ramallah says the Israelis are levelling the compound around the Palestinian leader, aiming to isolate him completely.

They accuse him of failing to rein in Palestinian militants involved in this week's suicide bombings inside Israel.

During the night, about 20 Palestinians left the compound and surrendered to the Israelis.

However, the army is demanding the surrender of other key figures - including the head of West Bank intelligence - they believe to be inside.

Chief Palestinian negotiator Saeb Erekat appealed to the international community to take action to end the Israeli "aggression", particularly against Mr Arafat.

In comments to the French news agency, AFP, Mr Erekat said the siege was part of a plan by Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon to destroy the peace process.

However, Israeli Defence Minister Binyamin Bin-Eliezer told army radio that Israel had "no intention" of firing at Mr Arafat or expelling him from the West Bank.

Saudi Arabia's foreign minister has said the cycle of violence in the Middle East "will never diminish unless the legitimate rights of the Palestinians are achieved."

The Palestinians faced a "human catastrophe" because of Israeli policies of siege, starvation, military incursions and destruction of property, Foreign Minister Saud al-Faisal told the UN General Assembly late on Thursday.

For much of the past year, the Palestinian leader has been confined in Ramallah, with most of his compound destroyed and his security forces decimated.

Helicopter fire

The Ramallah siege coincided with two Israeli army incursions into the Gaza Strip, with one group advancing to the edge of Gaza City and another penetrating two kilometres (one mile) near the towns of Beit Lahiya and Beit Hanun.

Witnesses in Gaza City saw tanks and helicopters fire into the densely populated Shijaia district.

Two Palestinians - a man and a woman - were killed by the Israeli fire. Witnesses said that troops blew up several metal workshops in Gaza.

Thursday's bombing in Tel Aviv was the second in two days - the first fatal suicide attacks in Israel for six weeks.

A Palestinian bomber blew himself up on a crowded bus in Allenby street, instantly killing five people and injuring about 50 others.

One of the wounded - a young man from Scotland - has since died of his injuries.

'Cruel terrorism'

Israel's cabinet met for a full emergency session for the first time in six months on Thursday night and decided to "isolate" Mr Arafat.

But the question of expelling the Palestinian leader was apparently dropped on the grounds that it would do Israel more harm than good.

The wanted Palestinians inside Mr Arafat's compound are accused of mounting attacks on Israelis.

They include the head of West Bank intelligence, Tawfiq Tirawi, accused by Israel of involvement in terrorist activities, and Mahmud Damra, the leader of Mr Arafat's Force 17 bodyguard.

Israeli sources are talking about an unlimited stay for the military.

They have tightened the curfew in Ramallah and other West Bank towns.
