Published on Sunday, January 27, 2002 by the Associated Press
Afghans Blame U.S. for Slaying Innocents During School Raid
by Dario Lopez-Mills
 
TARIN KOWT, Afghanistan - Afghans accused U.S. forces Saturday of attacking a school and killing people sheltering there in a raid the Pentagon claimed targeted Taliban fighters.

According to Pentagon accounts, special forces attacked two enemy compounds Wednesday night at Hazar Qadam, about 60 miles north of Kandahar, and killed about 15 people in a firefight. One U.S. soldier was wounded in the ankle. Twenty-seven prisoners were taken.


Bari Gul, center, and Abdul Salam, right, speak to the media Saturday Jan. 27, 2002, about an alleged incident involving U.S. Army Special Forces in the village of Tarin Kowt, Afghanistan. Bari Gul says his brother, Haji Sana Gul was among the several killed by U.S. special forces on Jan. 24. According to the men, the victims were anti-Taliban forces. (AP Photo/Dario Lopez-Mills)
U.S. officials initially described the target as an al-Qaida "leadership facility," but said Friday that the people there were Taliban fighters. They described the compounds, located a short distance from one another, as a munitions store holding hundreds of mortar rounds, rockets, rocket-propelled grenades and launchers, and more than 500,000 rounds of small-arms ammunition. They were destroyed by fire from a C-130 gunship.

But villagers in Uruzgan province, where the raid took place, said Saturday that those killed were neither Taliban nor al-Qaida fighters, but local people sent to negotiate the surrender of weapons from Taliban in the area.

In Tarin Kowt, a town between Kandahar and the provincial capital, also called Uruzgan, residents said a local official, Jal Mohamad Khan, sent a party of 18 men commanded by Tarin Kowt resident Haji Sana Gul to negotiate with Taliban forces on Wednesday.

They succeeded in confiscating a large cache of weapons and trucks, said Bari Gul, the brother of Haji Sana Gul. The Taliban apparently left the area, and Haji Sana Gul's men spent the night in a local madrassa, or Islamic school, with dozens of other people.

U.S. special forces swooped in around 3 a.m. Thursday morning and shot and killed many people in the madrassa, including Haji Sana Gul, then called in an air strike, Bari Gul said. Two of the dead were later found with their hands tied behind their backs, he said.

Special forces also went to a district building about a mile away and killed three more people, Bari Gul said.

Pentagon spokesman Maj. Mike Halbig said Saturday that the events described by the Afghans "don't fit with any of the information we have" about Wednesday's raid. He said U.S. special forces are trained to make sure they raid the correct targets.

In Kandahar, Yusuf Pashtun, an aide of Kandahar Gov. Gul Agha, told The Associated Press that he had spoken to officials from Uruzgan province who complained about being targeted and said police buildings were hit in the raid.

Pashtun acknowledged that there had long been reports that three former Taliban ministers were hiding around Uruzgan city, and that there had been reports that Mullah Mohammed Omar, the Taliban leader and the most wanted man in Afghanistan after Osama bin Laden, was there.

Pashtun said the officials told him that any Taliban officials who had been there were gone long before the raid.

"It looks like it was raided by mistake," Pashtun said. "The people of the district center are very much against the Taliban."
