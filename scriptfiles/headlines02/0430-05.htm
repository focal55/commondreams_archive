Published on Tuesday, April 30, 2002 by Reuters
Oil Company Execs to Testify on Gasoline Prices
 
WASHINGTON (Reuters) - Senior executives of several major oil companies will testify on Tuesday before a Senate panel investigating why American motorists are paying sharply higher gasoline prices.

The Senate's Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations issued a nearly 400-page report on Monday that said the main reason behind rising fuel prices was a series of oil company mergers in the past few years that gave firms more market power.

The Democratic-led panel also alleged that Marathon Ashland Petroleum deliberately withheld supplies in early 2000 to keep fuel prices high. A Marathon spokesman denied any wrongdoing by the company.

Several other big oil companies failed to have adequate U.S. gasoline supplies on hand to meet demand during the past two years, the panel's report said.

Sen. Carl Levin, a Michigan Democrat who chairs the subcommittee, said he was holding a two-day hearing to discuss how gasoline is priced and why it has become so volatile.

Gasoline marketing managers and vice presidents with BP, Exxon Mobil, Marathon Ashland Petroleum, ChevronTexaco and Royal Dutch/Shell Group's U.S. unit Shell Oil were scheduled to testify.

The Senate panel's report said motor fuel prices have become more volatile than ever. "Gasoline prices now regularly vary more in one month than they previously did in entire years," it said.

This spring, U.S. retail gasoline prices increased at a faster rate than at any other time in the past 50 years.

Prices have eased in the past few weeks. The Energy Department said on Monday that the nationwide average gasoline price was $1.393 per gallon, down 23 cents from one year ago. Prices are higher on the West Coast and tend to be cheaper in the South and the Gulf Coast regions.

The federal government has forecast that the nationwide price of unleaded regular gasoline will average $1.46 a gallon throughout the summer driving season.

The American Petroleum Institute said a series of federal and state investigations over the last 30 years have found no evidence of collusion or anti-competitive behavior in the oil industry. Gasoline prices rise sharply when there are refinery and pipeline interruptions, the oil industry group said.
