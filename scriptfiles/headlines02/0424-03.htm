Published on Wednesday, April 24, 2002 by Reuters
Egypt's Mubarak Says Israel Uses 'State Terrorism'
 
CAIRO - Egypt's president said on Wednesday that "state terrorism" by Israel to crush what he called legitimate Palestinian resistance would fuel an appetite for revenge.

But President Hosni Mubarak, addressing the nation in a televised speech, said his country would continue to tread the path of moderation and caution in dealing with its neighbor, without letting heated emotions dictate policies.

"(Israel) has prevented aid agencies and media from entering towns, villages and refugee camps in order to conceal the brutal crimes the Israeli army committed," Mubarak said, accusing Israel of "barbaric aggression."

"(Israel) uses arguments equating legitimate Palestinian resistance with occupation and terrorism...to avoid its ugly Israeli practices being depicted as state terrorism," he added.

Israel has threatened to block a United Nations mission to probe its devastating assault on the Jenin refugee camp during controversial military operations begun last month.

The operations "will only deepen the feelings of hatred, resentment, and the desire for revenge among not only the Palestinian people, but some 300 million Arabs," Mubarak said.

The speech marked the 20th anniversary of the full return of the Sinai to Egypt after its 1979 peace treaty with Israel. Sinai Liberation Day, a national holiday, is on Thursday.

Mubarak, Egypt's air force chief during its 1973 war with Israel, said Egypt would continue a policy of moderation and to practice restraint.

"Our duty now is remain alert, act with wisdom and reason, and make sure we plan our steps without emotional overreaction," he said, adding Egypt needed to protect political, economic and social gains.

Egypt's relations with Israel have come under increasing strain since Palestinians began an uprising against Israeli occupation in the West Bank and Gaza Strip in September 2000.

Egypt scaled back contacts with the Israeli government earlier this month in response to rising public anger, but left its vital diplomatic channels open.

Calls by protesters, opposition politicians and television commentators for more action continue.

Egypt and Jordan are the only Arab countries to sign full peace treaties with Israel. Both countries also maintain close political, economic and military ties with Washington.

Since the 1990-91 Gulf crisis, the Arab world has been dominated by Mubarak's moderate line and hard-liners once led by Saddam Hussein's Iraq have been sidelined.
