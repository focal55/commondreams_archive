Published on Friday, September 13, 2002 by Reuters
British Troop Movements Fuel Talk of War with Iraq
by Gideon Long
 

LONDON - British troops will start one of their biggest exercises in years this weekend amid speculation of a war with Iraq, but the government said a resolution to the crisis in the Gulf might not involve ousting Saddam Hussein.

The Ministry of Defense said 6,000 troops will move to a Royal Air Force base in eastern England on Sunday to kick off Operation Log Viper.

Around 1,000 vehicles will be used during the month-long exercise to cart tons of equipment through East Anglia and southeast England. Some of it will be taken to a special military port on the southern coast.

"It's the largest UK-based army exercise since 1998... it's taking place over a massive area," a ministry spokesman said.

The ministry says the exercise has been planned for over a year and is not related to the deepening crisis over Iraq.

It dismissed as "highly speculative" a report in the Daily Telegraph newspaper which said troops would begin deploying to Kuwait within two weeks in preparation for a possible attack.

President Bush turned up the heat on Saddam on Thursday, urging the United Nations to force him to disarm and saying action would follow if Baghdad failed to comply.

He has openly declared his intention to oust Saddam.

But in a further sign of differences between Washington and London, British Foreign Minister Jack Straw said toppling the Iraqi leader was not British policy.

Asked if a resolution to the crisis would necessarily mean a change of leadership in Iraq, Straw, speaking from New York on Thursday night, told BBC radio: "No it doesn't."

"You may get regime change as a consequence of military action, you may get regime change as the only way of enforcing the will of the United Nations, but the focus has to be on these weapons of mass destruction," he said.

"If we can ensure by rigorous unconditional inspection that Iraq is rid of those weapons of mass destruction, in a peaceful way, then the argument about military action recedes."

However, he acknowledged something had to be done.

"We can't just allow Iraq to carry on in this way, otherwise, as (U.N. Secretary-General) Kofi Annan said, we fail to face up to our responsibilities."

"All of us...would dearly wish to be rid of Saddam Hussein."

In his first public statements on the stand-off with Baghdad, British finance minister Gordon Brown threw his weight behind Prime Minister Tony Blair.

"I fully support the prime minister in all these issues and I think that is well understood," he told the Financial Times.

Blair has alarmed many within his ruling Labor Party with his unflinching support for Washington's tough line against Iraq, which has refused to allow the return of U.N. arms inspectors since 1998.

The prime minister warmly welcomed Bush's hawkish speech to the United Nations on Thursday and said he would recall parliament on September 24 to debate Iraq.

More than 160 members in the 659-seat chamber of the parliament have signed a motion expressing "deep unease" about Britain backing military strikes.
