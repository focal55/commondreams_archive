Published on Sunday, August 11, 2002 in the Boston Globe
Harvard Square Protesters Rally Against Coca-Cola, Gap
by Douglas Belkin
 

Pedestrians in Harvard Square had their pick of rallies yesterday; students protested Coca-Cola's health-care policy in Africa at 2 p.m. and a different group demonstrated later against the Gap's labor policies in Central America.

The rally against Coca-Cola, sponsored by the Harvard AIDS Coalition, drew about 50 students, who, according to organizers, condemned the '' exploitation'' of African workers. Protesters contend that Coca-Cola is the largest private employer on the continent, but does not extend health benefits to 100,000 contract employees and their families, scores of whom are HIV-positive.

''Why is it you can get a soda anywhere in Africa, but people infected with HIV can't get medication?'' asked organizer Florence Twu.

Attempts to reach Coca-Cola Co. for comment last night were unsuccessful.

Students protesting what they said were sweatshops the Gap clothing store chain runs in Central America argued that the average salary of the 100,000 textile workers in El Salvador is $5 a day - one third the daily salary considered to be poverty level by the government.

Ginny Coughlin, an organizer for the Union of Needletrades, Industrial, and Textile Employees, said that when some of the 10,000 employees working under contract for the Gap tried to unionize, the work was moved to different factories.

The protest, which organizers said drew about 100 people, highlighted a report by a group of students who recently returned from a fact-finding mission to some of the factories, as well as tearful speeches by Central American textile-factory employees. Coughlin said organizers are not yet calling for a boycott of the Gap, but are demanding accountability from corporate headquarters.

The store in Cambridge referred questions to a Gap spokesman. Calls to him were not returned last night, but a message on the company's Web site said, ''We want garment workers to be treated with dignity and respect.''
