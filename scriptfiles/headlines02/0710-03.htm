Published on Wednesday, July 10, 2002 in the Guardian of London
Bush Blames It All On Dotcom Era
by David Teather in New York
 
President George Bush yesterday proposed doubling the maximum prison sentence for corporate fraud and blamed the excesses of the dotcom era for the wave of financial scandals in the US in the past six months.

In a speech to business leaders on Wall Street, the US president detailed measures to restore shattered confidence in the financial system. "The business pages of American newspapers should not read like a scandal sheet," he said.

Shareholders and workers in the US have been stunned by a series of frauds, insider dealing and corporate greed that have toppled some of the nation's biggest companies.

The fear of further scandals has brought stock markets back to the lows they hit after September 11. They have also become a political liability for the Bush administration in the run-up to mid-term elections in November.

Mr Bush said the maximum prison sentence for executives found to be committing fraud should be increased from five to 10 years and perpetrators should be forced to return any gains made on the crime. He called for a new era of integrity among business leaders.

"There is a difference between ambition and destructive greed," he said. "These scandals have hurt the reputations of many good and honest companies. They have hurt the stock market and, worst of all, they're hurting millions of people who depend on the integrity of businesses for their livelihood and their retirement."

Mr Bush ordered the creation of a corporate fraud task force within the department of justice. He said laws covering obstruction of justice and document shredding would be beefed up, in a clear nod to the Enron scandal.

An additional $100m (£64.7m) was promised for the chief financial regulator, the Securities and Exchange Commission, which is buckling under the weight of new cases. The sum was less than the SEC had been seeking.

The president backed proposals from the New York stock exchange for all share option plans to be approved by investors, and said compensation packages should be explained in "plain English" in annual reports. He also urged companies to stop giving loans to senior directors: the former chief executive of WorldCom, Bernie Ebbers, received loans of $408m from his company.

The speech was widely criticized for not going far enough. Eliot Spitzer, the New York attorney general, who threatened legal action against Wall Street bank Merrill Lynch over alleged misleading advice, described the proposals as "enormously disappointing".

The house of representatives Democratic leader, Richard Gephardt, said: "So far the administration's approach has been a familiar strategy: use harsh rhetoric to condemn wrongdoers while delaying and watering down whatever reforms might have come out of Congress."

Shares on Wall Street were trading marginally lower after the speech.

The seeds for the current scandals at firms including Enron, Xerox, WorldCom and Tyco were sown during the "anything goes" hi-tech boom, Mr Bush said. "A lot of money was made but too often standards were tossed out."

Many of the proposals echoed plans by other politicians or financial watchdogs. They included an attempt to remove conflicts of interest by limiting the amount of consulting work that can be carried out by a company's auditor. This is part of a bill being debated in Congress that would also see the creation of an independent board to oversee the profession.
