Published on Saturday, November 23, 2002 by the Toronto Globe & Mail
Putin Warns Bush Against Unilateral Action on Iraq
Russian President also needles U.S. leader about the apparent survival of bin Laden
by Mark MacKinnon
 

RUSSIA -- Russian President Vladimir Putin warned George W. Bush yesterday against unilateral action in Iraq, and suggested the U.S. President needs to keep a close eye on his allies, as well as his perceived foes, in the war on terrorism.

After an 80-minute meeting amid the imperial splendor of the Catherine Palace in the countryside south of St. Petersburg, Mr. Putin repeated his long-held belief that any action against Iraq should be taken within the framework of the United Nations. He also suggested Iraq shouldn't be the top priority, and that the focus on Saddam Hussein is a distraction from the international antiterrorism fight.

Asked by a U.S. reporter about his position on military action in Iraq, Mr. Putin turned the focus to neighboring Saudi Arabia, a country seen by the White House as a front-line, if not entirely reliable, ally since last year's attacks on New York and Washington.

"We should not forget about those who finance terrorism," Mr. Putin said. "Of the 19 terrorists who committed the main attacks on Sept. 11 against the United States, 16 are citizens of Saudi Arabia, and we should not forget about that."

Mr. Putin also cautioned that while Pakistan, another ostensible U.S. ally, has been reasonably stable under the rule of President Pervez Musharraf, the country has a dangerous mix of both nuclear weapons and active militant groups that needs to be watched.

As if underscoring his point that the war on terrorism can't be constrained to Iraq, Mr. Putin then asked a question that has been on the minds of many of late: "Where has Osama bin Laden taken refuge?"

In the process, he gently poked at what has become a U.S. sore spot since audiotapes released earlier this month seemed to confirm the al-Qaeda leader is still alive.

Since the tapes surfaced, politicians in the United States and around the world have questioned whether Mr. Bush should not focus first on finishing the fight against al-Qaeda before tackling the Iraq question.

However, despite Russia's reservations about using military action to disarm Mr. Hussein, Mr. Putin and Mr. Bush did sign on to a joint declaration calling for Iraq to disarm.

"We call on Iraq to co-operate fully and unconditionally in its disarmament obligations or face serious consequences," the statement reads. However, it made no reference to the possible use of military action to enforce the demand.

While Russia, one of Iraq's few diplomatic allies, maintains that the recently passed UN resolution that sent weapons inspectors back to Baghdad does not contain any automatic triggers for war, the language of the statement was tough enough to send oil prices higher in trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday.

Mr. Bush made the trip to Russia yesterday to communicate to a skeptical public here that this week's decision to expand the North Atlantic Treaty Organization into former Soviet territory is nothing to be feared. The alliance's enemy, Mr. Bush said, is no longer Moscow, but international terrorist organizations such as al-Qaeda.

"The strategy of NATO is going to be based upon the fact that the Cold War is over," Mr. Bush said. "Russia is a friend; Russia is not an enemy."

After his meeting with Mr. Putin, the U.S. President flew onward to Vilnius to meet with the leaders of Lithuania, Latvia and Estonia, three of the newest NATO members.

The Baltic republics -- once part of the Soviet Union, and now elated to be embraced by the West -- were preparing a hero's welcome for Mr. Bush.
