Published on Friday, June 14, 2002 by Reuters
Palestinians Say Any U.S. Plan Must End Occupation
by Mohammed Assadi
 

RAMALLAH, West Bank - The chief Palestinian negotiator said on Friday that a promised U.S. proposal to create a Palestinian state must also set a deadline for ending Israel's occupation of the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

President Bush vowed on Thursday to lay out a plan for Palestinian statehood, but has yet to provide enough details to dispel uncertainty over American policy in the region.

Palestinian negotiator Saeb Erekat demanded that Bush set a timetable for reaching a clearly defined goal, which should be creation of a state in all of the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

"What is needed from the U.S. administration is to specify a mechanism and to specify a timeline that will end the Israeli occupation," Erekat told Reuters.

Israel's army said its troops shot dead a Palestinian man after he stabbed and slightly wounded a Jewish settler at a gas station in Kedumim, a West Bank settlement. Palestinian medics said they saw no weapon near the body.

Bush is preparing to unveil, probably next week, a framework for renewed negotiations. The idea is to take Bush's principles to a Middle East conference attended by cabinet ministers in the hope of getting the parties to agree on the path ahead.

"We must build the institutions necessary for the evolution of a Palestinian state which can live peacefully in the region and provide hope for the suffering Palestinian people," Bush said on Thursday.

ISRAELI RAIDS

Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon opposes any discussion of Palestinian statehood before an end to violence and sweeping reform of President Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority.

As both sides awaited Bush's proposals, Israeli forces raided two Palestinian-ruled areas overnight, hours after Arafat convened his new cabinet for the first time, calling for an end to violence and promising to set dates for elections.

In the latest of Israel's almost daily incursions, the army said it destroyed a bomb workshop and detained three people in the divided West Bank city of Hebron. Troops detained three more people in a raid on the town of Bir Zeit near Ramallah.

The Palestinian cabinet met in Ramallah after Israel ended a three-day siege of Arafat's presidential compound there, which prevented the session from taking place earlier in the week.

The Palestinian leader pared down his cabinet to 21 ministers on Sunday and has promised to streamline his security services as part of reforms demanded by the United States and Israel as well as many Palestinian parliamentarians.

"The municipal elections will be held as soon as possible and legislative and presidential elections will be held in either December or January," Arafat said, adding that precise dates would be set in the next few days.

ISRAEL SEEKS CHANGE

"We should give the path of the peace of the brave...the chance it deserves for the security and the peace of the Israeli people and the Palestinian people and the rest of the region's people," the veteran former guerrilla chief said.

Israel received Arafat's remarks coolly, demanding actions not words to rein in militants behind attacks on Israelis.

"Under Arafat the Palestinian Authority has become a terror-endorsing authority and this is why we need to see a real change," Israeli cabinet minister Danny Naveh told Reuters Television.

The Israeli army has kept up a campaign of rolling raids into Palestinian-ruled areas with the declared aim of rooting out militants.

In a grim new twist, police said they were probing an Israeli gambling ring in the southern town of Kiryat Malachi suspected of betting on where the next suicide bombing would take place.

A Tel Aviv weekly which reported on the scheme said Jerusalem's odds were the shortest at 3-2. Israeli law prohibits gambling, but gaming thrives in illegal hideouts.

At least 1,396 Palestinians and 509 Israelis have been killed since the Palestinian revolt against Israeli occupation began in September 2000 after peace talks stalled.
