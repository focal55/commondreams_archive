Published on Thursday, November 21, 2002 by the Boston Globe
In Shift, Harvard Reinvites Controversial Poet
by Patrick Healy
 

Harvard University's English department, in a surprising reversal, has reinvited writer Tom Paulin to give a poetry reading on campus, one week after English professors and Paulin canceled the event because of complaints over Paulin's statements that Brooklyn-born Jewish settlers in Israel were ''Nazis'' who ''should be shot dead.''

After an intense two-hour discussion Tuesday evening that Harvard poet Jorie Graham said was marked by ''profound, acute scrutiny and self-scrutiny,'' English professors voted overwhelmingly to ask Paulin again to deliver Harvard's prestigious Morris Gray Lecture, and thereby defend the department against charges it had damaged free speech at Harvard.

''Free speech was a principle that needed upholding here,'' English professor Peter Sacks said yesterday. ''This was a clear reaffirmation that the department stood strongly by the First Amendment.''

While some professors had voiced free-speech concerns before the Paulin speech was canceled last week, they were drowned out by the roar of Paulin's critics, including students who began organizing a boycott of the reading and swamping English professors with angry e-mailed messages.

As the controversy grew - and President Lawrence H. Summers told department members that he objected to Paulin's poetry reading but was leaving the fate of it up to them - noted poetry scholar Helen Vendler and a few other professors quickly huddled with Paulin and decided to cancel the event.

The cancellation drew significant attention at Harvard and other campuses nationwide, with many scholars and First Amendment advocates dismayed that the English department would distance itself from an award-winning poet such as Paulin. It also caused a firestorm in the Harvard English department, with several literature professors cringing at the specter of trampled speech - and some of them concerned that Summers had applied pressure on the department, and that he was conveying his opinions about Paulin by way of a tenured English faculty member, Elisa New, with whom he is romantically involved.

Both Summers and New have told English department members that they found Paulin's comments about Israel objectionable. Two English professors said that, in reading New's view in an e-mail, they were concerned she was making an argument for Summers, too. The professors, who spoke on condition of anonymity, say the romantic relationship has been a complicated factor in the English department; one described feeling ''paranoid'' when speaking to New, for fear the discussion would get back to Summers.

New, a scholar of Puritan poetry and Jewish literature, denied yesterday that she ever acted as a ''surrogate'' for Summers, in the words of two professors. She said she has consistently supported Paulin's poetry reading, and has called for a public forum where Paulin's speech could be challenged. ''I speak for no one but myself, and I carry no messages,'' New said in an e-mail.

Alan J. Stone, a spokesman for Summers, added, ''Anyone who knows Professor New knows she speaks only for herself. Anyone who knows Larry Summers knows he has no difficulty making his views known directly.''

On Tuesday evening, the first time the full English faculty had a chance to discuss Paulin since the reading was canceled, Vendler and New - along with professors Marjorie Garber, Henry Louis Gates Jr., Elaine Scarry, and others - argued strenuously for reinviting Paulin and sending a message that the English department supported free speech, even if it is controversial or offensive.

''Our feeling of accountability to colleagues in other departments, as well as other universities, was also, of course, in range,'' Graham said. ''We are not the only ones to use speech, to worry about its nature and power and reach, especially at present.''

Paulin, who has written extensively about the violence in Northern Ireland, was invited last winter, and had been scheduled to speak on campus last Thursday. Days before the reading, however, students and alumni contacted English professors to protest recent remarks by Paulin - in particular, a poem in which he refers to the Israeli Army as a ''Zionist SS,'' and an interview with an Egyptian newspaper that quotes Paulin describing Brooklyn-born Jews living in Israeli settlements as ''Nazis'' and ''racists'' and saying that they ''should be shot dead.''

''While we in no sense endorse the extreme statements by Mr. Paulin that have occasioned concern in the Harvard community, we support a university environment that is host to a diversity of views,'' Lawrence Buell, the department chairman, said in a statement yesterday.

Harvard professors said yesterday that Paulin told them he wanted to come to Harvard, but wanted to discuss the invitation further with English professors.

Free-speech issues have engulfed Harvard this semester, ever since Summers made a powerful speech at Memorial Church in September decrying campus activities that may be viewed as tacitly supporting anti-Semitism.

This month, Harvard Business School officials reprimanded two students for publishing a satiric cartoon in a campus newspaper that the officials found insulting. And Monday afternoon, a Harvard Law School committee announced plans to draft a proposed harassment speech code.

Summers, in a statement yesterday, affirmed Harvard's support for free speech, saying, ''We are ultimately stronger as a university if we together maintain our robust commitment to free expression, including the freedom of groups on campus to invite speakers with controversial views.''

Asked if Summers had spoken to Buell or other English professors about the initial cancellation, Summers's spokesman, Stone, said the subject came up ''in casual conversation with a few colleagues, but only in passing and not in a directive way.''

Several students in Jewish organizations at Harvard said yesterday that they were appalled the English department had honored Paulin with the Gray Lecture, but that they support his right to speak if invited, and plan to protest if Paulin comes. But some observers were astonished.

''For the people who made this decision to reduce it to an issue of free speech is not only a distortion of free speech in this society, but it is, even worse, an opportunity to use the concept of free speech as a shield against the assumption of moral responsibility,'' said Robert Leikind, director of the New England regional office of the Anti-Defamation League.
