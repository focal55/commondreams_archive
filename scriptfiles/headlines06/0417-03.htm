Published on Monday, April 17, 2006 by the Sydney Morning Herald / Australia
Rumsfeld's Fate up to Cheney
by Mark Coultan
 

As members of the US military establishment argue over the responsibility of the Defence Secretary, Donald Rumsfeld, for the failures of the Iraq war, it has been suggested that his fate may lie with his old friend Dick Cheney, the Vice-President.

"The possibility of Rumsfeld leaving has definitely crossed the President's mind," Time magazine quotes an unnamed former White House official as saying.

"The key to it is the relationship with Cheney, and I don't know where that is right now."

Richard Myers, who retired as the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff six months ago, has defended his former boss, and implicitly, his own rule as the US's most senior military officer, against a growing chorus of retired generals who have called for Mr Rumsfeld's head.

In the past few weeks six retired generals have called for Mr Rumsfeld's resignation, citing a series of mistakes in the planning and early execution of the war in Iraq.

Behind the criticism is the implicit acknowledgement that the war is going badly and progress is not being made, as President George Bush and the current military leadership maintain.

Wallowing in the opinion polls, it is the last thing Mr Bush needs, especially as his standard reply to criticism of the conduct of the war is to say that he is taking the advice of the military on the ground.

The problems in Iraq are fracturing old conventions about the relationship between the US military and the civilian leadership, an extraordinary development during a war. And it has opened a public split in the military, mirroring the deep divisions in the country as a whole.

And while no one - especially Mr Bush - has convinced the public of what the next right step in Iraq is, there is a growing consensus that the US went wrong right after the invasion: a lack of troops to enforce law and order, dissolving the Iraqi army and allowing a nascent insurgency to form.

General Myers denied that he or other officers had been intimidated by his boss, or felt uncomfortable when confronted with dissenting views on Iraq, and said that if there were people with dissenting views who had stayed silent, "shame on them". He said it was inappropriate for retired officers to speak out when they had had the opportunity to speak when they were in uniform.

He said the senior officers had given Mr Rumsfeld their best advice. "If we don't do that, we should be shot."

But he did concede that "some of the senior civilian leadership" should not have criticised the then army chief-of-staff General Eric Shinseki, who told Congress shortly before the war began that much larger troop numbers might be needed and warned of ethnic conflict in the aftermath.
