Published on Monday, June 19, 2006 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Internet Rewrites Political Playbook
Tech-savvy campaigns go online to find votes, volunteers, funds
by Rachel Gordon
 

Dan Wood describes himself as being "politically inactive, other than voting'' until 2004. That's the year Howard Dean, the Democratic former governor of Vermont, ran for president and revolutionized a Web-based campaign strategy that raised money and organized volunteers.

"I guess I got sucked in,'' said Wood, a 41-year-old computer programmer from Alameda.

That may be an understatement. Today, he runs his own political blog, "A Progressive Alamedan,'' served as a delegate to this year's state Democratic convention and keeps politically informed through a dizzying number of Web sites and e-mails.

He is one of millions of voters across the nation touched by the expansion of tech-savvy political campaigns that increasingly are relying on computers and cell phones to engage voters.

"It makes it easy for people to get involved,'' said Carol Darr, director of George Washington University's Institute for Politics, Democracy & the Internet in Washington.

It also vastly expands a campaign's reach -- at a fraction of the cost of the traditional campaign tools of mailers and commercials.

"Now we can press a button and instantly send a message to 50,000, 100,000 people,'' said Bob Mulholland, senior adviser to Phil Angelides, the Democratic state treasurer who is challenging Republican Gov. Arnold Schwarzenegger in November.

Mulholland said the use of technology is particularly important in this race, which he expects will draw national attention from both parties. Mulholland hopes Angelides can capitalize on the deep dissatisfaction with President Bush, for whom Schwarzenegger campaigned.

"I think this race will create a big buzz on the Internet,'' Mulholland said.

While one of the goals of a candidate's Web site is to attract volunteers and to cement support among the electorate, soliciting contributions online also is a crucial component. Special interest groups, such as those focused on taxes, the environment and immigration, have Web sites and e-mail lists that they hope will influence the outcome of an election. Blogs -- short for Web logs, which are journals and newsletters -- also are spreading political information.

Surprisingly, tech-focused campaigns don't just target the young. Studies have shown that seniors also are plugged in. The biggest factors in the tech divide are economics and education, said Gary Selnow, a San Francisco State University communications professor.

Darr, from the Institute for Politics, Democracy & the Internet, said online campaigns still generally resonate strongest with a particular segment of the population -- a crucial group called "influencers.'' They're the folks, she said, who tend to head the school PTA, get involved in service organizations, serve on the board of the chamber of commerce, hold a seat on the city council and organize their local unions.

"They end up being the honeybees pollinating the culture, or, to use a less elegant analogy, they're like birds pooping seeds.''

They talk up -- or down -- candidates in person and online.

Take Wood, the Alameda computer programmer. Not only does he publicize his thoughts on his blog, but he also sends blast e-mails.

He recently sent one to about 150 friends, acquaintances and relatives on behalf of Debra Bowen, a Democratic candidate for secretary of state. . The missive began: "I've written this note and I'm sending it to just about every Californian in my e-address book. If you're a voter and either a Democrat or independent, please read on. If not, please forgive the intrusion, and I'll catch you another time!''

The use of new technology crosses party lines.

"We're looking forward to using technology to connect voters not only to the campaign but to each other,'' said Julie Soderlund, a spokeswoman for Schwarzenegger's re-election bid. Soderlund said the Internet is being used to recruit and organize volunteers, solicit donations and provide a one-stop venue to view -- and hear -- the governor's campaign platform. The site includes a blog and slideshows. There are plans to include podcasts and special ring tones for cell phones that Schwarzenegger supporters can download.

Campaigns post their television commercials online. Steve Westly, who lost to Angelides in the Democratic gubernatorial primary, posted a last-minute, visual and audio pitch targeting undecided voters.

There also are gimmicks to keep Web site visitors interested: Angelides has a cartoon on its Web site called "Take a Tour of Arnold's Neighborhood,'' and site visitors can sign an online petition voicing opposition to the governor's transportation policies. Opponents of Janet Reilly, who ran in the Democratic primary for an Assembly seat representing parts of San Francisco and San Mateo, created an online interactive game in which people could dress her in various outfits to highlight aspects of her personal financial portfolio.

The better-funded campaigns have a tech guru on staff. Many make use of e-mail lists, an inexpensive tool that's fairly easy to manage. The savviest operations mine databases to tailor messages to individual voters based on such indicators as a person's buying habits, gender, ethnicity, marital status, age and income.

Kathleen Hall Jamieson, professor of communication and director of the Annenberg Public Policy Center at the University of Pennsylvania, said the use of e-mail in political campaigns has a very specific purpose: To provide voters with regular reminders that their favored candidates are still in the race, need their vote and their money. Sometimes the message is nothing more than announcing an upcoming campaign event or the release of favorable polling information.

"It's used as a motivational tool for supporters,'' Jamieson said.

E-mail is most effective, experts say, when the recipients "opt in,'' that is, ask to be added to a campaign's roster. Otherwise, more often than not, the unsolicited e-mail is deleted before it's even read -- much like junk mail that lands in someone's mailbox and ends up tossed unopened into the recycling bin.

Campaign Web sites also are usually viewed by people already interested in the candidate or cause, experts say.

"People don't go searching for counter-viewpoints. They want to have their beliefs confirmed,'' said Selnow of S.F. State.

Undecided voters usually will navigate nonpartisan Web sites, run by groups such as the League of Women Voters, to get information.

Menaka Mahajan wasn't quite sure what she wanted when she logged on to Google in search of a local campaign where she could volunteer, with the goal of making contacts to help land a job.

Fresh out of UC Irvine with a degree in international studies and a focus on politics, Mahajan, 23, found the Web site of Fiona Ma, Reilly's opponent in the June 6 Assembly primary and the eventual victor. Mahajan read through Ma's list of endorsements, looked at the photos and glanced at Ma's priorities. Sold.

She clicked on the "get involved'' button, sent an e-mail to the deputy campaign manager and in March started volunteering -- making telephone calls, walking precincts, organizing house parties.

What happened with Mahajan is an example of a broader trend, the marrying of technology with traditional shoe-leather campaigning -- using voter contact information solicited online to follow-up with a phone call or a knock on the door.

"People still like the personal,'' Wood said. "They still want to see the candidate up close."
