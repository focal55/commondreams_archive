Published on Wednesday, May 17, 2006 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Selling off Public Assets Part of Mineta Congestion Plan
Transportation chief pushes for private partnerships
by Edward Epstein
 

Saying that port, highway and airport tie-ups are strangling the U.S. economy, Transportation Secretary Norm Mineta outlined on Tuesday what he called an ambitious national strategy to combat congestion.

Several aspects of the new Bush administration program are likely to generate controversy, including plans to encourage the sale or lease of public roads, bridges or other transportation infrastructure to private investors. The administration also expects criticism on a "Corridors of the Future" project in which local and state governments will be asked to compete to gain fast-track approval for three to five major projects that would slash congestion by the greatest amounts.

Gov. Arnold Schwarzenegger said early this year he was interested in exploring more public-private transportation partnerships as a way of building or maintaining more highways and bridges in a state of more than 35 million people that is growing rapidly and where transportation dollars are stretched thin.

Sales of public transportation facilities in the past few years have set off a flurry of interest among public officials, including Schwarzenegger. The sales have included Chicago Mayor Richard Daley's decision to reach a 99-year, $1.9 billion lease deal for the Chicago Skyway, a toll bridge, and Indiana's pending $3.8 billion, 75-year lease of the state's toll road.

The new operators will run the tollways and bridges, maintain them and get the right to raise tolls to pay off the debt they incur by entering into the deals. There has been some criticism about privatizing public assets and opening the door to sharp toll increases.

Mineta said he has met recently with investment bankers and hedge fund operators who are interested in similar deals or in actually building and operating, for a profit, projects such as the few private toll roads in Southern California.

The proposal for private partnerships builds on studies such as one released last January by a blue-ribbon panel of the National Research Council. "The federal government should encourage states to experiment with arrangements for tolling and private-sector participation in road development," the report said

This is one area that could require a change in law because, until now, the federal government has not allowed tolls to be charged on roads built with federal highway monies.

California's legislature has put a $20 billion transportation bond package before voters in November, but even that isn't enough to meet the state's needs.

"Clearly the governor's plans intend looking" at "privately financing new lanes and roads," Daniel Sperling of the UC Davis Institute of Transportation Studies said at a conference in Washington, D.C., earlier this year. "We don't have the revenue, so we need new ways to finance new facilities."

But Martin Wachs of the Brookings Institution warned about the dangers of selling public assets. "Selling off an asset could be a popular notion, but it sows long-term problems. It's an attractive idea to avoid tax increases or user fees but my voice will take the other side," he said.

Mineta, appearing before the National Retail Federation, cited statistics that show the costs of traffic congestion. He said freight delays are estimated to cost American business $200 billion year. Motorists waste 3.7 billion hours and 2.3 billion gallons of fuel a year sitting in traffic. Airplane delays cost an additional $9.4 billion annually.

Mineta said his congestion relief program will include investigation of so-called "congestion pricing," the idea of charging motorists more to use roads at peak times; encouraging flex-time at jobs and more telecommuting; encouraging more express bus services in urban areas and expediting completion of highway projects that could help relieve the greatest congestion.

Mineta said the federal government could help accomplish this by, for instance, giving preference to highway projects that include express bus lanes or are located in areas where government and employers agree on flex-time plans.

Mineta zeroed in on the ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach, which handle more than 40 percent of the 9 million shipping containers that arrive each year in the United States. Congestion around Los Angeles has become a major economic bottleneck for the entire country. One idea he mentioned is creating truck-only toll lanes on Interstate 15 in San Bernardino County. Southern California authorities are already considering a $16.5 billion system of truck lanes.

Mineta said many of the ideas won't require approval by Congress, which last year passed a long-delayed $286.5 billion, six-year highway and public transit funding bill. President Bush signed the bill last August, after threatening to veto the larger bill that many in Congress wanted.

"We must embrace new solutions if we are to make headway," said Mineta, the former San Jose mayor and congressman who is the only Democrat in Bush's Cabinet.

Mineta, who served as mayor from 1971 to 1974, said he and other mayors across the country made mistakes in deciding where to site highways in their cities. Instead of combatting congestion and urban sprawl, he said, their decisions fed both.

"We created our own congestion. I intended to relieve congestion, but only added to congestion. Now as secretary of transportation, I am trying to undo that congestion. I have come full circle," said Mineta, who said he regrets Santa Clara County's decision four decades ago not to join BART. The idea of extending BART to San Jose is still being debated.
