Published on Saturday, February 4, 2006 by the Inter Press Service
World Social Forum
Civil Society to Remain Vigilant, Even With Progressive Gov'ts
by Humberto Márquez
 

CARACAS, Venezuela - The tension between civil society organizations and the leftist and center-left governments in power in a number of South American nations, which often depart from their common objectives, became evident at the portion of the 6th World Social Form held in the Venezuelan capital.

"It is easier to confront conservative governments than to support progressive ones," admitted Lori Wallach, an activist with the U.S. organisation Public Citizen. "That is the challenge now."

Rejection of free trade agreements with the industrialized North and transgenic crops, as well as the need for stepped-up efforts to achieve gender equality and universal access to education and heath care, were once again key issues discussed at the WSF, which this year is taking part in three phases: Jan. 19-23 in Bamako, Mali; Jan. 24-29 in Caracas; and in late March in Karachi, Pakistan.

The failure of neoliberal, free-market policies to come up with solutions to the problems facing the poor majorities in Latin America and the Caribbean, and in developing countries at large, was once again underscored by the social and grassroots organizations that met last week in Caracas.

The participating civil society organizations continued to build a platform for future action, starting with indigenous people, who are planning their third continent-wide conference for October in Guatemala.

In that meeting, "we will reconfirm our rejection of free trade treaties (with the United States and the European Union) in defense of our territories and our water," indigenous organizations stated in a declaration that also underlines the objectives of "food security for our countries, and comprehensive land reform."

"We are in favor of the recovery and defense of native seeds, and categorically reject the sale and use of seeds produced with 'terminator' technology (which produces sterile seeds), because it is immoral and undermines food security for humanity, while denying seeds to 1.4 billion small farmers around the world," said the indigenous groups.

Vía Campesina, a global movement, and the Confederation of Latin American Rural Organizations also called for "global uprisings" against these policies on Apr. 17, International Day of Peasant Struggles, and Oct. 16, World Food Sovereignty Day.

On the gender equality front, participating organizations called for efforts to fight "the marketing and exploitation of women, and sexist, racist and homophobic violence in all of its expressions," and to promote the South-South LGBT (lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender) dialogue.

Because of the dramatic health situation in sub-Saharan Africa, the region that is most heavily affected by HIV and AIDS, the Forum also agreed to make the right to health care one of the central themes of the 7th WSF to be held next year in Nairobi, Kenya.

For its part, Bogota will play host to a humanitarian camp aimed at promoting the universal right to health, from Apr. 4 to 7. Numerous organizations also agreed to establish an international peoples tribunal for the right to health during the same dates, in which they will emphasize that the inequalities provoked by neoliberal policies have given rise to a veritable genocide.

Meanwhile, the 4th World Education Forum, held during the 6th WSF itself, called for the defense of free, public, secular, mandatory and quality education that promotes a culture of peace.

The participants in this parallel forum noted that Latin America is witnessing the emergence of governments that have different characteristics but are similar in their attempt to break away from subordination to the neoliberal policies introduced in the 1990s.

The third week of November, or International Education Week, will be used as a framework to fight for new laws and policies in this sector.

Among the specific goals to be promoted are the guidelines set by the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO), which calls for governments to devote at least six percent of gross domestic product (GDP) to education, as well as initiatives to swap foreign debt for investment in education and programs geared to recognizing and valuing diversity in the educational sphere.

The fact that the 6th Forum was held in Venezuela, a country with a left-leaning government whose president says his goal is "21st century socialism", served to heighten the debate on whether this yearly gathering should be maintained as a space for meeting, reflection and exchange, or whether it is time to move on to establishing mechanisms for concrete action. This is a debate that will continue throughout future editions of the WSF.

Likewise, due to the leading role played by Venezuela in regional integration efforts, many of the discussions at the 6th WSF placed particular emphasis on the integration process underway in the developing South.

During one session, Chilean activist Sandra Larrain warned of the threat posed by some of these integration initiatives, because the agreements being negotiated in certain sectors will violate the constitutional framework of individual countries, and there are contradictions between the needs of the state and those of their citizens.

Larrain noted that even projects with a progressive façade, like the integration of South American oil and gas resources, serve to "guarantee the security of investments and market monopolies without state control, while consumers assume the costs."

A great deal of discussion also focussed on the concept of democracy. Participants spoke of countries that had been "liberated through elections," such as Venezuela, Brazil and Bolivia, but stressed that "it should be the people who develop their own projects."

For instance, one of the panels concluded that the Venezuelan government of Hugo Chávez "should consult the indigenous communities on gas pipeline projects," following a presentation on the proposed pipelines stretching from Venezuela to the Pacific coast of Colombia and to the Rio de la Plata between Argentina and Uruguay, and the environmental impact they will have on the thousands of kilometers along their path.

During the Forum itself, hundreds of indigenous representatives from northwestern Venezuela staged a march to protest the coal mining concessions on their ancestral lands granted to transnational corporations from North America, Europe and Brazil.

Delegates from the Confederation of Indigenous Nationalities of Ecuador (CONAIE) maintained that "we have fought in all the campaigns for independence, but when the time comes to design the new architecture of states, we are left out."

The leftward shift seen in many new governments in the region, particularly in South America, was also the subject of considerable discussion.
