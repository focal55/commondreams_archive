Published on Friday, January 6, 2006 by Reuters
Mourning Turns to Anger in Iraqi Shi'ite City
by Sami Jumaili
 

Umm Ali held her hands to her face and burst into tears, praying to God to punish those who killed her eldest son and at least 52 others in Thursday's suicide bombing in the southern Iraqi city of Kerbala.


Holding a poster of Iraq's most prominent cleric, Grand Ayatollah Ali al-Sistani, angry Shiites take to the streets in a march denouncing the recent attacks against Shiites after Friday prayers, Friday, Jan. 6, 2006, in the Sadr City area of Baghdad, Iraq. Thousands of Shiites in Baghdad's Sadr City slum rallied against the recent attacks that left over a hundred dead, chanting slogans against U.S. Ambassador Zalmay Khalizad and Sunni Arab leaders, especially hardliners that have been complaining of widespread fraud during the Dec. 15 elections. (AP Photo/Khalid Mohammed)
"My son was our only source of income," said the 48-year-old woman, dressed in black like hundreds of people in this holy Shi'ite city who are mourning their beloved ones.

"Now we have lost him my family will suffer," she said, explaining how her 21-year-old son Ali was working as a peddler in a market next to the golden-domed Imam Hussein shrine when the bomber struck.

"We have nobody to support us," she said as worshippers and pilgrims began arriving at the shrine for Friday prayers and the burial of the victims. "Do they call killing poor people resistance?"

Black banners condemning the bombing hung from buildings around the shrine as shopkeepers continued to sift through the debris at the bomb site, inspecting the remains of their burnt-out shops and stalls.

No one has claimed responsibility for the attack but no one in Kerbala doubted it was carried out by Sunni militants who have declared a holy war on Iraq's Shi'ite majority.

"They want to harm us because we are Shi'ites. They hate us," said Takeya Mohammed as she sat in a nearby medical center tending her son and husband, who were among the 148 injured.

Some mourners said the assault was clearly a reprisal against Shi'ites for their victory in last month's election, which some Sunni Arabs and secular politicians say was fraudulent and is being investigated.

ATTACK CONDEMNED

Senior Shi'ite clerics condemned the attack but, as they have after so many previous atrocities, urged their followers not to retaliate.

But for some Shi'ites, like Kerbala resident Mohammad Abbas Haidar, these calls for restraint were as exasperating as the bombings themselves. The time has come for Shi'ites to defend themselves, he said.

"This is too much," he said. "I wish they would give me the green light to fight these people and teach them a lesson."

Shopkeeper Jabbar Nasr agreed.

"Sunnis were behind this criminal act. I call on our religious Shi'ite institutions to give us permission to fight back," he said angrily. "How long should we remain silent?"

Shi'ite political leaders were outraged by the bombings, one of several which have killed around 180 people in Iraq over the past three days.

On Thursday, one senior Shi'ite official described the attacks as "an open war" against his sect.

Residents in Kerbala said they believed the bombers were trying to pressure the next government, which will be dominated by Islamist Shi'ites, to give in to Sunni campaigning for senior cabinet positions.

"What happened here was a warning to Shi'ite parties and the government that all Shi'ite provinces will face the same if they do not give in to Sunni demands," said Kasem Zain, 27.

Ali Mohammed Kadhem, a 40-year-old trader, also said the bombing was the fault of Sunni political parties.

"They always claim that they are sidelined from the political process and this is the result of their complaints."
