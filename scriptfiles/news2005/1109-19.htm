Day of Action: November 12, 2005
Tell Ford to Get Serious About Breaking Its Oil Addiction
 

WASHINGTON - November 9 - The stakes couldn't be higher--with even more deaths in Iraq, increased aggression in Nigeria, record gas prices, the devastation of Hurricane Katrina, and the Arctic melting faster than ever before, we can't afford to wait any longer to declare our independence from oil. It's time that we let Ford, the worst polluter in the automobile industry, know they must stop driving this chaos and destruction.

The United States is addicted to oil. Although Americans constitute less than 5 percent of the world's population, we consume 25 percent of its oil. This addiction contributes to a host of problems: rainforest destruction, global warming and human rights abuses in countries such as Nigeria, Equador, Sudan and Indonesia. Our oil dependence also contributed to the war in Iraq. A war that has caused thousands of senseless deaths, further endangered our troops and weakened our nation's security all because of our dependence on foreign oil. It's time to kick the habit, stop unneccesary wars and bring our troops home.

Unfortunately, Congress has failed to take meaningful action to address our oil dependence. Washington officials have repeatedly voted down even modest measures to increase the government's requirements on fuel efficiency. Fuel efficiency loopholes for light trucks and SUVs remain in place, further enabling our gas guzzling.

In the face of this Washington gridlock, citizens have few options but to take our desire directly to the automakers. It's time to take the "No Blood for Oil" slogan straight to Detroit.

IT'S TIME TO TAKE ACTION

A movement is underway to pressure the auto industry to take responsibility for how its outdated vehicles fuel our oil addiction. Currently, grassroots action is centered on one company—Ford.

Ford likes to say it is a company of "environmental heroes," but in fact Ford is an environmental laggard. Ford has ranked dead last in fuel efficiency for the last 6 years. The company also reneged on a promise to increase the fuel efficiency of its SUV's. Ford cars and trucks account for approximately nine percent of the total annual U.S. oil consumption, and get fewer miles per gallon than they did 20 years ago. In fact, many Ford vehicles on the road today get fewer miles per gallon than did the Model-T 80 years ago!

Ford has the technology and know-how to increase fuel economy to 50 mpg by 2010 and to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to zero by 2020. But the company lacks the will to innovate. As a symbol of American entrepreneurship, Ford should commit to doing everything it can to break our addiction to oil.
