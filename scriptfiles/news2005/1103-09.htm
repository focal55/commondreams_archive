Senate Fails to Reform Inequitable Agricultural Subsidies
 
WASHINGTON - November 3 - The Senate once again missed an important opportunity to reform the agricultural subsidy program that is rife with fraud and abuse by voting down the Grassley-Dorgan amendment to the budget reconciliation bill, said international agency Oxfam America today.

Senator Grassley and Senator Dorgan introduced an amendment to the budget reconciliation bill on the Senate floor today to cap farm payments at $250,000 and eliminate the loopholes that have allowed mega farms to collect subsidies that exceed $1 million. In the budget reconciliation effort, both the House and the Senate were tasked to find $3 billion in savings from the agriculture budget, mainly from three programs: anti-hunger, conservation and commodities. Last week, the Senate voted to slash funding for conservation programs to find a large part of the necessary funding. The amendment would have provided enough savings to alleviate a large portion of the cuts made to conservation programs, while protecting important nutrition programs from the chopping block.

"We are disappointed that the Senate missed an opportunity to get on the path to a more equitable and sustainable farm program," said Charly Moore, legislative director for Oxfam America. "The payment limits amendment offered hope for revitalizing small farms here and in developing countries, but vested interests convinced enough Senators to vote it down."

Current U.S. subsidies are calculated based on acreage and the quantity of volume produced, so the largest industrial-sized farms receive the most subsidies and gain the most advantage from loopholes in the law. This built-in incentive to produce and expand creates surpluses which cause prices to fall, putting smaller farms at a disadvantage. While large farmers benefit richly, small family farmers -- here and abroad -- struggle. In fact, according to the USDA, over half of all farmers and ranchers in America do not collect government subsidy payments. And of those who do, a very small percentage of producers (8 percent) receive a wide majority of the payments (78 percent).

"Congress should fund a sustainable farm program, not one that hurts family farms by encouraging consolidation and over production," continued Moore. "Instead, today's move demonstrated the power that a small number of farming operations have in maintaining an antiquated farm program."

Limiting agricultural payments could have also encouraged international trade negotiators at the World Trade Organization (WTO) in preparations for the Hong Kong ministerial in December. Negotiations have been stalled on agriculture, with the issue of agricultural subsidies taking center court.

"President Bush has stated his administration's commitment to reform trade distorting subsidies, but Congress isn't listening," said Moore. "This vote indicates to US trade negotiations what Congress thinks about real subsidy reform -- not much."
