New Allegations of Ethics Violations Against DeLay Continue to Pile Up
 

WASHINGTON -- March 10 -- For the second time in recent weeks, House Majority Leader Tom DeLay (R-TX) is facing serious new allegations that he violated House ethics rules.

Thursday's Washington Post raises questions about a foreign junket taken by DeLay, his wife and other GOP House members that was reportedly paid for by a registered foreign agent. House rules prohibit a "foreign principal" or registered lobbyist from paying the travel expenses for Members or staff.

The tab for the 2001 trip to South Korea was paid for by the Korea-U.S. Exchange Council, "a business-financed entity created with help from a lobbying firm headed by DeLay's former chief of staff," according to the Post.

In an especially telling remark in the story, Jan Baran, a respected GOP lawyer and former general counsel to the Republican National Committee, called Delay's trip "a problem," and said the questions raised about it should trigger an investigation by the Ethics Committee.

Meanwhile, the New York Times reported Wednesday that "DeLay was more actively involved than previously known in gathering corporate donations for a political committee that is the focus of a grand-jury investigation in Texas, his home state."

Last month it was reported that DeLay took a golf trip in 2000 to Scotland paid for by lobbyist Jack Abramoff, who is embroiled in an investigation that he allegedly bilked more than $80 million from six Indian tribe clients in public relations and lobbying fees.

DeLay is fast accumulating a laundry list of allegations that demand the attention of the Ethics Committee, which is already well acquainted with him. Last year, the panel admonished him three times for his conduct.

Common Cause will continue to urge for an investigation and sound the call for DeLay to step down as majority leader. He is ethically unfit to lead Congress.
