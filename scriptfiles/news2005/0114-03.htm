Coalition Says Keep Promise to Restore Everglades: Preserve Land and Water
 

WASHINGTON -- January 14 -- At its 20th annual conference in Naples, Florida, the 45-member Everglades Coalition today urged state and federal officials to preserve more land and secure enough water to save the Everglades. Presently, water for the natural system does not have legal protection. The coalition also said that land acquisition must be accelerated, growth that threatens the Everglades managed better, and water quality improved

"In this, its 20th year, the Coalition focuses its attention where the rubber meets the road for the Everglades: protecting enough land and water to restore this ecosystem," said Richard Grosso, State Co-Chair of the Everglades Coalition and Executive Director of the Environmental and Land Use Law Center. "It's now or never, and never is simply not an option for the Everglades Coalition."

In 2005 the Everglades Coalition calls for:

  • Expedited purchasing of lands for restoration, particularly natural areas;
  • Enforcement of Florida's growth management laws when Everglades restoration is threatened by development, including the relocation of the Scripps Research Institute to a site that does not interfere with restoration, and analysis of the EAA for its role in restoring the Everglades;
  • Congress to maintain implementation momentum by authorizing in 2005, two priority restoration projects: Picayune Strand (Southern Golden Gate Estates) and Indian River Lagoon; and
  • The Administration to request and urge Congress to appropriate adequate resources for carrying out restoration, particularly for the Modified Water Deliveries Project for Everglades National Park;
  • "Initial reservations" of water for the Everglades that protect beneficial water the natural system was receiving at the time CERP was authorized by Congress in 2000;
  • The "Guidance Memoranda," governing the way restoration projects are implemented, require that all water necessary for restoration be protected from permitting or diversion from the natural system;
  • The Corps and Department of Interior to reevaluate Tamiami Trail modifications, including the environmentally preferred skyway, to adequately restore the River of Grass;
  • Improved prevention and treatment of pollution by stormwater runoff from urban areas and farms including Southwest Florida.
  • Greater efforts to eradicate and control the spread of invasive species in the Everglades;
  • An agreement on interim goals that ensures restoration.

"As restoration gets fully underway, the decisions are getting tougher and the tradeoffs starker, so the leadership of government, the resolve of the public, and the commitment of stakeholders must be stronger than they have ever been," said John Adornato, III, National Co-Chair of the Everglades Coalition and Everglades Restoration Program Manager for the National Parks Conservation Association.
