Conservationists Urge Stepped Up Effort to Dismantle Dangerous Dams
 

WASHINGTON - October 20 - With Whittendon Pond Dam threatening to collapse and flood downtown Taunton, Mass., conservationists praised stepped up action to inspect potentially unsafe dams across the rain-soaked state -- and called on officials to closely evaluate whether dismantling some of them would make more economic and environmental sense than repairing them. On Tuesday, Gov. Mitt Romney committed the state to emergency inspection of 180 dams.

"We commend Mayor Nunes and state officials for acting swiftly to protect the public during this emergency," said Elizabeth Maclin, who directs the dam removal campaign for American Rivers. "When the crisis passes, we call on those leaders to help communities consider removing dangerous old dams to permanently protect those who live downstream."

There are thousands of neglected, aging dams like Whittendon Pond Dam all across the country that are accidents waiting to happen. Removing those dams that no longer serve a purpose -- carefully, during periods of low water -- is often a cost-effective and permanent solution to the problem. Removing dams also leads to cleaner water, better habitat, healthier fish and wildlife populations, and new opportunities for the community to enjoy the river. For these reasons, the pace of dam removal is accelerating. Almost 200 dams have been deliberately demolished since 1999 and 50 more are slated for removal in 2005.

Massachusetts has a modest dam removal program and has worked with local communities to remove several unwanted dams in recent years. In 2003, Silk Mill Dam was removed from Yokum Brook outside of Becket. In 2002, Billington Street Dam was removed from Town Brook near Plymouth. In 2000, Old Berkshire Mill Dam was removed from the Housatonic River near the down of Dalton.

Massachusetts still has a long list of likely candidates for removal. According to the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' National Inventory of Dams, there are more than 3,000 dams in the state of Massachusetts. More than 330 of those are classified high hazard - meaning their collapse would threaten life and property -- while 731 threaten at least property.

The state classified Whittendon Pond Dam as high hazard and in "fair" condition in its most recent inspection. Although the state of Massachusetts lists only 40 dams as being in poor condition, there are many more such as the Whittenton Pond Dam that are listed in fair condition but are nonetheless vulnerable to large storms and heavy rains.

"Massachusetts needs to act quickly to fix the dams worth keeping and dismantle those that aren't," Maclin said. "The state should start with those that are at greatest risk of collapse but shouldn't stop there."

American Rivers urged Massachusetts elected officials to examine the dam safety and removal programs in Pennsylvania and Wisconsin. These two states have developed regulatory and assistance programs that help communities evaluate and remove dams for both public safety and environmental reasons. More than 52 dams have been removed in Pennsylvania since 1999. Wisconsin communities have removed 39 dams since 1999.
