Income and Poverty Affect College Completion
 

WASHINGTON - October 12 - Even more than test scores and academic achievement, money often is the main factor in whether or not students achieve a college degree.

In this week's Snapshot, Economic Policy Institute economist Joydeep Roy explores great disparities in college completion based on socioeconomic status. Roy analyzed a Department of Education study that followed students for 12 years starting from the 8th grade and ranked students according to their 8th grade math scores. He finds that poverty and low-income status hinders college completion, even for the highest achievers. For example, the bachelor's degree completion rate for high-aptitude, lower-income students was just 29 percent, while higher-income students with the lowest aptitude still managed a 30 percent rate.
