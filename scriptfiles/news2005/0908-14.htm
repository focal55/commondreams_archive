Wells Fargo Charges Minority Customers More;
Findings Announced as ACORN Continues Campaign to Win Justice for Predatory Lending Victims
 
WASHINGTON - September 8 - ACORN, the nationwide community group, released a report today showing serious racial inequalities at Wells Fargo, the second largest mortgage lender in the country. The report found that Wells Fargo charged African-Americans and Latinos higher interest rates than whites.

Nationally, African-Americans who got a loan from Wells Fargo Bank or Wells Fargo Home Mortgage were four times more likely than whites to receive a high rate subprime loan, and Latinos were almost twice as likely as whites to get a high cost loan.

The report, "A Case of Black and White," examined the 2004 Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) for Wells Fargo in all fifty states, as well as for fifty metropolitan areas. Among the findings are that:

-- In Washington, DC, African-Americans were thirty times more likely than whites to receive a subprime loan. One out of every eight mortgages made to African-Americans was a high rate loan, compared to just one out of every 227 mortgages made to whites.

-- In the Los Angeles metropolitan area, Latinos were six times more likely than whites to receive a high cost subprime loan. One out of every eleven loans to Latinos was a subprime loan, compared to just one out of every sixty-one loans made to whites.

"This backs up what we have been saying all along," said ACORN's National President Maude Hurd. "Wells Fargo has been preying on communities of color and charging us higher rates and fees."

In 2003, ACORN launched a national campaign to shed light on Wells Fargo's abusive lending, to force them to change their predatory practices, and to win some compensation for the borrowers they had harmed.

Although Wells Fargo has denied they engage in predatory lending, they have gradually been making the changes that ACORN demanded, including a set of changes the lender announced just last week.

"ACORN members around the country who have been fighting Wells Fargo's predatory lending practices are glad that the company has finally acknowledged the damage their practices have caused and has agreed to change them," Hurd stated in response to the lender's announcement. "However, Wells still needs to compensate the families and communities its predatory lending has hurt. These business practice changes must also be made permanent and enforceable by Court order. Otherwise, Wells could revert to its old ways of doing business."

ACORN's report on the lending by Wells Fargo Bank and Wells Fargo Home Mortgage is the first public analysis of a specific lender's performance in individual states and metropolitan areas using the new 2004 Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) data. For the first time lenders had to report the Annual Percentage Rates (APRs) charged on their high rate mortgages. For last year on average, first mortgages with Annual Percentage Rates (APRs) above 8 percent and second mortgages with APRs above 10 percent were considered high rate.
