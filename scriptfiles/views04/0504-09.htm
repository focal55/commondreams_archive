The Pentagon's Feith-based Program: 'Leave no Dot Unconnected'
by Rick Mercier
 

SOMETIMES YOU just gotta have Feith.

Doug Feith, that is. He's the undersecretary of defense for policy and was long a true believer in invading Iraq.

When none of the United States' intelligence agencies could find a significant link between Saddam and al-Qaida, Feith created a unit in the Pentagon, named the Counter Terrorism Evaluation Group, that helped manufacture one.

How'd he do this? He and his cronies--who had no meaningful intelligence experience--took it upon themselves to draw conclusions from raw reports that real intelligence analysts found untenable at best, and downright kooky at worst.

As one CIA official told The New York Times last week, Feith operated according to the principle, "Leave no dot unconnected."

The problem with this principle, as another agency official told The Times, is that "if you work hard enough in this nasty world, you can link just about anybody to anybody else."

Especially if you have Feith. See, all you have to do, brothers and sisters, is believe-- do you hear me, beliEEEEEve!--and you, too, can produce Feith-based intelligence.

With the zeal of a convert, I have Feithfully compiled my own intelligence report on what I've concluded is a major threat to world peace.

After exhaustive research, I have discovered that a certain country's government may have helped install Saddam's Baathist party by assisting a 1968 coup.

I also have found that this nation cultivated significant military, intelligence, and economic ties with Saddam in the 1980s; its current defense secretary traveled to Baghdad to establish closer relations with Saddam, even though this government knew the dictator was gassing Iranians on the battlefield.

In fact, The New York Times has reported that this government provided crucial battle-planning assistance to Saddam so he could better fight the Iranians--and possibly use his chemical weapons more effectively against them.

Moreover, according to the London Independent, researchers have concluded that some of the chemicals that Saddam used to make his deadly poisons were supplied by companies based in the nation in question.

Although this country's government expressed a bit of squeamishness about Saddam's use of chemical weapons, it nevertheless refused to sever ties with the dictator. Indeed, even after Saddam used poison gas to kill thousands of Kurdish villagers, this government continued to back him (though it did condemn the attack).

In addition, it has been well established that this country supplied Saddam with precursors for his biological weapons programs in the 1980s. According to author William Blum, these exports continued until at least November 1989, despite the fact that everyone by then knew about Saddam's atrocities involving WMD.

As for Osama bin Laden, well, he was partly a creation of this nation's intelligence agency, which helped turn him into an up-and-comer in the radical Muslim world. This country's government abetted bin Laden in his recruitment of mujaheddin fighters for the guerrilla war against the Soviets in Afghanistan, apparently without giving much thought to the possible long-term repercussions.

That was an unfortunate oversight, because the multinational army of Islamic extremists that bin Laden created would later form part of the backbone of al-Qaida.

The government in question has had links to other terrorists besides bin Laden and Saddam. It has sold arms to the regime in Iran--a noted sponsor of terrorism and one of the two surviving members of the Axis of Evil--then used profits from those sales to fund terrorist activities in Nicaragua.

Some of the people involved in that scandal still serve in this country's government. In fact, the country's leader has recently nominated a key figure in the scandal to become ambassador to Iraq.

If you connect all of these dots, you cannot help but be profoundly disturbed. But, if you follow Feith-based reasoning to its logical end, there's even more reason for us to be shaking in our shoes.

Feith tells the Times: "The danger of Saddam's providing WMD to al-Qaida or another terrorist group--there you had a real problem, because his record on WMD was indisputable."

That indeed would have been a real problem--if there had been a real possibility of it happening.

But if it is sensible to fear that a state with a record of using WMD might provide those types of weapons to terrorists with which it has had contact over the years, then the government I have been describing must be viewed with grave concern. After all, it has killed a couple hundred thousand people with WMD, and it has a documented history of helping lunatics develop their own WMD programs.

So, glorious defenders of the Feith, it's clear what we must do: We must stop this horrifying threat to global security, whose name is the United States of America.

Whew, it's amazing what conclusions you can reach when you're guided by Feith.
