Landmine Survivors Go for Gold in Athens!
 

WASHINGTON - September 21 - Landmine survivors from Afghanistan, Bosnia and Herzegovina and Lebanon will be amongst the athletes from around the world competing in the Athens Paralympics 17-28 September 2004.

The needs and rights of landmine survivors will be amongst the issues highlighted at the Nairobi Summit for a Mine-Free World, 29 November - 3 December. There, State Parties to the 1997 Mine Ban Treaty will assess their progress in provision of assistance to survivors and devise ambitious plans for more comprehensive and sustained assistance for all, including access to emergency medical care, rehabilitation and income generation opportunities. Also to be discussed is the question of how to embed participation of landmine survivors in the work of the Treaty.

Afghanistan is sending two disabled athletes to compete in the 2004 Paralympic Games. Both belong to AABRAR (Afghan Amputee Bicyclists for Rehabilitation and Recreation), a member of the Afghan Campaign to Ban Landmines. Qaher Gulam Hazarat, 22 years old, will compete in the 50 km cycling event (LC3). Qaher, a double amputee, was injured in a land mine incident in 1996 when he was 14 years old. In 1997 Qaher saw a boy riding a bicycle with one leg, asked him where he learned to do this and the boy led him to AABRAR. Qaher participated in their Bicycle Rehabilitation Program in Jalalabad and has worked as a messenger for AABRAR's Disabled Cyclist Messenger Service since 2001, where he is still currently employed. Qaher is the major provider for his family of 11, including his parents and 8 other siblings.

Bosnia-Herzegovina (BiH) will send a total of eight athletes to the Athens Paralympics who are landmine survivors. They will compete in athletics, sitting volleyball and swimming. Mekic Halid, 46, from Foca, will compete in athletics. He participated in the shot putt event at the European Championships in Essen in 2001 in the F44 category where he took 6th place and in the World Championships in Lille in 2002. Sarajevo native Almedin Osmanovic, 36, started track and field events in 1993 beginning with the 100 and 200 meters sprint events. Almedin was the state champion several times, as well as the state record holder. He participated in the Atlanta Games in 1996, taking 14th place in the 100 m and 7th place in the final of the 200m race. He participated in Sydney in 2000 and since 2003 he has been the athletics team coach.

The Sarajevo sitting volleyball club SPID, is currently the championship title holder in BiH and Europe. The team includes the captain Sabahudin Delalic, 32 years old, Fikret Causevic, 31, their highest scoring member, Ismet Godinjak, 31, who has participated in two World and four European Championships and won bronze in Tallinn in 1997, Sarajevo in 1999, Hungary 2001 and in Finland 2003 achieved a gold medal. At the Sydney games in 2000 he won a silver medal, as well as a gold in the World Championship in Cairo in 2002. Other players are Hamzic Dzevad, 36, and Zikret Mahmic, 33, who won the title of best-disabled athlete in Bosnia-Herzegovina in 2000. Swimmer Admir Ahmethodzic, 22, was wounded during the war in BiH and evacuated to Italy. With the help of the Bologna swimming club in Italy, he began swimming in 1997. He has been a member of the National Paralympic Committee of the BiH swimming team since 2000. He participated at the Mediterranean Games in Tunis in 2001 and in the final came 8th in Class in the higher S 10 category, although his usual category is S 9. He also participated in championships in Croatia in 2001, in Spain, Austria and Belgium in 2002, and in Italy in 2003. His disciplines are the 100 and 400m Freestyles, 100 m backstroke, 100 m butterfly and 200m mixed.

Lebanon's Ali Srour, 21, will compete in athletics, running the 100m and 200m. Ali is from a small village in South Lebanon, Ayta al Chaab, and is the seventh child of a family of 10. After his injury in 2001, when he lost his left leg below the knee, he insisted on continuing his education, and went on to university studies. Ali has been involved in a preparatory program for Athens, including raising awareness of landmine issues in his home country. "When running, I forget everything. I have just one target in my mind: I need to succeed. Meanwhile I know that initially my success is in my participation rather than anything else. I really feel I did something different but similar in fact to all those other "normal" people who worked hard to participate in these international sport games".
