Civil Society Groups Decry Failure of G7 to Act on Debt at Conclusion of IMF/World Bank Meetings
Joint Statement from Civil Society Outlines Shared Vision for Poor Country Debt Cancellation
 
WASHINGTON - October 5 - Following the conclusion of the Annual Meetings of the IMF and the World Bank this weekend, debt campaigners from 7 nations and several global civil society coalitions today indicated their profound disappointment that a bold step was not taken to cancel the world poorest’s debt at these meetings.

The groups released the following joint statement today and called on G-7 leaders to act without delay fully cancel the debt of the most impoverished nations:

“As representatives of Civil Society Organizations present at the October 2004 Annual Meetings of the International Monetary Fund and World Bank we are agreed that:

  • We support immediate 100 per cent cancellation of all multilateral debt for the most impoverished countries, without harmful conditions imposed by creditors, as a critical step towards meeting human development goals set by the international community.
  • The responsible sale of IMF gold in a manner that will not affect the world price of gold is a viable and attractive option to finance this debt cancellation.
  • Debt must be cancelled in order to release resources to meet the development needs and must result in additional and sustained net financial flows to the most impoverished countries.

    We note with concern that Iraq's debt is being given special treatment; all decisions on debt cancellation must be taken through an equitable and transparent process.

    Signatories to the statement include ActionAid International UK, AFRODAD (Zimbabwe/Pan-Africa), Center of Concern (US), Christian Aid (UK), Debt and Development (Ireland), Halifax Initiative (Canada), Japan Network on Debt and Poverty (Japan), Jubilee USA Network, Jubilee Debt Campaign (UK), Jubilee Research (UK), Oxfam International, Plate-forme Dette et Développement (France), and the World Development Movement (UK).
