Massive Funding Shortfalls Slow Toxic Waste Cleanup
 

WASHINGTON - January 8 - The Environmental Protection Agency's Inspector General (IG) announced today that the Superfund toxic waste cleanup program faced a $174.9 million funding shortfall in fiscal year 2003, slowing toxic cleanup projects at at least 29 Superfund sites in 17 states.

For the second year in a row, the IG has reported dramatic funding shortfalls for the federal toxic waste cleanup program. Cleanups at America's 1238 Superfund sites have slowed by 50% under the Bush Administration, with only 40 sites cleaned up in fiscal year 2003, compared to an average 87 sites per year in the late 1990's.

"The dramatic slowdown in the pace of Superfund cleanups puts millions of Americans at risk of chemical exposure and disease," said U.S. PIRG Environmental Health Advocate Julie Wolk. "This report shows that, once again, the Bush Administration is failing to protect public health and the environment."

In addition, the Superfund trust fund, which used to pay for the bulk of orphan Superfund cleanups, is virtually bankrupt. While the Bush Administration touts the 70% of Superfund cleanups paid for directly by responsible polluters, 30% of sites do not have a responsible party from whom to recoup cleanup costs. Cleanups at these "orphan sites" had historically been funded by the Superfund trust fund, filled with money from the polluter pays fees. Taxpayers, who paid only 18% the cost of "orphan site" cleanups in 1995, will pay to up to 100% in FY2004.

"Without funding from the trust fund, Superfund competes with every other government program for funding," said Wolk. "Bush Administration should reinstate Superfund's polluter pays fees and make polluters, not regular taxpayers, pay for toxic cleanups," she concluded.
