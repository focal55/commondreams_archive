US “Nethercutt Amendment” Threatens Overseas Aid to Allies that have Joined the ICC
Experts Available for Comment on Appropriations Bill Due to Pass on Wednesday
 

WASHINGTON -- December 8 -- WHAT: The “Nethercutt Amendment”, which is part of the current omnibus Appropriations Bill due to receive final Congressional approval by tomorrow, will cut Economic Support Fund (ESF) aid to countries that have ratified the International Criminal Court (ICC) treaty but have not signed a bilateral ICC immunity agreement (BIA) with the United States. Unlike the funds slashed under Congress’ US Servicemembers’ Protection Act of 2002 which cut millions of dollars in military assistance to ICC states who failed to sign a BIA, the $2.5 billion dollar ESF budget includes funding for a wide range of governance programs. These include international counter-terrorism efforts, peace process programs, anti-drug trafficking initiatives, truth and reconciliation commissions, wheelchair distribution and HIV/Aids education. Countries threatened under this amendment include Jordan, Ireland, Cyprus, Peru, Ecuador, Venezuela and South Africa, among others. Legal experts from around the world have condemned the BIAs as illegal because they are contrary to international law and the ICC treaty.

WHEN/WHO: Originally introduced as an amendment to the House version of the foreign aid spending bill on July 15 by US Representative George Nethercutt (R-Washington State who has since lost his re-election race on November 2), the amendment failed in voice vote but later passed in a roll call of 241 to 166. The omnibus bill, scheduled for a Congressional vote prior to Thanksgiving holidays, was delayed until December 8 (Wednesday). It is expected that the Appropriations Bill will be passed in Congress by tomorrow and then sent to President Bush for his signature.

HOW: If passed into law, the Nethercutt Amendment will threaten countries who are “party to the International Criminal Court” that have not “entered into an agreement with the United States …preventing the International Criminal Court from proceeding against United Sates personnel present in such a country”. While many of the US allies who may have funding cut under this amendment have standing Status of Forces Agreements that already allow for full US jurisdiction over US personnel and officials operating in a given country, the Nethercutt Amendment requires ICC immunity for all US nationals as well non-national contractors.
