Bush Cuts Funding and Staffing of
Intelligence Unit Charged With
Hunting Down Bin Laden
 
WASHINGTON, D.C. – Despite a pledge to make the hunt for al Qaeda the number one objective of his administration, the President has dangerously underfunded and understaffed the intelligence unit charged with tracking down Osama bin Laden, al Qaeda's leader, a new report says.

In the months after the 9/11 attacks, President Bush promised America he would "[We] do everything we can to chase [al Qaeda] down and bring them to justice," Bush said. "That's a key priority, obviously, for me and my administration." But The New York Times reports that "Three years after the Sept. 11 attacks on New York and the Pentagon, the Central Intelligence Agency has fewer experienced case officers assigned to its headquarters unit dealing with Osama bin Laden than it did at the time of the attacks." The bin Laden unit is "stretched so thin that it relies on inexperienced officers rotated in and out every 60 to 90 days, and they leave before they know enough to be able to perform any meaningful work."

“This isn’t just another broken promise to the American people by President Bush, but a poor decision that makes us all less safe,” said Peter Schurman, spokesperson for MoveOn.Org. “Bush is bungling the hunt for Bin Laden.”

The revelation comes months after the Associated Press reported the Bush Treasury Department "has assigned five times as many agents to investigate Cuban embargo violations as it has to track Osama bin Laden's financial infrastructure. It also comes after USA Today reported that the President shifted "resources from the bin Laden hunt to the war in Iraq" in 2002.

Specifically, Bush moved special forces tracking al Qaeda out of Afghanistan and into Iraq war preparations. He also and left the CIA "stretched badly in its capacity to collect, translate and analyze information coming from Afghanistan." This has allowed the terrorists to regroup: according to the senior intelligence officials in July of this year, bin Laden and other top al Qaeda leaders are now directing a plot "to carry out a large-scale terror attack against the United States" and are overseeing the plan "from their remote hideouts somewhere along the Afghanistan-Pakistan border
