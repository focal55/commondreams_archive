Clock Ticks on Assault Gun Ban: Only 5 Legislative Days Remain for Bush to Keep His Word
President Promised Compassion in Nomination Speech, Yet Has Done Nothing to Save, Strengthen Assault Weapons Ban

 

WASHINGTON - September 7 - President Bush and Congress have just five legislative days to act on the federal assault weapons ban. If President Bush does not work to save the landmark law this week, the assault weapons ban will surely expire on Monday, September 13, 2004.

In an August 4 interview on National Public Radio, U.S. House Speaker J. Dennis Hastert (R-Ill.) said the assault weapons ban "might have a chance" of passage.

But an aide to the speaker has said previously that it would only pass the House if Bush pushes for it.

The U.S. Senate voted to renew the ban on a bipartisan 52-47 vote on March 2, 2004. "It is well past time for the President, who leads the majority party in Congress, to back up his words with action," said Josh Horwitz, Executive Director of the Coalition to Stop Gun Violence.

"George W. Bush needs to stop playing politics with public safety and the lives of law enforcement."

Promises of Support; No Action

President Bush has repeatedly pledged support for banning assault weapons. During the 2000 campaign, George W. Bush said "It makes no sense for assault weapons to be around our society."

During his acceptance speech at the Republican National Convention, President Bush said that protecting the American people is his "most solemn duty," and that he is running on a "compassionate conservative philosophy."

"Mr. President, the overwhelming majority of Americans, a bipartisan majority of the United States Senate, and police officers from across the nation are calling on you to protect us by banning assault weapons," said Horwitz. "There is nothing compassionate about an AK-47."
