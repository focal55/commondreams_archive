Royal Caribbean Again Puts Entertainment Before Environment, Says Oceana; Cruise Company Announces Major Renovations to Ship; Ignores Sewage Treatment Deficiencies
 

WASHINGTON - February 5 - Responding to an announcement that Royal Caribbean Cruises (NYSE:RCL) will be completely overhauling its Nordic Empress cruise liner to provide more entertainment options for passengers, Oceana today called on the industry giant to also improve the ship's lackluster wastewater treatment performance.

Stating in a press release that renovations would "extend to every area of the ship," Royal Caribbean notably excluded any mention of improvements being made to the ship's waste treatment apparatus. Currently, the Nordic Empress employs an antiquated Marine Sanitation Device (MSD) to treat its waste. MSDs have proven ineffective, with a recent study showing that 68 of 70 MSD- treated sewage samples from large cruise ships failed to meet federal water quality standards.

"This is the second time in three months that Royal Caribbean has invested heavily in either constructing or renovating a ship. As usual, the environment wasn't even a consideration," said Dana DuBose, Ocean's Cruise Pollution campaign director. "Royal Caribbean should spend a little less time bragging about its environmental record and a little more time doing something to earn it."

Royal Caribbean has stood out in an industry of polluters, touting itself as an environmental leader despite the fact that it is currently under court-ordered probation for past environmental offenses. The company has been fined more than $30 million for illegally dumping oily bilge and other hazardous materials, as well as falsifying records to the Coast Guard. Furthermore, only three of Royal Caribbean's 27 ships are equipped with the advanced wastewater treatment equipment needed to cleanse the millions of gallons of sewage they produce and dump into the waters daily. Royal Caribbean has refused to upgrade the remainder of its fleet.

"This action speaks volumes about Royal Caribbean's priorities," said DuBose. "They clearly have the money for upgrades, but rather than directing it toward preserving the oceans that many of their passengers come to see, they simply use it to dial up the glitz."

Citizens interested in joining or learning more about Oceana's campaign to stop cruise pollution are encouraged to call Oceana headquarters toll-free at 1-877-7-OCEANA, or visit the website at www.oceana.org.

Oceana is a non-profit international advocacy organization dedicated to restoring and protecting the world's oceans through policy advocacy, science, law and public education. Founded in 2001, Oceana's constituency includes members and activists from more than 150 countries and territories who are committed to saving the world's marine environment. Headquartered in Washington, D.C., Oceana has additional offices in key U.S. coastal areas, a South American office in Santiago, Chile, and a European office in Madrid, Spain.
