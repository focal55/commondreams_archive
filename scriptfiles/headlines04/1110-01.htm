Published on Wednesday, November 10, 2004 by the Long Island, NY Newsday
Morally Speaking, Iraq Was a Bigger Issue
by Carol Eisenberg
 

Is talk of the religious right's resurgence overstated? A new poll released yesterday by Zogby International showed that 42 percent of voters saw the war in Iraq as the most pressing moral issue affecting their choice for president - almost twice as many as those citing abortion and same-sex marriage.

Progressive religious leaders, whose groups sponsored the poll, jumped on the findings as evidence that Americans' concern for "moral values" goes beyond gay marriage and abortion.

"Both Democrats and Republicans voted on values, though not necessarily the same values," said the Rev. C. Welton Gaddy of the Interfaith Alliance, a liberal group. "Not only was there a resurgence of activity among religious conservatives and fundamentalists, but a similar resurgence occurred among progressives and liberals in the Christian community."

But religious conservatives dismissed the sponsors as "sore losers trying to pooh-pooh the influence of Bible-believing evangelicals," in the words of the Rev. Jerry Falwell, founder of the now-defunct Moral Majority.

"[GOP political expert] Karl Rove called me this morning to say, 'Thank you for over 30 million evangelicals who visited the polls last Tuesday and who were a major player in what happened . . . ,'" Falwell said. "And we have already formed a new group and begun meeting with a goal of getting 40 million people to the polls in 2008."

The Zogby poll, sponsored by the liberal Center for American Progress, Res Publica and Pax Christi USA, underscored the nation's deep division on issues of sexual morality and social justice issues.

While almost a quarter of all voters said they voted solely on abortion and gay marriage - a phenomenon tracked in last week's exit polls - the majority of 10,550 randomly selected voters said they were influenced by a range of moral concerns from issues of war and peace to greed. Asked to identify the single greatest moral crisis facing America, for instance, a third of voters selected materialism and greed, followed by poverty (31 percent) and then abortion and same-sex marriage (totaling 28 percent).

Amid Catholic voters, who were decisive in Bush's win, 31 percent selected materialism and greed as their top moral concern, 31 percent poverty, and 31 percent abortion and gay marriage. Though Catholics supported Bush 52-47 percent, the poll found 25 percent turned off by conservative leaders trumpeting "non-negotiable issues" and 20 percent who said those messages made them more likely to vote Republican. Fifty-six percent said those messages had no effect on them.

"Despite attempts to characterize Catholic political identity in one or two issues, Catholics recognized that there are a broad range of issues that their faith calls them to vote on - issues like economic justice, the war in Iraq, health care and more," said David Robinson, executive director of Pax Christi.

Those who track religious voters said the poll was useful in amplifying the exit poll findings in which 22 percent of voters said they were motivated by moral values.

"This poll helps confirm that there are religious conservatives who care deeply about abortion and gay marriage who did turn out and vote in large numbers for Bush," said John Green, professor of political science at the University of Akron. "That's very real. But what it also shows is that there were a significant number of progressive or moderate Christians who consider issues like the war in Iraq and poverty the top moral issues, many of whom voted for John Kerry."

The ethics divide

Voters from all political ideologies said in a recent survey that faith and moral issues broadly in.uenced their choice for president. Where liberals, moderates and conservatives parted company, however, was in the individual moral issues each found compelling.
