Published on Wednesday, February 4, 2004 by Reuters
Canada Magazine Poll Tells Bush: Hope You Lose, Eh
 

TORONTO - A majority of Canadians hope President Bush loses the upcoming election and do not really care who beats him, according to a magazine poll that hit newsstands Wednesday.

In an article entitled "Canadians to Bush: Hope you lose, eh," Maclean's magazine said a national survey found only 15 percent of Canadians would definitely vote for Bush if given the opportunity.

Forty percent said they would definitely vote for someone else, while 28 percent said they would consider choosing someone other than Bush. Seventeen percent said they did not know.

"Despite a spate of polls showing a broad desire for improved relations with the United States after the often rocky (former Prime Minister Jean) Chretien years, there is a sense that this administration isn't one we want to do business with," the newsmagazine said.

Forty-three percent said Canada is worse off with Bush as president than during the terms of previous U.S. presidents, while 12 percent said the country is better off and 33 percent said there is no difference.

Relations between the two neighbors have been strained recently by a slew of factors including, most prominently, Canada's refusal to join the U.S.-led war on Iraq.

New Canadian Prime Minister Paul Martin has focused on mending ties between the two countries, which have the world's biggest trading relationship. But the poll suggests that while Canadians want better relations with Americans, they are less enthused about closer ties with their president.

Fifty percent of respondents said they either extremely or somewhat disliked Bush, while 32 percent said they somewhat or extremely liked him.

"Clearly, there is something about George W. Bush that gets under the skin of Canadians," Maclean's wrote. "After all, vehemently disagreeing with the policies of American presidents is almost a national pastime.

"There has to be another explanation for our extreme reaction, the desire afoot in the land to see him turfed from office. That and the unprintable sentiment about him and the horse he rode in on."

The magazine attributed that, in part, to Canadians' paranoia about being overwhelmed by their giant neighbor and discomfit with Washington's policies to protect U.S. interests abroad, with or without the support of international bodies such as the United Nations.

The survey of 1,367 Canadians was conducted between Jan. 20 and Jan. 25 and has a margin of error of plus or minus 3 percentage points, 19 times out of 20.
