Published on Saturday, February 7, 2004 by Reuters
Ex-Envoy Wilson Says He's Campaigning to Oust Bush
by Chris Stetkiewicz
 

SEATTLE - Former U.S. ambassador to Iraq Joseph Wilson said on Friday the White House's "brass knuckles" rebuke to his public criticism of the Iraq war has left him more defiant than ever and he is now actively campaigning to defeat President Bush in November's election.

By allegedly exposing his wife as an undercover CIA operative in retaliation for a newspaper article questioning the war, White House officials gave a glimpse of Washington politics at its nastiest, Wilson told Reuters.

"People who have been around Washington a lot longer than I have say it's been unprecedented ... for an administration to do what they did to my wife," Wilson said. "What they lack in finesse they make up for in brass knuckles."

A newspaper column by Robert Novak last summer quoted two senior administration officials identifying Wilson's wife, Valerie Plame, as a CIA officer.

Her undercover status blown, Plame continues to work but her status is uncertain, Wilson said in Seattle where he is campaigning for Massachusetts Sen. John Kerry, frontrunner for the Democratic presidential nomination.

"Clearly, her life has been turned upside down, because she's still in this particular business and her career changes as a consequence of this, but she continues to go to work and do the best she can," Wilson said.

The CIA sent Wilson to Niger in early 2002 to check a report that Iraq had tried to buy "yellowcake" uranium there. He found no evidence to support the report, which was later found to be based partly on forged documents.

But in his January 2003 State of the Union speech President Bush cited an Iraq-Niger connection as evidence Saddam Hussein was trying to develop nuclear weapons.

In a New York Times article last July, titled "What I didn't find in Africa," Wilson accused the White House of twisting intelligence to exaggerate Iraq's nuclear efforts.

Bush last year called Plame's unmasking a "serious matter" and urged his staff to cooperate with a Justice Department probe.

"Whoever decided that his (Bush's) political agenda was more important than the national security of our country is still in place and still has the ear of the president," Wilson said.

Wilson has been a vocal critic of the Iraq war, saying tight surveillance and intense espionage had already handcuffed Saddam, who would likely have lost power eventually without the invasion. More than 500 Americans have died in the war and occupation so far.
