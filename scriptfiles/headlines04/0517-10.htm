Published on Monday, May 17, 2004 by Barre-Montpelier Times Argus (Vermont)
Activists Gather at Goddard to Set Course for a Better World
by Sky Barsch
 

PLAINFIELD - Another world is possible, and preferable, according to social justice activists who gathered at Goddard College this weekend.

The peace-seekers, who came from Vermont and beyond, listened to 69 speakers, met in groups, and watched videos dealing with social inequities and environmental concerns at the "Another World is Possible" conference.

While the workshops sought to give examples of actions those seeking change can take, the workshops also served as an energizer for those who attended.

"It's essential to get together in these settings to celebrate the work that we're doing," said Tim Keating, executive director of Rainforest Relief, a New York-based rainforest protection and advocacy group.

Keating was a speaker on Saturday, the second day of the three-day event, which featured social justice movers and shakers including Shujaa Graham, a former death row inmate and Black Panther, Zia Mian, a South Asia and disarmament issues scholar and activist from Princeton University, and Medea Benjamin, the founding director of Global Exchange, an international human rights organization.

Keating told those who listened to his discussion, titled "Rainforest Lunch," that Americans play a large role in depleting the rainforests of tropical hardwoods that are going extinct.

Keating said it was great not just to have interest in his presentation but to have the chance to hear from other, similar-minded people. He likened to hear from other, similar-minded people. He likened working for social change to "going up the stairs in Shea Stadium when the game is getting out."

"It's really what it feels like, you're paddling up river," Keating said in an interview, after plucking dandelion greens from the Goddard lawn. "But when you get together, it feels like you have 20 people in your boat."

The speakers spoke inside the Haybarn auditorium at Goddard, which was hung with posters saying "Another World is Possible," "War is Not the Answer" and "Can Democracy Survive Militarism."

The concept for the event came from the idea of the World Social Forums, larger-scale events being held around the world, according to an organizer. Several Vermont high school students recently made a trip to the World Social Forum in India.

Agustus Young, a 28-year-old Burlington man, volunteered to organize the "Another World is Possible" event and attended the discussions. He called the mission of the conference "very ambitious" in its intent to discuss economic, environmental and social issues.

Young said Sunday that that cooperative effort was essential, a theme he took away from the event: "It is the response of individuals to manifest the changes they want to see ... and to cooperatively manifest the changes they want to see."
