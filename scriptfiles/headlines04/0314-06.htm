Published on Sunday, March 14, 2004 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Between Anti-war Hero and Military Villain
Marine Reservist Jailed for Refusing Duty is Back Home
by Joe Garofoli
 

Stephen Funk is only 21, and would like to go back to his quiet life, but he knows he's become too much of a public, polarizing figure.

That comes with being what activists say is the first public conscientious objector of the Iraq war; certainly the only one jailed for his beliefs. Released from a North Carolina military prison last month after serving six months for "unauthorized absence" from his Marine Reserve unit based in San Jose, Funk is coming home to San Francisco as a hero to anti-war activists.


Stephen Funk says he knew as soon as he showed up for basic training that he didn't belong in the Marine Reserves. Chronicle photo by Jakub Mosur
Funk will be feted at 3 p.m. today at a "welcome home" fund-raiser in Oakland to benefit the anti-war organization Not In Our Name and his legal defense. It is billed as, "local anti-war hero returns home from military brig. ''

To the Marines, Funk was court-martialed for not showing up for 47 days after his unit was called to active duty Feb. 13, 2003. They court-martialed him, sent him to the brig, then gave him a bad conduct discharge. He has lost all his military benefits.

"He was never a C.O. (conscientious objector); he just never showed up," said Marine Capt. Jeff Pool, a Marine Forces Reserve spokesman. "As far as the military goes, he just wasn't where he was supposed to be when he was supposed to be."

To Funk, the truth is somewhere in between those perceptions.

"It's been really polarizing," Funk said recently as he sat in the offices of International ANSWER, which sponsors most of the antiwar rallies in San Francisco and across the country. He is scheduled to speak at an anti-war rally Saturday in San Francisco. "People were unrealistically calling me a hero and people were unrealistically calling me a demon.

"My decision was a very personal one. I did it for myself. I didn't do it to make a point or for some other reason," he said.

He did it because he did not want to kill anyone. And he didn't want to encourage anyone else to, either.

Funk is a bridge between two worlds. One is the Marines, where only 23 reservists applied for conscientious objector status in 2003; 17 requests were granted. Only one reservist asked the year before, according to a Marine spokesman.

As Funk retraces his life, it's hard to see the evolution of a Marine recruit from his other, liberal world. Before basic training, he had never fired a gun or even gotten into a fight. Attending what he calls "a very liberal" high school in his native Seattle, he read progressive icons Noam Chomsky and Howard Zinn and participated in the World Trade Organization street protests there in 1999. When he attended the University of Southern California, he protested the Democratic National Convention in 2000 and voted for Green Party nominee Ralph Nader for president.

But USC wasn't a good fit, and after one year, he moved to the Bay Area. Here, he continued to drift, which is when he got an unsolicited call from a Marine recruiter. He took the recruiter up on an offer to attend an event at Camp Parks in Dublin. He enjoyed the camaraderie, and soon after he got home, he decided to join. He didn't tell anyone until two weeks before shipping out.

"I was lacking direction and I needed a sense of belonging, and I wanted some discipline," he said. "I thought the Marines would provide that."

But he knew, he said, "as soon I set foot" in Camp Pendleton in San Diego County for basic training that this wasn't the life for him. He couldn't punch his fellow recruits during hand-to-hand combat drills, and when he was ordered to say "Kill! Kill!" during other exercises, "I'd say 'Jill! Jill!' Or 'Phil! Phil!' " Funk said.

Before he finished his Marine training, he started trying to figure out how to get out. He began his conscientious objector paperwork and attended several anti-war rallies in San Francisco. When his Marine Reserve pals found out, they began calling him "Anti-war Funk."

He didn't show up for training because his paperwork wasn't completed. When he handed it in, he realized that in addition to walking the media gauntlet for failing to report, he would have to face publicly something that only his immediate family and a few friends knew: He is gay.

He will continue to speak out about the war and his experience, but his main goal is to get a job and reapply to school. He wants a job working with animals. Still, the polarizing aspect of his life is never far behind.

When he filed a rental application for a Sunset District home, the landlord called to say he had been rejected; he had admitted he was a felon on the application. When he explained why, "the landlord said, 'Oh, I remember you.' And then she said OK. And knocked $100 off the rent."
