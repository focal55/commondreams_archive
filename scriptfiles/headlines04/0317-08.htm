Published on Wednesday, March 17, 2004 by Reuters
Spain's Zapatero Calls Iraq Occupation a Fiasco
by Marta Ruiz-Castillo
 

MADRID - Spain's next prime minister spurned the U.S. president's appeal for unity Wednesday and branded the Iraq occupation a fiasco as a probe into the Madrid bombings that killed 201 people entered a "decisive phase."


'THE OCCUPATION IS A FIASCO'
Spain's prime minister-elect, Jose Luis Rodriguez Zapatero (R), answers journalist Luis del Olmo's questions at radio station Onda Cero in Madrid. Zapatero rejected an appeal by US President George W. Bush to keep Spanish troops in Iraq, as the US Army kicked off a new campaign to crush insurgents in Baghdad. (AFP/Pedro Armestre)
Socialist Jose Luis Rodriguez Zapatero, who ousted Prime Minister Jose Maria Aznar in a shock election victory at the weekend, stood his ground on withdrawing Spanish troops from Iraq despite a plea from President Bush.

"I will listen to Mr. Bush but my position is very clear and very firm," Zapatero told Spain's Onda Cero radio. "The occupation is a fiasco."

Bush urged Spain and other allies in Iraq Tuesday not to cave in to pressure from the Muslim militant al Qaeda group by withdrawing their troops.

Spanish police focused on a Moroccan link in their investigation into last week's bombings of four trains in Madrid -- Western Europe's worst guerrilla attack -- in which 201 people were killed and 1,750 injured.

Interior Minister Angel Acebes, a member of the outgoing government, gave few fresh details about the inquiry, citing a judge's gagging order, but said: "It is a decisive phase."

"International connections" were one area police were working on, Acebes told a news conference.

As Spain stepped up security at airports and strategic installations around the country, Zapatero pledged a sweeping security shakeup and to stamp on any violent group, including Basque separatist guerrillas ETA.

"I am planning...to finish off terrorism," he said.

But Zapatero, who takes office next month, ignored Bush's plea, reiterating his election campaign pledge to withdraw Spain's 1,300 troops from Iraq if the United Nations did not take charge there by mid-year.

The comments have raised concern among some U.S. allies who are unclear exactly what U.N. role he wants to see in Iraq.

In Madrid, thousands of Aznar supporters protested, accusing the Socialists of "exploiting" the bombings to win votes.

Aznar's Popular party drew anger ahead of the ballot for hastily blaming ETA. Then as suspicions of al Qaeda involvement grew, the party suffered from perceptions it bore responsibility for sending troops to Iraq despite huge public opposition.

MOROCCO LINK

Spanish police arrested five people after the blasts on Thursday, including three Moroccans. Police are hunting for some 20 Moroccans, who may also be linked to last May's Casablanca attack in which 45 people died, including 12 suicide bombers.

El Pais newspaper said police believed the Madrid attacks were carried out by people linked to the Moroccan Islamic Combatant Group, which it said is financed by al Qaeda.

Police sources told Reuters Morocco has given Madrid a list of suspects believed linked to the Casablanca bombings who may be in Spain. The list led to the arrests of three Casablanca suspects before the train bombings.

"We're looking for people on the list, but not just them," a police source said. "The radical Islamic world is very complex and we don't know if any of those (suspected in Casablanca) may have links to other groups."

Police were analyzing documents seized Tuesday night in the Madrid district of Lavapies, state radio reported.

It said police continued to interrogate one of those arrested, Jamal Zougam, trying to establish if he had links to Islamist groups responsible for the Casablanca attacks.

A judge Wednesday ordered an Algerian man to be held in jail for two more days to see if his threats in January to "kill a load of Madrid people" were linked to the train bombings.

The man, identified as Ali Amrous, was arrested Monday in the Basque city San Sebastian. Court sources have said it was unlikely Amrous had a role in the attack, but investigators wanted to speak to other foreign nationals who were with him in San Sebastian in January.

La Vanguardia newspaper said the Madrid bombers spent 15 to 20 days studying train timetables and the stations. It said two groups of three bombers each placed the explosives that ripped apart four commuter trains.
