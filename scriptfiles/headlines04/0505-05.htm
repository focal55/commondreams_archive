Published on Wednesday, May 5, 2004 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Don't Read This Over a Burger
by Laurel Wellman
 

These days, mad cow disease is ever with us, so I was off to lunch to meet John Stauber, who's been described by "Fast Food Nation'' author Eric Schlosser as "[one] of America's bravest, finest journalists."

And, since Stauber earned this accolade in part by co-writing "Mad Cow U. S.A.," a book that, seven years ago, warned we weren't doing nearly enough to prevent mad cow disease in this country, I was meeting him at Greens.

Stauber, founder and director of the Center for Media and Democracy in Madison, Wis., claimed I'd be able to recognize him by his Green Bay Packers regalia, but somewhat unsurprisingly this turned out not to be true. He was, instead, a slightly preppy, bespectacled, academic-looking guy, and I spotted him because he was carrying a copy of another of his books, "Weapons of Mass Deception: The Uses of Propaganda in Bush's War on Iraq"; apart from his role as a critic of the U.S. Department of Agriculture's handling of mad cow disease, Stauber is a dogged critic of the administration.


John Stauber’s “Mad Cow U.S.A.” has made a comeback.
As we sat down, I confessed I'd been living in Britain when mad cow disease -- then officially deemed not transmissible to humans -- had been found there, and had occasionally eaten such things as steak and kidney pie.

"How's your memory?" joked Stauber.

Please, please say he was joking.

We studied the menu -- which, appropriately, featured nothing higher on the food chain than a legume -- and Stauber explained he'd first become concerned about mad cow disease when, in the early 1990s, he was involved in research aimed at making a case against the use of bovine growth hormone. The hormone itself was artificially manufactured in laboratories, but a scientist told Stauber that cows injected with it were also fed protein supplements made, in part, of other cows -- something Stauber found disturbing.

"I started researching mad cow, and in 1993 our organization filed the first legal petition to try to get the U.S. to put in place the sort of animal feed regulations that were being advocated by the World Health Organization," said Stauber. "One of the ironies was that the scientists who were in the forefront of advocating a ban on feeding cattle to cattle were U.S. scientists like Dr. Joe Gibbs and Dr. [D. Carleton] Gajdusek. But here in the U.S. absolutely nothing was done until 1997."

Stauber convinced Sheldon Rampton -- with whom he'd already written "Toxic Sludge is Good For You" and "Trust Us, We're Experts!" -- that they should start work on a book about mad cow disease (more properly known as bovine spongiform encephalopathy or BSE). Here, he argued, was "a case study in how a critically important disease spread through food -- rather than being dealt with with science and with a precautionary, science-based approach to protect human health -- is being dealt with as a crisis management public relations problem."

Rampton and Stauber's "Mad Cow U.S.A" was first published in 1997 with the ominous subtitle "Could it happen here?" Now that question has been answered, and the book has been newly re-issued with a back cover line reading, "The book that predicted it."

"We wrote it very carefully because we realized that at that time nobody knew how many people were going to die of human mad cow disease," said Stauber. "No one still has the answer to that question, and we won't really have the answer maybe for decades."

It's now accepted that BSE is caused by a misfolded protein, or prion; the disease's incubation period is unpredictable. "What amazed me was there was this infectious protein that was fatal, and you could die of this 40 years after ingesting the protein," said Stauber. He ordered a green salad and black bean chili; I chose the menu's current version of griddle cakes.

"In 1996, when Britain announced people were dying, it didn't surprise me in the slightest," he added. "Why wouldn't people die of this disease?"

The outbreak in Britain meant there was plenty of interest in BSE and its human form, variant Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease. Former cattle rancher Howard Lyman wrote the well-received "Mad Cowboy," while Stauber and Rampton's book was somewhat overshadowed by the release of Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist Richard Rhodes' "Deadly Feasts: The 'Prion' Controversy and the Public's Health" -- another book you'll want to keep in mind as a fun beach read.

But public discussion of whether BSE would emerge here all but died after Oprah Winfrey invited Lyman on her show and devoted an hour to the disease. The two were sued by a group of Texas cattlemen under a state law banning the "disparagement" of food -- and while Winfrey and Lyman prevailed in the 1998 trial, the whole incident made lots of other people nervous.

"Mad Cow U.S.A." went out of print, but Stauber and Rampton decided to make it available as a free download. Then came Dec. 23, 2003, the day mad cow disease was found in the United States, and over the course of the next month or so, the book was downloaded 80,000 times.

And Stauber -- who sends out periodic mad cow e-mail updates, though he and Rampton have also just released a new book, "Banana Republicans" -- believes much more has to be done. "You may not be a vegetarian, but the animals you eat need to be," he said. "We're still not following that axiom."
