Published on Friday, March 12, 2004 by Reuters
Horror Endured by Child Soldiers Revealed in Study
by Patricia Reaney
 

LONDON - Children abducted or recruited to fight in wars suffer horrific atrocities and many are beaten and sexually abused, Belgian researchers said on Friday.

Young soldiers are forced to kill other children or even members of their own family, and girls are given to senior staff to act as wives.


Amnesty International Secretary General Irene Khan (R) looks at campaigner and former child soldier from Uganda China Keitetsi. Human rights watchdog Amnesty International said atrocities against women including rape, murder, and mutilation must be stopped. (AFP/Nicolas Asfouri)
"I think this is the first time someone has investigated their experiences," Ilse Derluyn, of Ghent University in Belgium, told Reuters.

An estimated 300,000 children, some 12 or younger, are currently serving as soldiers or guerrilla fighters in conflicts around the globe.

Derluyn and her colleagues interviewed 301 former child soldiers who had been abducted by the Lord's Resistance Army (LRA), a rebel movement in northern Uganda. Their research is published in The Lancet medical journal.

Most of the children she spoke to had been soldiers for more than two years. The abduction of children is the main method of recruitment of the LRA.

Half had been seriously beaten, nearly 40 percent had killed someone or had abducted other children, 77 percent saw someone being killed and 35 percent of the girls had been sexually abused.

Six percent of the child soldiers witnessed the murder of their own father, brother or another relative. Nearly 40 percent had killed someone and two percent were forced to kill a family member.

One former Ugandan girl soldier who had been abducted in 1996 described how she was forced to murder a boy who had tried to escape.

"He was recaptured and I had to kill him, by beating him to death with sticks," she said.

The girl admitted looting villages, abducting other children and said she gave birth to a son fathered by an army commander and later had to abandon the baby.

"I don't know what happened to him," she told Derluyn.

Most of the children suffered from post-traumatic stress, but Derluyn said they showed remarkable resilience.

"They try to pick up their lives as much as possible," she said.

In an editorial, The Lancet questioned what the international community is doing to improve the future of child soldiers and called for the United Nations to draw up an action plan.

"For the rest of the world to look the other way while each night children hide from warlords is a terrible indictment of 21st century priorities," the magazine said.
