Published on Friday, November 7, 2003 by AMERICAS.ORG
Deadly Déjà Vu - Guatemalans Watch in Horror as Ex-dictator Ríos Montt Attempts to Regain Power
by Kari Lydersen
 

Mateo Castillo Gregorio and German Hernandez Reynoso were displaced from their homes in Guatemala in the 1980s largely because of a man named Efrain Ríos Montt.

During his 18 months as dictator of Guatemala after a 1982 coup, Ríos Montt ordered the killing and torture of thousands of opposition members. He has been charged with genocide and crimes against humanity for overseeing military-backed death squads. The death squads operated with impunity during the country's decades-long, bloody civil war.

Castillo fled to Chiapas, Mexico. Hernandez was displaced within Guatemala after his village, Saq Ja, was burned to the ground by the military.

Now, the two are carrying out the slow work of rebuilding their communities and their Mayan identities, both suppressed by years of violence and fear. They are members of a group called Centro Maya de Cultura y Desarollo (Mayan Center of Culture and Development). Supported financially and logistically by Christian Guatemalan Action, the Centro carries out a host of community and social programs in Saq Ja and other local communities. These include a women's group, nutritional information programs, educational programs and health promotion programs, all with a focus on Mayan spirituality and decision-making processes.

But as they rebuild, the man who played such a large role in the destruction and displacement of more than 400 Mayan communities in Guatemala is poised to return to power.

In order to run in the November 9 elections, Montt and his supporters had to pressure Congress and the judiciary. Guatemala's constitution forbids ex-dictators from running for office, but Montt argued that this constitutional clause, active since 1985, is not retroactive and therefore cannot apply to his time in office. By all indications, Montt will be a major contender in the election this Sunday.

Ríos Montt's campaign is shadowed by a violent past and present. On July 24-25, about 5,000 Ríos Montt supporters were bused into Guatemala City, where they rioted in the streets, attacked journalists, and closed down government offices. They were demanding that the Supreme Court allow Montt to run for president despite his record.

"This is a delicate situation," understates Castillo. "One of the presidential candidates is also one of those who committed atrocities during the Civil War. It is creating a situation where we again have violence, kidnappings, threats against human rights activists. There have been attacks against women in the capital--we're finding the raped and mutilated bodies of women. There are threats against people who talk against Ríos Montt. It's a tense situation."

Over the past year, Ríos Montt has mobilized thousands of peasants and former "guards"--paramilitaries who supported his and other reigns of terror in the past--to demonstrate in his favor. These former death squad members are also demanding back pay for their time served as "security guards" in service of the government.

While it might seem strange that the very peasants who were the victims of oppression and terror under Rios Montt and other military dictatorships over the years would now support him, he has been effective in buying them off with small bribes of money, food and trinkets.

"If Ríos Montt wins, we believe the situation we had in the past will return," said Hernandez, 41, who has six kids. "We don't know what we'll do."

As in most of Latin America, globalization and free trade policies have led to an increased divided between rich and poor in Guatemala. Many see Ríos Montt capitalizing on this misery to gain support among rural and poor farmers.

"The millionaires have the power to do anything, and we're just fighting to survive," said Hernandez.

Castillo noted that Guatemala has directly suffered from NAFTA, the controversial North American Free Trade Agreement that was instituted in 1994, even though it doesn't technically include Guatemala. "There are very visible effects of globalization and neoliberalism," he said. "[Under NAFTA] the U.S. has commercially flooded Mexico with cheap products like corn. So since Mexican farmers don't have a market there anymore, they flood our country with corn and sell it here at cheaper prices [than local corn]. So for many Guatemalans it's not even worth it to plant seeds anymore."

Castillo, 40, noted that it is ironic that now the U.S. government is speaking against Ríos Montt and his cohorts, considering that during the Civil War the CIA and other government entities actively supported the military and paramilitary death squads in their fight against supposed "communism."

In fact, on December 4, 1982, President Ronald Reagan met with Ríos Montt and described him as a "man of great personal integrity and commitment" who had been "getting a bum rap." Just three days later, forensic anthropologists discovered that Ríos Montt's military had slaughtered more than 300 people in the village of Dos Erres.

"Then the U.S. supported him, but now they don't," Castillo said. "It's a matter of what's convenient for them. During Guatemala's civil war, the U.S. helped perpetuate massacres there. Now the U.S. presence is more in the economic realm. These U.S. businesses and transnational businesses are looking for ways to exploit our country's natural resources, to make the poor poorer and the rich richer. This is globalization and free trade. It has very big effects on the community."

Along with the Mayan communities, the environment has also been taking a beating from the increased industrialization of the country by foreign investors. "If we don't respect the natural resources of the planet, we'll be destroyed," Castillo said. "Industry in Guatemala destroys the environment. There are mountains of trash everywhere, there are pesticides being used here that are banned in the U.S. The campesinos use them because they don't know any better."

Castillo and Hernandez were in Chicago for three weeks in October as part of a program with the Chicago-Guatemala Return Project, an organization that is an outgrowth of the Sanctuary movement of the 1980s, when U.S. churches gave respite to people fleeing oppression and violence in Central America. The goal of the Return Project is to rebuild communities including Saq Ja which were destroyed during the war.

Hernandez and Castillo hope that by spreading their message in Chicago, U.S. residents will become more aware of the situation in Guatemala, and more aware of their government's role in international economics and in supporting leaders like Ríos Montt.

"We've been happy to find that there is resistance against these policies in the U.S.," said Castillo. "What gives us hope is that we can join in resistance together."
