Published on Wednesday, March 19, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Of Culture and Conscience
by William Wong
 

War is imminent, an act of insanity by a man who was not elected by a majority of American voters and who is influenced by a gaggle of ideologues who apparently want America to take over the world with the most dazzling array of high-tech weapons in history.

Grassroots peace marches and vigils aren't making a difference to the Washington warmongers. It's really a different-looking protest crowd, not just the great unwashed of the Vietnam War-era, but soccer moms and their children, as well as white-haired women comfortable in their Saks Fifth Avenue outfits.

This is a melancholic moment for me. I am thinking about the strange juxtaposition of some celebratory moments for Asian Americans, for example, and the impending war.

In recent days, there's been a flood of laudatory articles in The Chronicle about the opening of the Asian Art Museum. In about a week, PBS will broadcast a three-part series applauding Chinese Americans for "making it," despite a history of racism and discrimination. For many Asian Americans, these are good times, if the kind of mainstream media and cultural attention represented by the Asian Art Museum and Bill Moyers' approval are a measure.

I recently got a plea from an upscale Asian American group to join the Asian Art Museum (for $125) so that I could be part of the crowd in a special preview. I read a newspaper account about a glittery preview evening at the museum, where Asian and non-Asian partygoers went all out, wearing all manner of "native, ethnic" outfits -- silk this, brocade that.

I chose not to join the Asian Art Museum, thus forgoing an opportunity to hobnob with my well-to-do brethren and their well-coiffed and bejeweled spouses, to say nothing of the best and brightest of the San Francisco social scene bedecked in, presumably, form-fitting cheongsams and silk jackets.

Instead, I have chosen to bear quiet witness in peace marches and vigils. In one San Francisco march, I noticed a sprinkling of Asian faces, mostly youngish (20s and 30s). I know other Asian Americans have long been active in the peace movement, including my sister Nellie Wong, a renowned poet and socialist; acclaimed writer Maxine Hong Kingston and civil-rights activist Yuri Kochiyama. In a smaller demonstration, however, I saw only three other Asian faces in a crowd that was mostly white.

I don't mean to ignite a class war among us yellows and don't want to disrespect the visionaries, organizers and supporters of such cultural institutions as the Asian Art Museum or PBS. Perhaps the timing is unfortunate that mainstream cultural events are focused on Asians and Asian Americans just as our mighty nation is about to bomb to smithereens a society that has descended from the cradle of civilization. Maybe it's an accident of history that that once mighty civilization is now led by a madman of its own, Saddam Hussein. That alone, however, does not justify what our own madman is about to do.

I challenge my Asian brothers and sisters to reorder their priorities, as hard as that may be to do at a time when mainstream America, which has profoundly disrespected us, seems to be infatuated with us. Celebrate the Asian Art Museum. Feel good that PBS is saying nice things about us. But delve deeply into your own conscience to discover what you feel about what our nation is about to do to countless thousands of innocent people in Iraq.

William Wong is author of Yellow Journalist: Dispatches from Asian America (Temple University Press, 2001): www.yellowjournalist.com.
