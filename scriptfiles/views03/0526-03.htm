Published on Monday, May 26, 2003 by the Philadelphia Inquirer
Patriotism in a Peaceful World
by Alison Wiley
 

I experience a dynamic tension on Memorial Day.

On one hand, I deeply respect people - in this case, soldiers and veterans - who work and struggle for causes larger than themselves, and are willing to die for their cause. I want to be as devoted and courageous as they are.

On the other hand, I want a world in which our children and grandchildren bravely advance freedom and justice, yet die peacefully, and are never maimed or murdered in wars. Is that vision disloyal to the dead, or less than patriotic on my part?

I look to history to help me understand patriotism and progress. I read that the ancient Aztec culture made thousands of human sacrifices annually because they devoutly believed those deaths would appease their gods and protect them from danger. Similarly, thousands of women termed witches were burned at the stake during Europe's Middle Ages in the conviction that those torturous deaths were for the greater good. In 2003, we are appalled at these and other historical cruelties. But the cultural fears of those times justified the slaughter as necessary.

Similarly, our modern fears justify our modern slaughters, our wars. We frame war as a last resort we employ only to protect ourselves. Yet, the surprisingly nonviolent revolutions and regime changes in the former Soviet Union and South Africa illustrate that human freedom and progress can happen, on grand scales, without war. Possibly, our great-great grandchildren will see the sacrifices of human life we made in the wars of the 20th century as inhuman and unnecessary - the same way we now understand the witch-burnings of medieval Europe and the human sacrifices of the Aztecs.

Entertaining that possibility does not diminish my respect on Memorial Day for the American soldiers who have died, and the veterans who have been crippled, physically and emotionally, by war. Rather, it increases my compassion and my desire to work for the ideals for which they suffered. I want their descendants to suffer less than they did, to win even better advances for humanity - as warriors of peace. That vision is my version of patriotism.

Alison Wiley (alisonwiley@aol.com) lives and writes in Portland, Ore.
