Published on Tuesday, November 18, 2003 by WorkingForChange
Dean's $77 Gamble
Decision to Reject Public Funding Could Advance Campaign Finance Reform Beyond McCain-Feingold
by Byron Williams
 

Within the span of one week Democratic presidential candidates Howard Dean and Sen. John Kerry (D-MA) have decided to reject federal matching campaign funds during the presidential primaries. With President Bush already deciding to forgo the matching campaign funds, does the decisions of Dean and Kerry further undermine 30 years of political contribution laws? When Dean kicked off his presidential bid, he committed to accepting taxpayer money and vowed to criticize any Democrat who didn't. Because of the reality of presidential campaigning in the 21st century, Dean now realizes that the Democratic nominee will face a severe cash disadvantage against Bush, who will abandon the system for the second time and raise upward of $200 million.

As of September 30, Dean and Kerry were the two leading fundraisers on the Democratic side, but their collective $45.5 million paled in comparison to President Bush, whose campaign had raised $85.2 million during the same period.

The Bush fundraising juggernaut leaves Dean and any other candidate who is serious about obtaining the Democratic nomination little choice. The president has no primary opponent and will be free to take aim at the eventual Democratic nominee until the GOP convention in early September. With a tough primary fight looming for the Democratic candidates, the matching funds restriction could leave the Democratic nominee unable to respond to the Bush attacks from March to until August, when the general election matching funds kick in.

Following in Dean's footsteps, Kerry has since decided that he too will opt out of the matching campaign funds during the primaries. Over the weekend, Kerry issued the following: "Today, I also issue a challenge to Governor Dean. Senator Russ Feingold has called on all Democratic candidates who forgo matching funds to pledge that they will not spend more than the limit of $45 million until the nomination is decided. I accept that -- and I call on Howard Dean to do the same. To show America that his decision was about beating George Bush and the special interests and not just about grabbing an advantage in the primaries."

The nobility of Kerry's challenge notwithstanding, an exegesis of his statement raises doubt about his ability raise $45 million, while appearing to maintain a feign commitment to the goals of the McCain-Feingold campaign finance reform legislation.

Though the Supreme Court is scheduled to rule early next year on its constitutionality, the McCain-Feingold legislation may already be obsolete. By eliminating soft money contributions, which benefited Democrats more than Republicans, and raising the hard money limits to $2000, McCain-Feingold effectively made it easier for the president to reach his goal of $200 million. Moreover, Republican and Democrats alike have already developed strategies to get soft money into the election.

Outside of the contributions from his personal resources, Kerry may find the bundling of $2,000 checks somewhat more difficult to obtain, placing Massachusetts's junior senator in a quandary. Though he boldly announced his plans to withdraw from the matching funds, Sen. Kerry is stuck somewhere between sounding "Dean Lite," with the fundraising methodology of "Bush Lite."

Unlike Bush and Kerry, Dean's decision to forgo matching funds, if successful, could actually move campaign finance reform beyond the moderate changes called for in McCain-Feingold. Dean has woven fundraising into the fabric of his campaign. Instead of fundraising being a necessary adjunct that opens the door to possible abuse, undo influences and time spent away from connecting with voters, Dean's utilization of the Internet has made fundraising a tool that involves the electorate. The byproduct could be increased voter turnout.

President Bush is packing out Westin Hotels across the country with $2,000 per plate luncheons for wealthy contributors to reach the $200 million plateau, and Sen. Kerry is hoping to do the same to reach $45 million. Dean is asking 2 million people to give him $100 each. At $77, Dean by far has the lowest contribution per individual rate among the major candidates. How much political influence might that buy in a Dean administration?
