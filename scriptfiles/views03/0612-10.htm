Published on Thursday, June 12, 2003 by CommonDreams.org
No Child Left Behind?
by Sarah Tobias and Sean Cahill
 

Lisa Stewart is 33 years old and has terminal breast cancer. Inevitably, she has to grapple with a whole range of healthcare and end-of-life issues. Confronting death is an excruciatingly painful process for most terminally sick people, and particularly for those who are young. Lisa's case has a special poignancy to it because she is a parent. When she dies, she will leave a five-year-old daughter along with her partner of 10 years. Yet while most families endure hardship and insecurity when an adult member is diagnosed with a terminal illness, only a small subsection experience the difficulties that Lisa's family has already had to confront. Lisa is a lesbian. Like their gay male counterparts, lesbian-headed households constitute a family-type that is largely unrecognized by current law and policy. Consequently, same-sex couple families are uniquely disadvantaged during times of extreme sickness and imminent death.

Take, for instance, the issue of health insurance. The fact that the North American system of health insurance is in crisis is a well-known fact -- over 40 million Americans currently lack health insurance. What is less well known is that gay men and lesbians are over-represented among the uninsured. People in committed same-sex relationships are often ineligible for the health benefits employers regularly extend to their employees legally married spouses, because access to these benefits is contingent on civil marriage -- a right denied to gay and lesbian couples regardless of how long they have been together. Even when companies offer domestic partner benefits to same-sex partners of gay men, lesbians, and bisexuals, these are usually taxed as income by the federal government, whereas spousal benefits are not -- an inequity that causes economic hardship to many gay and lesbian-headed families.

The non-recognition of gay and lesbian relationships, and the disadvantage this fosters, is especially problematic given the economic insecurity prevalent in the gay, lesbian, bisexual and transgender community. Contrary to the dominant myth, gay men and lesbians are actually less affluent than the general population. Based on Census and General Social Survey Data, economist Lee Badgett shows that gay men earn on average 17-28% less than their heterosexual counterparts. Although lesbians earn the same as heterosexual women, lesbian couples earn less than heterosexual couples because women on average earn less than men. Facing major fiscal constraints, few can afford domestic partner benefits that increase their taxable income, while large numbers remain without the means to buy health insurance independently.

Lisa Stewart joined the ranks of the uninsured when her illness forced her into unemployment. Her partner Lynn, who works in the South Carolina public school system, was unable to add Lisa to her health insurance policy. The South Carolina public school system does not recognize lesbian and gay couples as family, or offer domestic partner benefits. Seeking health insurance to cover her enormous chemotherapy bills, Lisa applied to the State Cancer Aid program. Yet while the South Carolina public school system refused her health coverage because she was not married to her partner, the State Cancer Aid program counted Lynn's salary and assets as household income -- and denied Lisa coverage on the basis of excessive resources. Lisa's only recourse was to separate households from her partner's in order to qualify for state aid.

Since current law and policy fail to recognize same-sex partners as family members, Lynn is also unable to take leave from work to care for Lisa under the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA). Much heralded by liberals, FMLA was passed in 1993 and applies to people working in companies with over 50 employees, and who have clocked 1,250 hours of work in the previous 12-month period. It provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid aid to care for immediate family members who are sick, as well as after the birth or adoption of a child. The legislation applies, however, to the "husband or wife as defined or recognized under State law for purposes of marriage in the State where the employee resides, including common law marriage in states where it is recognized." As it stands, then, FMLA prevents same-sex partners from taking time off to care for each other.

Lynn won't get Social Security survivors benefits when Lisa dies, either. Despite the fact that gay men and lesbians help fund the system, Social Security precludes same-sex partners from receiving these survivor or spousal benefits. Even divorced heterosexuals can sometimes count on a portion of their former spouse's Social Security income upon his/her death. By contrast, same-sex partners who have supported each other for a lifetime get nothing from the system. Pensions and 401Ks also discriminate against gay couples.

The irony of all this is that Lisa Stewart is lucky. She has an extended biological family that fully supports her relationship with her partner. If she didn't, her death could devastate Lynn and her daughter -- her family of choice -- economically, emotionally and as a unit.

Completing a variety of legal forms and documents is imperative when the law doesn't recognize your family, and especially when you want to prioritize your family of choice over biological relatives who may reject your primary relationship. But this creates a danger-zone for gay and lesbian families. If lesbians or gay men are incapacitated in the hospital without having first completed medical power of attorney forms, then their closest blood relatives, and not their partners, are authorized to make important healthcare decisions for them. Without a completed "consent to autopsy and disposal of remains" form -- a form that doesn't exist in all states -- the members of a lesbian or gay man's biological family, rather than their partner, gets to determine the place of burial, as well as whether or not to cremate the dead individual's remains. Without a will that spells out inheritance rights, a shared house in the name of one partner in same-sex relationship will automatically be inherited by the dead individual's nearest biological relative, not by the deceased lesbian or gay man's partner.

Of course, when a parent dies, the most vulnerable people in the whole scenario are the surviving children. The children of gay and lesbian parents lack an enormous range of basic securities, but most significantly they lack the automatic right to live with their surviving non-biological parent if their biological parent dies. Second-parent adoption -- a process by which the non-legal parent acquires parental rights through adoption, while the legal parent retains parental rights -- can alleviate this insecurity by preserving the integrity of the family. Yet second-parent adoption is not available in many states, including Colorado, Wisconsin, and Ohio. Where second-parent adoption does exist it is sufficiently expensive, generally costing thousands of dollars, that many lower-income lesbians and gay men cannot afford the security it offers.

Second-parent adoption isn't yet recognized in South Carolina. The best that Lisa Stewart can do is to ensure that upon her death Lynn is named the guardian of their daughter, and hope that this arrangement will protect their parent-child relationship.

Gay and lesbian-headed families exist in ever increasing numbers. According to the 2000 Census, one in three lesbian couples and 22% of gay couples are raising children. In other words, there are 67,129 gay male couples, and 100,624 lesbian couples for whom parenting is a part of daily life. Many thousands of single GLBT people are also raising children. Yet lesbian and gay parents exist in a strange, economically disadvantaged hinterland because most family policy gives preference to married, heterosexual couples and their children, excluding all other family types from adequate support. Poor lesbians are particularly vulnerable, as states around the country and the federal government promote heterosexual marriage and "a father for every child" through welfare policy. Gay men and lesbians are effectively required to purchase an approximation of security for their families. Many cannot afford this luxury.

Under these circumstances, the right's endless rhetoric about restoring the family to a nuclear unit comprised of a married man and woman together with their children is almost beside the point. Lesbian and gay headed-families are here to stay. So, as it happens, are single parent families, and other alternative family structures, all of which are just as worthy of state support as more traditionally constituted families.

The good news is that public opinion on gay and lesbian families is changing. A March 2002 ABC News Poll found that a plurality of Americans (47 - 42%) now support adoption by same-sex couples. A UCLA survey indicates nearly 60% of college students support gay marriage. More and more corporations, municipalities, and newspaper wedding pages recognize same-sex partnerships.

The time is long overdue to change policy to reflect the current reality of the American family. No-one should be forced to take cumbersome and expensive precautions to protect their family against discrimination. The U.S. lags behind dozens of nations, including Brazil, South Africa and Israel, in recognizing same-sex couples. An inclusive family policy that supports all family types is the best way to prevent the economic marginalization of gay and lesbian-headed families, as well as single-parent families and other families that do not fit the traditional mould. It is almost certainly the best way to ensure that children like Lisa Stewart's daughter are not left behind at the time when they are most vulnerable -- as they watch their parent weaken and die.

Sarah Tobias and Sean Cahill are coauthors of 'Family Policy: Issues Affecting Gay, Lesbian, Bisexual and Transgender Families', published by the National Gay and Lesbian Task Force Policy Institute.
