Published on Sunday, November 9, 2003 by the Miami Herald
Iraq War is America's Burden to Bear
Call up of Reserves Shows U.S. Failure to Win Support
Editorial
 

It isn't so much the unforeseen consequences of the invasion that inflame the debate over post-war Iraq. No war ever goes according to plan. But it has become all too obvious that administration officials brushed aside all pre-war objections that didn't conform to a rosy outlook. The result is that U.S. forces are bearing a much greater burden than ex pected, and that they will continue to bear it largely alone and for a longer period than anyone anticipated. Americans have a right to be dismayed over the conduct of the occupation thus far, and skeptical about plans for the future.

Force-rotation plans

U.S. forces led the way in Gulf War I and the occupation of Haiti in the '90s, but they enjoyed an international mandate and the support of a genuine coalition to get the job done. Now, with the call-up of 43,000 National Guard and Reserve soldiers last week and continuing reluctance by Turkey, India and other countries to contribute forces to patrol Iraq, it is clear that the burden is almost fully on U.S. troops. More clearly than ever, this is America's war -- and the latest force-rotation plans offer no reassurance of change.

There are some 133,000 U.S. troops in Iraq at the moment. Yet the Pentagon has announced plans to reduce total troop strength to about 105,000 next year, even though Iraq remains a large, unpacified country with porous borders in a volatile part of the world. If this announcement is intended as reassurance that all is going well, it falls wide of the mark. Following a week that saw the deadliest single day of casualties since the early days of the war in March -- a week when even the headquarters of the American civilian authority came under attack -- that seems enormously optimistic.

Maybe, as Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld has said, Iraqi troops will be ready to assume a bigger role in pacification, but it's worth keeping in mind that unjustifiable optimism has been a hallmark of this entire effort. When Gen. Eric. K. Shinseki, the outgoing Army chief of staff, said in February that it might take 200,000 troops to secure postwar Iraq, Mr. Rumsfeld and his civilian aides scoffed. Today, it's obvious that, if anything, the general was too conservative in his estimate.

Size of the armed forces

The underlying problem is the size of the U.S. military force, as acting Army Chief of Staff Gen. John Keane, Gen. Shinseki's successor, told Congress in August. In Iraq, he said, ''We're short of infantry, we're short of chemical-biological soldiers, we're short of military police.'' In addition to the Iraqi deployments, U.S. forces maintain a presence in Afghanistan, South Korea, Bosnia, Kosovo and the Sinai. With only 10 active-duty divisions, the 480,000-man U.S. Army has been stretched almost to the breaking point by the war and the occupation. The demands on the 900,000-member Guard and Reserve units are equally stressful.

The occupation has turned into a ''long, hard slog,'' Mr. Rumsfeld conceded after the Chinook shoot-down. He should be equally candid in providing a realistic assessment of future requirements instead of offering premature expectations of troop reductions timed to coincide with the political campaign.
