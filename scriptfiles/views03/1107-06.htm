Published on Friday, November 7, 2003 by the Austin American-Statesman (Texas)
Failing History's Lessons About Wars Without End
by Gary Chapman
 

The occupation of Iraq has turned into the reckless blunder that the White House's critics warned about prior to the U.S. attack in March.

The military victory over the Iraqi army was never in doubt. What mattered was the aftermath, and the management of a fractious and violent country after its army was defeated and its government dismantled and jailed.

Now we're stuck in what national security specialists call an "asymmetric war," unable to defeat the enemy but unable to leave. Diligent reporters and researchers have only just revealed how unprepared the U.S. government was for managing Iraq, and how deluded Pentagon and White House officials were about how events might unfold there. Now we know that our leaders simply ignored their intelligence experts.

Iraq was meant to be a test case of the concept known among academics and professionals as the Revolution in Military Affairs, or RMA, also known in political circles as defense transformation. Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld is a true believer in defense transformation.

The chief benefits of the RMA, according to its proponents, are "more bang for the buck," seamless interservice integration, "smarter" weapons that allow pinpoint-targeting accuracy, and smaller, faster and more deadly combat units.

This is why the U.S. military went into Iraq with fewer forces than commanders recommended, and why the pace of the war was so fast that routine military safeguards were neglected. Rumsfeld and his close advisers were determined to demonstrate to the military that the ideas at the core of the RMA were feasible in real combat.

But this also explains why the military was so unprepared for the "real" war, the bloody conflict we're in now. Combat troops trained to fight using the most deadly high-tech weapons are not ready for urban policework or peacekeeping among civilians, let alone guerrilla warfare. Earlier this year, the Pentagon announced that it was closing the U.S. Army's Peacekeeping Institute, a decision that was reversed in September because of the situation in Iraq.

Military officers have long known from experience that the path to promotion is not through peacekeeping, but through enthusiasm for the ideas of the high-tech RMA. Thus the U.S. military is caught in a terrible dilemma. Most of our soldiers are ignorant about Iraq and its politics, and only a few are able to speak the language of the people they are there to protect. Our troops have the wrong tools and the wrong training for their current mission. They have no visible strategy for success. It's not even clear that more troops with the same training would turn things around.

White House obfuscation about the trap we're caught in only makes things worse. The administration insists we're fighting the last remnants of diehard Saddam Hussein loyalists and a few foreign jihadists.

But Middle Eastern expert Ahmed Hashim, a colonel in the U.S. Army, has identified numerous Iraqi resistance factions, mostly disconnected from each other and diverse in their skills and their goals. "With so many motives and goals, there cannot be a single strategy to stabilize this situation, and a military solution alone will never work," wrote Hashim.

What's happening is a nightmare come true: multiple sources of guerrilla resisters who are attacking our troops and other targets, and getting better at it because they're learning our weaknesses and responses. Our staggering arsenal of high-tech weapons will be of little use in such circumstances. Our soldiers are brave and dedicated, but they're sitting ducks. Yet the president insists we won't leave. No one is likely to volunteer to help us.

This is producing a depressing déj vu. Jeanne Kirkpatrick, the former Reagan administration official and neoconservative hawk, said in 1979 that Vietnam should have "taught us the dangers of trying to be the world's midwife to democracy when the birth is scheduled to take place under conditions of guerrilla war."

Evidently, we didn't learn. And now we're stuck in quicksand once again.

Chapman is director of The 21st Century Project at the LBJ School.
