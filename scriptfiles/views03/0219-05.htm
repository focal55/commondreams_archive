Published on Wednesday, February 19, 2003 by WorkingforChange.com
If War Is Not a Foregone Conclusion, Then Why Does the Media Act Like It Is?
by Susan Estrich
 

The chatterers are still digging themselves out. They'd already bought into this war. They'd stopped asking why -- or why now -- weeks ago. They were counting countries and comparing bookings. Where's France? Who's got Rumsfeld or Ridge? But last weekend suggests that George W. Bush hasn't sold this war nearly so well outside the storm center as he has inside it. The fact that war may be a foregone conclusion is no excuse for not asking the hard questions. Back to basics.

Who were all the people out protesting? Just people, an 81-year-old friend told me, who hadn't been to a march in years and was nursing her legs. My daughter wanted to go with a delegation from her school, but she was home with a fever. Everyone complained that there were hardly any cameras there, a friend told me. But word got out even if the Democratic candidates who might be the natural leaders and attention-getters are, with the exception of Howard Dean, still fighting the last war.

What had been striking about the polls on this war is how, in every debate, everybody always had one that supported his position, rendering all of them virtually meaningless. The administration kept claiming everyone was behind them. People do like Bush, and as my father used to say, you can't beat a horse with no horse. In that atmosphere, the administration succeeded in convincing at least those who come in closest contact with it to translate its own determination to use force and thus the inevitability of war into the appearance of public acceptance of it; to extend the pre-emption policy of foreign affairs into media coverage of policy. The media and the smart boys (sorry, but they are mostly boys) tuned in and out before most people realized it was time for the World Series.

Why? You hardly hear anyone even ask it anymore; its about who's hoodwinking who, and how, and with what, and what the score is in the United Nations.

Why now? He's been a bad guy for a long time, poisoned his own people years ago.

What's the role of oil in all this? What will be the reaction in the Arab world if the United States is seen as going it alone? The country is now engaged in a debate that Democrats were afraid to have and the media passed over.

Here's how Washington works. You get sold. You have to believe the people you work for or report on are ultimately smart. If you're covering them, your success depends to a certain, large extent on their cooperation, on their coming on your show, giving you access. If you work with them, you don't get access, face time or promotions by telling them they're wrong; and that applies from bookers to Colin Powell, from lowly direct-reports to Donald Rumsfeld.

Yes is always the safest answer. “Here's how” beats “you're wrong”. At a certain point, no one asks why you're headed in a certain direction, but only how many you've added to your column, or who's for and against -- and you keep track.

But there's a war coming, and clearly millions of Americans are not persuaded and don't know the answers. To watch the press, you'd think the questions had long been addressed.

The emergence of an antiwar movement has almost been an afterthought for a debate that has never really happened.

Which makes the next set of questions even more difficult:

Are we allowed to keep raising these questions once the war begins? What was the lesson of Vietnam? That you aren't supposed to go to war without public support, or that you aren't supposed to protest when there are servicemen and women who are putting their lives on the line for their country? Do Pearl Harbor rules apply, even though we didn't exactly have a Declaration of War? Does a blank check from a band of cowardly lions count?

Washington is digging out. President Bush is reportedly determined to push on, despite what the world, the demonstrators or anyone else has to say.

Is war a foregone conclusion? So what if it is. That shouldn't deter the media from acting as if it isn't. Isn't that why we have a First Amendment?
