Published on Tuesday, November 18, 2003 by the Madison Capital Times / Wisconsin
Brits' Protests of Bush to Stay on Course
by John Nichols
 

When British rocker Billy Bragg kicked off the Tell Us the Truth Tour in Madison, he expressed excitement at the prospect of visiting cities across the United States to raise a ruckus about White House lies and the failure of the media to expose them. But he did admit to entertaining one regret.

"I'm sorry I won't be in London for the protests against George Bush," Bragg exclaimed.

When Bush arrives in London tonight for an official visit to his only major ally in promoting the unwise and unnecessary war with Iraq, he may finally be forced to recognize that British Prime Minister Tony Blair does not speak for the British people.

On the eve of Bush's visit, a front page headline in London's Independent newspaper quoted the widow of a British soldier killed in Iraq as saying that, because of Bush's unproven claims about Iraqi weapons of mass destruction, "my husband died in vain."

Former Blair Cabinet member Robin Cook said, "It is all too clear what Britain has done to advance U.S. foreign policy. It is hard to spot what President Bush has done to assist British interests."

And a new Gallup Poll showed that such sentiments are common in Britain: more than half of those polled said they consider the United States under Bush's leadership to be a threat to world peace.

It should come as no surprise then that mass demonstrations are expected. Playwright Harold Pinter, former Cabinet member Tony Benn and prominent members of the British Parliament will headline a "Stop Bush" rally tonight, along with Vietnam War veteran (and Wisconsin native) Ron Kovic, the author of "Born on the Fourth of July," who has flown from California to London to lead the protests. And a mass demonstration is planned for Wednesday, when Bush and Blair are to meet.

The Bush camp is clearly frightened by the prospect that the president will encounter the opposition from which he has been shielded in Washington. Bush aides and Secret Service officials demanded major lines on London's underground transportation network, the Tube, be closed during the trip, and that roads be closed in central London.

They also demanded that U.S. Air Force fighter planes and Black Hawk helicopters be allowed to patrol the skies over London during Bush's visit. And they requested "shoot-to-kill" authority that would give Bush's security detail immunity in cases where nonviolent protesters were killed.

Faced with outspoken objections from London Mayor Ken Livingstone, a passionate foe of the war, British officials dismissed the demands for extensive Tube and road closures. They turned down the demands for U.S. control of the air space over London. And British Home Secretary David Blunkett pointedly refused to grant "shoot-to-kill" immunity to the presidential security squads.

Pressured by the White House, Blair and his aides attempted to impose unprecedented controls on the routes of protesters in sections of the city where the president will be present. But, while Americans have come to accept extreme limits on their freedom of assembly rights, the British people reject such constraints on their ability to protest.

Thus, the attempt to restrict the routes of "Stop Bush" marches drew loud objections from the press, religious and labor leaders, and members of Parliament for Blair's own Labour Party. So the marches will go forward, pretty much along the planned routes.

Kovic will be at the front, telling Brits that they are not alone in opposing Bush's war. "I and other Vietnam vets can't help but see a mirror image of the Vietnam tragedy unfolding in Iraq. I think one of the most patriotic and democratic things a citizen can do, right now, is march against war and in favor of peace."
