Published on Tuesday, July 29, 2003 by CommonDreams.org
Painted Into The Amen Corner
by Randy Cauthen
 

No less an authority than John Dean has recently commented on the resemblances between the Watergate scandal and the Bush Administration's deceptions regarding Iraq. One of the stranger resemblances contains a lesson the administration apparently has not learned.

According to the conventional wisdom about Watergate, Nixon's fate was sealed by his decision to, in his own words, "stonewall," to bluff and invoke executive privilege and deny any knowledge of wrongdoing in the White House. For example, Barry Goldwater said in an interview with CBS that, had Nixon admitted to the American people at an early stage that he had made a mistake, his presidency would have survived. Nixon's aversion to such an apology has been cited as evidence of, depending on the commentator's politics, either loyalty to his subordinates or the delusion that he held enough power to avoid culpability for his acts.

Now, as the mainstream press begins to note that the administration's dishonesty extends a great deal further than the 16 little words that Condoleezza Rice sniffed about on CNN, Bush finds himself in a scandal that may eventually dwarf Watergate. He also, it seems, finds himself incapable of acknowledging his own public statements. On July 17, when asked whether he accepted responsibility for the words he'd used in the State of the Union address, Bush replied petulantly: "I take the responsibility for making the decision ... to put together a coalition to remove Saddam Hussein." Several days later, new White House Press Secretary Scott McClellan turned rhetorical back flips, clumsily, to keep from answering the same question.

In the wake of this dissembling, Bush's backers have been forced into multiple, contradictory stories about the State of the Union, and GOP operatives are starting to get nervous. For example, Republican National Committee member John Paul Hammerschmidt of Arkansas was quoted by the Associated Press this week as saying, "For the first time he's waffled a bit on the Niger-uranium story. They didn't confront that totally .... I'm not sure they've totally gotten their act together."

A number of journalists, both on-line and off, have speculated about why Bush can't make a simple and relatively painless apology. Some believe that Bush, having been rescued from the Vietnam draft and multiple business failures by his father's connections, simply doesn't know how to take responsibility for his own actions. Others note that his father's experience in the Iraq-Contra scandal may have convinced him that he, also, partakes of Reagan's famous Teflon. Most disturbingly, both Joe Klein of Time and Richard Cohen of the Washington Post have advanced the theory that Bush is so addicted to wishful thinking that he can't tell whether he is lying or not.

All of these explanations may carry some truth. Even Klein's and Cohen's idea that Bush is almost completely disconnected from reality has been borne out by his outrageous and under-reported statement on July 14 that the administration had given Saddam "a chance to allow the inspectors in, and he wouldn't let them in."

But Bush is also, as we all are, influenced by his rhetorical environment. In part, he is unable to accept responsibility for the Niger "mistake" because he is the victim of his own disdain of subtlety. In the world he has evoked for the American people, terrorists "hate us because we are good" and there is no possible gray area between "for us" and "against us." And a "good" leader cannot be mistaken in anything.

But even more than this, Bush is the unwitting victim of his allies on the right, the ultraconservative "Amen Corner" of radio hosts, columnists and miscellaneous dittoheads typified by David Horowitz and Ann Coulter. Commenting on the Niger controversy in an article called "The Party of Sabotage," Horowitz rolls out the (now nearly inevitable on the right-wing) comparison between anti-Iraq-war protestors and Neville Chamberlain, and ventriloquizes the Democratic Party as saying "We want congressional investigations [of Bush's claims about Iraq] to tie up the Administration, distract it from its war tasks and encourage our enemies." Similarly, Coulter took some time this week from schilling her book-length paean to Joseph McCarthy, Treason, to extrude a column equating Howard Dean's questions about Iraq with "the Odai and Qusai Hussein Memorial Fund," reminding the reader that Democrats incensed by Bush's Iraq claims "never before viewed lying as a negative," and concluding that "Clinton [who is, of course, another inevitability] also lied every time he said 'God bless America,' though he doesn't believe in God or America."

To be sure, there is an increasing awareness on the far right that the ultra-right, especially the extremely visible Coulter, is an embarrassment. And Bush has never gone nearly as far in voicing anti- American sentiments as his journalistic partisans and his subordinates have. For instance, Attorney General Ashcroft's public statement that critics of the Administration would "only aid terrorists .... [t]hey give ammunition to America's enemies" is much harsher than anything Bush has said; being a uniter rather than a divider, Bush prefers to ridicule dissent rather than demonize it, as when he compared the millions of Americans who protested the impending war on February 15 to a "focus group."

But it is hard to avoid parallels between Bush's preference for a black-and-white, heroes-and-demons view of reality and the words of Coulter, Ashcroft, and Horowitz. So it is also hard to avoid the conclusion that Bush can't back away from his previous "mistakes" because he tacitly approves of a political climate in which even the mildest criticism of the administration constitutes, well, Treason. Even more than his own arrogance and credulity, the would-be Emperor's courtiers are encouraging him to parade around naked, and people have started to notice.
