Published on Monday, September 8, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
The Timid Terminator
by Ruth Rosen
 

A FEW WEEKS AGO, Arnold Schwarzenegger was clearly the candidate to beat on Oct. 7. With his Hollywood charm seducing the camera, he almost succeeded in acting like a serious candidate.

Since then, he has demonstrated how unprepared he is to run for governor. Last week, for example, the Hollywood hulk proved too timid to join other candidates for the first debate in Walnut Creek.

Odd, isn't it, that a former bodybuilder, a super-macho actor who vanquishes his enemies in action films, should be too scared to tell voters how he would govern the state of California?

Aside from his cowardice, Schwarzenegger is a candidate who has little to offer as governor:

-- He has no experience running anything but his own career as a bodybuilder and actor. He has neither run for -- nor held -- an elected office.

What he knows about state administration, the Legislature or public policy could fit through the eye of a needle.

-- A candidate who didn't vote in two presidential elections and skipped 5 of 11 statewide races isn't serious about American democracy. Does he only vote when it fits into his schedule or if his name appears on the ballot?

-- Until last week, Schwarzenegger's handlers wouldn't let him participate in any debate, unless he received the questions in advance. (So that he could rehearse his lines?) Apparently that seemed too scripted, so they have now relented, saying he didn't want to see the questions beforehand in the one debate he has agreed to join on Sept. 24. But what does this earlier decision say about their confidence in his ability to speak to the public about specific policy recommendations?

-- Schwarzenegger may be able to buy a brain trust, but it doesn't appear to have helped him all that much. Unlike other candidates, he has offered no overarching plan -- or policy proposals -- for solving the state's daunting economic crisis. He has also dodged taking stands on affirmative action or Proposition 54, the racial privacy initiative.

In short, Schwarzenegger is a pretty face, a clever man, and an expert on stagecraft, but he has no business running for the governor of this state.

Instead of showering attention on his candidacy, concerned voters should pour their energy into registering every eligible voter. Last week, a man ran through BART cars with registration forms. I saw at least seven people take them, including the woman sitting next to me, who said she had moved and forgotten to renew her registration.

At the same time, I want to remind Californians why they should vote "no" on the recall.

Gov. Gray Davis, for all his faults, was guilty of no malfeasance, no high crimes or misdemeanors. True, there was plenty of voter grumbling about the budget crisis. But there was no evidence of a populist uprising against the governor -- until, that is, ultraconservative Republicans bought and paid for a recall, hoping they could hijack an election they lost when Davis beat Bill Simon last year.

Like the impeachment of Bill Clinton and the presidential election of 2000, the recall is an attempt to repeal another Democratic electoral victory, which is a dangerous subversion of representative democracy.

Schwarzenegger, of course, wasn't the candidate the ultraconservatives had in mind; he's way too liberal on social issues. But many desperate ultraconservatives, including Rush Limbaugh, have abandoned whatever principles they had and decided to support any Republican candidate they can get.

What a pity, though, that their candidate doesn't have the courage of his most famous cinematic character. But Schwarzenegger is the first to remind us, especially when he defends the many murders he has committed on the silver screen, that there is a big difference between movies and reality.
