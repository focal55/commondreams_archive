Published on Tuesday, January 7, 2003 by CommonDreams.org
'No to War': In an Internet Cafe in Istanbul
by Andrew Davison
 

ISTANBUL, Turkey -- In anticipation of a war against Iraq, the Turkish Doctors Union (Türk Tabipler Birligi), the professional association to which all doctors in Turkey belong, has recently announced measures to be taken in the event of a chemical or biological weapons attack. The first indicator of an attack is vapor in the air with an almond, garlic kind of odor. This is followed by the appearance of oil and water droplets in the surroundings. "Immediate headaches, coughing, watery eyes, visual defects, nausea, breathing difficulties and sudden bleeding are some of the major symptoms. Dead rats, flies, and insects may also indicate an attack" (See www.ttb.org.tr/savas/ and 'Turkish Daily News', Jan. 2, 03).

No wonder that, according to recent polls, over 80% of the people here are saying "Savasa Hayir," No to War.

Except by those who have traveled here, little is known of the socially impressive sensibility and liveliness of Turkish society, across all sectors, rich and poor, elite and marginal, conservative and dynamic. Certainly none of the military strategists on the 24 hour news channels in the United States who are busily calculating the "use of our bases in Turkey" and the "friendship" of Turkey as a "reliable ally" appreciate or know of the strollers in the marketplaces, parks, and malls (these days decorated with New Years Trees and Noel Babas, and playing host to sold out shows of "The Two Towers"). None of the strategists for "Showdown in Iraq," seem to care to consider the social context for the upcoming war, like the cosmopolitan diversity of the crowded, lively cafes here, where, even amidst the ongoing anguish of an impoverishing economic crisis (since February, 2001), friends, families, and lovers gather for conversation, tea or one of the local spirits, games of backgammon, and, in the evenings, song and dance. None seem to have ever heard the playful sounds emanating from the yards of the elementary schools and soccer fields, or witnessed the busy scholarly arenas of the universities, or encountered the relatively trustful public atmosphere that one finds here in Istanbul, a city of over 12 million, in which passengers on commuter buses pass their fare hand to hand from where they sit to the driver (who sends the change back, hand to hand) and children play and travel more freely than they do in even the mid-size towns of the United States.

And, yes, like the American administration, too few of the public  strategists in the US appreciate the rationality of protest in the region against a war that seems to be more about projecting American power and control (over oil, but not only that) than "fighting terrorism." At least the National Alliance in Afghanistan was *already* at war with the Taliban. Here, among those who support helping the Americans, the calculation is more that the costs are too great to be left on the outside, and that, once in, Turkey should seize the opportunity to secure -- what the authorities consider -- the country's "national interests" (both economically and strategically, in northern Iraq). "There are too many reasons why we have to be in the action," Prime Minister Abdullah Gül recently told the editors of the major newspapers.

Yahoo!News reported the burning of American, British, and Israeli flags at a demonstration last week here, but it made no mention of the legitimate worries and objections of tens of thousands of ordinary citizens and non- Governmental Organizations like the Doctors, Bar, and Women's Associations, to this planned, US-led assault. As Turkish nationals and supporters of democracy, those "saying no" are hardly pro-Saddam. They are concerned for their own welfare and the safety of their neighbors in Iraq, and they ask for the consistent application of international law, region-wide weapons reductions plans, and comprehensive conflict resolution efforts. They object in principle to the impatient instrumental use of their country ("Turkish soil and ports," what to Americans are "our bases") and of their friends or relatives in the army for "America's war" (to quote one friend with such connections), and they are deeply troubled by the sense that something very, very nasty is about to happen here.

Military calculations, like war itself, are much more easily made from afar, where the lives of those immediately affected and used -- including the American and allied troops who are poised to do their jobs -- remain remote. But if, despite the protests and pleas for restraint from those whose well- being hangs in the balance, the US administration decides to launch a war, the battles are not going to be remote from Turkey, or Kuwait, Jordan, Syria, Saudi Arabia, and Iran, or even Baghdad. What preparations are being made by the administration, its allies in the region, and the UN Security Council to protect the noncombatant doctors, lawyers, teachers, marketsellers, lovers and dancers, or the youngsters now playing computer games next to me from the effects of harmful gases and biological agents? US policy-makers took pride in the humanitarian efforts they made during the height of the war in Afghanistan (dollar donations from children; food relief efforts). Not only should they now listen to the reflective, non-belligerent voices of those directly affected -- for their objections to the current course of US policy will not disappear with Saddam. They should also, before deciding to launch a war, organize and take all necessary measures to protect the lives of all noncombatants in the region, here and in other countries (just as one sees in Washington, New York, and Tel Aviv), by distributing gas masks and home-protection kits, and by making antidotes to weapons of mass destruction readily available. Economic and political incentives for the local regimes to "join the coalition" are, emphatically, *not* enough. If all present efforts at a peaceful resolution fail, then, in Iraq too, the US must do more than simply try to avoid casualties; it should help people survive war.

If a nasty war looms in the near future, the consequences of not addressing these needs will be, quite possibly, truly catastrophic. As indispensable as the informative services of the Turkish Doctors Union are, if and when the dead rats transform the lively arenas of life here into potential mass morgues, no one should be left without the resources they need to survive.

Savasa Hayir, No to War.

A member of the Political Science Department at Vassar College in Poughkeepsie, New York, Andrew Davison is author of  Secularism and Revivalism in Turkey: A Hermeneutic Reconsideration (1998) and coauthor of  The Philosophic Roots of Modern Ideology: Liberalism, Communism, Fascism, Islamism (2001, with David Ingersoll and Richard Matthews). He is presently visiting Istanbul.
