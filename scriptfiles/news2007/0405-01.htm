World Publics Say UN Should Protect Against Genocide; Large Numbers Open to Intervention in Darfur
 

WASHINGTON, DC -- Publics around the world say the United Nations has the responsibility to protect people from genocide and other severe human rights abuses even if this means acting against the will of their own government, according to a study by The Chicago Council on Global Affairs and WorldPublicOpinion.org, in cooperation with polling organizations around the world.

Large numbers are open to UN intervention in Darfur, where Arab militias linked to the Sudanese government are accused of massacring the civilian population. But many seem to be uninformed about the situation in western Sudan and declined to answer.

"There seems to be a world-wide consensus that the UN Security Council has a responsibility to act to protect populations against genocide," said Steven Kull, editor of WorldPublicOpinion.org.

Respondents in 12 countries were asked whether the UN Security Council had the "responsibility" to authorize military intervention to protect people from severe human rights violations "even against the will" of the government committing such abuses. The most common response in all countries is that the UN Security Council has a responsibility to authorize the use of force in such cases.

The Chinese public shows the highest level of support for such intervention (76%), followed by Americans (74%), Palestinians (69%) and Israelis (64%).

In the 10 countries asked specifically about international intervention in Darfur, most of those who answer indicate that they would favor such action. But large numbers did not respond, suggesting they are uninformed about the conflict in Sudan.

Support for UN action is highest in France where 84 percent say the Security Council has either the "responsibility" to authorize intervention in Darfur (55%) or the "right" (29%) to do so. Close behind is the United States where 83 percent say the Security Council has either the "responsibility" (48%) or the "right" (35%) to intervene.

Respondents in seven countries were also asked whether they thought their country should contribute troops to an international peacekeeping force in Darfur. Majorities in France (84%) and the United States (65%) support contributing troops to a peacekeeping force in Darfur.

For details, please see http://www.thechicagocouncil.org or http://www.worldpublicopinion.org. WorldPublicOpinion.org is a publication of the Program on International Policy Attitudes at the University of Maryland.
