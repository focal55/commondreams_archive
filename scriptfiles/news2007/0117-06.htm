US, Ethiopian, Somali Forces Must Respect Civilian Lives
Advocacy organization urges caution in air campaign targeting al-Qaeda in Somalia
 

WASHINGTON - January 17 - Following news of recent air strikes targeting al-Qaeda in Somalia, the Campaign for Innocent Victims in Conflict (CIVIC) today called on U.S. and Ethiopian air forces to exercise caution and to adequately assess potential harm to civilians before launching air strikes.
 
The International Herald Tribune yesterday reported fear and claims of civilian deaths among nomads in southern Somalia following US and Ethiopian air raids in early January. CIVIC cautioned that the use of air power carries with it a high risk of civilian casualties, despite the use of precision weapons.
 
“We have seen the human cost of fighting insurgents in Afghanistan and Iraq, with far too many innocent lives lost,” said Sarah Holewinski, executive director of CIVIC.  “Somali civilians should not have to suffer the same fate.”
 
Under the law of armed conflict, military operations must use all feasible means to minimize loss of civilian life and property, and attacks must be withheld if expected to cause excessive civilian loss in comparison to a concrete military goal.  “Air power is notoriously indiscriminate. The US military and their Ethiopian and Somali counterparts should take all precautions to distinguish between civilians and combatants,” said Holewinski.  CIVIC urged military planners in Somalia to adequately assess the risk to civilians before launching air attacks and to evaluate their success following the attack.  That assessment includes keeping a full count of civilians harmed. Currently, the U.S. does not keep an official count of civilian casualties in Somalia.
 
CIVIC is a Washington-based organization founded by the late Marla Ruzicka, a passionate humanitarian killed by a suicide bomb in Baghdad while advocating for war victims in Iraq. CIVIC believes that civilians injured and the families of those killed should be recognized and aided by the governments involved, and is working toward smart, compassionate policies for civilians caught in the crossfire of conflict.
