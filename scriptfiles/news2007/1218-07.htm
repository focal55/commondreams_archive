Lax Standard Fails to Prevent Souring Seas
Group Petitions EPA to Address Threat of Ocean Acidification
 

WASHINGTON, DC - December 18 -- The Center for Biological Diversity formally petitioned the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to confront the threat of ocean acidification. The petition filed today calls upon EPA to strengthen the water quality standard for ocean pH and to publish guidance to help states protect waters of the United States from carbon dioxide pollution.

The oceans cover about 70 percent of the earth’s surface and absorb about 22 million tons of carbon dioxide each day. In turn, seawater reacts with carbon dioxide and as a consequence becomes more acidic. This process, termed ocean acidification, impairs the ability of marine life to build protective shells and skeletons among other adverse effects.

In a new report in the journal Science, scientists predicted that if human sources of carbon dioxide continue to increase, ocean acidification coupled with global warming will kill the majority of the world’s coral reefs by the end of the century (Hoegh-Guldberg et al. 2007). Already, the pH of the ocean has decreased 0.1 units on average due to anthropogenic carbon dioxide. If carbon dioxide emissions continue unabated, seawater pH may decrease an additional 0.4 units—more than a 100 percent change in acidity.

According to the petition, EPA’s current standard is outdated and woefully inadequate in the face of ocean acidification. A decline of 0.2 pH — allowed under EPA’s current water quality standard — will be devastating to the marine ecosystem. Twenty-five leading scientists researching the effects of carbon dioxide on oceans recently concluded that, “a decrease of this magnitude would pose a risk to the physiology and health of a variety of marine organisms” (Caldeira et al. 2007).

“The Clean Water Act is the nation’s strongest law designed to protect water quality,” said Miyoko Sakashita, attorney for the Center for Biological Diversity’s oceans program. “Because ocean acidification is the greatest threat to the water quality of the oceans, EPA has a duty to take steps to address ocean acidification under the Clean Water Act.”

The Clean Water Act requires the EPA to update water quality criteria to reflect the latest scientific knowledge. Since EPA developed the pH standard back in 1976, an extensive body of research has developed on the impacts of carbon dioxide on the oceans. Already researchers have noted impacts of ocean acidification in surface waters. Unless we take steps to dramatically reduce carbon dioxide pollution, seawater will begin to corrode the shells of shellfish, mollusks, and plankton that form the base of the marine food web.

The petition demonstrates that protecting the oceans from acidification requires EPA to adopt a more stringent pH criteria. Under the Clean Water Act, states must adopt water quality standards at least as strong as those established by the EPA. Once adopted, the standards form a basis for regulating pollutants. Here, a stronger pH standard could translate into regulation of carbon dioxide emissions. Additionally, the petition requests that EPA provide guidance to states on how to protect ocean waters from ocean acidification and improve monitoring.

“Unless we take steps now to prevent carbon dioxide pollution, it could cause the collapse of our marine ecosystems,” said Sakashita. “It is not too late to halt ocean acidification, and EPA needs to take action immediately to address this serious water quality threat facing our oceans.”

The Center for Biological Diversity has previously petitioned ten coastal states to declare their ocean waters “impaired” under the Clean Water Act due to ocean acidification. Responses to those petitions are pending. Today’s petition marks the first step towards federal regulation of ocean acidification under the Clean Water Act.

More information is available from the Center for Biological Diversity at http://www.biologicaldiversity.org/swcbd/programs/marine/acidification.html.

The Center for Biological Diversity is a nonprofit conservation organization with more than 40,000 members dedicated to the protection of endangered species and wild places.
