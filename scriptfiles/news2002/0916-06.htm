President Bush Fails To Lay Out Case For War
Evidence for Iraqi WMD Intent Strong, But Imminent Threat as Casus Belli Missing
   WASHINGTON - September 16 - President Bush today addressed the General Assembly of the United Nations, and laid out his case for war against Iraq. The President stressed that for ten years Saddam Hussein has led Iraq in defying UN Security Council resolutions and has tried at every turn to cheat weapons inspectors attempting to carry out the Security Council mandate to disarm Iraq in the wake of the 1991 Gulf War. Physicians for Social Responsibility, a leading disarmament group that shares a Nobel Prize with the International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War, after assessing the President's speech, announced it opposes US plans to invade Iraq.

The President stated Iraq's possession of Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD) is a serious threat, and the international community must act to force him to comply with the UN inspections regime. If Saddam Hussein fails to do so, an overthrow of the Iraqi regime will follow. The President, in his speech, and in a report issued today by the White House offered no evidence, however, of new activities by Iraq in the WMD field, and no evidence that Iraq poses an immediate threat to its neighbors or the United States.

"The President has still failed to answer the questions, why war? And why now?" said Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., M.P.H., Executive Director and CEO of PSR. "We can all agree that Iraq must comply with the UN Security Council resolutions calling for its disarmament, and that greater efforts are needed to enforce these resolutions. But the President seems intent on marching blindly off to war, ignoring the concerns in the international community, his own party and the country as a whole."

It is far from clear that international law permits military action without provocation or imminent, immediate threat of attack from Iraq. The minimum pre-requisite would be a resolution from the United Nations Security Council calling for military action. The US Congress should also vote a War Powers resolution if our armed forces are to be asked to fight. Nevertheless, the mere possession of WMD by a state has never been seen as a casus belli. The possession of nuclear weapons by the United States, and its refusal to act on its legal obligation to disarm under Article VI of the NPT seriously undermine the President's case for invasion of Iraq on the suspicion of WMD possession.

"If the US wishes to establish a serious international norm against the possession of WMD that would be fantastic news," said Martin Butcher, Director of Security Programs for PSR. "In that context, the disarmament of Iraq must happen alongside serious efforts to eliminate nuclear weapons from the arsenals of world's nuclear powers, and with strong enforcement of the conventions banning chemical and biological weapons. These are policies that, until now, the administration has vehemently opposed."

According to PSR, US policy toward Iraq has been inconsistent and caused much humanitarian suffering. A US attack would only add to the existing threats to human health and the environment. Most troublesome is the possibility that the United States would use nuclear weapons in an attack on Iraqi WMD sites. US Counterproliferation policy calls for early use of nuclear weapons in any conflict to destroy Chemical and Biological Weapons sites, as well as bunkers that may contain such weapons or facilities to produce them. This policy aims to preempt the use of WMD by another country and is at the core of the Nuclear Posture Review.

"The Bush administration's Nuclear Posture Review sought to reshape the nuclear arsenal as a force intended primarily for war-fighting," said Musil. "Now the President is pursuing a collision course with Iraq that could put the NPR into practice and see nuclear weapons used for the first time since 1945. This is unacceptable under any circumstances."
