"Reclaim the Media" Conference Challenges Corporate Stewardship of Media Resources
   SEATTLE - September 3 - Organizers of a community media conference taking place in Seattle this September hope their events will draw public attention to a number of issues bearing on the media's role in a democratic society. The conference, titled "Reclaim the Media: a Community Media Convergence," runs from Sept. 10-15, 2002, and will draw prominent independent media journalists, broadcasters and media reform activists from across the US and Canada. Complete information is available on the web at www.reclaimthemedia.org.

The conference is scheduled to "shadow" the National Association of Broadcasters' annual radio conference, also in Seattle. Reclaim the Media events will include rallies in protest of what activists charge are the anti-democratic policy priorities of the NAB, a powerful industry lobby which has often helped shape federal media regulation to the liking of the wealthiest media owners.

The keynote address, "Independent Media in a Time of War," will be given by Amy Goodman, co-host of Pacifica Radio's national news program "Democracy Now" (broadcast in Seattle on KBCS-FM and on the public access cable station SCAN, channels 29, 77). Other prominent speakers include "Alternative Radio" host David Barsamian, media policy reform advocate DeeDee Halleck, microradio visionary Ron Sakolsky and hip-hop political activist and Oakland-based public affairs radio host Davey D.

Conference events center upon a day-long teach-in to be held Friday the 13th at Town Hall. Panel topics, united by the theme of Media and Democracy, include media ownerhip, community media, microradio, labor media, media coverage of the war on terror, and others. Additional conference events include media-related workshops, lectures and performances, and three afternoons of music, public speakers and information tables at a rally in Freeway Park - right on the doorstep of the NAB's conference venue, the Washington State Convention Center.

Thematically, the Reclaim the Media conference will link together several reformist threads:

• Unprededented concentration of media ownership is adversely affecting diverse views and local accountability in print and broadcast media, as more and more stations and newspapers are bought up by remote megacorporations.

• Deregulation of ownership rules has had the most extreme effects in the radio industry, allowing Clear Channel to amass over 1200 stations nationwide, along with its billboard, concert promotion, television and other interests. The company has pioneered technology which fools listeners into thinking that remotely-produced, "localized" programming originates from their own communities.

• Recent and proposed regulatory changes to cable broadband services will give private companies monopoly control over local Internet service, and will allow them to invisibly filter content from competing companies, or based upon political or other criteria.

Reclaim the Media organizers expect to attract the interest of many NAB conference attendees, as well as the basic audience of Seattleites and national community media. According to Jonathan Lawson, "unions representing commercial broadcasting and newspaper employees have endorsed our conference for a reason. The NAB's priorities - bigger corporate profits at the expense of diversity, quality and local control - offend many radio engineers and announcers, even local station managers. After five years of unprecedented industry consolidation, many radio employees are answering to new bosses a thousand miles away, and are critiquing the industry from the inside, just as we are from the outside."

Reclaim the Media is one of a growing number of independently planned conferences and gatherings building a social movement focusing on media and democracy issues. When the NAB held their Radio Conference in San Francisco two years ago, community radio activists staged a counter-conference critical of the NAB's stance opposing Low-Power FM radio licensing. In March 2002, demonstrations at the Washington, DC headquarters of the FCC drew attention to public statements in which FCC Chairman Michael Powell ridiculed the idea that his regulatory agency should be guided by the "Public Interest" rather than by market trends. On October 19 of this year, media reform activists will commemorate "Media Democracy Day" in Albuquerque, Toronto, Vancouver and other cities.
