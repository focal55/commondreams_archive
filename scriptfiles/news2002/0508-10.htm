Top Washington DC Chefs Announce They're Releasing Chilean Sea Bass From Their Menus
   WASHINGTON - May 8 - Chefs, restaurant owners, and scientists came together today to announce their joint effort to save from extinction a delicacy whose overnight popularity has proven to be its downfall. Sixty of Washington D.C.'s top restaurants, caterers, and retailers took the first step today towards saving Chilean Sea Bass by pledging to stop serving or selling the fish until its population begins to recover.

D.C. area restaurants that have signed on to the campaign include (complete list available):

Galileo
Cesco
Jefferson
Equinox
Jaleo
Maestro
Nora & Asia Nora
Bistro Bis
Legal Sea Foods

"We're keeping Chilean Sea Bass out of our kitchens to ensure that future generations will be able to enjoy the fish," said Roberto Donna, chef and owner of Galileo, whose signature restaurant provided a backdrop for the event.

The restaurant industry accounts for 70 percent of all Chilean Sea Bass sales in the United States.

Chilean Sea Bass burst onto most menus only 10 years ago, and last year Bon Appetit named Sea Bass "Dish of the Year", while showing a photograph of and describing Chilean Sea Bass. But now, according to Destined for Extinction: The Fate of Chilean Sea Bass, the report released today, Chilean Sea Bass suffers from acute over-fishing by "pirate" poachers in the remote waters near Antarctica and is on the verge of extinction.

"We are pleased that so many Greater Washington area chefs are joining this national campaign to help save Chilean Sea Bass from extinction," said National Environmental Trust (NET) president Philip E. Clapp. "We've gained the support of more than 500 chefs across the nation who want to join in the fight. Since we've seen such an overwhelming response from DC area chefs, we are hopeful that more local restaurants and those in other cities will quickly follow their lead."

Despite regulations set by the Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR), the international governmental body that regulates Chilean Sea Bass, estimates indicate that nearly 80 percent of Chilean Sea Bass sold on the world market is illegally obtained.

Several weeks ago the U.S. Departments of Commerce and State issued a joint press release and fact sheet that confirmed illegal Chilean Sea Bass enters the United States and that "illegal poaching of Chilean sea bass threatens the Commission's (CCAMLR) conservation attempts."

"The non-stop pace of illegal fishing virtually guarantees that the entire species will collapse in less than five years unless we take immediate action," said Beth Clark, a former Antarctic research scientist and a U.S. delegate to CCAMLR. "Last year alone -- with regulations in place -- nearly 40,000 tons of Chilean Sea Bass were illegally fished in the waters around Antarctica."

"The practical reality is that current regulations are not enough to protect Chilean Sea Bass from extinction," said Andrea Kavanagh, campaign manager at NET. "Until we have a reliable system to track legal versus illegal catches of Chilean Sea Bass, we have to make it unprofitable for the pirates to continue poaching and ask consumers to "Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass."

"Not serving Chilean Sea Bass is the right thing to do," said Todd L. Gray, chef and owner of Equinox Restaurant. "Our guests strongly support us because they agree that it's not worth sacrificing an entire species for our short-term appetites."

In addition to asking for support from chefs around the country, today "Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass" kicked off their consumer campaign with a one-minute flash animation video. The short animated clip highlights the need to protect Chilean Sea Bass and asks consumers to join chefs in taking the pledge to not eat, buy or serve Chilean Sea Bass until stronger regulations are in place and enforced. The flash animation and consumer pledge can be accessed at:
http://environet.policy.net/marine/csb/flash/takeapass.html

The "Take A Pass on Sea Bass" Campaign was launched in San Francisco in February, Chicago in March, and Philadelphia in April. Similar efforts are scheduled to take place in New York and Los Angeles. More information about the campaign can be found at: www.environet.org/marine/csb
