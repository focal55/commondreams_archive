'People Pedaling Peace' Bike Ride Honors Victims of Gun Violence; Rally at Finish Line on Capitol Hill
  

WASHINGTON - June 25 -Over the course of four days this June, more than 25 dedicated cyclists with "People Pedaling Peace" made the 190-mile trek from Hampton, Va., to Washington, D.C., to honor and remember victims of gun violence.

A rally to celebrate their success took place at the finish line on Tuesday, June 25 at the Upper Senate Park in the shadow of the U.S. Capitol. The press conference featured remarks from Sandra McSweeney, founding member of "People Pedaling Peace"; Mary Leigh Blek, Million Mom March director, Heather Wrabetz, Columbine survivor; Michael Barnes, president of the Brady Campaign to Prevent Gun Violence united with the Million Mom March; Kellye McIntosh, youth organizing manager of Alliance For Justice, and other riders participating in the event.

Sandra and her husband Mike McSweeney formed the "People Pedaling Peace" bike ride last year after their 14-year-old daughter, Stephanie, was shot and killed by a stray bullet as she walked out of a Hampton roller rink in March of 2001. This year's "People Pedaling Peace" ride raised money to build a new playground in Stephanie's neighborhood so children can have a safe place to play.

"I wanted to come up with another way to bring awareness to the horror of crime," said Sandra McSweeney about her decision to form the ride. "We need to let people know that our kids are dying."

Several other individuals affected by gun violence also made the journey this year, including Elisha Encinias, a Columbine survivor who narrowly escaped the two gunmen in her classroom that tragic day in 1999 and Amber Hensley, who witnessed the deadly rampage at Thurston high school in Springfield, Ore.

Congressman Robert C. Scott, 3rd District, Va. was there to honor the McSweeney's, the cyclists from his district, and all victims of gun violence. His attendance not only showed support for his constituents, but for all Americans who believe in common-sense gun legislation.

The "People Pedaling Peace" cyclists rode not only in honor of the victims of gun violence, but they rode for stronger, more sensible gun laws in America. They are calling for Congress to strengthen the country's existing laws and support new ones, such as legislation to close the gun show loophole.

"I will be riding in memory of all those who lost their lives to gun violence, including my son Matthew who was killed in 1994," said Mary Leigh Blek, Million Mom March director. "I am committed to fight each and every day to strengthen the gun laws in this country because I know they save lives."
