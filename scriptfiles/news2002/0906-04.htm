Sierra Club Urges Congress to Protect America's Communities and Forests
   WASHINGTON - September 6 - The Sierra Club today urged Congress to reject the Bush Administration's proposed legislation that would increase destructive commercial logging, gut important environmental protections under the guise of forest fire prevention, and fail to adequately protect communities from fires.

"America needs a sound vision for forest management that provides for better community protection, responsibly uses federal resources and personnel and maintains important environmental safeguards," said Sierra Club Executive Director Carl Pope. "Instead of protecting communities, this legislation sells out America's forests to corporate special interests and muzzles the American public by squashing their rights to speak out on behalf of forest protection."

Unfortunately Senator Larry Craig (R-ID) is exploiting this summer's forest fires to push an amendment to the Interior Appropriations bill based largely on the President's proposal. The amendment would eliminate the effectiveness of important environmental laws, including the landmark National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) that has protected National Forests and other special places for more than three decades. NEPA, which was signed into law by President Nixon, ensures that citizens have the right to comment on federal projects that impact the environment. Providing the American public an opportunity to be included in the management of public lands is a basic right that Congress and the Administration should not take away.

"Gutting forest protections and eliminating public discourse on controversial logging projects will do nothing to address the problem of catastrophic wildfires, nor will it protect lives and communities," said Pope. "The Forest Service's priority should be protecting communities, not quenching the timber industry's thirst for profit. The timber industry wants to log the larger, more fire-resistant trees that will yield a profit, not the smallest trees and underbrush that will protect people near at-risk communities."

Senator Craig's amendment to the Department of Interior appropriations bill also includes language that would prohibit further public input on wilderness protection for Alaska's Tongass National Forest -- America's largest and wettest National Forest.

"When you include a provision to bar public input on protection for the Tongass, a rainforest immune from fires, in legislation purportedly aimed at reducing fire risk, you call your own bluff," said Pope. "This is a blatant attempt to increase logging in all National Forests and silence the public, regardless of fire-risk."

The Sierra Club and other environmental groups last month offered a new vision for protecting communities. The seven-point plan includes:

  • Do the most important work first. Make protection of communities from firs the Forest Service's Number One Priority.
  • Provide meaningful funding. This program should be a minimum of five years and funded at $2 billion a year to go directly to fireproofing homes and removing hazardous fuels in the Community Protection Zones.
  • Match personnel to work. Shift Forest Service personnel skilled in preparing brush clearing and thinning projects from backcountry, low priority areas to the Community Protection Zones.
  • Carry out immediately the vast majority of fuel reduction projects in the Community Protection Zones that raise no significant environmental issues.
  • Restore natural fires to have natural forests. Prescribed burns can help to reduce fuel buildup and restore healthy forest habitats.
  • Protect our ancient and wild forest from logging and logging roads.· Stop the attack on forest protection safeguards

"This plan will protect people and their communities, help reduce the likelihood of catastrophic wildfires and keep the wild areas of America's forests healthy and unspoiled," said Pope.
