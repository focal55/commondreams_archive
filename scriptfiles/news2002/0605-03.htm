55 Cities to Protest Medical Marijuana Raids June 6
MPP Communications Director to Join D.C.-Area Civil Disobedience
   WASHINGTON - June 5 - In the wake of new Drug Enforcement Administration raids on medical marijuana providers in California, the D.C.-based Marijuana Policy Project (MPP) is joining in a national day of protest on June 6. Organized under the banner of "Americans for Safe Access" (ASA), medical marijuana patients, their families, friends, and supporters are planning protests and nonviolent civil disobedience in 55 cities nationwide.

Bruce Mirken, director of communications for MPP, will join in civil disobedience in the D.C. area, honoring a longtime friend with AIDS whose health has been imperiled by the DEA's raids.

There will be two protests near Washington: In the late morning, activists will risk arrest to post a "Cease and Desist" order at a DEA facility. At 5:00 p.m. there will be a second demonstration at the DEA's headquarters at 700 Army Navy Drive in Arlington, Virginia. Information about other events planned around the country is available at ASA's Web site.

The June 6 date was chosen because the U.S. District Court in northern California is expected to reaffirm on June 7 that it is illegal under federal law to distribute medical marijuana, a ruling that may be seen by the DEA as a green light for more raids.

"This does not mean, however, that the DEA should be using its resources to target medical marijuana distributors, especially in these times of terrorism," said Robert Kampia, MPP's executive director. "Furthermore, the federal court decision will not in any way negate the eight state laws that allow patients to use, possess, and grow their own medical marijuana legally."

"I decided last week to join in the civil disobedience, after getting a call from my friend Mary Lucey in Los Angeles," Mirken said. Lucey, who served as interim AIDS Coordinator for the city of Los Angeles, found that marijuana was the only thing that allowed her to tolerate the harsh side effects of the anti-HIV drugs she must take to stay alive. "Marijuana is an absolutely essential part of her therapy, and the DEA's raids last October cut off her safe supply. Now she lives in constant fear. Our government has chosen, as a matter of policy, to torture the sick, and those of us who are healthy have a responsibility to stand up for them."
