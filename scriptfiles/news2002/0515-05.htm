Global AIDS Alliance Calls Kerry-Frist Global AIDS, TB and Malaria Bill 'Step in the Right Direction'
   WASHINGTON - May 15 - Responding to the today's release of the Kerry-Frist "US Leadership Against HIV/AIDS, TB, and Malaria Act of 2002," the Global AIDS Alliance called the bipartisan effort a "step in the right direction."

The bill authorizes increases in US government spending specific to global AIDS of about $1.7 billion in 2003 and to $1.9 billion in 2004. Were Congress to approve such spending through the appropriations process, it would represent a significant increase over President Bush's spending proposals for FY 2003.

"This bill is a step forward, and it shows that, because of mounting grassroots pressure, Congress is beginning to take AIDS seriously," stated Dr. Paul Zeitz, executive director of the Global AIDS Alliance, a broad consortium of civic groups, activists, humanitarian agencies and religious groups.

Unfortunately, the bill falls short of providing the US fair share ($2.5 billion per year) of the $10 billion per year needed from all sources, just to fight AIDS, according to UN estimates. The World Health Organization's Commission on Macroeconomics and Health has estimated $15 billion per year is needed to fight global AIDS. "The fight against AIDS has been rigorously costed out, and on these objective measures this bill still falls short," stated Zeitz.

In addition, the bill authorizes low levels of funding for the innovative, newly-established Global Fund to Fight AIDS, TB and Malaria, providing less than what experts have said is needed. The bill authorizes a FY2004 funding level for the Fund that is much lower than what has been requested by civil society groups and UN Secretary General Kofi Annan. The Secretary General has asked wealthy countries to double their contributions to the Global Fund each year.

"The Kerry-Frist Bill authorizes significantly less resources for global AIDS at too slow of a pace than is appropriate for a global leader like the United States," stated Dr.Zeitz.

In practice, the funding levels authorized by this Bill will not be available for programs for another 18 months. During that time, another 3-4 million Africa will needlessly perish from AIDS. "It is imperative that Congress approve $700 million in emergency AIDS funds for the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, TB, and Malaria, as an immediate, stop-gap measure," stated Dr. Zeitz.
