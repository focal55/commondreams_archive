New York's Drug Law Debate Heats Up
   NEW YORK - May 3 - May 8, 2002 marks the beginning of the 30th year of New York's controversial Rockefeller drug laws, the state's harsh mandatory minimum sentencing laws for drug offenders. Numerous events are planned calling on Governor George Pataki to deliver on his promise to adopt meaningful reform of the Rockefeller drug laws, including:
  • Interfaith press conference in Albany. Catholic, Protestant, Muslim and Jewish leaders call on Governor Pataki to reform the unjust Rockefeller drug laws and untie the hands of trial judges, so that they can divert appropriate non-violent drug offenders into community-based drug treatment programs.
  • Full page ads in El Diario and in the Manhattan Times, on May 6th and May 8th promoting the May 8th anniversary rally in Manhattan and asking Governor Pataki to initiate comprehensive reform of the laws.
  • A rally outside of Governor Pataki's Manhattan office led by Rev. Al Sharpton, Mothers of the New York Disappeared (mothers with children in prison under these laws), the William Moses Kunstler Fund and JusticeWorks Community (May 8, 12:00 noon, at 40th St. and 3rd Ave.)
  • Over 150,000 calls from recorded messages by former Bronx Borough President Fernando Ferrer and potential Presidential candidate Rev. Al Sharpton to New York voters, particularly in minority communities. The calls will urge voters to call Governor Pataki and communicate their support for a drug law reform plan that includes sentencing relief for the thousands of non-violent drug offenders currently serving mandatory sentences -- over 94% of whom are Latino or African-American.

Proponents of reform say the current broad consensus to change the Rockefeller Drug Laws offers an unprecedented opportunity to accomplish reform this year. The timing could not be more crucial, given New York's budget crisis, to follow the lead of other states who have saved millions of dollars in taxpayer money by diverting nonviolent drug offenders into community-based treatment.

"There are three basic components central to any plan to achieve meaningful reform of the Rockefeller drug laws," says Deborah Small, Director of Public Policy and Community Outreach at Drug Policy Alliance. "One, substantial reduction in the current sentencing scheme with retroactive application for appropriate drug offenders currently incarcerated. Two, untying the hands of judges so that they can send suitable candidates to community-based treatment instead of prison. And three, investing financial resources into drug treatment, job training and education programs as a more effective and inexpensive alternative to prison."

Drug Policy Alliance is the nation's leading organization working to end the war on drugs and promote new drug policies based on common sense, science, public health and human rights. The Alliance, headquartered in New York City, has offices in Sacramento, Oakland, San Francisco, Washington, DC and Santa Fe, New Mexico.
