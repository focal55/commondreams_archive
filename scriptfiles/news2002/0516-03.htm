Report: Better Background Check Laws Could Prevent 120,000 Illegal Gun Sales Each Year
Current Loopholes Exempt 40 Pct of Gun Sales
   WASHINGTON - May 16 - About 120,000 additional illegal gun sales could be prevented each year by improving federal background check laws, according to a new report released today by the Educational Fund to Stop Gun Violence and a coalition of law enforcement and public health organizations. The report cites the effectiveness of criminal background checks, and recommends that Congress extend the provisions of the Brady Act, which requires background checks on sales by licensed dealers, to all gun sales, including sales at gun shows and via the Internet.

"This report by the Educational Fund to Stop Gun Violence contains some important -- and timely -- findings, considering our current national priorities," said Sen. Dianne Feinstein (D-Calif.). "The numbers are clear -- criminal background checks work. And if we want police and prosecutors to better enforce the law and stop those who supply guns to criminals, we must extend our effective national background check system to cover all gun sales."

Current federal law requires criminal background checks only for sales conducted by federally licensed gun dealers. This policy prevented almost 700,000 illegal gun sales through licensed firearm dealers from 1994 through 2000, but left 40 percent of transactions, many of which took place at gun shows, via classified ads, and over the Internet, completely unregulated.

The report shows that if the current rejection rate for attempted purchases from licensed dealers also applied to non-dealer transactions each year, an additional 119,680 illegal gun transactions would be prevented annually. Experts pointed out that this figure may underestimate the number of illegal sales that could be prevented, since criminals are more likely to try to purchase guns from non-dealer sources.

"The loopholes in criminal background check laws in many states help support a vast, profitable, and deadly illegal gun market," said Josh Horwitz, executive director of the Educational Fund to Stop Gun Violence. "Expanding background checks to all sales -- especially those at gun shows -- will help save lives, without impeding the ability of law-abiding Americans to buy and sell guns."

The new report recommends a comprehensive federal policy requiring criminal background checks for all gun transactions, regardless of where they occur.
