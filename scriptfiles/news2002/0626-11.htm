New York City Council Calls For Moratorium on Executions
Council joins nationwide campaign; 73 elected bodies have now recognized problems with the death penalty
  

WASHINGTON - June 26 - The New York City Council today voted 39 to 12 to call for a moratorium on executions until the death penalty is investigated for issues of fairness, justice, equality, due process and cost.

Resolution 12A was sponsored by Deputy Majority Leader Bill Perkins of Harlem, who has long called for such a moratorium.

"This is a momentous occasion for this Council," Perkins said. "With more than 100 innocent Americans sentenced to death since 1973, it is high time we questioned whether or not our judicial system is weighing the evidence properly, whether or not racial factors, economic factors and factors relating to good defense are what is making the difference in people's cases. A moratorium on executions is both timely and necessary and we hope that the Governor will take the resolution to heart and do the right thing."

New York City is the largest of the 73 municipalities that have adopted resolutions in favor of a moratorium. The resolution calls for a halt to executions while a commission is set up to study New York's death penalty statute.

Approval of the resolution by the City Council followed hearings held two weeks ago before the Fire and Criminal Justice Committee. Committee Chair Yvette Clarke noted that two states, Illinois and Maryland, have imposed a moratorium on executions and that within New York State the cities of Buffalo, Rochester and Mt. Vernon and the towns of Greenburgh and Newcastle have already passed moratorium resolutions. "People are beginning to realize that a system that is unfair and plagued with problems should not be permitted to continue," Clark said.

"Many disturbing questions have been raised about the use of the death penalty in the United States since New York reinstated capital punishment in 1995," said David Kaczynski, executive director of New Yorkers Against the Death Penalty. "Most states have experienced grave problems with the death penalty and a resulting decline in confidence in the criminal justice system."

Kaczynski noted that governors in two states, Gov. George Ryan of Illinois and Gov. Parris Glendening of Maryland, have put a temporary halt to executions. In Illinois, a commission recently issued a 207-page report containing 85 recommendations aimed at preventing the execution of innocent people. In Maryland, a report examining the racial aspects of the death penalty is expected to be released in September.

"Many of the same questions of fairness and accuracy that have plagued other death penalty states need to be addressed here in New York," said Kaczynski. "Indeed, New York fails to meet most of the 85 reforms suggested by the Illinois commission. I hope that New York State can heed this clear call from New York City and join the other states that have declared a moratorium."

Jane Henderson, co-director of the Quixote Center, said more than 2,100 groups and municipalities have now called for a moratorium on executions. "Fourteen states have considered moratorium legislation within the past year and the Governor of Maryland just declared the nation's second moratorium," Henderson said. "Every poll conducted on the issue has shown that a majority of Americans support halting executions. This changing tide is truly the result of concerned citizens taking a stand."

Steven W. Hawkins, executive director of the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty, commended the New York City Council for its vote as well as the local organizers who made it happen. "Across the United States, the question is not whether we will put a halt to executions but when," Hawkins said. "The rapidly expanding moratorium campaign is the result not of court rulings nor of principled political leadership on a national level. Rather, it is the result of determined and effective organizing at the grassroots level. New Yorkers Against the Death Penalty and the Quixote Center offer an example of what we need to be doing in every city, large and small, across the United States."
