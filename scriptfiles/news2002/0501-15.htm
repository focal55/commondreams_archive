Immigrant Rights are Human Rights:
National Legislative Action Day For Legalization And Immigrant Rights
   PHILADELPHIA - May 1 - Hundreds of immigrants will descend on the nation's capitol as part of the National Movement for Legalization and Immigrant Rights, Wednesday, May 1. Community-based organizations, labor groups, immigrant rights networks and faith-based organizations seeking justice for immigrants and their families will address members of Congress to demand the right to permanent residency for all undocumented immigrants living in the United States.

Before September 11, the Legal Immigration Family Equity (LIFE) Act of 2000 gave undocumented workers immigrants "amnesty" to petition for permanent residency. Now in an atmosphere of fear and suspicion, undocumented immigrants have been indiscriminately arrested and detained and, therefore, cannot represent themselves on Capitol Hill.

At a 10:00 a.m. press conference in the Cannon Building, Veterans Subcommittee Hearing Room 340, immigrant constituents from across the 50 states will call on the President and members of the House and Senate to stand by immigrants - now more than ever. They seek a new legalization program that recognizes the contributions immigrants make to this nation. A rally is also scheduled from 4:00-5:00 p.m. in Upper Senate Park.

Recently, Democrats Luis Gutierrez, Illinois, and Sheila Jackson-Lee, Texas, introduced legislation to the House of Representatives supporting comprehensive legalization program for all undocumented residents in the United States. Last year, the American Friends Service Committee (AFSC) urged US Congress to grant permanent legal status to all undocumented men, women and children who have established new lives in the U.S. In its Statement on Legalization for Undocumented Immigrants in the United States, the AFSC Board of Directors also calls upon the US Government to:

Respect human rights and international law;
stop militarization of the border;
remove the unequal treatment to which undocumented persons are subjected;
support legalized entry to those under duress or fleeing natural disaster, regardless of national origin and political affiliation; and support those pursuing family re-unification.
Founded in 1917 to provide conscientious objectors with an opportunity to aid civilian victims during World War I, AFSC is grounded in Quaker beliefs respecting the dignity and worth of every person. AFSC has programs in the United States, and in Africa, Asia, Europe, Latin America, and the Middle East that focus on issues related to economic justice, peace-building and demilitarization, social justice, and youth. In 1947, the AFSC and the British Friends Service Council received the Nobel Peace Prize on behalf of the Religious Society of Friends, for humanitarian service, work for reconciliation, and the spirit in which these were carried out.
