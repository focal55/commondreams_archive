Starvin' For Justice 2002
Fast and Vigil to Protest Death Penalty at Supreme Court
  

WASHINGTON - June 26 - In the wake of two landmark U.S. Supreme Court rulings, activists from across the country will converge on Washington, D.C. for five days of activities aimed at raising public awareness about the death penalty. The activities, scheduled for Friday, June 28 through Tuesday, July 2, commemorate the historic 1972 and 1976 Supreme Court rulings that suspended the death penalty in the United States and later allowed executions to resume.

Organized by the Abolitionist Action Committee (AAC) and endorsed by the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty, the main event will be a fast and vigil on the sidewalk in front of the U.S. Supreme Court beginning at 12:01 a.m. Saturday, June 29 and ending at midnight, July 2. June 29 marks the 30th anniversary of the Furman v. Georgia decision suspending executions, while July 2 marks the 26th anniversary of the Gregg v. Georgia ruling allowing executions to resume.

ATTENTION REPORTERS, EDITORS AND PRODUCERS: A noon rally will be held Monday, July 1 on the sidewalk in front of the Supreme Court. Photo-ops will be available as well as spokespeople to comment on the Supreme Court's recent rulings and overall trends relating to the death penalty in the United States.

On Friday, June 28, seven abolitionists arrested in January for unfurling a banner reading "Stop Executions!" on the steps of the Supreme Court will go on trial in D.C. Superior Court. Such speech is not allowed on the Supreme Court steps. The trial is scheduled to begin at 8:30 a.m. before Judge Gerald Fisher in Courtroom 217 or 218 on the second floor.

Abe Bonowitz, spokesman for the Abolitionist Action Committee, said the primary goal of the fast and vigil is to educate the public. "We are going to maintain a presence at the Supreme Court between the dates of the anniversaries of when the death penalty was ruled unconstitutional in practice in 1972 and when new laws were upheld in 1976," Bonowitz explained. "We are going to educate the public about the death penalty and ask people to stand with us against our nation's culture of violence and for alternatives to the death penalty."

"The death penalty in 1972 was arbitrary and capricious," said Steven W. Hawkins, executive director of the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty. "The death penalty in 2002 is arbitrary and capricious. The difference between 1972 and 2002 is that the abolition movement in the United States is now committed to a strategy of legislative repeal - organizing on a grassroots level, state by state, legislature by legislature. It is no longer a question of whether we will abolish the death penalty, but when."

For more information or to learn how to support Starvin' For Justice 2002, please visit www.abolition.org

The Abolitionist Action Committee is an an ad-hoc group of individuals committed to highly visible and effective public education for alternatives to the death penalty through nonviolent direct action.

Founded in 1976, the National Coalition to Abolish the Death Penalty is the only fully-staffed national organization devoted specifically to abolishing the death penalty and is comprised of more than 100 local, state, national and international affiliates.
