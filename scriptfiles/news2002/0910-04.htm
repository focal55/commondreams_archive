On the Eve of 9/11 Anniversary, Chemical-Using Industries Try to Stop Bipartisan Security Measure
   WASHINGTON - September 10 - One year after September 11th, lobbying organizations for the chemical and oil industries are trying to stop legislation to address chemical plant security from becoming law.

The U.S. Senate is considering taking up the Chemical Security Act as an amendment to the homeland security bill. The bill was voted for by nine Republicans, nine Democrats, and one independent when the Senate's Committee on the Environment and Public Works passed the bill on July 25.

"Despite broad bipartisan support for legislation to address what is universally viewed as a major security threat, the chemical industry is apparently still trying to kill common-sense security legislation," said Meghan Purvis, toxics and environmental health associate with U.S. PIRG, a public interest advocacy organization. "This common-sense chemical security legislation should be part of the Homeland Security plan. We hope the Senate makes public safety its priority."

Across the United States, thousands of industrial facilities use and store hazardous chemicals in quantities that could threaten communities if deliberately attacked by terrorists. According to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 123 facilities have at least 1 million people living close enough to be at risk of injury or death in the event of a chemical release. The Army Surgeon General ranked the threat of an attack on an industrial chemical facility second only to bioterrorism in the number of Americans who could be affected. Congress passed a bioterrorism bill earlier this year.

The Chemical Security Act, S. 1602, introduced by Senator Corzine (D-NJ), would require high-priority facilities to conduct vulnerability and hazard assessments, and develop plans for improving site security and reducing chemical hazards. The legislation passed unanimously out of the Senate Environment and Public Works Committee in July and will be offered by Senator Corzine as an amendment to the homeland security bill being debated by the Senate.

Despite the broad support demonstrated in the Senate so far, industry lobbying organizations are working aggressively to attempt to kill the bill. On August 29th, 30 trade associations faxed a letter to the US Senate asking members to oppose S. 1602 or a version of it if attached to the Homeland Security Act. Supporters of the bill immediately responded that most of the arguments raised by industry lobbyists were either inaccurate or misleading.

For example, the lobbying organizations' letter claimed that S. 1602 would create a "clearinghouse" of facilities' security information that could "potentially be used by terrorists," even though the bill withholds facilities' security assessments and plans from public view and creates strict information-handling requirements. The industry letter also claimed that the bill would "disrupt ongoing security enhancements and federal partnerships," even though the bill's authors addressed this concern by including an early compliance provision, which allows a company to have its ongoing security programs reviewed for compliance with the bill even before federal agencies issue regulations to implement the law.

Industry groups are also trying to create an image of grassroots opposition to the bill. In late August, the American Chemistry Council issued a request for "immediate action," calling on plant managers and workers in Ohio to call Senators and oppose the Chemical Security Act. However, the United Steelworkers of America, the union representing the largest number of chemical workers in the U.S., supports the bill.

In the year 2000, the total lobbying expenditures of the lobbying organizations opposing the chemical security bill amounted to $34,653,779. Supporters of the Chemical Security Act include the United Steelworkers, environmental and public interest organizations, the American Public Health Association, and Physicians for Social Responsibility.

U.S. PIRG is the national lobbying office for the state Public Interest Research Groups. State PIRGs are non-profit, non-partisan public interest advocacy organizations.
