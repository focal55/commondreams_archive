Senators Flag Gutted Whistleblower Protections as Post 9-11 Security Threat
Amendments Strengthening Whistleblower Rights To Be Considered in Homeland Security Act -- Guests Available to Comment
  WASHINGTON - September 10 -

WHAT?

As the Senate debates the Homeland Security Act (HSA) in this 9-11 anniversary week, a pivotal legislative fight is brewing over whistleblower protections for 170,000 employees of the proposed Homeland Security Department. The existing Whistleblower Protection Act has been rendered practically unenforceable by hostile court decisions. Critics complain the House version of the HSA further undermines internal administrative recourse for federal security workers who are threatened with reprisals and dismissal for speaking out on security problems. Below are examples of whistleblowers who braved reprisals to disclose serious failings in everything from airport security to border patrols. Such disclosures, if taken seriously by agency management instead of met with reprisals and covered up, might have helped thwart the 9-11 terrorist attacks, or future attacks. Those whistleblowers, along with whistleblower rights advocates and members of Congress leading the debate this week on including stronger whistleblower protections in the Homeland Security Act, are available now for interviews.

WHO?

 These and other whistleblowers and whistleblower protection advocates are available for interviews immediately:

MEMBERS OF CONGRESS:

Senators Charles Grassley (R-Iowa) and Carl Levin (D-Michigan)

-- Lead sponsors of an amendment to the Homeland Security Act strengthening whistleblower protections.


WHISTLEBLOWER RIGHTS ADVOCATES

Martin Edwin "Mick" Andersen -- Former senior advisor to the Justice Department who flagged leaks and abuses at the International Criminal Investigation and Assistance Program. He was stripped of his security clearance and lost his job, though his disclosures prompted investigations which confirmed his allegations, and now works with the Government Accountability Project.

Tom Devine -- Legal Director, Government Accountability Project, helped or represented over 2,000 whistleblowers during last 23 years, and helped write and led citizens' campaigns to pass, the Whistleblower Protection Act, Military Whistleblower Protection Act, and equivalent corporate whistleblower laws.

WHISTLEBLOWERS

Diane Kleiman -- US Customs Special Agent who flagged serious security lapses at American Airlines and drug abuse among special agents. Six months prior to 9-11 she warned the US Attorney's office in New York "a tragedy would occur because of lack of security at JFK airport and corruption within Customs."

Bogdan Dzakovic -- Federal Aviation Administration team leader who flagged gross security lapses at airports which detected simulated bombs only 3-10% of the time. He was threatened with firing and false charges of in-office violence.

Mark Hall and Robert Lindenmann -- National Border Patrol Agents who defied an INS gag order went to Congress and the NBC Today Show with news of serious security lapses along the northern US border, including failure to perform criminal background checks on those caught crossing illegally. The INS tried to summarily demote, transfer, and fire them.

Linda Lewis -- A USDA Emergency Programs Specialist responsible for analyzing evacuation plans and food supply protection against chemical and radiological attack. She flagged serious weaknesses in federally mandated emergency preparedness and testing that leave the public vulnerable to attacks. In retaliation, she was suspended for the last three years, subjected to psychiatric exams, and recently stripped of her security clearance. Darlene Catalan and Sandy Nunn -- Former US Customs Special Agents at the Los Angeles port who flagged systemic failure to inspect contents of 98-99% of the more than 300,000 foreign tanker cars and container ships entering each month. Their holds on suspicious shipments were routinely overridden. They brought their findings to Congress and the FBI, and in retaliation were investigated, suspended without pay and harassed into resignation.

Cathy Harris -- Senior US Customs Inspector at the Atlanta International Airport flagged racial profiling and intrusive searches of black female travelers while drugs flowed freely through the airport. Congressional investigations validated her findings and legislated changes, but she was placed on 15 months' unpaid stress leave, filed two bankruptcies and lost her home.

BACKGROUND

The Senate is now contemplating amendments to the HSA which would strengthen gutted whistleblower
protections for the roughly 170,000 workers of the new security agency, and provide a potentially far-reaching counterweight to curtailment of the labor and civil rights of 2 million federal workers in the wake of 9-11.

President Bush this week reiterated a demand for "management flexibility" in overseeing the Homeland Security Department, including full executive discretion over hiring and firing, and repeated his threat to veto the Homeland Security Act and cast Senate Democrats as soft on defense if the Senate "ties its hands" by stipulating any limits to that discretion.

The White House says it champions federal workers rights, but as The Wall Street Journal reported September 9, Mr. Bush himself has promised to preserve "whistle-blower protection[s]...even while insisting that Congress give him authority to waive them." The White House claims that authority on national security grounds, but security experts counter that waiving whistle-blower protections materially harms national security.

"Government agencies too often want to cover up their mistakes, and the temptation is even greater when bureaucracies can use a potential security issue as an excuse," said Senator Charles Grassley (R-Iowa) who along with Senator Carl Levin (D-Michigan) is introducing an amendment on stronger whistleblower protections to the Homeland Security Act. "At the same time, the information whistleblowers provide is all the more important when public safety and security is at stake."

"Excessive secrecy enforced by repression can threaten national security by covering up government breakdowns and sustain unnecessary vulnerability to terrorism," Senator Robert Byrd (D- West Virginia) said in a recent speech.
"Congress overwhelmingly approved state-of-the-art corporate whistleblower protection as an encouragement for private sector workers to defend America's financial markets. Our homeland security requires similar rights for government workers to make disclosures in defending American families against terrorism."

Byrd's speech cited the examples of National Border Patrol Agents Mark Hall and Robert Lindenmann, who became targets of INS reprisals for disclosing serious security failures along the US-Canada border. Hall and Lindenmann are among the whistleblowers now available for media interviews, along with members of Congress leading the current debate on strengthening whistleblower protections for federal security workers.

Failing to eliminate such reprisals, say security experts and rights advocates, violates not only workers' rights, but also the imperative to maximize Americans' security in the post 9-11 world.
