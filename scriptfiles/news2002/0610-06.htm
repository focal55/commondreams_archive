Whose Kids Will Ride Healthy School Buses? Congress Readies Funds to Replace Polluting Buses, School Districts to Vie for Funding
   WASHINGTON - June 10 - In response to warnings that school bus pollution could be harming America's children, Congress may soon help school districts replace their oldest, dirtiest school buses with clean fuel models, saving localities at least $85,000 per bus. Unfortunately, less than one percent of children that ride in school buses will benefit from these cleaner school buses, while millions of children will continue to ride high-polluting models.

"More than 20 million children ride in diesel school buses, which belch toxic soot from tailpipes," said Patricia Monahan, senior analyst for the Union of Concerned Scientists. "While the legislation will replace about 2,000 polluting buses, over 400,000 dirty buses will remain on the road. Congress is poised to take an important first step in reducing school bus pollution, but we have a long way to go to solve this problem."

Tight budgets force school districts to choose between books and buses. The national legislation relieves this financial burden by providing funds for districts to replace older, high-polluting buses with clean-burning buses powered by natural gas, other alternative fuels, or cleaner diesel. These cleaner buses can reduce the amount of toxic soot emitted from diesel school bus tailpipes by over 98 percent. School districts only contribute $15,000 to the cost of a bus that normally would come with a price tag between $100,000 and $140,000. Unfortunately, the program is tucked in controversial energy legislation that deepens our nation's oil dependence. Under the leadership of Reps. Boehlert (R-N.Y.) and Udall (D-Colo.), the U.S. House of Representatives included $300 million for cleaner school buses in a bill they passed last summer. Senator Evan Bayh (D-Ind.) championed similar legislation which passed the Senate in April. A final decision on the energy package will be decided later this year. It is crucial that as Congress finalizes the federal budget, it fully fund the program at the $300 million level.

UCS recently conducted the first pollution analysis of school buses across the country to determine where children are being harmed the most. More than 20 states flunked or nearly flunked the report card. California and Washington ranked worst, while only six states and the District of Columbia ranked "ahead of the curve." A study from Yale University found that children riding school buses breathe five to 15 times more particulate soot than children playing outside.

"Parents in all states should work with their local leaders to ensure that they receive a fair share of the new school bus funding," said Monahan, author of the UCS school bus report.

Parents should visit http://www.cleanschoolbus.org to learn more about school buses in their state.
