Statement by Alliance for Justice President Nan Aron:
Status of Judicial Nominations
   WASHINGTON - May 9 - The following is a statement by Alliance for Justice President Nan Aron:

After years of obstructing President Clinton's judicial nominees, some Republicans are now threatening to shut down the Senate simply for doing its job -- carefully scrutinizing all candidates for these prestigious lifetime positions. Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Patrick Leahy has pledged to move non-controversial, consensus nominees quickly, and he has done precisely that. The Senate has confirmed 52 judicial nominees to date, of whom nine are nominees to the courts of appeals.

As Senator Leahy has said repeatedly, reviewing the records of problematic, divisive candidates takes more time. President Bush's nominees are problematic and present a real threat to established rights that Americans have come to cherish.

Would you believe that some Republicans want the Senate to rubberstamp nominees who:

-- Denied a workers' compensation claim by a man who had been severely beaten by a fellow employee after the man responded truthfully to a supervisor's question regarding whether the fellow employee had left work early (Deborah Cook, 6th Circuit nominee);

-- Are regarded as so anti-consumer and supportive of business that the conservative Washington Times would describe them as "offer(ing) business the best opportunity in years to free itself from government regulations" (Miguel Estrada and John Roberts, D.C. Circuit nominees);

-- Strongly criticized fellow state supreme court justices for expediting their decision in a case involving a pregnant minor who had already endured a month of court proceedings over whether she could have an abortion without notifying her parents, and who was criticized by then fellow state Supreme Court Justice, now-White House Counsel Gonzales for engaging in an "unconscionable act of judicial activism" for her actions in that case (Priscilla Owen, 5th Circuit nominee);

-- Waited eleven years to resign from an all-male club, at which important professional contacts are made, after promising the Senate Judiciary Committee that, if confirmed to the district court, he would resign right away if he were unsuccessful in persuading the club to change its policy. He finally did withdraw from the club in December 1999, but only after the appellate seat for which he is now being considering became open. (D. Brooks Smith, 3rd Circuit nominee);

-- Dismissed a claim for invasion of privacy by a woman whose doctor had allowed a pharmaceutical company representative in the room during the woman's breast exam, a decision reversed by the state appellate court (Carolyn Kuhl, 9th Circuit nominee)

These are just a few examples of actions taken by some of this administration's very problematic judicial nominees. They raise serious questions about the respect these nominees have for the longstanding role of the federal judiciary as guarantor of equal justice for all and for Congress's role in enacting critical rights and protections.

The Republicans message today is very different from that of then-Senate Majority Leader Lott, who in 1996 said on the Senate floor: "Should we take our time and look at these people who are nominated to be Federal judges for life and hold sway over us in ways that exceed our imagination.... Yes. Do I have any apologies? Only one: I probably moved too many already."

By contrast, Senate Democrats have confirmed relatively non-controversial nominees at a rapid pace and made clear that they will undertake a thorough, deliberative evaluation of all appellate nominees. To do any less would be to further jeopardize established rights and protections that Americans cherish.
