Environmental, Consumer Groups Criticize Auto Industry Attempts to Rewrite CAFE Report
Call on NAS to Cancel Upcoming hearing on GM's complaints
  

WASHINGTON - September 28 - A coalition of environmental and consumer groups today sent a letter to the National Academy of Sciences (NAS) and the National Research Council (NRC) requesting that they cease further changes sought by General Motors Corp (GM) to its recent report on automotive Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards. The groups urged the cancellation of a meeting planned for October 5, 2001 to re-open the Academy's recent report.

"The NRC can't seem to say "no" to General Motors," said Ann Mesnikoff, Global Warming and Energy Representative for the Sierra Club. "We're calling on the National Research Council to stop allowing itself to be manipulated by GM."

The report on CAFE standards, released on July 31, 2001, found that technology exists to safely improve the fuel economy of cars and light trucks. Although the report was produced by a panel described by the New York Times as consisting "mainly of engineers and consultants who have worked for the auto and oil industries, along with some economists and retired oil executives," it was welcomed by environmental and consumer groups because it concluded that we can move forward to increase fuel economy with existing technologies that will curb global warming, save consumers money at the gas pump, and reduce our dependence on oil.

On August 22, 2001, the NRC held a closed-door meeting with four representatives of General Motors and one from DaimlerChrysler. Following that meeting, the NRC announced that it would hold an October 5 meeting to discuss, and possibly alter the report, despite the fact that proper peer review and evaluation of the report was completed before the report's release.

A letter from Alliance to Save Energy, American Council for an Energy Efficient Economy, the Center for Auto Safety, Natural Resources Defense Council, Sierra Club, and Union of Concerned Scientists, asks the NAS and NRC to cease modifying the report and cancel the October 5 meeting.

"Just as General Motors and DaimlerChrysler have taken issue with the final report, we also have identified problems, missed technologies, and flaws in the analysis. No doubt this report, or any report the NAS issues, is open to criticism, but it is unprecedented that a dissatisfied interested party would prevail upon the NAS to secure changes to a final report even under the guise of clarifying language," reads the letter in part.

Dear Dr. Alberts and Mr. Colglazer:

We strongly object to the meeting the National Research Council plans to hold on October 5, 2001 to discuss, and alter, the report entitled Effectiveness and Impact of Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards. It is our understanding that this meeting is the result of an August 22, 2001 closed-door meeting between National Research Council (NRC) staff and four representatives of General Motors and one from DaimlerChrysler -- parties that have a major economic interest in the findings of the Academy. This process is troubling. It undermines the objectivity of the Academy and the NRC and raises questions about the finality of NRC Reports. We request that the October 5th meeting be cancelled.

The report the NRC released on July 31, 2001 was peer-reviewed and final, subject to editorial correction. The National Academies' Study Process states:

*As a final check on the quality and objectivity of the study, the Academies appoint additional independent experts with a range of views and perspectives to review and comment anonymously on the draft report prepared by the committee.

*The review process is structured to ensure that the report addresses the committee's charge and does not go beyond it; the findings are supported by the evidence and arguments presented; the organization and exposition of the report are effective and logical; and that the report is impartial and objective. After revisions are made by the committee to satisfy the institution's rigorous review process, the report is transmitted to the agency that sponsored the study and released to the public. Names of the principal reviewers are made public when the report is released.

*The reputation of the National Academies for objectivity, integrity, independence, and competence is one of its most valuable assets. For this reason, the procedures designed to ensure excellence apply to each study undertaken and to each step of the study process."

The peer-review process took place in July and the report was revised prior to the July 31st release. Now, after the ex parte August 22, 2001 meeting the NRC is improperly reconvening the CAFE committee and opening up the report to further discussion and changes. This violation of process undermines the reputation of the Academy and its objectivity, integrity and independence.

The industry, including General Motors, has had an active, if not dominant, role in the preparation of the report: various companies provided testimony and their employees, representatives, and contractors participated in the Committee process and its technical analysis. One of the peer-reviewers, William Agnew, is retired from General Motors Research Laboratories. We are now very disturbed that the National Academy of Sciences and the NRC is providing General Motors with further, undue influence over modifications to the content and characterization of the fuel economy ranges included in the final report the Committee issued to Congress and the public.

Several of us have had conversations with Alan Crane, a staff member of the NRC. Mr. Crane has stated that while the October 5th meeting will not yield changes in the ranges of fuel economy improvements or "break-even" analyses in the report, it will lead to changes in how those ranges are described. The NRC has publicly stated that the auto industry's private communications have already persuaded the NRC that report was overly optimistic. We understand that these concerns are now being addressed in the report as it undergoes editorial revision.

Just as General Motors and DaimlerChrysler have taken issue with the final report, we also have identified problems, missed technologies, and flaws in the analysis. No doubt this report, or any report the NAS issues, is open to criticism, but it is unprecedented that a dissatisfied interested party would prevail upon the NAS to secure changes to a final report even under the guise of clarifying language. The Union of Concerned Scientists, however, is prepared to present a technical criticism of the report should the October 5 meeting take place.

It is appropriate for all sides to release their criticism of the report publicly and in writing. The October 5 meeting is inappropriate and raises legal questions. The charge to the NAS from Congress was to convene a committee and finalize a report by July 1, 2001. The CAFE Committee completed its work and issued its report on July 31. The work of the CAFE Committee is over and there is no basis for the NRC to convene a 10th meeting of this Committee more than two months later. Moreover, it is highly improper for the NRC to effectively facilitate a critique, instigated by the auto industry. Any changes, beyond editorial changes, made to the report or issued in a supplemental report or statement will be unacceptable.

In our view, any separate memo or other form of statement by the NRC or the CAFE Committee on the topic of CAFE would constitute an improper extension of the study and will be considered part of the Report. In this regard, we believe that the NRC has already violated the integrity of its process through statements to the press and others since August 22.

The report issued on July 31, 2001 must not be opened. We strongly object to the October 5th meeting and request that the NRC cancel the meeting and that the report not be modified, even under the pretext of providing clarifications or reinterpretation.
