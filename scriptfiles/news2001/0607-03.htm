Better Late Than Never:
Administration Resumes Negotiations with North Korea
  WASHINGTON - June 7 - Chances for eliminating North Korea's development and export of ballistic missiles and controlling its nuclear program improved considerably yesterday after President Bush announced that the U.S. would resume wide-ranging negotiations with Pyongyang.

"The President made the right decision," said Steve LaMontagne, a specialist on nonproliferation at Council for a Livable World Education Fund. "Quiet diplomacy is the most effective and least costly way to stop nuclear and missile proliferation."

The announcement comes after three months of tense verbal sparring between North Korea and the new administration. In a major foreign policy misstep, President Bush told South Korean president Kim Dae Jung last March that the U.S. had no intention of resuming negotiations with Pyongyang anytime soon, only a day after Secretary of State Colin Powell told reporters that the U.S. would pick up where the Clinton administration left off in engaging North Korea.

In the ensuing weeks, North Korea threatened several times to end its voluntary moratorium on ballistic missile tests as well as to pull out of the 1994 Agreed Framework under which Pyongyang agreed to suspend its nuclear program in exchange for two light-water nuclear reactors and annual deliveries of heavy fuel oil.

With the resumption of negotiations, the U.S. may now be able to complete an agreement with Pyongyang that would limit its missile activities, which have been the main justification for quick deployment of a costly and controversial U.S. anti-missile system. If such an agreement is reached, the pressure for early deployment will diminish substantially. Negotiations will also provide a forum for the U.S. to address its concerns about the verification of nuclear and missile agreements.

"The administration may be starting to realize that there's not going to be any workable or affordable missile defense anytime soon," said John Isaacs, President of Council for a Livable World.

"The best option for dealing with North Korea is to talk to them," added Isaacs.
