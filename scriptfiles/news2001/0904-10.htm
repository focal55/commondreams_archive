Revelations on U.S. Biological Weapons Defense Programs Raise Questions
Experts Available for Comment
  WASHINGTON - September 4 - A report concerning secret U.S. germ weapons programs have raised serious concerns among biological weapons analysts about the possible negative effect of these programs on global efforts to stem the proliferation of germ weapons. Details on the programs, which the Clinton administration initiated and the Bush administration reportedly "embraced," were described in today's New York Times. The Bush administration recently rejected a proposal to strengthen the Biological Weapons Convention, which outlaws biological weapons, by adding a protocol aimed at making it harder to cheat on the accord.

While both Clinton and Bush administration officials contend the programs do not violate the treaty, which permits members to conduct research and work on defensive programs, the secrecy shrouding the programs could lead other countries to charge the United States with carrying out offensive weapons work under the guise of defending against such threats. Such allegations could undercut U.S. non-proliferation efforts. In addition, President Bill Clinton and his top advisers were reportedly not fully informed of the scope of the programs, raising serious questions about the oversight and accountability of these programs.
