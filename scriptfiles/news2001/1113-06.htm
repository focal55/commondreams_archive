Organizations Call for Halt to Bombing So Food Can Get Through
Local & National Call-In & E-Mail Campaign, Demonstrations Planned
   BOULDER, COLORADO - November 13 - Heeding the recent calls of national and international aid organizations, the Rocky Mountain Peace and Justice Center today urged Coloradans, and U.S. citizens in general, to press for a halt to the bombing of Afghanistan that would allow desperately needed food and medical supplies to get through.

Numerous relief agencies (including the United Nations' World Food Program, the International Red Cross, Oxfam, Christian Aid and Doctors Without Borders) have issued pleas to the U.S. government to help avert what the UN's Food and Agriculture Organization website calls "the threat of an impending mass starvation in the country [of Afghanistan]." Continuous bombing has created a situation so unstable that in October, despite their best efforts, these organizations were only able to deliver one quarter of the food necessary to stave off the impending starvation of 7.5 million. With a matter of days remaining before Afghanistan's extraordinarily harsh winter makes many roads impassable, social justice organizations are attempting to galvanize Americans to action regionally and nationally.

The RMPJC's information (see website) asks citizens to call or e-mail their senators and President Bush on Friday, November 16th, to demand a cessation of bombing that would allow neutral agencies to truck in food and medical supplies while roads are still navigable. Also planned are a 24-hour solidarity fast with meditation and prayer, from 1 p.m. on November 16th to 1 p.m. on November 17th, and silent vigils in Boulder, Denver and Fort Collins from 11 a.m. to 1 p.m. on November 17th. The RMPJC will hold a peaceful demonstration at Senators Allard and Campbell's offices early next week to further emphasize the urgency of the crisis.

"We believe that the people of the United States, no matter what their position on the war, don't want millions of people to die of starvation," said Judith Molling of Boulder's RMPJC. "I don't think most people in this country know how dire the situation is in parts of Afghanistan," added Danielle Short of Denver's American Friends Service Committee office. "If trucks [carrying food] can't get through within the next couple of weeks, it will be too late for millions of people."

Colorado volunteers have been working to share their concern with schools, churches and social justice organizations across the U.S. Said volunteer Mark Sweitzer "It's almost Thanksgiving, and we have so much. I keep thinking of how little many people in Afghanistan have to be thankful for."
