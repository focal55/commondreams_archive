Peace Activity Spreads Across US Campuses
Students Demonstrate for Peace and Justice on 150 Campuses
   September 20 - Teach-ins, vigils, and demonstrations were held on campuses across the country at noon today. The events emphasized the need for a cautious, reasoned and long-term solution to the terrorist attacks that will result in safety for all people. Anna Brogan of Eastern Michigan University cautions, "There has never been an effective military solution to terrorism. We question the wisdom of America seeking a military solution now."

The students and community members have concerns stemming from threats and hate based on race, ethnicity, and religion that have occurred since last Tuesday?s attacks as well as over the potential loss of civil liberties. The actions affirmed the sanctity of life by seeking peace and justice in the wake of Tuesday's tragedy. Students and campus groups are reclaiming their historical role as advocates for peace to initiate discussion nationally on protecting human life around the world, especially the safety of Arabs and Muslim communities domestically and internationally.

The activities offered sympathy and a strong sense of solidarity with the victims and families affected by the tragedy in New York, Washington, and Pennsylvania.

The events were spread across a diverse geographic range of campuses. Small schools as well as historically active and large campuses participated. Over eight thousand students and community members took part in the demonstration on the Berkeley campus that included a march from Sproul Plaza. Last night a sit in was organized protesting a racist cartoon against Arab and Muslim-Americans in a local paper. Nineteen people were arrested.

At the University of Kansas, students raised $25,000 in 48 hours for relief for the families and victims of the September 11th tragedy before holding two rallies, one on campus and one in the town square with community members in Lawrence. The University of Arkansas in Fayetteville had a teach-in for 600 people to learn more about Afghanistan. The students at the University of Wisconsin at Madison submitted a petition against a protracted military campaign to Congressional Representative Tammy Baldwin. "This was something that campuses across the whole nation could relate to," said Liz Bruna, a student from Madison. At Wesleyan College in Connecticut, where the idea for the national day originated, 500 students participated in what Mary Thomas called "a transition from mourning to action." After a vigil, students at Wesleyan also hand delivered a petition to their representative, Rob Simmons.
