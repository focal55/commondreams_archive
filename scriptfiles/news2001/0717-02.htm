Sierra Club Opposes Hansen Energy Bill's "Drill First, Ask Later"
Calls on House to Block Bad Legislation and Support Quicker, Cleaner Solutions
Statement by Melinda Pierce, Sierra Club Senior Washington Representative
   WASHINGTON - July 17 - "The Sierra Club opposes HR 2436, Representative James Hansen's (R-UT) bill being considered today in the House Resources Committee. This bill's 'drill first, ask later' approach would destroy precious landscapes by opening protected areas in Alaska to oil development, and setting the stage for damaging development on public lands across the country. Instead, sensible solutions to America's energy needs lie in investing in energy-efficient technologies, developing renewable resources like wind and solar power, building cleaner and more efficient power plants, and focusing on responsible production.

"In addition to the legislation's controversial provision to allow oil drilling in the Arctic Refuge, the Hansen bill will pillage our natural heritage by weakening important land protection measures in the West, and by considering some of our last pristine wild places for risky industrial development.

"We don't believe that we need to sacrifice national treasures like the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge to meet our country's energy needs. Drilling the Arctic Refuge can not and will not solve our country's energy woes but it will forever destroy one of our nation's last great wilderness areas.

"Government estimates predict less than a 6-month supply of oil may lie beneath the fragile landscape of the Arctic Refuge and even the oil industry itself admits that any oil discovered would take a decade to come on line. With 95 percent of Alaska's vast North Slope already available to oil and gas leasing, the Sierra Club calls for permanent protection for the coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge, which represents the last five percent that remains off-limits to drilling.

"We cannot drill our way out of the problem, no matter what this legislation and the Bush Administration claim. Our nation consumes 25 percent of the world's oil, yet we sit on less than three percent of the world's known oil reserves. To achieve true energy independence America needs a quicker, cleaner, cheaper and safer long-term energy strategy.

"The Bush Energy Plan and the Hansen Bill promote the wrong choices for America's energy future. We should not pillage our public lands for a short-term fix of oil. We need a long-term energy strategy based on conservation, renewable energy, and stronger efficiency standards such as making our cars and light-trucks go farther on a gallon of gas. Such a plan would save money, prevent pollution, and protect national treasures like the Arctic Refuge for future generations."
