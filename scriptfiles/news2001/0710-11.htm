The U.S. and Small Arms: Rhetoric vs. Reality
   WASHINGTON - July 10 - John Bolton, Under Secretary of State for Arms Control, gave the wrong speech to the UN small arms conference. The issue is not domestic U.S. gun control, but rather the transfer of small arms and light weapons across international borders. U.S. rhetoric glosses over weaknesses in U.S. policy while trumpeting dubious accomplishments in several areas, including:

End-use Monitoring Rhetoric: "To ensure that arms are delivered to legitimate end-users, our government rigorously monitors arms transfers, investigating suspicious activity and acting quickly to curtail exports to those recipients who do not meet our strict criteria for responsible use." - John Bolton

Reality: In FY 1999 the State Department's Blue Lantern end-use monitoring program conducted only 360 end-use checks on approximately 45,000 weapons export licenses valued at $46.8 billion. Thus, the U.S. conducts end-use checks on only .8% of its weapons export licenses. Furthermore, an August 2000 General Accounting Office audit found that "The [Pentagon] has not effectively implemented the requirement that its field personnel observe and report on foreign government's use of U.S. defense articles and services transferred though the Foreign Military Sales Program."

Controls on Arms Brokers Rhetoric: "Unchecked "rogue" brokers operating with impunity due to a lack of regulation are a major source of illicitly trafficked arms around the world. ... The U.S. prides itself as having one of the most comprehensive regimes governing international arms brokers in the world." - Ambassador Donald J. McConnell, Deputy Assistant Secretary of State

Reality: Under U.S. brokering law, in place since 1996, there has not been a single prosecution of an arms broker. According to a recent study by the Fund for Peace, U.S. brokering regulations are not being vigorously enforced despite clear evidence of criminal behavior. Shortfalls include: "understaffing and inadequate operating procedures, gaps in the exchange of information among US Government agencies, lack of transparency, enforcement problems related to extraterritorial jurisdiction and possible invocation of ignorance of the law."

Transparency Rhetoric: "The ["Section 655 report"] includes detailed information, compiled on a country-by- country basis, concerning the quantity and value of defense articles (including firearms and ammunition) properly authorized for export under the Arms Export Control Act." - U.S. Department of State Fact Sheet

Reality: The ‘655 report' is helpful, but it does not report the quantity and value of defense articles actually delivered to other countries pursuant to licenses issued by the State Department, nor the quantity and value of small arms and light weapons sold by the Pentagon's foreign military sales program. Moreover, the U.S. Congress is rarely, if ever, notified of small arms exports because they do not exceed the $14 million notification threshold.

If the U.S. delegation continues with its reckless and obscure comments about domestic U.S. gun ownership it will remain part of the scourge of the global proliferation of small arms and light weapons which are the weapons of choice in 46 of 49 conflicts since World War II.
