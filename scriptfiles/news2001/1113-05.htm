AFSC General secretary Visits North Korea
Observes Agricultural Methods and Impact of AFSC Aid
   PHILADELPHIA - November 13 - Mary Ellen McNish, general secretary of the American Friends Service Committee (AFSC), an international humanitarian and social justice organization, has returned from a visit to the Democratic Peoples Republic of Korea (DPRK) where she observed the impact of AFSC technical assistance on the country's recurring drought and food shortages. The trip was taken at the invitation of the Korean Committee for Solidarity with the World's People, a quasi-governmental agency, and included representatives from the Mennonite Central Committee (MCC) which supports the program financially.

"I was impressed by the hard work and dedication of the Korean farmers and scientists whom I met," said McNish. "They have suffered severe economic problems, but they are finding ways to cope and branching out in new directions."

AFSC has been working with North Korean farmers since 1997 to improve food production. Assistance is going to three large farms located in the western coastal plains, one of the major rice growing regions which feeds the country. The three cooperatives have a combined population of about 16,500 people and farm 14,000 acres. Fertilizer contributions and other inputs from AFSC have helped the farms raise production by about 30% compared to their 1998 yields. Due to this assistance, the farms have maintained a reasonable level of production even in the face of adverse weather this past year.

"Over the past five years we've developed good relationships with the farm managers," stated McNish. "It's very important that we in the U.S. learn about the day-to-day lives of ordinary people in countries which we may tend to regard as enemies. This is where peace is made - working together on practical problems at the grassroots level and improving people's livelihoods."

AFSC has also hosted six study delegations to US universities for DPRK agricultural researchers since 1998, including two in 2001. The subject matter of the delegations has ranged from rice and corn breeding to improved efficiency in animal production.

McNish explained, "Delegations promote a people-to-people relationship, and offer an opportunity to reach across the barriers that have kept North Koreans and Americans apart for many decades. It's important to keep informal channels of communication open. The situation is complex, but the officials with whom we met in North Korea clearly expressed a desire for improved relations with the U.S."

Assistance to the three farms for the 2000-2001 cropping season included 800 tons of fertilizer, nearly a half million square meters of clear plastic for rice, corn and vegetable cold beds, and smaller amounts of improved seed, herbicides, and basic equipment. AFSC has been working on the Korean Peninsula since the 1950s when the AFSC helped South Koreans rebuild houses destroyed by war and worked in medical projects for war refugees. The first AFSC delegation visited North Korea in 1980. Founded in 1917 to provide conscientious objectors with an opportunity to aid civilian victims during World War I, AFSC is grounded in Quaker beliefs respecting the dignity and worth of every person. AFSC has programs in the United States, and in Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East that focus on issues related to economic justice, peace-building and demilitarization, social justice, and youth. In 1947, the AFSC and the British Friends Service Council received the Nobel Peace Prize on behalf of the Religious Society of Friends, for humanitarian service, work for reconciliation, and the spirit in which these were carried out. Additional information about the American Friends Service Committee can be found on the web site www.afsc.org.

The American Friends Service Committee is a Quaker organization that includes people of various faiths who are committed to social justice, peace and humanitarian service. Its work is based on the belief in the worth of every person and faith in the power of love to overcome violence and injustice.
