Clean Air Trust Assails DOE Diesel Report; Report Appears Aimed at Helping Oil Industry
  WASHINGTON - May 7 - The nonprofit Clean Air Trust today assailed a new diesel fuel report by the Energy Information Administration (EIA), a branch of the U.S. Department of Energy.

"This report uses selective information in an obvious attempt to help the oil industry undermine EPA's new diesel fuel standards," said Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust.

"It raises real doubts about the Bush Administration's intentions about the clean diesel standards," O'Donnell added. "Will this lead to yet another flip-flop?" he asked, noting the President used an earlier EIA study to justify a flip-flop on carbon dioxide controls.

The diesel fuel standards were announced late last year by the Clinton Administration. They were embraced earlier this year by EPA Administrator Christie Whitman and recently cited by President Bush as evidence of his positive approach to environmental protection.

The standards would require low-sulfur diesel fuel for highway vehicles by 2006. Low-sulfur fuel is needed to achieve much cleaner diesel trucks and buses because the sulfur poisons pollution control devices. EPA estimates the standards would prevent 8,300 premature deaths a year and that it would produce $70 billion in annual benefits by reducing exposure to smog, soot and toxic air contaminants.

The American Petroleum Institute and the National Petrochemical and Refiners Association have filed suit against the rules, claiming they could cost the oil industry too much money and lead to fuel shortages.

"These industry opponents of the standards appear to be the primary resources for the new EIA study," said O'Donnell. The study parrots their complaints about the standards, he said.

The study appears to have ignored those companies, including BP Amoco and Tosco, that have supported the diesel cleanup, O'Donnell noted.

O'Donnell added that EIA "gave short shrift to new technologies that will dramatically reduce cleanup costs." Several new, lower-cost methods were unveiled in March at a refiners' meeting. For example, a new process by SulphCo, Inc. of Nevada could remove the sulfur from diesel fuel for a mere 2.5 cents per gallon -- only half the cost estimated by EPA and far below the even-higher costs projected by doomsday-sayers in the oil industry.

The EIA study was requested last summer by key members of the House Science Committee, "who were obviously biased against the cleanup," said O'Donnell. He noted those members, led by Rep. James Sensenbrenner (R-Wis.), told EIA that they believed EPA had not adequately studied the potential impacts of the diesel cleanup.

"Once again, it appears that EIA is being used as a tool by those who seek to portray environmental protection as an obstacle to energy production," said O'Donnell. He noted that Vice President Cheney relied on an earlier EIA report in his recent call for a massive expansion of energy production facilities.
