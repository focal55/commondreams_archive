Americans United Praises House Vote on Private School Vouchers
Armey Voucher Crusade Brought to a Screeching Halt, Says SU's Lynn
   WASHINGTON - May 23 - Today's floor votes on private religious school vouchers represent a major setback for the voucher movement, according to Americans United for Separation of Church and State.

In two separate votes, members of the House of Representatives overwhelmingly rejected voucher amendments offered by House Majority Leader Dick Armey (R-Texas). The proposals were amendments to H.R. 1, the Bush administration's education bill.

Americans United said Armey's drive to add voucher language to the House education package flew in the face of the will of the people.

"The only place vouchers are popular is in Dick Armey's office," said AU Executive Director Barry W. Lynn. "House members showed great wisdom today by following the lead of the American people and rejecting Armey's scheme."

The first amendment, which would have created a massive national voucher program for so-called "failing schools," lost by a 273 to 155 vote. The second, which sought to create five pilot voucher projects, was also defeated, 241 to 186.

Armey fought for vouchers, arguing his plan is necessary for families in need. At a press conference yesterday, Armey described his voucher amendments as "amendments with a heart."

AU's Lynn believes Armey's efforts are both unwelcome and unpopular with the audience the congressman is targeting.

"Armey claims the American people, especially poor families in our nation's inner cities, want a school voucher program," Lynn added. "But the evidence proves just the opposite."

In referenda in California and Michigan last November, voters from all racial, religious and socio-economic groups rejected school voucher plans by huge margins. California voters opposed Prop. 38, a sweeping voucher scheme, by a 71 to 29 percent margin. Meanwhile, Michigan voters turned down Proposal 1, a more limited voucher plan, by a 69 to 31 percent margin.

According to exit polls conducted by The Los Angeles Times and the Detroit News:

* African-Americans opposed vouchers by a 68-32 percent margin in California. Blacks in Michigan rejected vouchers by nearly four to one, with Detroit voters saying no by a staggering 72-28 percent margin. Whites, Latinos and Asian-Americans also voted against vouchers by large margins.

* Low-income voters were staunchly opposed to vouchers. In California, those with a family income of $20,000 or less voted against Prop. 38 (73-27 percent) at about the same rate as those who made $75,000 or more (70-30 percent).

* Republicans, Democrats and Independents all voted against vouchers. In the California referendum, 83 percent of Democrats, 72 percent of independents and 53 percent of Republicans opposed vouchers. In Michigan, all political camps voted against the scheme, except those who identified themselves as "strong" Republicans.

* Parents of public school students in Michigan voted by more than two to one against vouchers. In California, 68 percent of parents opposed Prop. 38.

* Seventy-eight percent of Americans prefer fixing public schools while only 16 percent want to move to a voucher system, according to a national exit poll of voters reported by The Washington Post last November.

The California and Michigan results are consistent with years of results in other states. In 22 referenda since 1967, voters have rejected ballot proposals to direct tax aid to religious and other private schools.

Said Americans United's Lynn, "The people have spoken, Rep. Armey. You need to give up this misguided voucher crusade and focus on improving our public school system."

Americans United is a religious liberty watchdog group based in Washington, D.C. Founded in 1947, the organization represents 60,000 members and allied houses of worship in all 50 states.
