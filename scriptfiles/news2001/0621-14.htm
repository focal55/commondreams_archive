Electronics Industry Recycling Progam Passes the Buck
Consumers and Taxpayers, not the companies, paying the cost
   SAN JOSE, CA - June 21 - Amidst the flurry and media attention on the growing electronics waste problem, the Electronics Industry Alliance recently unveiled a pilot project for electronics recycling, but is passing some costs of the program onto their customers and taxpayers.

The pilot project was announced a few days after the release of a report "Poison PCs/Toxic TVs: California's biggest environmental problem you've never heard of." The report, released by the Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition, Californians Against Waste and Materials for the Future, details the growing piles of electronics waste in the US, the toxics contained in computers and monitors, and the hazards of improper disposal. The report estimates a cost to California taxpayers of almost $1 billion for handling e-wastes that consumers and businesses will throw away. The report is available at the Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition website: http://www.svtc.org

"We're sitting on top of a gigantic wasteberg - and in order to find solutions, the manufacturers of computers must take responsibility for their products," said Ted Smith, Executive Director of the Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition, one of the lead authors of the new report. "They need to re-design their products to phase out the toxics materials and make computers and monitors recyclable.

"We are pleased to see the industy's initiative, but their program falls short of full producer responsibility and they are passing the buck and the costs of their program on to consumers and the local taxpayers," said Smith. "A recycling program should include: collection, reuse and recycling of all raw materials, done at no extra cost to consumers or taxpayers at the time of disposal. After paying over a thousand dollars to purchase the system, it is difficult for consumers to justify the extra fee to get rid of the product," said Smith.

The pilot project comes at a much needed time," said Smith. "Companies can study the options for a year, but until they look at re-design issues, the e-waste piles will continue to grow. Disposal options are being cut off because this equipment is hazardous waste. That is why it is all the more important for producers to embrace extended producer responsiblity for their products and not pass on the the costs to either consumers or taxpayers."

The Department of Toxics Substances Control, which is the California EPA department that deals with hazardous waste disposal, announced this March that products like computer monitors and televisions cannot be disposed of in municipal landfills.

Press conferences were held this week in several California cities and resolutions are being drafted urging computer and other electronics producer to take responsiblity for reuse and recycling of their electronics products and not solve the problem by passing on the costs to local taxpayers.
