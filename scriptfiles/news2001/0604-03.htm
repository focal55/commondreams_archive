Sierra Club Exhorts President Bush to Protect Our Special Places
Volunteers Protest President's Flip Flop On Florida Coast Oil Exploration and Review of Homestead Air Force Base Decision
   EVERGLADES NATIONAL PARK - June 4 - Sierra Club activists rallied at President Bush's photo opportunity outside the Everglades National Park today, calling on President Bush to stop his plans to exploit and destroy fragile, coastal areas, national wildlife refuges, and other wild lands for oil and gas exploration. Sierra Club members also chastised the Bush administration for calling for a review of the ban on building a commercial airport at the Homestead Air Force Base. The Air Force decided against allowing Homestead to be converted into a commercial airport in January. The Sierra Club will also run a print advertisement on Tuesday, asking President Bush to keep his promise not to drill off Florida's Gulf Coast.

"Americans don't want oil drills looming off of our beaches or roaring jets polluting our national parks," said Carl Pope, executive director of the Sierra Club. "Instead of trying to use our national parks as green camouflage, it would be more honest if President Bush was standing in front of an off-shore drilling rig."

According to the May 25, 2001 Chicago Tribune, President Bush favors drilling off the coast of Florida in Lease Sale 181, in the Gulf of Mexico. While campaigning at the Florida Republican Party Convention in October 1999, President Bush promised to work with Governor Jeb Bush to support an offshore drilling ban in Florida. Now, he appears to be bowing under pressure from the oil industry and is clearly out of step with Floridians and all Americans who do not want our fragile, coastal areas spoiled by oil spills and industrial pollution. In addition, President Bush is considering oil and gas development in wildlife refuges, national monuments and other public lands.

"We can meet our energy needs without ruining our favorite beaches and coastlines," continued Pope. "President Bush's energy plan takes a drill, dig and destroy approach to finding fossil fuel. Instead, Americans want a balanced approach that gives us quicker, cleaner, cheaper and safer solutions, like energy-efficient technologies, renewable power like solar and wind, and responsible production."

In just a few short months, the Bush Administration has advocated drilling for oil in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge and other special places. They have also opened the door to logging in our last wild National Forests, delayed regulations to clean up pollution from hard rock mining in public lands, and proposed undercutting citizen involvement in the nation's premiere wildlife habitat protection law--the Endangered Species Act. The Bush Budget emphasizes mineral development for the coal, oil and gas industries at the expense of sound land management programs.
