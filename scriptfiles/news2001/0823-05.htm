VPC Urges Federal Judge To Uphold Montgomery County Law Stopping Use Of Public Funds To Pay For Gun Shows
   WASHINGTON - August 22 - The Violence Policy Center (VPC) has joined Montgomery County, Md., Maryland Attorney General Joseph Curran, and Marylanders Against Handgun Abuse (MAHA), to support the County's decision to stop subsidizing gun sales at gun shows with public funds. Joined by MAHA, the VPC filed a friend-of-the-court brief in federal district court in Baltimore, in support of the County's new ordinance. Passed by the County Council on May 16, 2001, and signed into law on May 29, 2001, the ordinance prohibits facilities that host gun shows at which guns are sold from receiving public funding. The Montgomery County Agricultural Center, site of the County fair, is one such facility, and gun show promoter Frank Krasner sued to stop the County gun show law from going into effect.

Drawing upon the VPC's pioneering study, Gun Shows in America Tupperware(r) Parties for Criminals, which brought national attention to the gun show phenomenon, the VPC/MAHA brief outlines the facts about gun shows, which too often provide a convenient location for criminals, children, and gun traffickers to get guns. In 2000, there were more than 4,000 gun shows in the United States and 21 gun shows in Maryland. At the Montgomery County gun shows, for example, certain types of 50 caliber sniper rifles, which can shoot down helicopters, and certain types of assault weapons can be sold without Brady background checks by unlicensed sellers to anyone, no questions asked.

"Montgomery County should ensure that the public's hard-earned dollars are not used to pay for gun shows where guns are sold," said Mathew Nosanchuk, VPC litigation director and legislative counsel. "All too often, gun shows provide a convenient means for felons, fugitives, and children to get guns. There is no reason that County residents should be forced to subsidize this kind of potentially dangerous activity in their own backyards."

In the friend-of-the-court brief filed today, the VPC soundly refutes the plaintiffs' claim that a restriction on County funding for gun shows where guns are sold somehow violates the First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. Said Nosanchuk, "selling guns at gun shows is not political speech, it's not protected commercial speech, and it's certainly not protected assembly. It's selling guns, and the County has every right to stop paying for it directly or indirectly."

The VPC and MAHA are represented in this case by Wilmer, Cutler & Pickering in Washington, D.C. Attorneys Randolph Moss, Stuart Delery, David Molot, Rachael Hill, and Laura Fein prepared the brief.

The Violence Policy Center is a national non-profit educational organization working to reduce gun death and injury in America.
