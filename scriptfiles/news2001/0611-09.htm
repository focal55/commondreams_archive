Victims of Drug War on Campus Adding Up
60,000 Expected to Lose Financial Aid
Educators Speak Out Against New Law – Students Organize on 140 Campuses
   WASHINGTON - June 11 - Students, educators, financial aid administrators and members of congress are asking newspaper editors to make the case for repealing the 1998 Higher Education Act drug provision. According to new Department of Education data, the number of students at risk of losing financial aid in the 2001 – 2002 year due to past misdemeanor or felony drug convictions has increased dramatically. The Coalition for Higher Education Act Reform says that new data confirms their predictions, made last year, that strict enforcement of the 1998 HEA drug provision would result in loss of aid by tens of thousands of students.

In a recent letter sent to new Drug Enforcement Administration head Asa Hutchinson, 13 leading education associations representing admissions officers, community and state colleges, financial aid administrators and student groups, called the 1998 Higher Education Act drug provision a “fundamentally flawed piece of legislation.” Students for Sensible Drug Policy has grown to 140 chapters on college campuses and have launched a major campaign (http://www.raiseyourvoice.com) to pressure members of congress to support the repeal measure. On campus, 69 student governments have passed resolutions in support of repealing the HEA drug provision.

Here’s what some editors are saying about the Higher Education Act drug provision.

“Students who’ve already paid the criminal penalties for drug convictions – through drug treatment, prohibition, community service or time in jail – are being made to pay again through the loss of college aid. That prevents them from following a productive path that may be the best insurance against recidivism. And the hammer falls unequally on college students, putting up a barrier to higher education for convicted students who are poor and need financial aid that doesn’t exist for convicted students who are better off.” – The Fresno Bee, May 27, 2001

“It’s a merciless crackdown with the worst possible motives. People go to college to improve their lives, as positive s step as anyone can take. It requires commitment, and, for many who’ve never gone that route, a great deal of courage. For those who’ve previously been arrested for whatever reason, college can be the turnaround of their life. Now the government is singling out some of those people and telling them not to bother.” – The Gainesville Sun, April 29, 2001

“This amounts to an additional penalty for drug use, applied only to those of modest enough income to need federal help. Though Rep. Souder said his intent was to apply this sanction only to students already receiving aid, the law has been interpreted to cover first-time applicants too. So this provision could have the effect of cutting off students who are trying to overcome past troubles with drugs and pursue the education that will help them get ahead. They ought to get help, not hindrance.” – The Washington Post, April 26, 2001.

“Such uneven – and unfair – enforcement is a predictable consequence of lawmakers’ attempt to use federal financial aid to engineer student behavior.” – USA Today, June 13, 2000.
