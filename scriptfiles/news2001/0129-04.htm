Clean Air and Energy -- the Big Myth and the Reality
  WASHINGTON - January 29 - The drumbeat began Jan. 18, when President-designate George W. Bush put much of the blame for California's energy problems on clean air standards: "If there's any environmental regulations that's preventing California from having a 100 percent max output at their plants -- as I understand there may be -- then we need to relax those regulations," he told CNN.

Within days, that myth began spreading like the flu -- almost as if it were a coordinated campaign:

-- Bush spokesman Ari Fleischer said easing clean-air standards "is an option" to address Californa's troubles. The President's chief economic adviser, Larry Lindsey, repeated that message.

-- Rep. Billy Tauzin (R-La.), new chairman of the House Energy and Commerce Committee, asserted that clean air standards were constricting operation of existing power plants in California. A Tauzin spokesman piled on: "The clean air laws have created part of the problem in California," he said.

-- Rep. Joe Barton (R-Texas), new chairman of the House Subcommittee on Energy and Air Quality, will examine the California situation. "Some of the (national clean air health) standards may be too tight," he told the Ft. Worth Star-Telegram.

-- The Senate Energy and Natural Resources Committee has scheduled a hearing on California Jan. 31 with "clean air rules in the crosshairs," according to one published report.

-- Rep. Dana Rohrabacher (R-Calif.) asserted in a letter to his congressional colleagues that "environmental extremism" was "almost exclusively" the cause of California's problem.

-- The National Petrochemical and Refiners Association jumped on the bandwagon when it announced it would sue to overturn EPA's new diesel clean-up standards: "We can see today in the California electricity market what happens when policymakers take continued supplies of energy for granted."

-- And the auto industry -- in a failed attempt to persuade California to repeal its zero-emission vehicle program -- made the shameless argument that vehicle requirements would have a measurable impact on California's energy supply.

In other words, California has become a poster child for demagogues who would seek to roll back clean air protections.

The Facts Give a Lie to the Myth

It's time to give this mythology a breather and look at the facts:

-- About myth number one (the President's assertion that clean air restrictions are preventing California plants from operating): I would commend to everyone's attention an excellent article in the Jan. 25 Los Angeles Times, which notes that "power plants throughout California are running around the clock, cranking out as many megawatts as possible to ward off blackouts." The sole exception is a small utility run by the city of Glendale -- and it is supposed to be running at full production within a matter of days. Sorry, Mr. President. Time to hire some better-informed advisers.

-- About myth number two (the insinuation that the Clean Air Act had blocked construction of new electric power plants in California): This is flat out false. The law does require, in areas such as Los Angeles that are in violation of national clean air health standards, that increased pollution from major new sources be offset by reductions from existing pollution sources. But, according to the California Energy Commission, no electric power plant in California has been rejected for lack of offsets. In other words, the Clean Air Act has not blocked construction of a single plant in California.

Some Key Reasons for the Power Shortage

If not clean air, what has caused the California problem? The real reason, of course, is the disparity between electricity supply and demand. There are a number of factors for this gap, but here are some keys (let's leave aside the unusually dry weather that restricted hydroelectric power in the Pacific Northwest, and the charges that energy suppliers are manipulating supplies, OPEC-style, to drive up prices and gouge consumers):

-- In the early 1990s, California had more than enough electricity to meet demand. Indeed, during the state's severe recession, there was an oversupply.

-- In the mid-1990s, when California began considering complicated and controversial electricity deregulation, potential electricity suppliers decided to wait and see how it would play out before seeking to build new plants. Statistics from the California Energy Commission document this phenomenon (check http://www.energy.ca.gov): Virtually no one even applied for a permit to build a new plant in the mid-1990s.

-- California's local power companies exploited this uncertainty. In 1995, the state's major electric utilities, including Southern California Edison and San Diego Gas & Electric, helped kill a state plan that would have authorized creation of new power plants sufficient to power 1.4 million homes, according to an investigative report published in the Jan. 21 Los Angeles Daily News. "The utilities argued that they could buy plenty of cheaper power elsewhere -- an assertion that appears to be the epitome of shortsightedness," the Daily News reported. "Edison does not foresee a need to add any new capacity resources until 2005 at the earliest," Southern California Edison said in a 1995 filing to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. The power companies were afraid that they might have to buy -- heaven forbid -- pollution-free wind power from independent producers.

-- Once the deregulation issue was hashed out, energy companies once again began to seek -- and get -- permits. According to the Energy Commission, "Restructuring occurred in March 1998. Since April 1999, the Energy Commission has approved nine major power plant projects with a combined electric generation capacity of 6,278 megawatts." Fourteen additional projects are currently under commission review. It is worth re-starting: clean air controls did not block any of these projects.

-- It is fair to note that some proposed plants -- even those planned with advanced, super-clean technology -- face tough scrutiny from nearly local communities. But that "not-in-my-backyard" sentiment is not unique to California nor is it a product of the Clean Air Act.

-- As for electricity demand? Of course, it has grown by leaps and bounds since California's economy recovered from the recession. The Internet revolution has obviously accelerated demand far beyond anything envisioned in the early 1990s. But supply is starting to catch up.

Lessons Learned?

What are the lessons learned from this episode? Beware when a politician -- even a soon-to-be President -- tries making clean air a scapegoat for problems related to energy or the economy. Ditto for a similar claim from a big polluter. And check the facts before succumbing to mythology.
