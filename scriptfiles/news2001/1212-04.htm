ABM Treaty Withdrawal Neither Necessary Nor Prudent, Says Arms Control Association
Press Conference, Dec. 13
  

WASHINGTON - December 12 -

WHAT: Arms Control Association Press Conference

WHO:
-- Joe Cirincione, director, Non-Proliferation Project, Carnegie Endowment for International Peace
-- John Rhinelander, former legal advisor to the ABM Treaty/SALT I delegation
-- Lisbeth Gronlund, senior scientist, Union of Concerned Scientists

WHEN: Dec. 13, 9-10:30 a.m.

WHERE: Choate Room, Carnegie Endowment for International Peace,
1779 Massachusetts Avenue NW, Washington, D.C.

BACKGROUND: President Bush will, within days, reportedly announce the United States' intention to unilaterally withdraw from the Anti-Ballistic Missile (ABM) Treaty, which could negatively affect U.S. relations with its allies, China, and Russia. Moscow has warned it may withdraw from other arms control agreements in response to a unilateral U.S. withdrawal from the ABM Treaty.

Russian President Vladimir Putin opposes scrapping the 1972 ABM Treaty, which he has said Russia would be willing to amend to accommodate U.S. missile defense testing. However, U.S. officials have refused to offer or even discuss amending the treaty.

Congressional leaders and key U.S. allies have also questioned the wisdom of abandoning the ABM Treaty, which could put at risk agreements to reduce and eliminate the threats posed by weapons of mass destruction and undermine the current war against terrorism. They have also questioned the value of rushing ahead with unproven national missile defenses, which do nothing to guard against more likely means of attack using weapons of mass destruction.

Several uncertainties and questions exist regarding a unilateral U.S. withdrawal:

-- How might unilateral withdrawal from the ABM Treaty influence our ability to persuade Russia, China and other states to help curb the spread of nuclear weapons in the years ahead?
-- How might the absence of the ABM Treaty affect efforts to verifiably reduce the substantial stockpiles of offensive nuclear arsenals in Russia and the United States? How will China respond?
-- How might Presidents Bush and Putin, before U.S. withdrawal from the ABM Treaty, be able to reach an understanding to allow more robust national missile defense testing while maintaining the basic framework of the ABM Treaty?
-- Does the ABM Treaty really hinder testing of U.S. strategic missile defenses?

The following experts will address these and other questions:

-- Joe Cirincione, director, Non-Proliferation Project, Carnegie Endowment for International Peace
-- John Rhinelander, former legal advisor to the ABM Treaty/SALT I delegation
-- Lisbeth Gronlund, senior scientist, Union of Concerned Scientists
-- Daryl Kimball, executive director, Arms Control Association
