Court Date Delayed for Greenpeace Activists, Freelance Journalists
Trial For Stop Star Wars 17 Will Begin January 8, 2002
   LOS ANGELES - November 8 - A Los Angeles Federal court agreed yesterday to delay the November 20 trial date of 15 Greenpeace activists and two freelance journalists facing felony charges arising from a peaceful protest against the proposed missile defense agenda. The defense lawyers asked for a continuance of the court date during a hearing on November 5 because of the political climate in the United States following the September 11 attacks on the World Trade Center. The trial is now scheduled to begin on January 8, 2002.

The charges relate to a peaceful protest at Vandenberg Airforce Base California against the last test of the Star Wars missile system on July 14, 2001. The test was delayed for forty minutes.

The activists facing trial are from Australia, Canada, Germany, India New Zealand, Sweden, the UK and the United States. The journalists are from the UK and Spain.

During the hearing on November 5, an additional Greenpeace activist, Henk Haazen, joined the case by pleading activist pleaded not guilty to the charges. The New Zealand based Greenpeace activist will now face trial alongside the other defendants. Haazen flew from Auckland to Los Angeles and voluntarily turned himself in to the FBI on October 31 after he was named in a superseding indictment. He was granted bail and released from custody the following day. He will remain in the central district of California until the trial.

Both the activists and the freelance journalists who were documenting the protest face the following charges:

  • Conspiring to willfully and knowingly violate an order and direction from a Captain of the Port of the U.S. Coast Guard regarding a safety zone, knowingly enter Vandenberg Air Force Base without permission, and aiding and abetting each other, a Class D felony with a maximum six year prison term and $250,000 fine.

  • Willfully and knowingly violating an order and direction of a Captain of the Port of the U.S. Coast Guard, a Class D felony with a maximum six-year prison term and $250,000 fine.

  • Knowingly entering Vandenberg Air Force Base without permission, a misdemeanor with a maximum sentence of six months incarceration and a $5,000 fine.

    If found guilty on all counts, the sentences are likely to be served concurrently. All defendants have pleaded not guilty to the charges. It is the first time in history that peaceful protestors at Vandenberg Air Force Base have been charged with felonies.
