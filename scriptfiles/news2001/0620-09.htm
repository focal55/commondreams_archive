Sierra Club Attacks Sec. of Interior's Attack on Grizzly Bear Reintroduction
   SAN FRANCISCO - June 20 - The Sierra Club reacted angrily today to news that Secretary of Interior Gale Norton had proposed to withdraw a program that would reintroduce grizzly bears into their natural habitat in Idaho and Montana. The reintroduction program was established last year by the US Fish and Wildlife Service to help the recovery of this endangered species.

"The Secretary of the Interior is continuing to make it clear that in her vision for our public lands there is very little room for wildlife, but ample space for oil wells," said Carl Pope, Executive Director of the Sierra Club. "This is yet another in a series of decisions which give priority to the oil and timber industry, mining companies and polluters, at the expense of wildlife, recreation, and the broader values of the American people."

Today, there are fewer than 1,000 grizzlies, less than 1 percent of their original population, in the lower 48 states. The plan involved introducing five bears into more than 5700 acres of the Bitterroot Mountains in Idaho and Montana each year for five consecutive years, starting in 2002. Scientists anticipate that with this program the grizzly population would have increased by 300 bears in one hundred years.

"We are extremely disappointed that the Bush Administration is dismantling a program that aims to protect the grizzly bear species," Pope said. "This is the time when we should conserve grizzly habitat and preserve the species, not cater to the needs of oil and timber companies."

Timber cutting and road building displace bears and fragment their habitat. It means reduced cover, reduced food sources and increased interaction with humans. Oil and gas development further jeopardizes the grizzly's future. The web of pipelines, powerlines, roads and well pads makes habitat unusable to grizzlies. Over 200 oil and gas wells have been drilled in the Greater Yellowstone area and President Bush's energy plan urges more oil wells to be built in the region.
