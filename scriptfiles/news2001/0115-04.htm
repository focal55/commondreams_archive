Leading Environmentalists Urge Senate to Oppose Ashcroft
   WASHINGTON - January 15 - The following was released today by Friends of the Earth:

-- Testimony of Dr. Brent Blackwelder, president, Friends of the Earth, Before the Senate Committee on the Judiciary on the Nomination of Senator John Ashcroft for United States Attorney General

January 12, 2001

Friends of the Earth is a global environmental advocacy organization with offices in 54 countries. On their behalf, and on behalf of our thousands of members throughout the United States, I appreciate the opportunity to submit testimony on the nomination of former Senator John Ashcroft as United States Attorney General. Friends of the Earth strongly opposes confirmation of John Ashcroft as United States Attorney General because of potential harm to both the environment and the federal laws designed to protect it.

The Attorney General, as head of the Department of Justice (DOJ) and chief law enforcement officer of the federal government, has sweeping discretionary authority to uphold and defend our nation's environmental laws. DOJ's Environment and Natural Resources Division (ENRD), in particular, has the responsibility to engage in litigation concerning the protection, use and development of the nation's natural resources and public lands, wildlife protection, cleanup of hazardous waste sites, and defense of environmental challenges to government programs and activities.

The Energy and Natural Resources Division of the Department of Justice currently harbors 691 authorized positions and operates on an annual budget of about $91.5 million -- 88 percent growth from 1989. From 1994-1997, U.S Attorneys initiated investigations of an average of 946 individuals and organizations suspected of an environmental offense. Of those, an average of 462 was charged with criminal violations. Coupled with a workload that includes an average of 211 civil violations and over 600 citizen suits filed, ENRD is vested with the considerable task of serving as "the nation's environmental lawyer, and the largest environmental law firm in the country."

As a senator for the state of Missouri, John Ashcroft had a consistent record of promoting the interests of polluters over citizens' rights and supporting attempts to weaken or roll back federal environmental protections. To place the discretion of enforcement and the responsibility of leading the nation's largest environmental law firm squarely in the hands of someone with a long record of opposing the laws he will be sworn to uphold would be a grave mistake.

John Ashcroft's anti-environmental record is clear. The following are but a few examples from a career of flagrant opposition to environmental protection:

Derailing Environmental Protection Laws

-- In the 106th Congress, Senator Ashcroft was one of only six senators to cosponsor a bill that would have drastically weakened the Clean Air Act by taking away one of the most critical enforcement tools for states that fail to meet clean air requirements -- sanctions on federal highway aid (see: S. 495, To amend the Clean Air Act to repeal the highway sanctions, 106th Congress, 1st Session). Senator Ashcroft's hard stance on the Clean Air Act comes at a time when industries are challenging current soot and smog standards in the U.S. Supreme Court. With a growing number of regions that have chronically dangerous smog levels, the importance of protecting the Clean Air Act for public health and the environment is apparent.

-- In the 105th Congress, Senator Ashcroft voted against a filibuster of a bill that would have weakened land use protections by amending most federal environmental laws and allowing polluters to newly challenge federal environmental protections. The bill also would have overridden existing local procedures to allow developers to challenge city and county zoning laws directly in federal courts instead of existing local administrative appeals and state courts (see: Roll Call Vote No. 197, July 13, 1998). So called "takings" bills such as this would undermine local control over land uses in the name of protecting the property rights of the few, and have been opposed environmental groups, the federal and state judiciary, and forty-one state attorneys general, among others.

-- Also in the 104th Congress, Senator Ashcroft voted to build on the infamous "salvaged timber rider" by extending indefinitely the time for old growth timber sales directed by this provision. The rider, attached to the 1995 Budget Rescissions Act, allows timber companies to purchase and log parcels in our national forests without regard to federal environmental laws (see: Roll Call Vote No. 33, March 14, 1996). These riders have had the effect of ravaging old growth and healthy trees under the guise of "salvaging" dead or diseased timber while destroying wildlife habitat and degrading water quality in streams and rivers.

Protecting Polluters over People

-- In 1995, Senator Ashcroft defended a sweeping bill (S. 343, the Comprehensive Regulatory Reform Act of 1995) that would have undercut legal safeguards for environmental, human health, consumer and labor protections (see: Roll Call Vote No. 315, July 20). This bill, and other so-called "regulatory reform" bills like it, would create avenues of legal maneuvering for businesses to eliminate, stall, or revise safeguards. Such proposals that are clearly designed for the sake of industries would also leave the public with no recourse against abusers.

-- During debate of that same bill, Senator Ashcroft voted to defend a provision that would allow polluting industries to avoid reporting most of their toxic chemical releases as required by the 1986 Community Right to Know Act (see: Roll Call Vote No. 306, July 13, 1995). The annual Toxic Release Inventory (TRI) listing, as it is called, has enabled communities to track the operating performances of local facilities and given these communities more power to hold companies accountable and make informed decisions about how toxic chemicals are to be managed.

-- Recently, Senator Ashcroft voted to protect the mining industry by attempting to block a rule that would require hardrock mining companies to cover potential cleanup costs through bonds or insurance deposits and hold mining companies accountable to strong environmental performance standards (see: Roll Call Vote No. 224, July 20, 2000). The rule, known as the "3809 rule" (43 CFR 3809), was finally published this year after a long battle. It will serve to protect the public by forcing one of the nation's largest toxic polluters to act more responsibly.

As this sampling of votes shows, former Senator John Ashcroft consistently sought to undermine or gut the very laws that help to protect fragile ecosystems and safeguard human health. If our environment is to survive, we need someone who will actively and effectively prosecute those that harm it. John Ashcroft's record indicates that he would do otherwise.

In summary, the position of Attorney General has substantial discretion regarding how to address the diverse topics that the United States Department of Justice defends and prosecutes. Based on his record, Friends of the Earth sees no indication that John Ashcroft will use this discretion for anything other than the outright appeasement of polluting industries and weakening of our nation's environmental protection laws. As such, Friends of the Earth urges you to oppose his nomination.  

Brent Blackwelder
President
Friends of the Earth
