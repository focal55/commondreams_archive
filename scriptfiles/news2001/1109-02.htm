Bush-Putin Summit: Key Facts about Arms Control
Key questions on nuclear reductions agreement
  

WASHINGTON - November 9 - Do Russia and the U.S. come to a written agreement submitted to Congress or a statement of intention? What verification or transparency procedures have been included? Will the retired nuclear weapons be dismantled so neither side can reverse the cuts? Will the remaining nuclear weapons be taken off alert to reduce the risk of accidental launch?

Nuclear weapons for each country

United States: 7,206 strategic nuclear weapons, 10,500 all nuclear weapons
Russia : 5,826 strategic nuclear weapons, 20,000 all nuclear weapons
Also: 850 metric tons of weapons-usable nuclear material in Russia in need of better security.

Previous arms control agreements

ABM Treaty (1972): limits each side to one anti-missile site; allows testing of ground-based system.
Start I (1991) limits each side to 6,000 nuclear warheads
Start II (1993) limits each side to 3,000 - 3,500 warheads; never entered into force)
Start III would limit each side to 2,000 - 2,500 warheads (1997 Clinton-Yeltsin framework agreement)

U.S. spending on missile defense

$8.3. billion requested for 2002, an increase of $3 billion from last year
$148 billion since the late 50's with few results; includes $84.9 billion since Reagan's Star Wars.

U.S. spending on reducing Russian nuclear weapons in 2002

Department of Energy nonproliferation programs in Russia: $300 million
Nunn-Lugar program: $403 million

Progress under Nunn-Lugar so far

5,336 warheads deactivated
422 ICBMs destroyed
367 silos eliminated
83 bombers eliminated
184 SLBMs eliminated
18 nuclear ballistic missile subs destroyed
