Med Student Organization Praises 'ER' For Addressing Racial Disparities in the Physician Workforce
   RESTON, VA - February 5 - Studies have shown that underrepresented minority physicians are more likely to practice in underserved communities. At the same time, the number of health professional shortage areas in the U.S. swell, which contributes to significant racial disparities in health outcomes. However, the nation's medical schools are failing to train a physician workforce reflective of our ever-diversifying population.

This critical issue was brought to the public's attention during last Thursday's episode of "ER" on NBC, when Eriq La Salle's character, Dr. Peter Benton, was appointed to a position to improve the recruitment and training of minority physicians.

The American Medical Student Association (AMSA) is working on the answer to this question about the physician workforce and health disparities. AMSA's 30,000 physician-in-training members applaud NBC and Warner Brothers (the show's producer) for focusing attention on this problem of enormous consequence to the health of millions of Americans. Founded in 1950, AMSA is the nation's largest, independent medical student organization.

"Medical education institutions have a social obligation to train doctors that will offer the best health-care services possible to our diversifying population," said Sindhu Srinivas, MD, AMSA national president. Srinivas continued, "In order to fulfill this, medical schools must recruit a student body which reflects our nation's changing demographics. If we fail in this effort, the divide between the health of the rich and poor, minorities and Caucasians, urban and suburban, will only continue to expand."

AMSA has begun a national effort to compile a set of best practices for medical school admissions. Schools will complete a detailed survey that will qualitatively and quantitatively examine their admission processes and philosophies. The results of the survey will be presented in a scorecard that ranks the schools based on a scale determined by a review committee. The best practices will be highlighted and promoted to each school to foster positive change. With this, AMSA believes medical education will undergo significant and needed reform to better serve our nation.

For more information, visit AMSA on the Internet at www.amsa.org.
