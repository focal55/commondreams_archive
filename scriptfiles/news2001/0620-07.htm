Mandatory Arbitration is a Threat to Civil Rights
Statement of NOW President Patricia Ireland
   WASHINGTON - June 20 - The National Organization for Women (NOW) stands with members of Congress and other allies to reverse a dangerous trend that threatens to set back women’s and civil rights gains in employment more than 35 years. It's called mandatory arbitration, and earlier this spring, a bare majority of the U.S. Supreme Court gave it an unwise and unwarranted boost.

On March 21, 2001, five members of the U.S. Supreme Court said in Circuit City v. St. Clair Adams that employers could require employees to check their rights at the door as the price of admission to the workplace. Unless overturned by Congress, the Circuit City decision will restrict workers' rights to sue for damages related to job discrimination, harassment and other abuses. We must have congressional action to remedy this injustice.

NOW strongly supports Senator Russ Feingold and Congress members John Conyers, Dennis Kucinich, Barney Frank, and Ed Markey, along with dozens of their colleagues, in their determination to amend federal law in language so clear that even these five justices cannot mistake the meaning: workers cannot be coerced into giving up their rights on the job and their access to courts to enforce those rights.

Mandatory arbitration is nothing more than a way for employers to evade the stricter civil rights law enacted after Anita Hill raised the nation’s consciousness about sexual harassment. The Civil Rights Act of 1991 for the first time gave women the right to a trial by jury and damages in employment discrimination cases under Title VII of the 1964 Civil Rights Act. In addition to providing compensatory damages as a remedy for the employee, the 1991 act also allows limited punitive damages as a deterrent to employers.

The new law’s provisions raised the stakes for employers and gave them a new incentive to find ways around it. Increasingly, other employers have looked with envy at the securities industry's notorious system of mandatory arbitration. Employers as different as JC Penney and Hooters are now imposing this unfair system on their employees.

Mandatory arbitration systems are often stacked against the employee. Some do not allow employees to have their own attorneys at the arbitration proceedings; others give employers the sole right to choose the arbitrators. Many companies disallow discovery, the ability of the employee to subpoena relevant documents and other evidence prior to the hearing.

Employees may face a panel of arbitrators with no experience, interest or expertise in equal employment or other laws that cover workers on the job. They are less likely to be fully compensated for the damage they have suffered or the attorneys fees and costs of the arbitration; indeed, they often face arbitration fees that are many times what a court’s filing fees would be, sometimes paying even more than the damages they are awarded. Except in cases of extreme legal error, the employees have no ability to appeal the outcome of the arbitration.

The only way employees can potentially have their day in court is to team up and form a class to challenge the company in federal court. But this strategy means finding enough people with similar complaints who are willing to participate; and class actions are often difficult to organize, expensive to litigate and disfavored by judges.

Mandatory arbitration can also work to the disadvantage of employers. Class-action suits are also costly for them. In addition, with no public record of trial verdicts or appellate decisions and no definitive, predictable interpretation of the laws, employers also are hard-pressed to anticipate what they are allowed and not allowed to do.

If proponents of arbitration are correct in their argument that it is faster, cheaper and better than the judicial system, then surely employees and their attorneys will opt for arbitration in a voluntary system. However, those plaintiffs who wish to file their complaints in a court of law must not be denied that opportunity.

The National Organization for Women will not rest until arbitration is optional for every employee. We challenge every member of Congress: Reaffirm your commitment to this country’s equal employment opportunity laws. Open the doors for those who are locked out of court and restore their right to make their cases before an impartial judge and a jury of peers.
