Wage Gap Not Just a Matter of Nickels and Dimes
State Press Conferences held to Announce Equal Pay Legislation
   WASHINGTON - February 2 - Culminating on Feb. 7, state legislators, union leaders and grassroots advocates in at least 12 states across the country (AL, AZ, CO, CT, GA, ME, NM, NV, OH, TX, WI, WV) will sound a challenge to secure equal pay this legislative session for the thousands of working families who are unable to become self-sufficient or reach their full earning potential because they are not paid fairly based on their race and gender.

This Challenge to Secure Equal Pay for Working Families is coordinated by the Center for Policy Alternatives, a non-partisan policy and leadership center in Washington, D.C. "We know that a dramatic wage gap exists and that both women and men want this resolved. It's time to deliver on this issue," states Linda Tarr-Whelan, President of the Center for Policy Alternatives.

On average, families lose over $4,000 each year due to the gender wage gap alone. Women earn 72 cents for every dollar earned by men, according to the Census Bureau. African Americans are paid 78 cents for every dollar earned by whites and Latinos earn 67 cents, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. More than half of women workers are concentrated in sales, clerical and service jobs including child care, elder care and nursing-jobs that continue to be underpaid because they are seen as "women's work." Studies show that these positions typically pay less than jobs traditionally held by men, even if they require the same skill, effort and responsibility to do the work. Research also shows that the wage gap exists regardless of occupation or title-women who are managers, physicians and computer programmers are paid less than their male counterparts.

Several states are proposing very workable, straightforward solutions. One approach -- the Equal Pay Remedies and Enforcement Act -- toughens the penalties for violations of existing state equal pay law and creates a statewide job evaluation study to assess the causes of wage inequities and identify state-specific recommendations. Today, two out of three workers are employed by firms that use some form of job evaluation. Other approaches include raising the wages for specific sectors that are traditionally underpaid, or prohibiting wage discrimination for jobs of comparable worth in the public or private sectors, and instituting stronger tracking and reporting requirements.
