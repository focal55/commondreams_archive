Bush Administration's Dangerous Games:
Missile Defense Tests International Patience with Rogue Superpower, Group Says
   WASHINGTON - July 12 - As the Bush Administration prepares to conduct a $100 million missile interceptor test this Saturday, it has given notice that this and all further tests mean nothing. The Administration wants to pursue an anti-missile system regardless of whether it can work or whether it complies with international law. Deployment of the anti-missile system also disregards the concerns of U.S. allies. President Bush is declaring the U.S. the ultimate rogue state.

As the latest test nears, allied concerns about international law and cooperation have been rejected out of hand. The Administration has announced its intention to abrogate or violate the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty in the short rather than the long term. Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz told the Senate Armed Services Committee that testing for a missile defense system would "bump against treaty restrictions" in months rather than years. This crisis in international affairs has been precipitated by the administration's decision to build a "test bed" for missile defense at Fort Greely in Alaska.

This is an ill-concealed and dangerous attempt to circumvent the ABM Treaty. By building an illegal interceptor site without calling it such, the Administration is rushing a direct confrontation with the rule of international law and the system of non-proliferation and arms control that has for years kept the horror of nuclear war at bay.

"The American people are being offered the illusion of security at a cost of hundreds of billions of dollars," said Martin Butcher, director of security policy for Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR). "Prevention is the only cure for the disease of proliferation and nuclear war."

All this brinkmanship is in service of a system that doesn't work. Previous tests of the National Missile Defense have failed badly. Despite being set up in as simple a fashion as possible in order to ensure success, the Pentagon has shown itself time and again unable to reliably intercept incoming missiles.

"PSR calls on Congress to prevent the Administration from making this appalling mistake," said Kimberly Roberts, PSR's associate director of security programs. "Our Senators and Representatives must do all in their power to preserve the treaties and alliances that have helped keep us safe from nuclear war for thirty years. They should block any funding for projects which breach the ABM Treaty."
