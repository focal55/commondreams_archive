Brady Campaign Denounces NRA Rhetoric that Campaign Finance Reform Bill Jeopardizes Free Speech
   WASHINGTON - July 12 - Michael D. Barnes, president of the Brady Campaign to Prevent Gun Violence (formerly Handgun Control) today issued a statement denouncing the National Rifle Association's (NRA) claim in a full-page ad in The Washington Post this week that certain provisions of the McCain-Feingold campaign finance reform bill violate free speech. Specifically, the NRA objects to the bill's language that would prohibit outside groups from using "soft" campaign financing money to fund political issue advertisements within 60 days of a general election, or 30 days of a primary election if those ads refer to a specific federal candidate. A similar version of the McCain-Feingold bill was introduced in the House by Christopher Shays (R-Ct.) and Marty Meehan (D-Mass.), and will soon come to a vote before the full House of Representatives.

"I have two questions for the NRA. One: When will NRA leaders stop using blatantly misleading statements and sensational rhetoric in its attempts to frighten the American people into opposing responsible measures that are in the long-term, best interests of the United States? And two: What is the NRA so afraid of?

"This legislation will affect all advocacy groups, including ours. Of course the NRA doesn't want to change the status quo because, as The Los Angeles Times pointed out this week, it got its money's worth in the last election. The Bush Administration is beholden to the NRA due to its enormous campaign contributions.

"Look at the evidence from this week alone: first, an Under Secretary of State spouted the NRA line when he told a United Nations conference that its sensible proposals to thwart illegal firearm trafficking were a threat to legal gun ownership. Then, Attorney General Ashcroft directs the Department of Justice to reverse the U.S. Government's long-standing position on the Second Amendment.

"This bill does not violate the First Amendment, rather, it represents an opportunity to 'level the playing field' and reform a structure that is in dire need of repair. It promotes a system where it is not just money that talks. Perhaps that is what has the NRA so nervous," Barnes stated.
