The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities to Hold Press Briefing on Available Budget Surplus, Tax Cuts
   WASHINGTON - January 2 - News Advisory:

What:
Press Briefing: How Big is the Available Budget Surplus and How Large a Tax Cut Can it Accommodate?

When:
10 a.m. Thursday, January 4, 200l

Where:
Holeman Lounge
National Press Club

Speakers:
-- Robert Greenstein, executive director, Center on Budget and Policy Priorities
-- Robert Bixby, executive director, Concord Coalition
-- William Gale, senior fellow, Brookings Institution

Robert Greenstein of the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, Robert Bixby of the Concord Coalition, and William Gale of the Brookings Institution will discuss the implications of an expected increase in projected budget surpluses at a briefing in the Holeman Lounge of the National Press Club at 10 a.m. Thursday, Jan. 4,
2001.

At the event, the Center will release a new study, "Can The New Surplus Projections Accommodate A Large Tax Cut?", which evaluates how much is likely to be available for new initiatives under forthcoming Congressional Budget Office surplus estimates, and whether the available surplus is likely to be sufficient to accommodate the Bush tax cut.

CBO is expected to issue new projections later this month.

The center's analysis and the briefing will examine three questions:

1) How big are the projected non-Social Security surpluses likely to be under the new CBO projections?

2) How much of that amount will actually be available for new initiatives, including a tax cut?

3) How does this anticipated surplus compare to the magnitude of the tax cut proposed by President-elect Bush during the campaign?

For additional information, please contact Jim Jaffe jaffe@cbpp.org or Michelle Bazie bazie@cbpp.org in the center's communications office, 202-408-1080.

---
The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities is a non-partisan research organization and policy institute that conducts research and analysis on a range of federal and state government policies and programs, with an emphasis on those affecting low- and middle-income people. It is supported primarily by foundation grants.
