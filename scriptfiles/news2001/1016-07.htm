Currying Favor in South Asia: Congress Discards Sanctions on Military Aid to Pakistan, India
   WASHINGTON - October 16 - Today, the U.S. House of Representatives gave the President a green light to sell weapons to a region whose political volatility was again demonstrated in yesterday's border skirmish between India and Pakistan. The legislation, passed with little debate, waives U.S. law that prohibits direct assistance to a country whose elected head of government was deposed by a military coup. Additionally, Congressional notification periods for certain types of arms transfers were reduced. The bill is unnecessary and harmful to U.S. interests for the following reasons:

U.S. CANNOT CONTROL ITS WEAPONS EXPORTS

Past experience in Iran, Iraq, and Afghanistan shows that the U.S. cannot control its weapons exports, nor their recipients. Over and over, the United States faces enemies it previously armed. In the 1980s, the United States supplied arms worth $500 million a year to anti-Soviet fighters, including the Taliban. The weapons include high-powered sniper rifles and Stinger missiles which now threaten U.S. troops.

U.S. LAW PROHIBITS U.S. ARMS EXPORTS TO GOVERNMENTS IN POWER DUE TO A MILITARY COUP

Congress passed legislation, Section 508 of the Foreign Operations Appropriations Act for fiscal year 2001, prohibiting the export of U.S. weapons and military assistance to countries whose duly elected head of government is deposed. Reversing this policy without credible evidence for the re- establishment of democracy demonstrates that the U.S. will brush aside democratic principles when they are inconvenient. This undermines future U.S. efforts to promote democracy in unstable regions.

PRESIDENT HAS EMERGENCY POWERS

Pursuant to Section 614 of the Foreign Assistance Act, the President exercised his authority to provide U.S. weapons and military assistance when U.S. national security interests are threatened by authorizing $100 million in military aid to Pakistan after September 11th. Additionally, Sec. 516 of the Foreign Assistance Act authorizes the President to transfer excess defense articles in times of national emergency. The President is required to notify Congress if he proceeds under these provisions. Congress should not reduce its oversight role unless the President certifies that assistance provided under these sections is insufficient and Congress consents.

U.S. AID SHOULD BE HUMANITARIAN, ECONOMIC AND SOCIAL

The United States should target its aid toward Pakistan and India immediate needs. In the UN's Human Development Index, which measures a country's social and economic needs, India is listed at #115, and Pakistan at #127 - out of a listing of 162 countries. The influx of refugees will only exacerbate those countries' economic problems. Economic and humanitarian assistance is much more critical -- and appropriate -- than new weapons.
