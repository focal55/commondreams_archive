Wellstone Presses for Conservation, Clean Renewable Energy
Says Bush Plan Ignores Short-Term Needs, Environment, Health and Safety
   WASHINGTON - May 17 - Following the release of President Bush's energy plan today at a visit to St. Paul, Minnesota, U.S. Senator Paul Wellstone (D-MN) joined Minority Leader Tom Daschle (D-SD), Senator Mark Dayton (D-MN) and others to strongly criticize a policy that promotes more fossil fuel drilling and production, loosening of pollution controls on coal and oil refineries, and deep cuts to existing federal support for research and development of clean, renewable energy sources. Wellstone characterized the President's remarks at the St. Paul event, which emphasized upbeat themes on energy conservation and the environment, as "greenwash".

"It is ironic that the President chose to announce this unbalanced policy at District Energy in downtown St. Paul, a facility which uses clean, renewable energy technology, just like that made possible by federal research funds. The President's budget wants to cut funding for those programs by 50%," Wellstone said. "The Energy Task Force Report fails to offer any solutions to the short-term energy problems facing consumers across the country – spiraling gas and electricity prices – and for the long-term, it looks backwards to more coal, oil and nuclear power; less clean, non-fossil sources like solar, wind and biofuels."

Wellstone vowed to press Congress to restore the Bush cuts of 50% to renewable energy funds in the FY 2002 budget, and to restore funding for energy conservation research and development, which the President's budget cut by 46%. Contrary to the widely reported suggestion by the Vice President that conservation is little more than "a sign of personal virtue," a recent report by scientists at national laboratories concluded that improved energy efficiency could reduce growth in electricity demand by nearly half.

Wellstone also promised to seek a Bush commitment to the largely untapped energy produced by America's farmers: ethanol and biodiesel. Greater use of these fuels would decrease the need for imported petroleum products, help improve air quality across the country by reducing carbon monoxide and other toxic emissions, and provide an important economic stimulus to rural America. A 3% national market share for ethanol and biodiesel, which Wellstone is pushing, would replace about 9 billion gallons of gasoline annually or between 500,000 and 600,000 barrels of crude oil each day.

In the sort-term, Wellstone challenged the President to exercise the 'bully pulpit' to force big oil refiners to bring down spiraling gasoline prices that are hitting consumers' pocketbooks in Minnesota and across the nation hard. While Vice President Cheney has said there is not much the Administration can do about gasoline prices in the short-term, a Wellstone-initiated investigation by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) into Midwestern gasoline prices last summer found that big oil refiners acted to further their own economic interest by not expanding capacity to produce reformulated gasoline (RFG). While it may not strictly have been illegal, the action confirmed suspicions of market manipulation.

"The big oil companies don't want our country to move to renewable energy development. They support more drilling in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge and the weakening of environmental, health and safety standards. Minnesota is a cold-weather state at the wrong end of the fossil fuels pipeline; we import nearly all of our energy today. We should be seeking to reduce our dependence on unsustainable fossil fuels, not increasing that dependence. The future of the economy in my state is clean, efficient, renewable energy. If the President wants to go there to talk about energy policy, he ought to reconsider his budget cuts in this area, and make a real commitment on renewable energy." Wellstone said.

Wellstone has been strongly critical of the Bush administration's position that fossil fuels exploration and nuclear power is the only solution to the country's long-term energy needs. Wellstone rejects the Bush administration's plans to help big business overturn environmental, health and safety laws. He has spoken out against the administration's plan to open our most pristine wilderness areas to oil and gas drilling when the vast majority of America's energy resources are already open to development.
