Full-page Ad in Today's New York Times Targets BOISE CASCADE: "9 Out of 10 Americans Don't Want to Visit a Lumberyard to See Their National Forests."
Summer Ad Campaign Launched as Boise Cascade Comes Under Fire for Plans to Log Old Growth Forests in Oregon
   SAN FRANCISCO - June 7 - Today marked the launch of a high profile, summer advertisement campaign targeting the logging behemoth Boise Cascade. A full- page ad running in the New York Times today called on the timber giant to "catch up to modern values and stop destroying our nation's last ancient forests." [View a PDF version of the ad.]

The national ad campaign begins as Boise Cascade finds itself under increasing public scrutiny for its recent role in gutting the popular U.S. Roadless Initiative as well as its efforts to log old growth forests in southern Oregon against the will of local residents. The town of Williams, Oregon is up in arms over a Boise Cascade proposal to log and use herbicides in a pristine, old growth watershed. The Town Council passed a unanimous resolution to buy the land from the logging company. Boise Cascade has refused and is negotiating with the local Sheriff's Department to provide security at the logging site.

Today's advertisement, sponsored by Rainforest Action Network, draws attention to the widespread public support for protecting old growth forests. According to a recent Los Angeles Times poll, 9 out of 10 Americans say it is important to protect wilderness areas. The ad also notes that hundreds of leading companies-including Kinko's, Home Depot, and Lowe's-have all responded to public concern for forests and pledged to stop selling products made from endangered forests.

Despite the fact that the U.S. has already lost 95 percent of its old growth forests, Boise Cascade continues to log the country's last, ancient forests. In addition to the controversy in Williams, Boise Cascade has also come under fire in other areas of Oregon for its old growth logging in the Umpqua National Forest, home to spotted owls, eagles, falcons, lynx, and wolverines. Boise Cascade also sells wood products from endangered forests in South America, Southeast Asia and around the world.
