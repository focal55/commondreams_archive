Clean Air Trust Warns of Dirty-Air Potential of Bush Energy Plan
  WASHINGTON - May 16 - Noting that at least 25 states have already had serious smog problems in 2001, the nonprofit Clean Air Trust today warned that the Bush Administration's energy policy could worsen air pollution.

"Afraid of a political backlash, the administration has tried to soften its rhetoric in the past week," noted Frank O'Donnell, the Clean Air Trust's executive director, "but it's important for people to examine the policy's fine print, because it could lead to a rollback of clean-air protections. It could ultimately mean higher-polluting electric power plants and factories, and more toxic gasoline."

O'Donnell added that the White House also appears headed on a course that would cause a new gasoline crisis in California -- while reducing the state's ability to fight pollution.

O'Donnell noted that the Clean Air Trust has discovered that at least 25 states have already experienced levels of pollution greater than the public health standards for ozone, or smog this year (See attached list of states). Among these is Pennsylvania -- including Lancaster, where the President will stage a photo-op Friday to tout his energy plan. Eleven additional states have experienced "moderate" air pollution problems.

"It's remarkable that so many states have experienced smog problems before the traditional start of the summer smog season," noted O'Donnell. "Literally tens of millions of people have been exposed to dirty air. This should be a warning that we need to consider the impact on air quality of any new energy strategy. We should not retreat from a battle not yet won."

New Source Review

O'Donnell cited several areas of major concern with the energy plan, including a key clean-air enforcement tool known in the jargon of the bureaucracy as "new source review." It is based on a relatively simple concept. Today, the Clean Air Act requires major new sources of pollution such as refineries or coal-fired electric power plants to use the best available pollution control technology. Existing pollution sources are treated more leniently. But when existing plants make major modernization investments that often cause significant increases in pollution, they legally become new sources -- and need to upgrade their pollution controls.

This provision of the law is designed to prevent big corporations from playing a shell game -- of constantly rebuilding and expanding old plants in an effort to avoid modern pollution controls. There is clear evidence that some electric power companies, refineries and other industries have attempted to evade the law in this way. In fact, the EPA and states (led by New York) brought suit against big power companies for violating the law. EPA separately brought enforcement cases against refineries and pulp and paper mills. As recently as last week, EPA reached a legal settlement with a major refiner. About half a dozen lawsuits against power companies are still pending.

Because this enforcement tool has been so effective, electric power companies, coal companies and major oil companies such as Exxon-Mobil have sought to stop the enforcement, relax the rules, and pardon the polluting power plants. Vice President Cheney has described this as a "tough call," and said he will urge EPA to review the enforcement program.

It is unclear at this point if EPA will simply look at weakening future enforcement of the Clean Air Act, or whether it will also consider dropping the court cases -- and pardoning the polluters. (EPA Administrator Christie Whitman has said that she does not favor dropping the existing cases.) "In either event, 'review' means delay, and delay means more air pollution," O'Donnell said.

One important note on this point: Vice President Cheney and Energy Secretary Spencer Abraham have repeatedly noted that new refineries have not been built in recent years, and they have implied that environmental restrictions were the cause. Not so, says top DOE analysts.

In a May 11 presentation to the National Research Council, DOE senior analyst Barry McNutt noted that clean air had not blocked new refineries. "It just didn't make sense (economically) to expand capacity," McNutt noted. "Historically it was just a financial concern."

McNutt's view was bolstered in congressional testimony yesterday by John Cook, director of the petroleum division of DOE's Energy Information Administration. "Markets have been sluggish over the last few years," said Cook. "That is the principal reason for slow refining capacity growth."

Reformulated Gasoline

Oil companies, led by Exxon-Mobil, have also nagged Vice President Cheney to "simplify" the current system for distributing cleaner-burning or "reformulated" gasoline. In the process, the oil companies have grossly distorted the current system in an effort to mask their very effective price gouging. (For industry profiteering, note the recent FTC report on last summer's gas prices. Although the FTC didn't find hard evidence of collusion, it did note "decisions by some firms to maximize their profits" by curtailing production and keeping available supply off the market.)

Here are some quick facts: contrary to the industry's claims, there are really only three types of reformulated gasoline -- the cleanest type, used in California, one that uses the additive MTBE (used on the East Coast and in part of Texas) and one that uses ethanol as an additive (chiefly in Chicago and Milwaukee). Readers should note that it was an industry decision whether to use MTBE or ethanol.

In addition, several states have adopted fuels that are sort of half-steps towards reformulated gasoline. We must point out, however, that the oil companies themselves encouraged these "boutique" fuels -- which the industry is now denouncing -- rather than agree to make reformulated gasoline.

Last year, the American Petroleum Institute reached an informal agreement with the American Lung Association and Northeastern state clean-air regulators on a plan that would eliminate the need for mandatory use of either MTBE or ethanol. As part of the agreement, the oil companies said they would make gasoline that retained the clean-air performance of reformulated gas. That plan foundered because of opposition from the powerful ethanol lobby.

Last week, the American Petroleum Institute urged Congress to eliminate the requirement for MTBE or ethanol, but it did not renew its pledge to keep making gasoline as clean as today's reformulated gas. If the Bush policy agrees with the oil companies on this, it would lead to gasoline that produced more smog and more toxic chemical pollution. That would also happen if the administration, as rumored, decided to suspend cleaner-gasoline requirements.

It is possible to envision a national or regional fuels program that simplifies the current distribution system, but those fuels need to be clean to avoid increased pollution. They shouldn't be the lowest common denominator. The administration could also create new pollution problems by preempting state efforts to reduce toxic chemical pollution from gasoline. The administration should consult with states on this issue and not stick them with unnecessarily dirty fuel.

Will Bush Cause New Energy Problems in California?

In a related issue, the state of California has applied for a waiver from the requirement that cleaner-burning gasoline contain mandatory levels of MTBE or ethanol. California supplied evidence that refiners could make even cleaner gasoline under California rules without these additives.

The EPA agreed with California's assessment (call us if you want the EPA proposed ruling on this), but the issue is now at the White House. We have heard strong rumors that the White House plans to over-rule EPA and reject the California waiver request.

If so, it would lead to gasoline shortages in California starting in 2003, according to the California Energy Commission. It would, according to EPA, also mean more air pollution in California than if the state is allowed a waiver.

"It would seem the height of hypocrisy if the White House rejects the California request," said O'Donnell, since it would mean bigger energy problems and more pollution in California -- the exact opposite of what the Administration claims that it seeks."
