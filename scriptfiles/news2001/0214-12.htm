FCC Report Shows Cable TV Industry Raised Rates Again In 2000
Consumers Union says head-to-head cable competition nationwide could save consumers $4 billion a year
   WASHINGTON - February 14 - Gene Kimmelman, Co-Director of Consumers Union's Washington DC Office, made the following statement today in response to the Federal Communication Commission (FCC)'s annual report on cable industry prices, which showed that both "competitive" and "noncompetitive" cable operators increased rates by 5.8% in the twelve months leading up to July 1, 2000:

"Another day, another couple of bucks on your cable bill. That's what happens when the government deregulates a monopoly.

"With the fifth anniversary of the Telecommunications Act one week behind us, this report demonstrates why policymakers need to require effective competition before deregulation.

"The FCC's analysis has consistently shown that cable rates in communities with two cable operators in direct competition are 20 percent lower than the rates in communities served by one operator. That means that if communities nationwide had just two cable companies going head-to-head, consumers would save more than $4 billion a year. Obviously, this report shows that it's time for Congress and the FCC to clamp down on cable monopolies and find new ways to jump-start competition.

"The FCC, as directed by Congress, separates cable operators into two categories -- 'competitive' and 'noncompetitive.' These categories are not necessarily what you would expect them to be. The 'competitive' category lumps cable operators that have real head-to-head competition with the cable monopolies that sign up less than 30 percent of their communities as subscribers. This is an artifact of the '92 Cable Act and does not provide a meaningful comparison between competitive and noncompetitive markets. The only way to truly compare the effect of competition in the cable industry is to compare communities with one cable operator to those with more than one."
