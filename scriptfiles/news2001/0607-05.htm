National Academy Of Sciences Report Concludes Human Activity Warms The Globe
  WASHINGTON - June 7 - Addressing key questions about global warming, the National Academy of Sciences (NAS) confirmed the concerns of the environment and public health community in a new report. The NAS declared global warming a real problem impacting key vulnerable populations. The report also confirmed that global warming is caused, at least in part, by the actions of humankind.

"This should be the final science needed to take action on global climate change," said Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR) Environment and Health Director Susan T. West, M.P.H. "Human activity is changing our climate, threatening the health of our children and other vulnerable populations. We must take action now."

The National Academy of Sciences report was produced to address questions the Bush Administration had about the UN Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) findings that climate change will have negative impacts on human health across the globe. The NAS report confirms those findings, and urges action today as well. The report states that "national policy decisions made now and in the long term future will influence the extent of any damage suffered by vulnerable human populations and ecosystems later in this century."

"We believe that this report, affirming the findings of the IPCC's third assessment and clearly linking national policy to our future health, must move our country to action." said West. "The NAS reports specifically indicts carbon dioxide as an important greenhouse gas, though the Bush Administration energy plan ignores it completely. We must put America's health above the interests of the coal and oil industries."

Carbon dioxide (CO2) and other greenhouse gases cited in the report, including methane, nitrous oxide and chlorofluorocarbons contribute to a climate shift leading to disastrous consequences: Heat-related illness and injury, severe weather and sea-level rise, and global increases in vector-borne diseases such as malaria, dengue fever and West Nile Virus. Carbon dioxide emissions are directly related to poor air quality. Poor air quality leads to increases in cardiovascular disease and respiratory illnesses such as asthma.
