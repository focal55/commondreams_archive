Buying Leadership II:
A Look at the Race for House Minority Whip
   WASHINGTON - October 9 - As Washington attempts to find some level of normalcy in the midst of war, House Democrats tomorrow are scheduled to take up an election not unlike that of a high school contest for class president. At stake is the Democrats' No. 2 in command, the position of House minority whip, an office named for its responsibility of whipping up support among the rank-and-file for the party's legislative positions.

Rep. David Bonior (D-Mich.), who has been the Democrats' whip for more than a decade, is set to resign the position in January in order to concentrate on his bid to become Michigan's next governor. Though Bonior only announced his intentions last month, his would-be successors--Reps. Nancy Pelosi (D-Calif.) and Steny Hoyer (D-Md.)--began butting heads almost three years ago in their quest to join the party leadership. In the process, they also became major campaign contributors.

It's a contest based almost entirely on personal relationships. Back in 1999, Hoyer and Pelosi each launched a lobbying campaign not unlike those pursued by major industries, aimed solely at wooing fellow Democrats. At the time, both hoped to fill an additional leadership position that would have been created only if Democrats had recaptured control of the House during the 2000 elections.

In their efforts to curry favor with Democratic colleagues, Pelosi and Hoyer criss-crossed the country raising money for other Democrats. Perhaps more significantly, the two made numerous contributions from their own campaign coffers to colleagues in need.

During the 1999-2000 election cycle, Pelosi was the third biggest congressional contributor to federal parties and candidates, giving more than $1.1 million to Democrats via her personal campaign account and her leadership committee, PAC to the Future. She was surpassed in giving only by Speaker of the House Dennis Hastert (R-Ill.) and Majority Whip Tom DeLay (R-Texas), who each contributed roughly $1.3 million. Hoyer ranked No. 5 on the list, contributing roughly $927,000 to the Democratic party and other candidates.

In the opening months of the 2002 election cycle, the checks are still flowing. During the first six months of this year, Hoyer contributed $190,700 to Democratic party committees and candidates on the federal level, while Pelosi contributed roughly $180,000. The list of recipients on both sides includes lots of freshman Democrats, many of whom got money from both Pelosi and Hoyer during their 2000 campaigns.

As of Monday, Pelosi was reporting that she had 120 commitments of support from fellow Democrats, while Hoyer claimed 105 supporters. However, both cannot be right, since only 215 people will be eligible to vote (the 210 Democratic members and five non-voting delegates of the House--including District of Columbia delegate Eleanor Holmes Norton. If all voters participate, the winner will need 108 votes to secure a victory.
