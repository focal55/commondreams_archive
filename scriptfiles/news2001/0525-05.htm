Wrong People Behind Bars, Says Peace Group
Calls SOA Sentences Part of "Crackdown"
  NEW YORK - May 25 - "We lock up peacemakers and give money and medals to warmakers," says Frida Berrigan of the War Resisters League. "What's wrong with that picture?"

Berrigan, a member of the longtime antiwar organization's Executive Committee, is talking about the "SOA 26," two dozen-plus activists convicted Tuesday of criminal trespass in Columbus, Georgia. Yesterday a federal judge sentenced them to jail terms ranging from probation to one year for stepping peacefully onto the grounds of the U.S. Army's School of the Americas at Ft. Benning last November 19.

They were among 3,600 taking part in what has become an annual protest demanding the closure of the school because of its links to the abuse of human rights. All 3,600 stepped onto the base, but the 26 committed their act in defiance of previous "ban-and-bar letters" prohibiting them from trespassing at Ft. Benning for five years. At least seven of the 26 are members of the War Resisters League; one, Clare Hanrahan, of Asheville, NC, is currently a member of the League's National Committee, and Hazel Tulecke of Yellow Springs, Ohio, has served on that body in the past.

"The War Resisters League is proud to have been represented in Ft. Benning and proud that members of the League are among the SOA 26," says the League's National Office Director, Melissa Jameson. "They show by their example that Gandhi's ideal of noncooperation with injustice is very much alive in our midst."

After the sentencing yesterday, a number of activists who had come to Columbus for the trial—Berrigan's younger sister Kate among them—returned to Ft. Benning in solidarity with the defendants. They were arrested and released.

The sentences are long for simple trespass charges, observes the League's Disarmament Coordinator Christopher Ney. Noting that New York's Rev. Al Sharpton was also sentenced to jail time earlier this week, in his case for trespass at the U.S. Navy base on the Puerto Rican island of Vieques, Ney said, "These sentences might represent a crackdown on civil disobedience."
