Missile Defense Experts Hold Capitol Hill Meeting:
'What the Bush Administration Isn't Telling You'
  WASHINGTON - July 12 - News Advisory:

What:
Press briefing with missile defense experts critical of administration policies. For Washington-based journalists covering the Saturday, July 14 flight tests of missile defense systems which will try to shoot down a mock warhead over the Pacific with interceptors launched from Vandenberg. The last test on July 8, 2000, failed. Briefing sponsored by Business Leaders for Sensible Priorities.

When:
Friday, July 13, 10:30 a.m.

Where:
Cannon House Office Building, Room 121 (first floor)

Who:
-- Rear Admiral Eugene Carroll (US Navy, ret.), recently retired as Vice President of the Center for Defense Information, the first naval officer to serve as director of all military operations for all U.S. forces in Europe and the Middle East.

-- Craig Eisendrath, Senior Fellow, Center for International Policy; co-author of the influential pamphlet ;Shield of Dreams; and of the forthcoming book The Phantom Defense: America's Pursuit of the Star Wars Illusion; drafter of the 1967 Outer Space Treaty outlawing weaponization of space.

-- Rep. Janice Schakowsky (D-IL), who said today, "Missile defense is a program that is destined for failure on so many levels. Rather than spending billions of dollars on a 'shield of dreams,' we should devote those resources to investments that will improve people's lives in our communities."

-- Rep. John Tierney (D-MA), (invited) who waged a successful fight with the Defense Department to make public the report of former Pentagon chief civilian test evaluator Philip Coyle on missile defense. He wrote in The Boston Globe. The report describes how the tests are being dumbed down to ensure public perception of success (and) finds that the system can't defend against accidental or unauthorized launches.... Despite these warnings, President Bush proposes accelerating deployment."

-- Frank von Hippel, Chairman,Federation of American Scientists, Princeton professor and former Assistant Director for National Security at the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy

-- Alan Kliegerman, CEO of AKPharma,Inc., representative of Business Leaders for Sensible Priorities, a group of 600 CEOs opposing missile defense.

Sponsored by Business Leaders for Sensible Priorities, 'the briefing will address key technical, strategic and political dimensions of Saturday's tests not revealed in the just completed July 12 briefing with Rumsfeld and other missile defense proponents, such as how the tests are rigged to increase the likelihood of appearing successful, why they tend to fail anyway, and the implications of the recently published Coyle report on the total ineffectiveness of missile defense technology under more realistic conditions. The report concluded the program was so immature that "a rigorous assessment of the potential system performance cannot be made" and was kept secret by the Defense Department.

Speakers will address Pentagon's announcement that it would seek 2002 funding for what it calls a "test" facility in Alaska, but which could become a command center for an operational system of interceptors in case of "emergency" by 2004, i.e., within the term of the Bush presidency, directly violating the ABM Treaty. Asked by The Wall Street Journal this week what would constitute emergency, a Pentagon spokesperson said that as far as Rumsfeld is concerned, "There already is an emergency."

The appearance of a successful test on Saturday would help this accelerated plan politically, although Rumsfeld said last month he wants deployment fast-tracked even without successful results. Critics charge the plan announced this week amounts to stealth deployment, obviating Congressional oversight and disregarding procurement rules on testing major systems. Ineffective, destabilizing and enormously expensive, missile defense risks voiding existing arms control agreements, touching off a new nuclear arms race and weaponizing space, they say, damaging rather than strengthening American security.

Amid a tightening budgetary climate, proposed 2002 allocation for missile defense would be increased $3 billion to $8.3 billion to pay for the accelerated program. Opponents say in addition to possibly violating procurement rules, such an allocation would take needed funds from more beneficial programs. For the additional $3 billion the Pentagon wants to accelerate missile defense programs next year, 500,584 children could be enrolled in Head Start, 42,857 affordable housing units could be built, 2,226,973 uninsured children could receive health insurance, or 28,290 new teachers could be hired.
