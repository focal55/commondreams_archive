'Election Protection' Volunteers, Hotline, Legal Team To Safeguard Voters In Virginia's June 19 4th CD Election
   WASHINGTON - June 18 - When voters in Virginia's 4th Congressional District arrive at their polling places tomorrow many will find new help on hand to make sure they can cast their ballots unimpeded by many of the obstacles that plagued the Nov. 7 presidential election. It has now been reported that an estimated 2.5 million voters were disenfranchised last November -- including voters in Virginia.

More than a hundred volunteers are being deployed at polling places wearing t-shirts to remind voters, "You have the right to vote." Volunteers will give every voter a "Voters' Bill of Rights" and will offer immediate assistance to any voter who requests it. Backing up the on-site effort will be a team of voting rights lawyers, reachable through a toll-free hotline.

"After November 7, it was clear that something had gone very wrong not just in Florida but around the country," said Ralph G. Neas, president of People For the American Way Foundation. "It is a tragedy that millions of voters were denied their right to vote -- and we'll be in the 4th District to make sure that tragedy is not repeated."

"Election 2000 taught us that it's not enough to urge voters to register and go to the polls, it's also crucially important for voters to have someone to be their advocate at the polls to help them solve problems when things go wrong," Neas said. "That's what Election Protection is designed to do. We're testing it in the 4th District now and planning to expand nationally in 2002."

The Election Protection project was created by the non-partisan People For the American Way Foundation and is being carried out in partnership with a number of other national and local civil and voting rights organizations. It is the second prong of a two-part PFAWF effort that pairs traditional get-out-the-vote (GOTV) activity with massive voter education and direct voter assistance to help assure that attempts to vote are not thwarted at the polls.

The most intensive GOTV activities are focused primarily on Petersburg and other areas in the district with the highest percentage of African American voters, who have traditionally been underrepresented at the polls and more likely than other voters to suffer disenfranchisement. The on-site Election Protection activities will occur in these areas. (Virginia is one of the states covered by the federal Voting Rights Act because of a history of disenfranchising African American citizens.)

Partnering with PFAWF in various aspects of this effort are: the NAACP National Voter Fund, the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund, the National Coalition on Black Civic Participation, and the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law. Together with People For the American Way Foundation, the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund and volunteer lawyers from the firm of Latham and Watkins are spearheading the effort to provide legal advice and assistance to voters who require such help in order to vote.

In the three-day lead-up to the special election, which is being held to fill a vacancy created by the death of Rep. Norman Sisisky, and on election day, itself, PFAWF and its partner organizations are carrying out a massive voter education effort to alert voters to the special election and inform them of their voting rights, as well as protecting their rights at the polls.

Activities include:

  • Over the weekend, volunteers hung reminders about the election and the Voters' Bill of Rights on 25,000 doorknobs in Petersburg and Lawrenceville.
  • $65,000 worth of radio ads are being broadcast through election day district-wide over stations that serve the African American community.
  • Nearly 21,000 voters are receiving live phone calls reminding them to go to the polls on Tuesday.
  • On the Sunday before election day, at least ten African American ministers distributed fliers urging their congregations to "Get your souls to the polls." The same message is featured on 10,000 signs displayed in stores, churches, and homes.
  • On election day, full-page ads featuring the Voters' Bill of Rights and a reminder to vote will appear in the the Hopewell News and the Progress-Index, which serve readers in Petersburg and the surrounding area.
  • On election day, two sound trucks will circulate through the neighborhoods urging voters to the polls.
  • Teams of Election Protection monitors will be stationed at polling sites on election day, backed up by toll-free access to a team of 25 volunteer lawyers.

The Voters' Bill of Rights that is being distributed throughout Virginia's 4th Congressional District includes nine specific points to assure that voters are not disenfranchised:

1. If you are in line at the correct polling place any time between 6 a.m. and 7 p.m., you have the right to vote.

2. If you are registered to vote, but do not have a photo ID when the poll worker asks for one, you have the right to sign your name and vote.

3. If you have moved, but are still qualified and registered to vote in the same precinct or district, you have the right to vote.

4. If a poll worker cannot find your name on the list and has attempted to check your registration with the registrar, you have the right to cast a "conditional ballot" and vote.

5. If you make a mistake and "spoil" your ballot, you have the right to receive another ballot and vote.

6. If a disability prevents you from accessing the voting booths, you have the right to vote up to 150 feet outside the polling place, as directed by election officials.

7. If you cannot read or write, or are disabled, you have the right to request special assistance to help you vote.

8. You have the right to vote without being intimidated or forced to vote for someone for whom you do not wish to vote.

9. You have the right to bring your children (15 years or younger) into the voting booth with you.
