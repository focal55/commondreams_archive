Editors Criticize U.S. Stance at U.N. Conference on Arms Trade
   WASHINGTON - July 17 - Editorial writers across the United States have responded with indignation to John Bolton's speech at the United Nations on July 9th, in which he stated that the U.S. would not sign any document that infringes on U.S. citizens' constitutional right to bear arms. As the following excerpts point out, the Bush administration, attempting to satisfy its conservative base, is continuing to act unilaterally without regard to the impact its policies are having on allies and enemies alike. To the detriment of U.S. national security, the administration's position will only impede the progress that the conference hopes to make in preventing the countless deaths caused by small arms proliferation.

Baltimore Sun, July 12, 2001: "Bush administration grandstands on the wrong side at U.N."
"The Bush administration's posture is the most egregious of its isolationist gestures in international affairs, some of which it later clarified into reasonable positions. The U.S. delegation can yet find that its scruples about legitimate possession of personal weapons are not compromised. The goal should be to restrain the trade in weapons that are most sought by terrorists, child armies and narco- trafficking gangsters. Surely, the President cannot object to that."

Minneapolis Star Tribune, July 13, 2001: "Stop the world's gun runners"
"It's pretty hard to see a downside to slowing the flow of grenade launchers and AK-47s to the world's civil wars. Yet somehow Bush has managed. This must be because he's looking through NRA glasses, which can make almost anything look like a threat to American gun owners."

Milwaukee Journal Sentinel, July 15, 2001: "Curtail Traffic in Small Arms"
"The U.S. cannot avoid its share of the responsibility for the proliferation of these weapons, since it is the world's leading exporter...The illegal commerce in weapons that kill a half-million people every year is- or should be - a matter of concern to our country's top arms control official. It deserved more than the facile, reflexive and ignorant scorn it received."

New York Times, July 11, 2001: "An American Retreat on Small Arms"
" ... by signaling its skepticism about small arms curbs, Washington may have stalled what little momentum there was toward broader international cooperation against the small-arms trade. His speech affords political cover for other major weapons producers like Russia and China that have been recalcitrant about effective international curbs, not to mention the cash-poor governments in Africa, Asia, and Latin America that have been their faithful customers."

Rocky Mountain News, July 11, 2001: "Tackling the Armed Brigades"
"[John Bolton] did not explain the relevance of the Second Amendment to the U.S. Constitution to the people of Sierra Leone or Indonesia. Somalia, where everyone keeps, bears and uses assault weapons, has dissolved into murderous anarchy, and the Somalis are spreading surplus weapons, and bloodshed, into neighboring Kenya ... But armed brigands, with seemingly unlimited access to modern weapons, are a world problem; if we don't like this proposed solution ... we have an obligation to present an effective proposal of our own in a cooperative spirit."

St. Louis Post-Dispatch, July 16,2001: "Exporting Bloodshed"
"As it did in rejecting the Kyoto protocol on global warming, the Antiballistic Missile Treaty and the formation of a permanent International Criminal Court for war crimes at The Hague, the Bush administration again has displayed arrogant apathy toward the rest of the world's problems and a refusal to take a more productive role in resolving them."
