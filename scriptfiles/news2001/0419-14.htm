Boise Cascade's Logging Operations Under Fire at Shareholder Meeting
First-hand Accounts from Mexico, Chile, and U.S. Expose Boise Cascade's Global Logging Disaster to Its Shareholders
   BOISE, IDAHO - April 19 - Shareholders and company executives attending today’s Boise Cascade Annual General Meeting received first-hand testimonies from an international delegation of forest and human rights activists about the path of environmental destruction and social unrest left by the corporation’s operations abroad.

Following a press conference and rally on the State House steps, forest and human rights advocates from Chile, Mexico and the U.S. appealed directly to Boise Cascade shareholders to pressure the logging company to stop exporting hit-and-run logging practices and instead end its business in old growth wood.

International speakers included Patricia Vera, International Coordinator of "Defensores del Bosque Chileno" (Defenders of the Chilean Forest), from Santiago, Chile, and Enrique Rodriquez Krebs, representing Rodolfo Montiel and the Organization of Farmer Ecologists of the Sierra de Petatlan and Coyuca de Catatlan, Guerrero, Mexico. Rodolfo Montiel is a Mexican farmer imprisoned on trumped up charges after he protested logging operations-including clear cuts to fuel Boise Cascade’s mill--near his home.

“Forest advocates from around the Americas are here to let Boise Cascade shareholders know that they are invested in an industry dinosaur, a company unwilling to adopt modern business ethics and adapt to the changes in the marketplace,” commented Jennifer Krill of the Rainforest Action Network (RAN). “Even as the company’s customers reject the use of old growth wood and front-line communities in logging areas abroad say “enough!” Boise Cascade disgracefully moves forward with business as usual.”

Boise Cascade has also come under intense public scrutiny in recent months for its role as plaintiff in a suit designed to prevent some of the country's largest remaining wilderness areas from being protected. Called the most popular federal policymaking decision in U.S. history, the roadless policy generated 1.6 million letters of support from citizens across the country, five times more than any other policy announcement.

Earlier this month RAN launched a campaign targeting 500 customers of Boise Cascade, urging each company to discontinue business relations with the company. Boise Cascade's practices run counter to the purchasing policies of many of its top customers such as Home Depot, Kinko's, Lowe's and others. More than four hundred companies-including an estimated twenty-five percent of the U.S. lumber market-have pledged to help protect the world's last remaining old growth forests by eliminating the purchase and use of wood and paper from these regions.

Yesterday Ecopledge.com announced Boise Cascade as one of their five target companies this year. Ecopledge.com, is a high-profile advocacy service that works with consumers, investors and students to “harness their power to leverage concrete changes in corporate environmental behavior” through pledging not to work for, purchase products from, or invest in companies with particularly egregious environmental track records.

Boise Cascade acts as a primary distributor of wood products from old growth forests globally. The company sells wood that has been ripped from endangered forests in the Amazon Basin, tropical rainforests in Southeast Asia, temperate rainforests in British Columbia and Chile, among other areas. According to the World Resources Institute, more than three-quarters of the world's old growth forests have already been logged and degraded, much within the past three decades.
