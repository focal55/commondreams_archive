Bluewater Network Condemns Use of Jetskis to Retrace Lewis and Clark Expedition
Corporate Publicity Stunt Makes Mockery of Historic Expedition
   SAN FRANCISCO - February 23 - Today, Bluewater Network blasted a corporate publicity stunt to retrace the epic journey of famed explorers Meriwether Lewis and William Clark with noisy and highly-polluting personal watercraft (PWC). PWC, better known by the trade name jetski are designed and marketed as adrenaline pumping thrill-craft. Unfortunately, those thrills come at the expense of air and water quality, visitor enjoyment, public health and safety, natural quiet, and fish and wildlife.

In a press release, a major PWC manufacturer states that "environmentally friendly" PWC equipped with a type of direct injected (DI) two-stroke engine will be used for the ride. However, government agencies and universities have discovered that PWC, even with the DI technology, still produce staggering amounts of air and water pollution. According to the California Air Resources Board (CARB), DI two-strokes churn out nearly ten times more smog forming pollution than a comparable four-stroke engine. In addition, CARB reports that DI machines spew into the water several highly damaging pollutants, including polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH). According to researchers at Michigan State University, PAH -even in the minute levels of parts per trillion - are toxic to plants and wildlife. As for noise, even the new technologies run under normal operating conditions can produce noise levels as high as 101 dBs - similar to conventional PWC. By comparison, a busy city street produces 85 dBs.

Moreover, prototype engines are unlikely to improve the dismal safety record of the contraptions. According to the most recent US Coast Guard data, PWC represent roughly five percent of all numbered boats on the water but are responsible for more than 30 percent of all accidents, roughly 50 percent of boat collisions, approximately 40 percent of all boating injuries and ten percent of all boating related fatalities.

"Using jetskis to retrace the path of Lewis and Clark makes about as much sense as driving a Jeep Grand Cherokee along the forced march of Cherokee Indians over the Trail of Tears," said Sean Smith, Public Lands Director for Bluewater Network. "Furthermore, given the dismal environmental performance and the horrific safety record of jetskis, one wonders if Lewis and Clark would have successfully completed their journey if they had been confronted by the craft."
