Uninsured Rolls May Rise With Layoffs
Med Student Organization Says Universal Health System Could Provide Best Solution
   RESTON, VA - February 7 - Thousands of recent corporate and manufacturing layoffs will likely increase the rolls of Americans without health insurance, according to the American Medical Student Association (AMSA), the nation's largest, independent medical student organization.

"It is unconscionable that there are more than 42 million Americans without health insurance and those ranks will only continue to swell until we take the bold, necessary step of ensuring quality health care for all," said Sindhu Srinivas, MD, AMSA (http://www.amsa.org) national president. "We continue to put forward small piecemeal proposals for segments of our population but no one deserves to be left out. It is time for true reform."

This crisis could be tackled with a national movement to create a single-payer health-care system where each and every American, regardless of employment status, would have access to comprehensive, quality health-care services.

In this type of system, patient, physician and government representatives determine the scope of coverage, under public scrutiny, rather than behind the closed doors of a corporate boardroom. This simplified model of health-care delivery significantly reduces administrative waste and allows health-care dollars to be spent on clinical services rather than profit and excess administration. Medicare has proven this by spending less than 3 percent on overhead while the private sector spends an average of 12 percent-14 percent. Sen. Paul Wellstone's (D-MN) Health Security for All Act and Rep. McDermott (D-WA) & Conyer's (D-MI) American Health Security Act are two legislative options which deserve serious consideration, and either would be more successful at true reform than distractions such as a 'patients bill of rights.'

A study conducted by The Lewin Group found the state of Maryland could save over $300 million dollars a year under a single-payer system, while covering every resident including the presently uninsured. A similar study conducted by the Massachusetts Medical Society found that state could save upwards of $1 billion dollars if it enacted a single-payer system, again, while covering everyone including the uninsured. The average American would likely see lower out-of-pocket costs for services and employers would be able to reduce health expenditures. These monetary savings could then be used to improve the health-care infrastructure.

As part of its ongoing public education campaign on this issue, AMSA recently compiled a chart comparing congressional and interest group proposals for addressing health reform, and that chart can be found online at http://www.amsa.org/lad/uhcinitiative.html.
