Hundreds Of Millions Expected To Be Added To Corps Of Engineers Budget
TCS Offers Analysis of Army Corps Budget
   WASHINGTON - June 19 - A House of Representatives appropriations committee today is expected to bust the Bush Administration's budget for the Army Corps of Engineers by more than $500 million, according to Taxpayers for Common Sense, a national budget watchdog organization.

"House appropriators have their hand stuck in the water project cookie jar," said Jeff Stein, budget analyst with Taxpayers for Common Sense. "Congress continues to spend and spend, while the Administration wants them to go on a diet."

The Corps of Engineers has argued that its budget was cut more than any other U.S. agency's was. However, the Administration's request of $3.9 billion is roughly comparable to recent budget requests for the Corps. The Administration proposes a budget that is actually a 2.4 percent increase over the $3.81 billion average spent on the Corps from FY 1995-2001, or taking into account inflation, a 2.5 percent spending cut.

The President's budget recognizes that the Army Corps of Engineers is an agency in need of reform. In the last year, the Corps of Engineers has been rocked by scandals. Three senior Corps officials were reprimanded for manipulating the results of a major study to determine whether a $1.2 billion lock expansion project on the Upper Mississippi River was justified. At the same time the agency's construction backlog swelled to $54 billion -- 40 times its annual construction budget.

The Bush budget allocates funds for 1,350 earmarked projects at a cumulative cost of $3.58 billion, a 10 percent reduction from last year's total funding. Hundreds of new projects are expected to be added in today appropriations committee meeting.

"There is strong bipartisan support to bust the budget," continued Stein. "Nothing will get in the way of this political pork parade."

Several Members of Congress have suggested that the truncated transition following the election caused the budget cuts because new Administration officials had less time to thoroughly evaluate the nation's water resources needs. However, the TCS analysis found that the Corps budget is well thought out and follows priorities as set out in President Bush's budget blueprint, which was first released in early February. The budget proposes several key reforms:

-- Shifts funds away from low priority, underused waterways. Savings: $62,000,000.

-- Reduces the federal cost-share requirement for beach sand-pumping projects from 65 percent to 35 percent. For the first time in five years allocates funding for beach projects in an Administration's budget request, funding 49 beach projects. Overall savings on beach projects: $23,000,000.

-- Does not fund wastewater treatment and water supply, also known as environmental infrastructure projects. Savings: $90,000,000.

-- Does not allocate funding for 25 of the nation's most wasteful and unnecessary water projects. Savings: $79,547,000.

"By increasing the budget, Congress is sending the message that when an agency cooks up numbers and breaks the rules, Congress will reward them with more money," concluded Stein. "That's a ridiculous way to way policy."

An analysis of the Bush Administration's Army Corps budget and a state-by-state comparison of all Corps project funding are available at http://www.taxpayer.net/corpswatch.
