DOE Readies Attack On EPA Diesel Fuel Cleanup
But Exxon-Mobil Profits Show `Crying Poor' Is Bogus
  WASHINGTON - April 23 - The Energy Information Administration, a branch of the U.S. Department of Energy, is said to be readying a new attack on the Environmental Protection Agency's low-sulfur diesel standards.

But Exxon-Mobil's new profit information out today -- showing a huge increase in profits in part due to refineries -- may undercut any new claim that the oil industry can't afford to clean up its dirty diesel fuel.

Here's what we know: By May 4, the Energy Information Administration is expected to issue a new analysis of the low-sulfur diesel rules issued by the Clinton Administration and reaffirmed by current EPA Administrator Christie Whitman.

As you may recall, some staffers within DOE bitterly opposed the diesel cleanup, and it is conceivable that this new, tightly held report is the starting gun in yet another effort to agitate against the standards.

(You may also recall that the Energy Information Administration, or EIA, is same agency that did the study cited by President Bush as his "new information" reason for flip-flopping on carbon dioxide controls. We also know that the Bush Administration -- while taking credit for not killing the Clinton diesel cleanup -- has winked to the oil industry that it is open to taking another look at the diesel rule in the future.)

On its carbon dioxide report, the EIA was subject to harsh criticism for using flawed assumptions that lead to an overstatement of the control costs. We recommend that you closely examine the assumptions in the upcoming diesel report.

For example, the oil industry has been crying poor ever since the EPA first noted several years ago that it would seek to clean up diesel fuel. The costs will be so high -- so goes the industry patter -- that oil companies will close down refineries and that will lead to fuel shortages and the resulting Armageddon.

Will the energy agency repeat this misinformation -- or will it consider new technologies that accomplish the cleanup at a much lower cost than previous estimates?

Several new lower-cost methods were unveiled last Month at the annual meeting of the National Petrochemical and Refiners Association. For example, a new process by SulphCo, Inc. of Nevada could remove the sulfur from diesel fuel for a mere 2.5 cents per gallon - only half the cost estimated by EPA and far below the even-higher costs projected by the doomsday-sayers in the oil industry.

Regarding the industry's alleged poverty, note that Exxon-Mobil today reported that its first-quarter profits increased 51 percent -- to a whopping $5.1 billion - on the strength of refining margins and higher natural gas prices. (It also might be worth thinking about those profits when you look at costs at the pump this summer and hear the industry blame those awful clean gasoline standards.)

We will be sure to update you on this energy agency study.
