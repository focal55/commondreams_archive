Military Experts Critical of Administration's Pentagon Spending Plan
   WASHINGTON - February 28 - Experts at the Center For Defense Information (CDI), anindependent research organization which monitors military affairs, today questioned the Bush Administration's decision to add billions of dollars in Pentagon spending.

"This budget returns us to Cold War spending levels," said Colonel Daniel Smith, USA (Ret.), CDI's Director of Research. "The U.S. already spends substantially more for military forces than any other nation, with no significant threats to our national security. We should be looking at options for reducing military spending, not increasing it."

The Bush Administration is requesting $310.5 billion for the Defense Department in Fiscal Year 2002. They are also requesting $14.3 billion for the defense functions of the Department of Energy. In all, the Pentagon is seeking $324.8 billion in military spending, $14.2 billion more than last year.

"This is just the first round in the bidding," said Christopher Hellman, CDI's budget specialist. "Congress has yet to work its will, and then there is any supplemental spending that might be added next year, something that has become an annual event. I'm confident that by the time we close the books on Fiscal Year 2002 we'll see $10 billion to $15 billion more in the Pentagon's budget, above the increases requested by the Administration."

As part of the proposed increase, the Administration is including $1.4 billion for pay and benefits and $400 million for improved housing. The request also includes an additional $2.6 billion for research and development.

"Additional funding for research and development is a good investment, both for the military and the country," said Hellman. "Pay and benefits are an important component of attracting and retaining quality personnel, which are the cornerstone of our military. To be honest, given the extent of the military's housing problems, $400 million won't get you very far. But at least it's a start. But the fact is, there's plenty of room in the current Pentagon budget to fund these initiatives. Further funding increases aren't needed."

The Administration is also seeking additional military base closures, something that the Pentagon has requested – and Congress denied – for each of the past several years.

"For political reasons, in the past base closures were 'dead on arrival' on Capitol Hill," said Colonel Smith. "But there's savings to be had here. Hopefully the political dynamic has changed."
