“Mandatory" Cruise Line Standards Are Sleight of Hand
Viewed As Tactical Gesture By Environmentalists
   SAN FRANCISCO - February 5 - The “mandatory” environmental standards announced by the International Council of Cruise Lines (ICCL) today are little more than a savvy public relations maneuver by the cruise industry, according to the environmental group Bluewater Network.

“Today’s announcement, while a heartening sign of increased environmental awareness by the cruise ship industry, is a tactical gesture that must be understood in context they are squirming because they are under the regulatory microscope right now, and are trying to stave off the heightened oversight and stricter regulation of cruise ship pollution that will hopefully ensue,” says Kira Schmidt, director of Bluewater Network’s Cruise Ship Campaign. “The standards, which are weaker than those which are likely to be required under new regulations, lack teeth because they are completely unenforceable, and the punishment for violation -- being kicked out of the ICCL -- is a meager deterrent at best.”

Bluewater led a coalition of 55 environmental groups in a March 2000 petition urging the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to address gaps and loopholes in environmental laws which have essentially given cruise ships a license to pollute. In response, the EPA established a formal process to examine the cruise ship pollution problem, and after three public hearings and lengthy inter-agency consultation, is poised to release its Assessment Report later this month, which is likely to recommend certain regulatory measures. In addition, Congress passed legislation in December which restricts the discharge of sewage and waste water from cruise ships in Alaska waters and requires the Coast Guard and EPA to establish monitoring, sampling and effluent standards. The California legislature also passed a state law, sponsored by Bluewater Network, to more closely monitor cruise ship pollution. A similar bill is currently before the Alaska legislature. The cruise ship industry has lobbied fiercely against all of these efforts at increased regulation.

“It is commendable that the ICCL cruise lines are finally addressing their environmental impacts by seeking to improve environmental performance through voluntary guidelines, but it is not enough. Self-regulation by industry, particularly by one such as the cruise industry, which has a history of egregious environmental violations, will not provide the necessary protection for our environment,” says Schmidt. “Voluntary industry standards must be complemented by strong environmental regulations with stringent oversight and enforcement mechanisms. The cruise industry has evaded regulatory and public scrutiny for too long, but those days are over, and no announcement of self-regulation will turn this tide. With the current development of stricter laws to control cruise ship pollution, the oceans will finally receive the protection they deserve.”

Ms. Schmidt also points out that ICCL represents less than two-thirds of the cruise ships operating in North America, so many cruise ships will not be following the standards.
