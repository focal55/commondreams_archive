Civil Liberties, Civil Rights, Religious and Labor Groups Urge Bush to Reject Religious Discrimination in 'Faith-Based' Initiative
White House Plan To Aid 'Faith-Based' Charities Sparks Growing Concern
   WASHINGTON - January 31 - George W. Bush's plan to set up a massive federal aid program for "faith-based" charities is drawing increasing fire from religious, civil rights, civil liberties and labor groups.

In a Jan. 30 joint letter to Bush, 19 national organizations warned that the proposal raises "constitutional and policy concerns" and urged the president to forbid religious discrimination at faith-based agencies that accept federal funds.

"Specifically," the groups said, "we seek your commitment that your proposals will make clear that religious institutions that receive public funding for the provision of social services may not discriminate when employing people in taxpayer funded positions based on an applicant's religion or the religious beliefs of the employer."

Signers of the letter included the NAACP, the Leadership Conference on Civil Rights, Americans United for Separation of Church and State, the American Civil Liberties Union, the National Education Association, the American Counseling Association and the Baptist Joint Committee on Public Affairs.

The Rev. Barry W. Lynn, executive director of Americans United for Separation of Church and State, said concern about the Bush "faith-based" plan is growing, even among the religious leaders.

"The Bush proposal is sparking a firestorm of controversy," said AU's Lynn. "I don't think President Bush knew how much opposition there would be from so many different kinds of groups across the spectrum."

At a press conference yesterday, leaders from the Catholic, Protestant and Jewish communities criticized the president's proposal. They warned that it violates church-state separation and puts needy people at risk of religious pressures when they seek government help.

Lynn noted that Bush is now having to work hard to try to recruit support for his plan. Today he met with representatives of Catholic Charities. Many religious leaders, however, are worried about the Bush concept.

"Bush is meeting strong resistance to his sales pitch on this," said Lynn.  "Many religious leaders are very uncomfortable with the idea that they should compete with each other for a slice of the funding pie."

Americans United is a religious liberty watchdog group based in Washington, D.C. Founded in 1947, the organization represents 60,000 members and allied houses of worship in all 50 states.
