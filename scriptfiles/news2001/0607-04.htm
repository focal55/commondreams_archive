In Congress Today Global AIDS Alliance Calls for Massive Fight to Stop AIDS in Impoverished Countries
   WASHINGTON - June 7 - In Congressional testimony today, Dr. Paul Zeitz, a medical doctor with a specialization in international public health and epidemiology, called for a massive increase in U.S. government action to stop AIDS, particularly in Africa. He called for a comprehensive response, citing the need for deeper debt cancellation to free up additional resources to fight AIDS and a multibillion dollar emergency appropriation from the U.S. Congress. He also called for access to AIDS medications including through generics and global bulk purchasing.

Dr. Zeitz represents the Global AIDS Alliance, a broad, international coalition of religious, humanitarian, consumer and social justice groups, many of which are active in providing services in Africa and other regions in impacted by AIDS.

"The years I worked in Zambia brought home to me the reality of the AIDS crisis. My Zambian work colleagues started dying," Dr. Zeitz told the House Committee on International Relations. "I was shocked and devastated when I learned that the AIDS death rates that I was observing won't peak for another five years until 2005 and then they will stay high for at least another 10 to 15 years. The international and the U.S. response is failing."

Zeitz said USAID and other agencies need the resources to do their job. "Bipartisan Congressional support is required to ensure that new and supplementary resources are appropriated for USAID and other Agencies. The low level of U.S. foreign assistance persists in spite of widespread support among the American people for greatly increased assistance to impoverished countries, as documented in several recent surveys. We can and must turn this around."

Zeitz praised the establishment of the Global AIDS and Health Fund. "The United States should commit to provide its share of the cost of the $10 billion per year Global AIDS and Health Fund by appropriating $3 billion this year. President Bush's recent announcement of a $200 million contribution to this Fund, is a small start, but remains woefully inadequate." Zeitz also voiced concern about a top-down approach to date. "So far, civil society has had a limited and marginalized role in discussion regarding the Fund."

Dr. Zeitz also called for full access to treatments. "It is outrageous from both a practical and a moral standpoint that more than 99 percent of Africans are not able to obtain practical life-saving medications. With access to antiretroviral medications, prevention is more likely to succeed. And, we can keep parents alive to raise their children."

Of the 23 countries that received HIPC debt relief so far, 15 still have annual debt service payments higher than what the country is currently able to spend on basic health care. "That doesn't make sense from a public health standpoint. Immediate debt cancellation by the IMF and the World Bank for countries enrolled in the HIPC program could provide over half a billion dollars per year for the HIV/AIDS fight. This would cost the American taxpayer nothing and would not negatively affect the lending ability of these institutions."
