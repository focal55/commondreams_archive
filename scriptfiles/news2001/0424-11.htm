Quebec Summit Long on Rhetoric, Short on Results, WWF Says
  WASHINGTON - April 24 - The final declaration of the just concluded Summit of the Americas shows a disappointing lack of commitment to incorporating social or environmental concerns into free trade negotiations, WWF said today.

While the declaration talks about balancing economic development, social development and the protection of the environment, WWF noted that it carefully avoids any direct commitment to including these issues in the Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA) negotiations themselves. The language of the Quebec Declaration appears to contradict a statement signed by Western Hemisphere trade ministers just a few weeks prior to the Quebec Summit. That statement, released on April 7th in Buenos Aires, declared that the FTAA negotiations themselves would take account of broad social and economic issues, including environmental protection. The Quebec Declaration thus leaves in doubt whether hemispheric leaders have embraced the need to address conservation and sustainable development in the context of "free trade."

"The results in Quebec reveal how far we still have to go if we are to make trade in the Americas serve the real needs of people and of our planet," said David Schorr, director of WWF's Sustainable Commerce Program. "We are still a long way from getting a commitment to sustainable trade in the FTAA."

Some of the rhetoric surrounding the Quebec City summit was more hopeful. President Bush told the assembled leaders that the commitment to open trade under the FTAA "must be matched by a strong commitment to protecting our environment." The Bush administration also announced it would continue the Clinton Administration's policy of conducting environmental assessments of trade agreements.

"It is clear -- and it is a welcome sign -- that the Bush administration has not simply written off environmental concerns in the context of the FTAA," said Schorr. "But words need to be matched by serious actions to put conservation and sustainable development at the core of trade negotiating goals."

WWF noted with particular concern that the Quebec Declaration repeats the assertion that increased trade will automatically lead to prosperity and ultimately to an improved environment.

"The 'grow now, clean up later' approach has been widely discredited among serious environmental economists," said Schorr. "It's just crazy to think that all you have to do to achieve sustainable development is open your borders and sit back while the private market takes care of the rest."

WWF has long called for a balanced approach to trade and environment, and believes that the proposed Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA) potentially offers important opportunities to achieve real gains for conservation and sustainable development. Unfortunately, the FTAA negotiations so far have kept trade talks isolated from environmental considerations, and from public input. Protests and riots in the streets of Quebec were the predictable result.

"While WWF does not condone violent protest, the refusal of governments to open the FTAA process to the public is completely unacceptable," said Schorr. "Trade policies remain far too subservient to the interests of a wealthy commercial elite. If the only way people can make their legitimate voices heard is in the streets, then governments will need to get used to diplomatic dinners scented with the odor of tear gas."

In a statement released during the Summit, WWF laid out five tests the FTAA must meet if it is to truly promote sustainable development. The FTAA should:

1. Deliver concrete benefits for conservation and sustainable development,
2. Strengthen good environmental governance,
3. Avoid anti-environmental biases, such as those found in NAFTA's controversial investment rules,
4. Embrace and open, balanced and participatory processes, and
5. Build capacity in developing countries.

"If the FTAA is to help address the real needs of the Western Hemisphere -- its people and its ecosystems -- governments across the region will have to go beyond rhetoric to embrace a process that delivers real benefits for communities and for the environment," said Schorr.
