Take Part in an Important Biological Survey in the Central African Republic -- Without Ever Leaving Your Desk
   WASHINGTON - May 7 - World Wildlife Fund is leading a team of animal, plant and insect researchers into the heart of the Dzanga-Sangha Reserve in the Central African Republic. Follow the scientists in the field from the WWF website at http://www.worldwildlife.org/expeditions as they plunge through the rainforests of the CAR to take a census of animals from ants to elephants.

All month long, the team will perform a biological inventory of the reserve's wildlife and plant life, trudging through the mud and fending off mosquitoes to analyze the threats and opportunities facing one of the world's most important and remote forest regions. Daily dispatches from the field will give a feel of what it's like to actually be a part of the scientific team.

The Dzanga-Sangha Reserve is a jewel in the crown of the Northwestern Congolian Lowland Forest Ecoregion, a WWF Global 200 site that spans parts of Gabon, The Congo Republic, Cameroon, CAR and the Democratic Republic of Congo. The reserve is one of the last remaining undisturbed lowland tropical forests in Africa and home to one of the continent's largest intact forest elephant populations. Also present are western lowland gorillas, giant forest hogs, bush pigs, bongos, African forest buffaloes and six species of duiker. Sixteen of CAR's 20 primate species exist here, ranging from dwarf galagos to huge gorillas. It is also home to the BaAka pygmies, indigenous hunter-gatherers who rarely grow taller than five feet. In helping establish and protect the reserve, WWF works closely with the BaAka on conservation, health and education programs.

The expedition team consists of experts in mammals, primates, birds, reptiles, amphibians, insects and plants, as well as local biologists and students who are being trained in biological inventory techniques.

The team will use a variety of assessment techniques including mist netting, ultraviolet light trapping, Winkler bag extraction of leaf litter, and recording of amphibian and primate noises. The team will also use classic journalistic "gumshoe" methods of questioning local people about the presence of primates and other species in the area.
