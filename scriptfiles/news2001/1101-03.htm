Nuclear Fright on Halloween, Says Friends of the Earth
House Committee Passes Nuclear Disaster Insurance Bill
   WASHINGTON - November 1 - A key Congressional Committee passed Halloween legislation yesterday that frightens environmentalists and taxpayers, according to Friends of the Earth. The bill guarantees heavy government involvement in the nuclear power industry and puts taxpayers on the hook in the case of a nuclear disaster.

The Energy and Commerce Committee of the House of Representatives passed a bill to reauthorize the Price Anderson Act for 15 years. The legislation limits the liability of nuclear power plant owners for damage associated with nuclear accidents. This action comes at a time of unprecedented concern over the security of nuclear power plants in light of the airplane attacks of September 11.

"Nuclear power came masquerading to Congress as a safe, clean, and reliable energy source," said Erich Pica, Economic Policy Analyst at Friends of the Earth. "But the events of September 11 show that the disguise hides vulnerable, dangerous terrorist targets in 103 communities around the United States. If any of these facilities were attacked, God forbid, the health and well-being of millions of Americans could be at risk."

Yesterday, the Federal Aviation Administration announced a 10 nautical mile no-fly zone around existing nuclear power plants and other Department of Energy facilities. This follows announcements by governors in Connecticut, New York, New Jersey and Massachusetts to deploy National Guard troops at nuclear facilities.

"The federal government and five governors understand the risk of nuclear power plants," continued Pica. "But apparently the Energy and Commerce Committee got a group rate on ostrich costumes and is sticking its head in the ground."

Rep. Edward Markey (D-Mass.) successfully attached an amendment to the bill that would require the President to study the potentially threats to nuclear power facilities, and mandate the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to take corrective action.

Originally enacted by Congress in 1957, the Price-Anderson Act is the largest federal subsidy to the nuclear industry. It limits the liability of the nuclear industry in the event of a nuclear accident in the United States. The Act covers large power reactors as well as small research and test reactors, fuel reprocessing plants and enrichment facilities. Price Anderson also covers incidents that occur through operation of nuclear plants as well as transportation and storage of nuclear fuel and radioactive wastes.
