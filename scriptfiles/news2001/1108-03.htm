Sierra Club Wins Legal Battle for Labor & Environment
Judge Agrees that Bush Executive Order Breaks Federal Labor Law
  RICHMOND, CA - November 8 - The Sierra Club, the State Building and Construction Trades Council of California of AFL-CIO, the National Economic Development & Law Center, and the Office of the Mayor of Richmond celebrated a judge's decision yesterday that overturned an executive order banning the use of Project Labor Agreements that fund major construction projects.

The Sierra Club and National Economic Development and Law Council, represented pro bono by Milberg, Weiss, Bershad, Hynes & Lerach, filed an amicus brief to a lawsuit put forth by the City of Richmond and Building and Construction Trades Department in Washington, DC. The lawsuit challenged a Bush Administration Executive Order (13202) issued on February 22, 2001 that barred federal agencies from using project labor agreements (PLAs) on major construction projects. Judge Emmet G. Sullivan issued an order Wednesday holding that the Executive Order was unconstitutional in that Bush exceeded his authority to issue the order and because the subject matter of the order was preempted by the National Labor Relations Act.

"The Sierra Club is delighted with the federal court's ruling supporting the use of Project Labor Agreements," said Tim Frank, chair of the Sierra Club Challenge to Sprawl Committee. "If we want our cities to thrive, we need to clean them up and bring in quality development. Project Labor Agreements assure quality environmental clean-up projects."

The Sierra Club and State Building Trades pointed out that by facilitating the coordinated use of skilled labor on complex projects, Project Labor Agreements reduce cost, save time and increase the quality of the work done. This is essential in environmental clean-up projects. The superior environmental training of workers and the reduced time and cost all help make it possible for communities to clean up toxic sites and redevelop blighted areas.

One project that was affected by the original Executive Order was the Richmond Transit Village. This housing development would provide increased transportation options to thousands of Richmond residents, limit suburban sprawl, reduce the number of cars on the road, and decrease air pollution.
