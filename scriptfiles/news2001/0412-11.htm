Bush Administration Does Right's Bidding On Contraceptives
   WASHINGTON - April 12 - With a chilling move that seems to signal a new government willingness to abridge privacy rights and interfere with access to even the most widely accepted forms of birth control, President Bush has stunned women and individual rights advocates across the nation. His budget proposal to stop requiring health insurers to provide government employees with a full range of contraceptive options could sharply limit access to birth control, and not only for millions of women employed by the government. It could also pave the way for a similar rollback among private sector employers.

"If there was any shred of doubt that the radical right is calling the shots in the White House, this action dispels them," said Ralph G. Neas, president of People For the American Way. "The far right wants to turn back the clock to the days not only before legal and safe abortion but even before birth control pills, which they claim are a kind of abortion. This action by President Bush is the first major and dangerous step down that road."

Opposition to contraceptive methods such as IUDs and birth control pills is a long-held ultra-right tenet. This issue arose during the confirmation battle over Attorney General John Ashcroft because of his avid advocacy as a U.S. Senator of both a constitutional amendment and federal legislation so extreme that they would have banned IUDs and birth control pills as well as abortions.

Ashcroft's advocacy as a senator also included attempts to limit women's access to birth control. In 1998, he was one of just eight senators, including Jesse Helms and Bob Smith, who signed a letter opposing pending legislation to require federal employee health insurance plans to cover the cost of prescription contraceptives. The letter states: "we are concerned with what appears to be a loophole in the legislation regarding contraceptives that upon failing to prevent fertilization, act de facto as abortifacients. Therefore we believe this amendment is a precedent setting attempt to mandate coverage of other abortifacients." Ashcroft and the other letter-signers urged that the provision requiring coverage of contraceptives be dropped. By building a birth control exception into the proposed budget, the White House is taking a major step toward the extreme policy that the far right tried and failed to institute just three years ago.

"Now that we have far right advocates like John Ashcroft shaping the nation's policy, we can see that the 'compassionate conservative' badge that George W. Bush wore so proudly as a campaigner was no more real than the emperor's clothes," said Neas.
