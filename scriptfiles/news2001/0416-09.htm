American Forests Earth Day Progress Report:
'A Million Trees for Earth Day'
Public Urged to Help As Deadline Approaches
   WASHINGTON - April 16 - American Forests (americanforests.org) is in the home stretch of its "A Million Trees for Earth Day" campaign, which urges people everywhere to help plant a million trees for Earth Day, April 22. Whether they plant trees in their yards or communities, or in one of American Forests' Global ReLeaf ecosystem restoration projects, the organization is encouraging everyone to do something good for the planet on Earth Day. The "Million Trees" campaign runs from March 1 - May 1.

"The Internet gives people the power to take a direct action this Earth Day that will literally last a lifetime," said Deborah Gangloff, executive director of American Forests. "The simple act of planting 10, 25, or 100 Global ReLeaf trees allows anyone to make real and measurable impacts on environmental concerns ranging from global warming, to the loss of wildlife habitat, to air and water pollution."

The public can plant Global ReLeaf trees through American Forests' website, www.americanforests.org. Every $1 donated plants one native tree in an ecosystem restoration project. Trees can also be planted by calling 1-800-545-TREE, or by mail at American Forests, PO Box 2000, Washington, DC, 20013.

From helping to regenerate forests burned by wildfire in California to planting trees in war-torn Sarajevo, to restoring habitat for Siberian tigers in the Russian Far East, American Forests is mobilizing people and organizations of all kinds to improve local and global conditions for generations to come.

Students in hundreds of classrooms are helping to plant Global ReLeaf trees in conjunction with American Forests' environmental education program, A Tree for Every Child. Schools that plant 100 trees in Global ReLeaf restoration projects receive an American Forests Famous & Historic Tree to plant at their school or in the community. The public can also order Famous & Historic Trees to plant at home for Earth Day.

Companies and businesses of all sizes are participating in "A Million Trees for Earth Day." On April 11, 40 students from Benjamin High School in Los Angeles helped dozens of IKEA employees plant hundreds of saplings in Angeles National Forest. The planting of live oak, canyon oak, and holly leaf cherry tree saplings will help regenerate the forest. Home furnishings retailer IKEA sponsored the ecosystem restoration project conducted through American Forests' Wildfire ReLeaf program, which plants native trees in burned areas.

Thousands of trees are being planted as part of "A Million Trees for Earth Day" by customers of Eddie Bauer through the specialty retailer's Add a dollar, Plant a tree spring campaign at stores, online at www.eddiebauer.com, and through its catalogs.

Home products manufacturer Triangle-Pacific is planting 200,000 trees as part of its support of "A Million Trees for Earth Day." Shaklee Corporation, which manufactures and distributes health and wellness products, is encouraging its 500,000 distributors across the United States to plant trees; Batteries Plus and Anheuser-Busch each are planting 20,000 trees as well.

At www.americanforests.org people will find information ranging from the best way to plant a tree in their yard to an easy way to plant trees for Global ReLeaf in one of 50 ecosystem restoration projects underway this year. Every dollar donated online plants a native tree and counts toward the million-tree goal. Individuals can also log in the trees they plant at home or in community action projects.

Since 1990, American Forests has been planting trees for Global ReLeaf in areas damaged by human or natural causes. Global ReLeaf has planted trees in more than 500 projects and extends through every state and territory in America, as well as 21 countries worldwide. In 1996 the organization set a goal to plant 20 million trees for the new millennium. To date more than 17 million trees have been planted and American Forests expects to plant the 20 millionth this fall.

American Forests, founded in 1875, is the oldest national nonprofit citizen conservation organization. Its three Centers -- Global ReLeaf, Urban Forest, and Forest Policy -- mobilize people around the world to improve the environment by planting and caring for trees. American Forests publishes American Forests magazine and can be found on the Internet at www.americanforests.org.
