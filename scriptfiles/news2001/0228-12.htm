Clean Air Trust Hails EPA Decision to Move Forward with Diesel Cleanup
  WASHINGTON - February 28 - The non-profit Clean Air Trust today welcomed Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Christine Todd Whitman's decision to move forward with a plan to clean up big diesel trucks and buses and diesel fuel.

"Every American will breathe easier because of this decision," noted Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Trust. "It will mean fewer asthma attacks in children, fewer cases of chronic bronchitis and other breathing problems, and fewer people dying prematurely."

O'Donnell noted that the EPA itself has calculated that the diesel cleanup would prevent more than 8,000 premature deaths and 360,000 asthma attacks each year. The diesel cleanup would produce the clean-air equivalent of removing 13 million of today's trucks off the road.

The EPA plan was announced by former Administrator Carol Browner last December. It would reduce pollution by new big diesel trucks and buses by up to 95 percent by 2007. To enable use of high-tech pollution control devices, EPA also would require refiners to remove 97 percent of the sulfur from highway diesel fuel. Sulfur is a poison, much as lead was a poison to automobile catalytic converters.

The cleanup plan had been thrown into doubt on Jan. 20, when White House Chief of Staff Andrew Card ordered federal agencies to review recent major rules, including the diesel cleanup. New EPA Administrator Whitman reviewed the rule closely and agreed that it is needed to protect public health and that it can be achieved at a reasonable cost.

O'Donnell noted that yesterday, the U.S. Supreme Court upheld EPA's national health standards for fine particle soot, a pollutant linked to tens of thousands of premature deaths annually as well as other breathing problems.

"Diesel trucks and buses are major sources of fine particle soot," O'Donnell noted. "The Supreme Court ruling underscored the urgent need to clean up this dangerous pollution."

O'Donnell noted that petroleum refiners have sued to block the diesel cleanup. He said health and environmental groups hope that the EPA and Justice Department will "vigorously defend" the new standards in court. He added that health and environmental groups would soon announce they intend to intervene in court on EPA's behalf.
