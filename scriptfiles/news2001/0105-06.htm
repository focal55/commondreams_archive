President Clinton's Final Decision on Wild Forest Protection Plan is a Conservation Victory
  WASHINGTON - January 5 - The final decision on the plan to protect roadless areas in national forests that the Clinton Administration will announce Friday afternoon is a conservation victory as grand as the trees it will protect, according to Tiernan Sittenfeld, U.S. PIRG Conservation Advocate.

"We are pleased that President Clinton and the Forest Service have listened to the American public and responded to the overwhelming public mandate to protect our last pristine wild forests," said Sittenfeld. "Our grandchildren will praise President Clinton for protecting our wild heritage for future generations."

The new Roadless Area Conservation Rule will protect 58.5 million acres of America's last wild places from destructive activities like road building and most commercial logging. "This conservation plan is one of the most sweeping environmental initiatives in decades and will ensure that our national forests continue to provide clean water for communities across the country, recreational opportunities for millions of people, and critical habitat for rare and endangered species," said Sittenfeld.

Sittenfeld warned that special interests and their allies in Congress have already threatened to roll back the new wild forest protection plan in spite of the two million Americans who commented in support of the policy. Sittenfeld cited recent statements by Shawn Keough, executive director of Associated Logging Contractors, "To allow a policy like this to go unchallenged would be a travesty". In addition, Rep. John E. Peterson (R-PA) has stated, "When the roadless policy comes before Congress for consideration, as is expected early next year, I will do everything in my power to stop it".

"Given the overwhelming public support for protecting our last wild places, it would be wrong for some members of Congress to side with the special interests instead of their constituents. We urge members of Congress to defend this new rule in order to save our natural heritage for future generations," concluded Sittenfeld.
