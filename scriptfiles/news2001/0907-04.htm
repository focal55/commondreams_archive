Number Of Minority Students Opting For A Medical Career Up
A Relief But Not A Solution, Says AMSA
  RESTON, VA - September 7 - For the first time in five years, the number of minority applicants to medical schools has increased, according to a study in the Journal of the American Medical Association. 4266 students from underrepresented minority groups (black, Native American, Mexican American and Mainland Puerto Rican) applied to the entering class in 2000, an increase of 2 percent from 1999 figures.

"After watching this troubling trend over the past five years, we are heartened to see even this slight increase," said Jaya Agrawal, national president of the American Medical Student Association (AMSA), the nation's largest, independent medical student organization. Agrawal is a fourth-year medical student at Brown University. Studies indicate that minority physicians are 40 percent or more likely to practice in underserved communities, according to Agrawal. "Unfortunately, too many of these communities are already woefully underserved by physicians, getting their care in hospitals and clinics that are severely under-funded and under-staffed, and too often go without the latest in technology," Agrawal continued.

Though the reasons for the increase are not clearly defined by the study, AMSA (www.amsa.org) congratulates the nation's medical schools on their efforts, and especially applauds the efforts of local youth 'pipeline' programs that work to interest young underrepresented minority students in the health sciences and medicine.

AMSA, however, remains concerned that academic medicine might lose even this modest momentum in light of a wavering national commitment to affirmative action and the diminishing support of student aid targeted at those with exceptional financial needs. Especially troubling are recent reports about the decline in federal support for the National Health Service Corps and its loan repayment program, designed to support students who have an interest in practicing in underserved areas. These proven successful programs fill a vital need that serves many thousands of Americans every day and continuing and expanding these services can only serve to combat health care access and disparity issues nationwide.

AMSA calls on all the stakeholders-medical schools, minority communities, along with state and federal agencies-to make a concerted national effort to plan and coordinate a full-court press on this issue. "We must work together on many fronts toward our common vision of equal and fair health care for all," concluded Agrawal.
