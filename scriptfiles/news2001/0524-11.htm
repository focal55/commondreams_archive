A Welcome Change: Jeffords' Switch Places New Hurdles For Missile Defense Deployment
  WASHINGTON - May 24 - The Council for a Livable World today welcomed Vermont Senator James Jeffords' abandoning the Republican party because it may delay President Bush's goal of deploying quickly a national missile defense system.

Democratic control of the Senate will make it more difficult -- but certainly not impossible -- for President Bush to deploy an anti-missile system. Both incoming Senate Armed Services Committee Chairman Carl Levin and Senate Foreign Relations Committee Chairman Joseph Biden have already been critical of the President's missile defense plans. If the President approves construction this year at the Alaskan radar site, the first step of a missile defense system, he will have to overcome the opposition of Chairman Levin and other Democrats.

The Council pointed out that these two Senators, plus Majority Leader Tom Daschle critically responded to the President's May 1 missile defense speech at National Defense University.

"This abrupt change in leadership places significant hurdles in President Bush's hellbent drive to deploy a national anti-missile system," said John Isaacs, President of the Council for a Livable World, a Washington, D.C.-based pro-arms control group. "With Senators Daschle, Biden and Levin leading the fight to prevent rapid deployment of an ABM system, we have a much better chance of winning."

Arms control treaties, opposed by many Republicans, including Sen. Jesse Helms (R-NC), outgoing chairman of the Foreign Relations Committee, will receive more sympathetic treatment in the Armed Services and Foreign Relations Committees. Sens. Biden and Levin are likely to hold hearings and feature witnesses critical of abandoning the ABM Treaty and proceeding with missile defense.

"It is also important," continued Isaacs, "that Sen Jeffords cited missile defense as one of the major issues on which he disagrees with the Republican position. In 1998, Senator Jeffords voted twice with the Republican leadership of missile defense procedural votes when Republicans tried to force a vote on the issue.

While the Democratic Senate is likely to continue appropriating large sums for missile defense research and testing, it will be more resistant to deployment funding and Defense Secretary Rumsfeld's anticipated requests for arms in space.

"While there has not been a major change in votes, Democrats now control the Senate's legislative agenda, set up hearings and invite the bulk of the witnesses. This will definitely put a crimp in Bush's Star Wars plans," added Isaacs.
