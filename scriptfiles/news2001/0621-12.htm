Global HIV/AIDS Fund Flawed Without Deeper Debt Cancellation
  WASHINGTON - June 21 - The international community cannot address the HIV crisis in the poorest countries without also agreeing to cancel more debt, campaigners said today.

With dignitaries gathering in New York for the United Nations special session on AIDS next week, the Drop the Debt campaign released new figures showing that debt cancellation could have a dramatic effect through releasing in-country resources to repair deteriorating social infrastructure. The figures show how deeper debt cancellation would help improve the effectiveness of funds later released through the Trust Fund.

-- Sub-Saharan Africa spends $37 million per day repaying debts. Therefore the $200 million grant by the U.S. to the Global Health Fund is repaid in less than a week.

-- In Malawi, thirty per cent of school teachers are infected with HIV. One day's worth of debt repayments would allow 160 new teachers to be trained.

-- In Zambia, where one child in seven is an orphan because of HIV, four days of debt repayments could cover the annual costs of housing and feeding 10,000 children. This year, Zambia will pay 20 times as much on debt as on dedicated HIV spending.

-- The $14.2 billion Sub-Saharan Africa spends on debt per year is considerably more than the $7 to 10 billion the UN estimate is needed each year to combat HIV/AIDS.

Over the last decade, the heavy burden of debt in many poor countries has prevented adequate levels of investment in health and education systems, clearing the way for the spread of HIV. For example, for most of the 1990s, Mozambique spent $5 per capita on health care, while $9 per capita went to foreign creditors. There is now just one hospital bed for every thousand people, while over ten percent of the adult population has HIV/AIDS.

Where debt service payments have been reduced in Africa, the benefits are clear. In Uganda, twice as many children are going to school; in Mozambique health spending is rising by $13 million a year. However, even the countries that have benefited from the international debt package are still spending more on debt than health.

Lucy Matthew of Drop the Debt said: "Deeper debt cancellation will inject the resources needed to revive health and education systems in Africa. Funds freed this way will repair and then pump-prime basic infrastructure, making subsequent resources from the Trust Fund far more effective. This crisis demands a more carefully designed rebuilding of infrastructure in Africa -- or funds, goodwill and above all lives will be wasted."

--- Notes

-- 5,500 Africans die each day from HIV related diseases. The UN estimate that by 2010 there will be 40 million AIDS orphans. 25 million African's are infected with HIV.

-- Core contributions to the Global Health Fund so far are $200 million from the U.S., $140 million from France, and $100 million from the Bill Gates Foundation. Other countries are expected to contribute, but there is little evidence that amounts will reach the $7 to $10 billion annually that Kofi Annan is asking for to combat the crisis.

-- There has been progress on debt cancellation for some of the poorest countries. As part of the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries initiative, 23 countries have now had their annual debt payments reduced -- but only by just over one fourth. The seven richest countries have promised to cancel 100 per cent of the debts of these countries, but not all creditors have matched this. Multilateral institutions such as the World Bank and IMF are the remaining biggest creditors to these countries. If they were to cancel these debts, the impact of HIPC would be doubled.

-- A "Dollars, Debt, Drugs" march to raise awareness of solutions to the AIDS crisis in the poorest countries will take place on Saturday, June 23 in New York, organized by the Global AIDS Alliance, supported by Jubilee USA and Drop the Debt. Drop the Debt argues that deeper debt cancellation should go hand in hand in with more flexible sourcing of AIDS drugs and more aid for health and education, in an integrated package to fight global AIDS.
