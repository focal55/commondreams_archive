Women's Groups Mobilize Against Bush Tax Plan on National Day of Action
   WASHINGTON - April 11 - Following is a statement of Nancy Duff Campbell, co-president, National Women's Law Center:

Today, all around the country, women are taking action against President Bush's tax plan because they know it leaves them out and puts their children last. They're urging their Senators and Representatives to oppose it with rallies, petitions, personal meetings -- whatever it takes to convince them that this tax plan is irresponsible. On behalf of the Coalition on Women and Taxes, I am honored to stand with the thousands of women who are speaking out today.

No one knows better than America's women what it takes to manage a family budget. And the nation's budget is no different. Any tax cut must be modest enough in size to leave room for critical national priorities. But women are mobilizing because the Bush budget, to make room for a tax cut for the wealthiest 1 percent of taxpayers, would cut child care and early learning programs, Medicaid, health care access for the uninsured and doctor training, and would ignore pressing needs for child support improvements. It uses funds from the Medicare Trust Fund that are needed to pay hospital costs for other purposes and leaves no money to shore up Social Security. And this is only the beginning. If the surpluses projected over the next ten years do not materialize, more cuts are sure to follow and women will be especially hurt. They depend on these programs more than men because they tend to earn less during their lifetimes, live longer, and are more likely to have uncompensated family care-taking responsibilities.

Women around the country are mobilizing because they are learning more about President Bush's tax plan, and are feeling the sting of being left out of its benefits. The President has talked about the need to help the single mom who is struggling to support her family. But the truth is that a single mother with two children would not begin to benefit from the Bush tax plan until she is earning $22,000 a year. For a mother leaving welfare for work at the median hourly wage of $6.60 an hour, that means holding two jobs, working 64 hours a week, every week of the year, before she gets a tax cut. In stark contrast, the richest 1 percent of Americans -- whose average income is over $1.1 million per year -- will receive between 36 percent and 45 percent of the tax cut's benefits, even though they pay only 20 percent of all federal taxes.

Some of the leading women's groups organizing actions in the field today include the American Association of University Women, the Coalition of Labor Union Women, the Feminist Majority, the League of Women Voters, the Older Women's League and the YWCA. Today is one day of action, but we're not going away. In fact, our numbers are growing. We will continue to fight this irresponsible tax plan and irresponsible budget. Our families deserve nothing less.
