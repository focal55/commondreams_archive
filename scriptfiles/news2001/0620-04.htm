ACORN File Complaints Against Predatory Lender
  WASHINGTON - June 20 - ACORN is campaigning against the unfair and abusive lending practices of Household Finance, a company that also works under the name Beneficial.

Today ACORN is filing complaints with regulatory agencies in several states. In each state, ACORN will be reporting cases of predatory lending and requesting that the state take action against Household.

For copies of the complaints, or to speak with borrowers, contact David Swanson at 202-547-2500.

ACORN has already filed complaints in New York, Missouri and Minnesota. In Missouri, the state attorney general and the banking commissioner have begun looking into the matter. Today ACORN will be filing complaints in Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Pennsylvania, New Mexico and the District of Columbia. ACORN will file complaints in California on Friday.

Household's practices are very similar to those for which the Federal Trade Commission is prosecuting the Associates.

"Household Finance is guilty of a widespread pattern of unfair business practices, aggressively and deceptively marketing loans that cheat homeowners out of their savings and their equity, especially low- and moderate-income, elderly, and minority families," said ACORN National President Maude Hurd. "We are reporting these cases, and we are going to follow up to make sure something is done about them."

Household & Beneficial's abusive lending practices include:

-- Financing over priced "single premium" credit insurance into borrowers' loans, often without their understanding of what they have been sold. -- Charging high fees and interest rates unrelated to borrower's credit risk -- Refinancing loans and collecting excessive points and fees, when these refinancings only hurt the borrower.

Household makes its loans through aggressive and deceptive sales techniques. Some borrowers report having been promised lower payments, only to face higher ones, still more are unaware of some of the most abusive terms of their loans. They do not know they have been sold credit insurance, or they do not know how expensive it is and how little it does for them. They do not know their loans have prepayment penalties, and have often been assured that they do not. They do not know that they could have qualified for less expensive loans.
