Crying Wolf Over Arms Export Controls
Government Study Refutes Defense Industry Claims for Relaxed Export Controls
  WASHINGTON - May 7 - Defense industry arguments to loosen U.S. arms export controls are dealt a blow by the figures in a General Accounting Office report obtained in advance by the Insider. The report, titled Export Controls: State and Commerce Department License Review Times are Similar, invalidates the industry's arguments that the State Department's sluggish review of arms export licenses hampers U.S. defense firms from competing for arms sales and cooperating with allies.

The GAO examined the time it takes the Departments of State and Commerce to review and issue export licenses. It found that the State Department spent an average of 46 days reviewing munitions licenses during fiscal year 2000. By comparison, the average time for the Commerce Department, which reviews licenses for dual-use technology, is 51 days. Last year, Commerce employed 51 license officers to review 11,000 licenses. State had only 23 license officers to review 46,000 licenses. A government source familiar with the license review process told the Insider that State has hired a number of license officers since the study was conducted, and that the average review time for the first quarter for 2001 is probably even lower.

The GAO study undermines the central tenet of the defense industry's argument: that the ‘antiquated' licensing system is too slow and therefore a threat to U.S. national security. The industry argument contends that licensing delays are the reason foreign defense firms and countries are purposefully avoiding U.S. defense equipment, parts and services. This in turn weakens the U.S. industrial base and threatens U.S. security. In fact, the GAO found that two- thirds of the licenses reviewed by State took an average of 23 days and one-third of all licences were issued within two weeks.

Assertions that the sky is falling on the industry's overseas export prospects because of licensing delays are grossly overstated. Over the past ten years the United States has led the world in arms sales, U.S. allies are getting the equipment they need, and defense industrial cooperation between the U.S. and its allies is increasing. It is reassuring to know the State Department is investing the necessary amount of time to review licenses that impact U.S. national security and foreign policy.

Suggestions that the U.S. government should speed up the review of munitions licenses ignores a simple fact -- licenses that State and Commerce review are often for sensitive systems, parts and technologies that can kill people. While some licenses take over three months to review, they are most likely for sensitive equipment or complicated arms deals that require the input of numerous government agencies. It should take the government longer to review these exports than it would for a loaf of bread.

Policy-makers should not be swayed by spurious arguments based on anecdotal evidence from the defense industry and its vocal champions. This study confirms that it is not time to scrap the old export control system for one that is authored by the defense industry.
