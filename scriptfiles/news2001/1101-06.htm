The Not-So Friendly Skies:
Airline Pilots and the Aviation Security Bill
  WASHINGTON - November 1 - When the House takes up legislation later today aimed at tightening security at the nation's airports, it won't simply be a showdown between Democrats and Republicans.

Instead, the question of how to make airports safer in the aftermath of Sept. 11 has drawn interesting battle lines among Washington's special interests, making friends of usual enemies and, in some cases, pitting some closely-aligned groups against each other.

That rift is no more obvious than in the split between labor groups representing airline pilots. On one side is the Air Line Pilots Association, representing workers at most of the nation's biggest airlines, while on the other is the Allied Pilots Association, which lobbies for American Airlines pilots.

On Capitol Hill this week, both groups have been among the most enthusiastic supporters of plans to tighten airport security. The difference: Allied supports the House GOP's bill, which would leave the job of screening baggage up to private contractors. ALPA, meanwhile, is pushing for the Democratic-backed legislation, which would require baggage screeners to be federal employees. Earlier this month, the Senate approved the Democrats' approach in a unanimous vote.

Perhaps the split isn't surprising. While most unions overwhelmingly favor Democrats with their campaign contributions, transportation unions--including those representing airline workers--tend to give significant sums to Republicans. And the GOP has found no bigger booster in the sector than Allied, which tends to support Republicans more strongly than other air transport unions do.

During the 1999-2000 election cycle, Allied contributed just over $161,000 in soft money, PAC and individual contributions to federal parties and candidates. Two-thirds of that money went to Democrats. On the other hand, Democrats took almost 87 percent of ALPA's reported $967,120 in contributions during the last elections.

That pattern of support hasn't changed much the opening months of the 2002 election cycle. In the first six months of this year, Allied contributed $58,500 to members of Congress, 59 percent to Democrats. Contribution numbers are more current for ALPA, which reports its contributions monthly to the Federal Election Commission. Through Sept. 30, the group had contributed more than $400,000 to members of Congress, 83 percent to Democrats. That includes more than $40,000 in contributions made after Sept. 11, including a $5,000 check to Rep. Jim Oberstar (D-Minn.). Oberstar is the top Democrat on the House Transportation Committee and the key sponsor of the Democrats' aviation security bill.

But it's not only pilots that are divided over the legislation. With the House vote expected to be tight, the list of strange bedfellows is endless, especially among other unions. Service Employees International Union, which represents private contract security workers, usually is among the Democrats' biggest supporters, but SEIU ($1.2 million in 2001; 94 percent to Dems) supports the Republican-backed security bill. On the flip side, unions representing federal workers, including the American Federation of Government Employees ($123,750 in 2001; 91 percent to Dems), are major supporters of the Democratic proposal, partially because they stand to gain members if approved.
