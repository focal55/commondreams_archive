U.S. PIRG Praises Reps. Markey and Johnson, and Sen. Lieberman for Introducing Arctic Wilderness Bill
   WASHINGTON - February 28 - The U.S. Public Interest Research Group applauded Representatives Ed Markey (D-Mass.), and Nancy Johnson (R-Conn.) and Senator Joe Lieberman (D-Conn.) for introducing the 2001 Arctic Wilderness Act to protect the fragile Arctic National Wildlife Refuge from oil and gas drilling by designating the coastal plain as wilderness.

"Now is the time to save the Arctic Refuge from President Bush, Senator Murkowski, and their allies in the oil industry," said Athan Manuel, director of PIRG's Arctic Wilderness campaign. "BP Amoco and ExxonMobil want to ruin one of America's last wild places for six months worth of oil. We thank Reps. Markey and Johnson and Sen. Lieberman for recognizing that it doesn't make sense to ruin America's Arctic."

The Arctic National Wildlife Refuge protects some of America's most spectacular wilderness and wildlife, including polar bears, caribou, grizzlies, musk oxen and millions of migratory birds. The coastal plain is the calving ground for the 130,000-member Porcupine caribou herd and is sacred to Gwich'in people, a subsistence culture.

On Monday, Senator Frank Murkowski (R-Alaska) introduced an energy package that included a provision that would mandate oil drilling in the Arctic Refuge. The package was the latest attempt by Senator Murkowski, Chairman of the Energy and Natural Resources Committee, to change a two-decade-old law that protects an area scientists call "America's Serengeti."

U.S. PIRG also called on BP Amoco to accept an Arctic Refuge shareholder resolution filed with the London-based oil giant. BP Amoco is trying to have the resolution disqualified on the grounds that there are too many American investors signed on as co-filers.

Last year a similar resolution sponsored by U.S. PIRG, Greenpeace, and Trillium Asset Management calling on BP Amoco to switch investment from oil exploration to renewable energy and stay out of the Arctic Refuge was supported by an unprecedented 13 percent of BP Amoco shareholders.

"It appears that BP's Sir John Browne I is trying to emulate King George III," said Athan Manuel, director of U.S. PIRG's Arctic Wilderness campaign. "BP Amoco would rather censor American investors than discuss important environmental and human rights issues."
