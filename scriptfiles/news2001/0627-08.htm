Following U.N. Meeting, Global AIDS Alliance Challenges United States and G7 for Real Action to Stop Global AIDS
   NEW YORK - June 27 - Today the Global AIDS Alliance (GAA), a broad coalition of debt cancellation advocates, AIDS treatment activists, faith-based organizations, human rights groups, and humanitarian agencies, in the United States and Africa, called the U.N.'s Declaration of Commitment on HIV/AIDS "an advancement in the fight for an expanded and comprehensive response to the epidemic." But the group questioned whether wealthy nations, particularly the United States, were prepared to back it up with action.

"Sadly, 2.5 million people will die this year from AIDS, and another 3 million may die next year, yet there is no sign of bold action emerging from this meeting," stated Co-Director Chatinkha Nkhoma, based in Malawi.

The GAA also called attention to the fact that while the U.N. meeting has heightened political pressure for concerted action, decisions on such key issues as the design and operation of the Global Fund are still being made behind closed doors among the wealthy donor nations, with little civil society input. "To find where the real decisions are being made you should follow the money, and that leads you to the G7 governments, not to the U.N.," stated Dr. Paul Zeitz, co-director of the GAA. Zeitz is a public health specialist and medical doctor who designed AIDS response activities in Zambia. "That reflects a domineering, colonialist pattern of behavior, not a real partnership with people in affected nations."

"Is the U.S. really committed to the 'Declaration of Commitment'?" asked Dr. Zeitz. "President Bush should have been here personally and addressed the U.N. Assembly. In fact, the United States is already in clear violation of the declaration itself by opposing deeper debt relief, by not clearly supporting bulk procurement of medicines (including generics) on the basis of competitive bidding, and by proposing fiscal year 2002 spending on global AIDS that still falls far short of targets contained in the agreement being signed today."

United States spending for fiscal year 2001 for all bilateral programs on global AIDS is only $439 million. Only about half of this is specifically for programs in Africa, where the impact of the epidemic is being most acutely felt. For fiscal year 2002 President Bush has only proposed spending $446 million for bilateral programs, plus $200 million in proposed resources for the Global Fund, for a total of $646 million. Today, the House Appropriations Committee approved $415 million for bilateral global AIDS programs and $100 million for the global fund, in clear contradiction of the Declaration's goals.

The United Nations and global experts estimate that at least $9.2 billion per year in grants are is needed. As the United States makes up 30 percent of the global economy, the U.S. fair share for this program should be $ 3 billion per year. According to a just released study by the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, the share of the U.S. federal budget allocated to foreign aid has sunk to about one-half of 1 percent, a record low, and less than any other developed nation. This is far below the "commitment" the United States is signing today in the "Declaration of Commitment."

While the Declaration calls for effective and comprehensive debt relief, U.S. Ambassador Michael Southwick has stated in briefings at the United Nations that deeper debt relief is unnecessary in the context of HIV/AIDS because "the HIPC program is a success." In fact, of the 23 countries that have received debt relief so far, in 15 the annual debt servicing payments will still be higher than what the country is currently able to spend on basic health care. In total, debt service costs for the poorest nations will reach $2 billion a year through 2005, in spite of the Heavily Indebted Poor Country Initiative (HIPC) debt relief program, if no further action is taken.

At least four nations made explicit appeals for deeper debt relief during the UNGASS, including Nigeria. On Monday, Sen. Hillary Rodham Clinton also called for broader relief and closer linkage of debt relief and the response to HIV/AIDS. "During the three days of this meeting, 17 countries in Africa which have already passed the first stage of the debt relief process will have paid out $111 million in debt payments," stated Zeitz. "Yearly they will still pay out over $1 billion in debt payments. This does not make good public health sense. We do not see debt relief as a magic bullet ands we never have. But the IMF and World Bank debt still must be cancelled and savings redirected to such useful purposes as fighting HIV/AIDS. We call on the G7 Finance Ministers, meeting in just a few weeks, to plan on dropping the debt at the G7 Summit in Genoa Italy on July 19, 2001."
