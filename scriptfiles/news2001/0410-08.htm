Budget Numbers Add Up to Major Loss for the Environment
   WASHINGTON - April 10 - The federal budget unveiled by the Bush administration on Monday is a significant step backward on the environment. It is riddled with short-sighted proposals that appear to reflect the administration's emerging bias against fighting global warming, against taking modest steps to protect America's vanishing animal species and habitats, and against conserving Earth's natural resources.

A particularly striking example, because it contradicts a direct campaign promise, is the proposal to provide only $13 million to fund the Tropical Forest Conservation Act, a landmark law enacted with bipartisan support in 1998. That minimal level of funding would be achieved with money diverted from other international environmental programs.

"During the campaign, President Bush promised more than once to work with Congress to fund the tropical forest program at a minimum of $100 million a year," said Brooks Yeager, WWF's vice president for global threats. "But now this promising effort to use debt to save tropical forests has been left high and dry by the very administration that promised to stand behind it." Earlier, the new administration abandoned another campaign commitment--to seek limits on emissions of carbon dioxide, a gas that is a major contributor to global warming.

The Tropical Forest act, which is up for reauthorization this year, provides for restructuring debt in countries that agree to repay in local currencies deposited in trust funds for forest preservation or restoration. With projects just getting under way in several countries, the need for funds is increasing, not declining.

In the same budget, the president proposed a 7 percent cut in biology programs at the U.S. Geological Survey, a level that Yeager said would "virtually decapitate" the agency's biological resources division. And the budget would cut all funding for the National Biological Information Infrastructure, an innovative effort to build an integrated data system covering all biological survey information for the United States, linked to information compiled by universities and museums.

Pound-foolish provisions such as these, coupled with proposals to reduce funding for solar and wind energy research while advocating oil and gas exploration in the Arctic National Wildlife Refute, represent ill-advised retreats from the path to a sound energy and environment future.

"President Bush has acknowledged the threat global warming poses to the environment. Yet, his budget will only exacerbate this threat, by slashing important energy efficiency and renewable energy programs that help curb carbon emissions," said WWF Climate Change campaign director Jennifer L. Morgan.
