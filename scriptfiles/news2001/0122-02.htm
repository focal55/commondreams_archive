What if Bush Spikes Clean Diesel Rule?
Thousands of Deaths, Massive Health Damage
   WASHINGTON - January 22 - The Bush administration has moved to delay the effective date of all new federal regulations, including a landmark EPA plan to clean up big-rig diesel trucks, buses and diesel fuel.

The delay, announced Jan. 20, is ostensibly to permit new appointed officials to review rules such as the diesel clean-up plan. At her confirmation hearing last week, New Jersey Gov. Christine Todd Whitman, the EPA administrator-designate, noted that the Bush administration would review the clean diesel rule. It remains unclear if the administration aims to revise the rule.

What would be the impact of any rollback?

When fully in effect, the EPA plan would prevent 8,300 premature deaths each year, as well as 5,500 annual cases of chronic bronchitis and 17,600 cases of acute bronchitis in children, according to EPA's regulatory impact analysis.

The EPA plan also would prevent over 360,000 asthma attacks and more than 386,000 cases of respiratory symptoms in asthmatic children annually. In addition, it would prevent 1.5 million lost work days, 7,100 hospital admissions and 2,400 emergency room visits for asthma every year. In addition, the plan would improve visibility, reduce acid rain, and reduce eutrophication and nitrification of water bodies.

EPA calculated that the diesel cleanup would cost about $4.2 billion per year -- but would produce more than $70 billion dollars in annual benefits by reducing exposure to smog, soot and toxic air contaminants.

"Given the massive health benefits of the diesel cleanup, it would be a terrible blunder for the new administration to weaken or delay these new standards," noted Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Clean Air Trust.
