Sierra Club Meets with Bush Administration to Debate Energy Policy
Statement of Carl Pope, Executive Director of the Sierra Club
   WASHINGTON - June 5 - "Today was the first step in a new dialogue with the Bush Administration about America's energy policy. We had a cordial exchange of ideas with the Administration but we still have profound differences about our basic beliefs about the best way to solve America's energy needs. We continue to be extremely disappointed with the President's "dig, drill and destroy" policy and hope to move him to a position that is better for all Americans. We'll have to wait and see whether President Bush is sincere about finding the best energy solutions.

"We offered the Administration a quicker, cleaner, cheaper and safer energy policy based on energy efficiency, renewable energy and responsible additions to supply. If President Bush adopts our plan and robustly implements it, we can provide Americans with energy without drilling the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, regressing back to dangerous nuclear power, or weakening the Clean Air Act to burn more coal.

"During our discussion, we highlighted three key pillars of our energy plan: renewable energy, responsible increases in supply and energy efficiency. We urged the Administration to support a Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS), which would require 20 percent of our electricity derived from clean, renewable energy by 2020. They expressed a willingness to continue a dialogue about adopting an RPS to promote solar, wind, geothermal and biomass energy.

"We voiced our concern about pollution from power plants, urging the Administration to support bi-partisan legislation that would cut dangerous pollution. But instead of committing to protecting our families, the Administration again rejected curbing global warming pollution from power plants.

"Finally, we asked the Administration to work to increase energy efficiency, specifically by increasing miles-per-gallon standards for cars and light trucks. Raising fuel economy for cars and SUVs to 40 miles per gallon is the biggest single step President Bush can take to cut oil consumption and curb global warming. The White House staff signaled it would await a forthcoming report on the miles-per-gallon standards from a panel that is weighted heavily toward the auto and oil industries, without any environmental representation.

"We look forward to continuing our dialogue with President Bush's Administration. While we will continue to have major differences about energy policy, we are hopeful that President Bush will adopt an energy plan that is quicker, cleaner, cheaper and safer than the one he proposed last month."
