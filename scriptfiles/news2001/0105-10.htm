Poultry Giants Leave Thousands of Chickens Suffering, Dying From Exposure, Starvation at Collapsed Chicken Houses in Arkansas
December Storms Result In Collapsed Buildings, Trapped Birds, And No Humane Evacuation Plan
   WASHINGTON - January 5 - The ice storm that left thousands of Arkansas residents without power over the holidays has also devastated the state'ss large population of chickens housed on factory farms, The Humane Society of the United States has found. Snow and ice storms that swept through Arkansas in mid- and late-December collapsed barns, injuring and killing thousands of chickens owned by Tyson Foods, Inc. and Foster Farms. Weeks after the disasters occurred, inadequate efforts had been made to either rescue the chickens or humanely euthanize the injured and dying birds.

Representatives from The Humane Society of the United States (HSUS) who were on the scene after the storms have captured on video dead and dying chickens among the debris of collapsed chicken houses. HSUS came upon the disasters last weekend, when they found thousands of chickens, used as broiler-breeders (meat chickens), had died from building collapses or that were suffering and dying from exposure to intense cold weather conditions, starvation and thirst. The footage was shot at a Tyson farm in Logan County and a Foster Farm location in Hot Springs County.

The video footage can be seen on The HSUS Animal Channel at www.animalchannel.net. Additional information on the Arkansas disaster and factory farms can be found on The HSUS Web site at www.hsus.org.

Arkansas is the second largest producer of broiler chickens in the U.S. behind Georgia. Tyson Foods, Inc., ranks number one in broiler production while Foster Farms ranks eighth, according to the National Chicken Council.

At the Foster Farm location, chicken barns with an estimated 13,000 chickens, collapsed after the mid-December storms. Company representatives recently came to the farm and shot thousands of the surviving birds. The dead chickens, and many others still alive, were left at the scene. HSUS has learned that both companies rounded up and removed most of the remaining live chickens from the disaster areas earlier this week, but also left many behind.

Unfortunately, farmers are contracted to house and care for the chickens but do not actually own them. According to industry practices and the farmers themselves, farmers are prohibited from making any decisions to humanely euthanize the birds, as they are legally not their property. The farmers are under direction from the companies they work for on how to respond to such situations.

"This was a recipe for disaster," said David Kuemmerle, HSUS program manager for Farm Animals and Sustainable Agriculture. "Factory farms such as Tyson and Foster Farms house chickens in intensive confinement systems that are inherently inhumane. When thousands of chickens are kept under one roof it is virtually impossible to provide for safe evacuation, on site care of surviving birds, or even the humane euthanasia of birds that are too injured or inaccessible to human rescuers."

A year ago, the industry in Arkansas suffered a loss of more than $25 million in damages when ice storms collapsed more than 500 chicken houses. Despite that catastrophe, poultry companies have yet to implement responsible disaster plans for their facilities. As mere commodities, animals are insured against loss along with the buildings in which they are kept.

When disaster hits, animals suffer through their injuries, or die of starvation, thirst and exposure. "The effects of such disasters on factory farms are not only detrimental to the animals, but can pose serious threats to human health and the surrounding environment as well," said Suzanne Millman, PhD, HSUS Director of Scientific Programs for Farm Animals and Sustainable Agriculture. "The state of North Carolina has still not fully determined the long term effects to communities as a result of massive hog manure lagoons that broke or overflowed during flooding in 1999. We need to rethink the way we are raising farm animals and find alternatives to raising them in such large concentrations, where animals are treated inhumanely, and seriously threaten the health of consumers and the environment," Millman said.

The Humane Society of the United States is the nation's largest animal protection organization with more than seven million supporters.
