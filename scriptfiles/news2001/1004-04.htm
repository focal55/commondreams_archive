Senate Committee Suspends Barriers on Arms Exports to Pakistan & India
Weapons and Military Assistance Not the Prescription for South Asia
  WASHINGTON - October 4 - Today, the Senate Foreign Relations Committee removed the remaining sanctions on U.S. arms exports and military assistance to India and Pakistan. Lifting the last legal barriers prohibiting the sale of weapons to these countries is dangerous both in light of regional instability in South Asia, and the risk U.S. weapons being used against U.S. interests in a future conflict.

Exporting weapons to South Asia, one of the most politically volatile regions in the world, could further destabilize the region and exacerbate any future conflict between India and Pakistan. India and Pakistan have fought three wars and countless border skirmishes over the past 50 years and heightened tensions even further in 1998 by conducting nuclear explosive tests. Pakistan itself is politically unstable, which may be a reason behind the administration's hesitation to use Pakistan as a staging ground for military action against Afghanistan. The United States should not inject weaponry into this mix.

The United States must learn two lessons from past experience as a close ally and supplier of weapons and training to Iraq, Iran and the Afghan resistance: today's allies can be tomorrow's bitter enemies, and the United States can not control the weapons it exports. Should either regime in the region collapse, it is unclear whether the new authorities would side with the Taliban against U.S. efforts to combat terrorism. More importantly, a change in regime could impact whether U.S. military assistance and weapons are used against U.S. interests. Reports say the Taliban may posses as many as 100 U.S.-supplied Stinger anti-aircraft missiles which now may threaten American troops. Congress and the President must be careful not to create a similar situation in the future.

On September 22, the President waived most of the economic and military sanctions imposed on Pakistan and India after their nuclear tests. The President has also authorized $100 million in military aid to Pakistan under emergency provisions in existing U.S. law. Considering that India and Pakistan have a very low ranking in the United Nation's Human Development Index, a measure of a country's economic and social development, and face a huge social, economic and humanitarian emergencies, any future U.S. assistance should address their pressing humanitarian needs, not their ability to wage war with each other or against terrorists.

It is too soon to tell what, if any, military assistance India, Pakistan and other countries in the region will need to fight terrorism. Therefore, it is premature to waive carefully crafted U.S. arms export law intended to promote regional stability.
