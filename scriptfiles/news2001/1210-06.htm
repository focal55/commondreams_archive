Defending Free, Open Debate in Time of War
Scott Harshbarger on Patriotism, Free Speech and Attorney General Ashcroft
  WASHINGTON - December 10 - Common Cause hosted a press conference Monday January 10 at the National Press Club in Washington to support free and open debate during wartime and to urge Attorney General Ashcroft to fulfill his oath to preserve, protect and defend the Constitution.

Also participating were: Laura Murphy of the American Civil Liberties Union; Morton Halperin from the Council on Foreign Relations, Jerry Berman from the Center for Democracy and Technology, Joe Onek from The Constitution Project; and Stephanie Foster from the People For the American Way.

"Thank you all for coming.

At Common Cause, we believe this is a just war, and we support President Bush and our war effort. We all oppose terrorism in all its forms. We all oppose those who would seek to end what the free world has fought for. And that is why we stand by the Constitution. That is why we seek to make sure that we as a nation even in times of crisis do not in any way negate what so many have fought and died for.

We had just hoped for more. We hoped Attorney General Ashcroft, too, would rise to the occasion, like the President and Secretary Powell and Secretary Rumsfeld. As patriots, we hoped he'd at least have tried to show us the way in this crisis, not just representing his client, or following the public will, or playing to the lowest common denominator or fears, but showing us how to have it all - safety and security and our values, which include healthy, open, positive patriotic debate...

We are here, a diverse bunch, because what unites us as Americans is so much greater than what divides us. Attorney General Ashcroft's remarks suggest that isn't so. He went over the line last week in claiming his critics give aid and comfort to our enemies. Mr. Ashcroft swore an oath to protect and defend the Constitution, but that doesn't make him its only defender. The oath is just a reminder -- it is every American's duty to hold government accountable, just as it is every American's responsibility to protect and defend the Constitution.

A true patriot does not criticize those who do so, he recognizes that questioning government is one of the highest forms of patriotism. Instead, Mr. Ashcroft's remarks may have a chilling effect on our Constitution and our values.

There is no doubt that during wartime, the United States must act strongly to defend all our interests at the expense of others' rights.

As a former prosecutor, I am inclined to give Attorney General Ashcroft the tools he thinks he needs to do his job, especially on a temporary basis. Because after all, no one can deny there is a clear and present danger to ourselves and our fellow citizens.

But we expect an Attorney General to lead, to be a tough but smart advocate for our law and for law and order. To protect and to preserve our Constitution, especially in a time of war. Above all, to encourage debate, discussion, education, input and then to take the heat and act, but never to chill debate and discussion by saying it aids the enemy. An Attorney General should set an example for citizens, lay people, civil and military authority, of what it means to live within and for a government of laws and constitutional values.

The Attorney General exempted Congress from his criticism, but if you follow his logic, Congressman Bob Barr, a former U.S. attorney and CIA analyst, and Senator Patrick Leahy, the intended victim of one of the anthrax letters, are helping Al Qaeda. That's flat-out wrong, and while Senator Leahy and Congressman Barr can take care of themselves, I worry for other Americans who criticize the Attorney General.

As the President has cautioned, this is a new kind of war. Mr. Ashcroft's comments are especially disturbing because, as Attorney General, he is the member of the cabinet responsible for ensuring today's crisis does not become precedent for tomorrow's abuses.

Mr. Ashcroft should be clear that he has two mandates to protect our national security: to lead the country's law enforcement efforts, and to protect and defend the Constitution.

He must do both."
