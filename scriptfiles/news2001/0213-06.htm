American Friends Service Committee Nominates Peace Brigades International for Nobel Peace Prize
   PHILADELPHIA - February 13 - The Board of Directors of the American Friends Service Committee (AFSC) has nominated Peace Brigades International (PBI) for the 2001 Nobel Peace Prize. The AFSC was a co-recipient of the award in 1947 on behalf of Quakers worldwide.

In its nomination letter, AFSC highlighted Peace Brigades International's mission statement, which says in part: "PBI has for more than twenty years work[ed] to open a space for peace in which conflicts can be addressed in a nonviolent way. PBI applies a strategy of international presence and concern that supports local initiatives and contributes to developing a culture of peace and justice…."

PBI's volunteer unarmed bodyguards from countries throughout the world provide "protective accompaniment" for human rights workers and others in regions of conflict. They offer international awareness and nonviolent protection to local workers for peace and human rights through independent observation and analysis of the conflict situation. PBI volunteers also conduct peace education training on request.

Currently, Peace Brigades International's largest project of 30 international volunteers is in Colombia accompanying local human rights workers and civil society organizations working for a peaceful solution to Colombia's conflicts. In Haiti, PBI is providing peace education training at the request of Haitian civil society organizations. New projects are underway in East Timor/Indonesia and in Mexico. PBI also participates with other organizations in the Balkans Peace Teams and in SIPAZ, an international coalition of organizations working in Chiapas, Mexico.

The volunteers in the field are supported by PBI's fourteen Country Groups in North America, Europe, Australia, and New Zealand. The Country Groups recruit and support volunteers and organize an international network of cooperating organizations and governments to keep a protective watch on volunteers and local human rights workers threatened with death. Wherever possible, PBI works with all parties to a conflict in order to establish and inform the parties of PBI's presence.

Previously, Peace Brigades International worked in Guatemala, El Salvador, Sri Lanka, and in North America on violence involving native peoples.

In making the nomination, the AFSC Board of Directors also noted that:

1. PBI has a sustained, deep commitment to nonviolence in working for peace and human rights and provides a successful model for how ordinary people with extraordinary courage can support local workers for peace in some of the most dangerous of the world's conflicts.

2. PBI's extraordinary record of accomplishment is powerful proof that nonviolence and peaceful approaches to human rights and social change can work when supported by the international community. In more than twenty years of work in some of the world's most violent conflicts, no-one under PBI protection was killed while they were being protected, nor have any PBI volunteers been killed.

3. The work is important. The projects where PBI is currently working, Colombia and Indonesia, are extremely important regional conflicts, as is the cooperative work PBI volunteers support in the Balkans and in Chiapas.

4. PBI has received the Aachen Peace Prize from the Foundation for Peace in Aachen, Germany, and was a finalist for a Hilton Foundation award. It was honored by the international community in Colombia for the work it has done there, but, PBI has not received as much international attention as its work warrants. The recognition provided by a Nobel Peace Prize would greatly enhance their ability to work in conflict situations.

5. Peace Brigades International is well known to Quakers. Individual Friends (Quakers) were deeply involved in PBI's original formation, and have been involved over the years as PBI staff, volunteers, and donors. Its mission, program, and methodology are compatible with the high honor of the Nobel Peace Prize.
