Norway's Whale DNA-Base Fails First Test, Says WWF
   WASHINGTON - November 8 - Norway's highly-touted whale DNA register, claimed as an ironclad means of preventing illegally caught whale meat from infiltrating legal markets, has failed to produce definitive matches for samples of whale meat sold throughout Norway, according to WWF, the conservation organization.

Norway's failure to confirm that DNA from whale meat sold inside its borders is from legal sources comes as the Norwegian government is seeking to re-open international trade in whale products with Japan.

A study released by TRAFFIC, the wildlife trade monitoring arm of WWF and IUCN, collected 19 samples of whale meat at Norwegian markets and provided DNA samples to Norway in a double-blind test under agreement with government. Fifteen months after receiving the whale DNA samples, the Norwegian government has failed to officially confirm any as coming from legal sources.

"Norway has given up its claim to a credible DNA system that will protect whales," says WWF Vice President Richard N. Mott. "A DNA system that is closed to independent scrutiny will keep the public in the dark and give illegal whaling and smugglers a green light."

Norway, and its whale market partner, Japan, have sought to blunt world criticism by claiming that comprehensive DNA registers of whales caught under pre-set quotas will allow all whale products sold in their markets to be confirmed as coming from a legal catch.

Despite the apparent failure of its linchpin system, Norwegian authorities continue to insist the DNA register has been audited and approved as "fully operational."

WWF fears that if international trade in whale meat is re-opened, the profits from sale to Japan will spur Norway to increase its self-set annual quota, possibly doubling the number. Norwegian whalers currently kill over 500 Northern minke whales a year despite the global moratorium on whaling agreed in the International Whaling Commission (IWC).

Trade in whale products is banned under the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), although Norway and Japan have exempted themselves from its requirements. Both countries have repeatedly sought to scale back CITES protections for whales but each time their proposals have been rejected by the 150-country governing body of the CITES treaty.

"WWF calls on Japan and Norway to comply with the letter and spirit of the CITES treaty, and not re-open trade in whale meat," said Mott. "Commercial whaling has historically been impossible to police, and Norway's DNA system gives no confidence to believe this has changed."

World Wildlife Fund (WWF), known worldwide by its panda logo, leads international efforts to protect the diversity of life on earth. Now in its fourth decade, WWF works in more than 100 countries around the globe. TRAFFIC is the wildlife trade-monitoring program of World Wildlife Fund and IUCN -The World Conservation Union.
