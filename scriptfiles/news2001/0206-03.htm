NRA Demonstrates Hubris and Hypocrisy on Gun Lock Giveaway Programs
   WASHINGTON - February 6 - Once again, the National Rifle Association has demonstrated its hypocrisy when it comes to supporting law enforcement. On January 8, the New York Times profiled a successful gun lock giveaway program in Maine. The program is sponsored by the Maine Citizens Against Handgun Violence Foundation, a nonprofit organization dedicated to research, education and awareness of gun violence and gun safety issues in the state. The Foundation purchases gun locks with private funds and distributes them free of charge via local police agencies.

On January 27, the Associated Press reported that the National Rifle Association opposes the Maine gun lock giveaway program.

THE HYPOCRISY:

In the AP article, NRA spokesman Andrew Arulanandam is quoted as saying that his organization is "not happy that law enforcement officers who are paid with public money, with taxpayers' money, are involved. They should not be working so closely to an advocacy group."

Yet the NRA supports Project HomeSafe, a gun lock giveaway program run by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), a gun industry trade group closely aligned with the NRA. Like the Maine program, Project HomeSafe relies on police participation to distribute its locks. According to the program's website, police involvement in the giveaway is "critical to the success of the program" and "(p)olice departments will manage the distribution of the gun locking devices..." In June 2000, the NRA promoted the Project HomeSafe program in a fax alert to its members.

"The NRA's criticism of the Maine gun lock giveaway program is the height of hypocrisy," said Handgun Control President Michael Barnes. "The NRA criticizes the Maine gun lock giveaway program, yet it actively promotes an almost identical program sponsored by the gun industry. Obviously, the NRA objects to the Maine program because the Maine Citizens Against Handgun Violence Foundation's sister organization, Maine Citizens Against Handgun Violence, supports common-sense gun laws. Like a petulant child, the NRA only opposes a statewide gun lock giveaway program that is successful without -- and despite -- its participation and approval."

THE HUBRIS:

The Associated Press article also reports:

The NRA said it would have been more likely to support the (Maine) program if it had been consulted before it began.

"As the national expert on gun safety, we should have been contacted," Arulanandam said.

"Clearly, the NRA feels that law enforcement officials aren't qualified to judge the merit of a gun lock giveaway program on their own," said Barnes. "And the NRA calling itself 'the national expert on gun safety' is so absurd, it's laughable. This is an organization that believes children can be left unattended with guns so long as they're told not to touch them. This is an organization that promotes the absurd notion that the more people we have walking our streets carrying hidden handguns, the safer our society will be."

THE IRONY:

In October 2000, the gun industry had to suspend its Project HomeSafe program after police departments in Tennessee found that the program's locks were faulty and could easily be defeated. The NSSF had to issue warnings to police departments across the country that had received hundreds of thousands of these defective locks for distribution. In January 2001, the NSSF informed Project HomeSafe participants that it would be working with the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) to replace the locks.

"Given the fact that guns are exempt from the CPSC's oversight and that the gun industry resists any attempts to implement standards for its products," said Barnes, "I find it ironic that the gun industry is working with the CPSC to replace the faulty locks it distributed. We support the use of child safety locks, but if gun manufacturers were truly concerned about safety, they would be doing more than just giving away gun locks. They should be putting integrated locks and other safety devices on their products to make them childproof. They should be working with the CPSC and the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms to institute industry-wide standards for firearms. If the NRA and the gun industry really cared about reducing guns deaths and injuries, they should be supporting legislation to keep guns out of the hands of criminals and children, rather working against it.

"The NRA's attitude toward the Maine gun lock giveaway is disgraceful. It is an insult to the Maine Citizens Against Handgun Violence Foundation, to the gun owners who are taking advantage of the gun locks and to members of the Maine law enforcement community who wholeheartedly support this program."

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, was founded in 1974 by Dr. Mark Borinsky and N.T. "Pete" Shields, two victims of gun violence. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works with law enforcement, public health, religious, and community groups across the country to strengthen and protect federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban all guns. HCI has over 500,000 members and supporters nationwide, making it the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization.
