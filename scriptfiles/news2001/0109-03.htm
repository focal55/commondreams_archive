National Women's Law Center Opposes Ashcroft, Chavez
  WASHINGTON - January 9 - The nominees for Attorney General, John Ashcroft, and for Secretary of Labor, Linda Chavez, will roll back decades of legal advances and public policy gains for women and their families if confirmed by the U.S. Senate, the National Women's Law Center said today in officially opposing them.

Since 1972, NWLC has been at the forefront of every major battle to secure women's rights, including reproductive rights, affirmative action, employment discrimination and sexual harassment laws, and other civil rights and workplace fairness measures -- some of the very laws that Ashcroft and Chavez stand to undermine or even dismantle in their potential respective roles.

NWLC Co-President Nancy Duff Campbell said, "Historically, the Center has rarely taken positions on Executive Branch nominees and this is the first time we have ever opposed a cabinet head. We take this unusual step because these two nominees have been selected for posts with significant responsibility for the well-being of American women and they both have demonstrated records of antagonism to the constitutional principles and federal laws that have advanced and safeguarded women's progress for more than three decades."

"Senator Ashcroft's record demonstrates not only a lack of commitment, but actual hostility to equal protection of the laws and equal opportunity. Yet, a demonstrated commitment to these fundamental principles is essential if the American public is to have confidence in an Attorney General's fair and even-handed enforcement of the law," said Marcia D. Greenberger, NWLC Co-President.

NWLC cited examples of Ashcroft's positions, including:

-- Ashcroft is not only staunchly anti-choice in opposing abortion, but he also opposes important forms of contraception. He has voted in favor of overturning Roe vs. Wade and he is an original sponsor of a proposed amendment to the Constitution, the "Human Life Amendment," and a proposed federal statute, the "Human Life Act," both of which would have prohibited all abortions except those to protect the life of the mother and defined abortion to include key forms of contraception. These proposals contained no other exceptions, even when a woman has been raped or is the victim of incest.

-- Specifically, the sweeping language of Ashcroft's proposed "Human Life Amendment" and "Human Life Act" defined human life as beginning at "fertilization," and therefore could be invoked to ban some commonly-used forms of the pill and the IUD, which sometimes work by preventing a fertilized egg from implanting in the lining of the uterus.

-- Ashcroft led the effort to defeat the confirmation to federal district court of Judge Ronnie White, the first African-American on the Missouri Supreme Court, even though Judge White was fully qualified. In the process, Ashcroft grossly distorted Judge's White's record. In addition, he has blocked and delayed other qualified nominees to the federal bench, including many qualified women.

-- Ashcroft opposes affirmative action and has done a number of things indicating a serious insensitivity on racial issues, such as accepting an honorary degree from Bob Jones University, which is infamous for its racially discriminatory policies.

"The Attorney General is charged with enforcing the constitutional protections and laws that have been essential to women's opportunity and self-determination for thirty years. The American public cannot have confidence that Senator Ashcroft would enforce these protections with the same vigor and determination he has displayed in opposing them," said Greenberger.  

Greenberger added, "Linda Chavez is another cabinet nominee who opposes the very laws and programs she will be overseeing. Her long and outspoken opposition to laws promoting fairness in the workplace -- employment discrimination and sexual harassment laws, affirmative action, equal pay, family and medical leave and numerous other measures -- make her wholly unsuitable for this position."

For example, the Department of Labor administers critical anti-discrimination programs, most notably the federal contract compliance program subject to Executive Order 11246, which prohibits discrimination by all private companies receiving federal contracts (including all forms of sex discrimination in employment, such as compensation discrimination and sexual harassment) and requires federal contractors over a certain size to implement affirmative action programs (with flexible goals and timetables) to ensure equal job opportunities for women and minorities.

-- Chavez opposes affirmative action in employment, education, and contracting, and she specifically opposes the goals and timetables component to the Executive Order program that is critical to its effectiveness. She has written that President Reagan's failure to eliminate the goals and timetables component of the Executive Order program was a "tremendous defeat" for those like her in that Administration. She has further testified (incorrectly) that the goals and timetables provisions are the same as quotas.

-- Chavez has ridiculed as "cockamamie" the observance of Equal Pay Day, which is the day each year that the average woman's pay catches up to what the average man earned at the end of the previous year, and is used by women's organizations and public officials to symbolize and highlight the continued problem of pay discrimination based on sex. She has also disputed the notion of a glass ceiling for women in the workplace.

The Department of Labor also implements and enforces the Family and Medical Leave Act, and implements basic worker protections guaranteeing a minimum wage, overtime pay, worker safety and other measures.

-- Chavez opposed enactment of the Family and Medical Leave Act and supported President Bush's veto of it and opposes increases in the minimum wage. She supports legislation (introduced by Senator Ashcroft) that would seriously weaken current law on the 40-hour work week and overtime pay.

In addition, just last year the Labor Department issued long-awaited regulations under Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, implementing Title IX's nondiscrimination requirements in education and training programs funded by the Department of Labor.

-- Chavez filed comments opposing the implementation of the Department of Labor's Title IX regulations on the erroneous ground that they would broaden the purpose and scope of Title IX.

Finally, numerous additional public statements further demonstrate Chavez's outspoken hostility to the federal laws she would oversee at the Department of Labor. For example:

-- Chavez has said sexual harassment suits have made this a "nation of crybabies," and complained that "ever since the courts started interpreting a 'hostile work environment' to constitute sexual harassment, bad taste has become a federal offense....Men (are) often the targets of...witch hunts." She has ridiculed the proposition that -- as the Supreme Court has now endorsed in a case where NWLC was counsel -- schools may be held liable for sexual harassment by one student of another.

-- Chavez opposed the Civil Rights Act of 1991, which reversed a series of Supreme Court decisions in the late 1980s that made it harder for plaintiffs to prevail in employment discrimination cases, and which also added compensatory and punitive damages as remedies for Title VII civil rights violations. Moreover, she lauded President Bush's 1990 veto of the bill and complained that the version he signed was still objectionable.

"The Center takes the unusual step of formally opposing the nominations of Senator Ashcroft and Ms. Chavez because, if confirmed, they would pose a serious threat to women and their families," said Campbell.
