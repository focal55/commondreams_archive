Sierra Club to Launch Ads Urging Congress to Pass Meaningful Campaign Finance Reform
  

WASHINGTON - August 29 - The Sierra Club announced today that it will run newspaper ads in four states and Washington, DC, calling on Representatives to pass real campaign finance reform legislation, for cleaner elections and a cleaner environment. When Congress returns from its summer vacation next week, Representatives will have a chance to bring the Shays-Meehan campaign finance reform bill to the floor of the House for a fair and open debate. In July, the House leadership thwarted efforts to bring the bill to the House floor, trapping it in the House Rules Committee.

"Americans don't want to see our clean air or drinking water safeguards sold to the highest bidder," said Carl Pope, Executive Director of the Sierra Club. "The Senate took the first step to stop the flow of polluters' cash into campaign coffers. Now, the House must step up to the plate to give elections back to all Americans, not just the wealthy corporate polluters."

With 205 Representatives already signed onto the discharge petition to get the Shays-Meehan bill out of the House Rules Committee, only 13 more Representatives are needed to bring the Shays-Meehan to House floor. The ads, which run August 29, urge readers to call Reps. Doug Ose (R-CA), Rodney Frelinghuysen (R-NJ), Wayne Gilchrest (R-MD) and John Thune (R-SD) and ask them to release the bill and allow it to be debated and voted on before the full House. The ad will also run in Washington, DC in early September.

"Elected officials should answer to people in their districts, not to the oil, mining, chemical and timber industries that buy undue influence with campaign dollars," continued Pope. "We urge these Representatives, whose constituents care deeply about cleaning up elections, to work for real campaign finance reform and vote to bring the Shays-Meehan bill to the floor of the House."

Like the companion McCain-Feingold bill in the Senate, the Shays-Meehan bill bans soft money -- unregulated contributions given to political parties -- and is an important first step in reforming our campaign system by taking huge, unlimited and unreported donations out of the campaign system. This means that polluting industries will no longer be permitted to dump unlimited amounts of money into campaigns in an attempt to gain undue influence. The Senate passed the McCain-Feingold measure on April 2 by a vote of 59-41.

The oil and gas industry handed out more $32 million to candidates in the 2000 elections alone; now the Congress and President Bush want to open the pristine and irreplaceable Arctic National Wildlife Refuge for oil exploration. The automobile mobile industry donated almost $18 million to candidates and parties in 1999-2000; Congress then voted against raising miles per gallon standards on sport utility vehicles. The mining industry spent more than $6 million dollars on campaign payments; just months after taking office President Bush rejected a plan to remove arsenic from our drinking water.
