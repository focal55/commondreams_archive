Statement of National Environmental Trust President on Bush-EU Talks on Global Warming
   GOTHENBURG, Sweden - June 14 - The following is a statement by Phillip Clapp, President of the National Environmental Trust, on the Bush-EU Talks on Global Warming:

Despite all the words of cooperation, the President found himself completely isolated today on global warming and the Kyoto Protocol.

Even Italian Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi, previously a Bush ally on Kyoto, said at a Brussels press conference yesterday that his country had "signed up to the Kyoto Protocol" and would honor it.

While they knew he opposed the Kyoto Protocol, it has been shocking to Europeans to hear President Bush mouth word-for-word the arguments against action on global warming made by OPEC and oil industry lobbyists for the past decade. This may not surprise Americans any more, but this is the first time European leaders have seen it up close.

The President's statement this afternoon that his energy plan would cut U.S. global warming pollution is absolutely false. The vast majority of his proposals are aimed at increasing fossil fuel production in the United States. Over the next decade, the President's energy plan will increase U.S. global warming pollution by at least 35 percent.

The central issue for the Europeans is simple: Kyoto aside, will President Bush agree to any binding commitment to reduce U.S. global warming pollution? The President had no direct answer, although his statements implied that he will only support voluntary measures. Ten years of voluntary programs in the U.S., begun by the President9s father and continued through the Clinton Administration, have had no impact -- U.S. emissions grew by 13 percent in the 1990's.

There has also been growing concern among European governments that the Bush Administration is trying to torpedo talks aimed at completing the Protocol next month. While the President assured the Europeans that the U.S. would participate, Administration officials would not commit to having a U.S. policy proposal ready for the talks in Bonn (July 16-27), and the White House is pressuring the Japanese government not to cooperate in reaching a final deal European nations can ratify by next year.

Even if the President refuses to act now on global warming, he should get out of the way and let the rest of the developed world get on with it.
