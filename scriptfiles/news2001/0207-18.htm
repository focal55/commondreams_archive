Mr. President, Now is the Time for Leadership on Guns
   WASHINGTON - February 7 - Following a shooting incident at the edge of the White House grounds today and an office shooting in Illinois on Monday, Handgun Control called on the Bush Administration to show leadership on the gun issue.

Michael D. Barnes, President of Handgun Control said today, "This nation has just witnessed two high-profile incidents involving guns in the past three days -- one of which ended tragically in the deaths of five people, the other involving a man with a gun just outside the President's own backyard. Our sympathy goes out to the families of the victims and the survivors of these tragedies."

"Every day in this nation, 80 people die from gunfire," Barnes continued. "This Administration claims to want to reduce the level of gun violence in this country. Both our new Attorney General, John Ashcroft, and President Bush have stated for the record that they want to enforce the gun laws on the books. Now is their chance to back their words with deeds. The nation is waiting."

Yesterday, White House spokesman Ari Fleischer was asked about the massacre at the Navistar plant in Illinois. In that shooting, the gunman possessed several firearms, despite the fact that he had been twice convicted of a felony. When asked what the President had to say about the incident, Fleischer seemed at a loss as to what steps could be taken. Instead, he concentrated on the President's support for trigger locks, while reiterating support for keeping guns off school grounds, for the assault weapons ban, and for federal penalties for gun crimes.

"As Mr. Fleischer noted, no law can stop every single shooting, but does that mean we have done all that we can? Of course not," said Barnes. "Now is the time for President Bush to step forward and do more than simply talk about 'enforcing the laws on the books.' All the enforcement in the world won't help if our laws are full of loopholes and our enforcement agencies are hamstrung by lack of support. We need to require background checks on all gun sales. We need comprehensive tracing of crime guns to find their source. We need better record-of-sale information to enable law enforcement to identify criminals who may have guns, like the Illinois shooter, and to catch gun traffickers. There are concrete steps that this President can take to make enforcement of our gun laws swift and effective, beyond simply giving out trigger locks."

Ironically, a front-page story in today's Washington Post reports that most gun locks -- including hundreds of thousands given away by the gun industry -- have come under fire from the Consumer Product Safety Commission for failing safety tests.

"There are no federal safety standards for trigger locks, just as there are none for guns -- another area in which the Bush Administration could show leadership," said Barnes. "Clearly, guns and gun locks should be regulated in the same manner as all other consumer products."

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, was founded in 1974 by Dr. Mark Borinsky and N.T. "Pete" Shields, two victims of gun violence. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works with law enforcement, public health, religious, and community groups across the country to strengthen and protect federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban all guns. HCI has over 500,000 members and supporters nationwide, making it the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. More information about HCI and its sister organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on our Web site at www.handguncontrol.org.
