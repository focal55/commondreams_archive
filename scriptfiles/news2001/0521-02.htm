WWF Urges Rapid Ratification of POPs Convention
  STOCKHOLM, Sweden - May 21 - As diplomats gather in Stockholm this week to sign a global chemicals treaty, World Wildlife Fund is urging governments to quickly ratify the convention that will help protect wildlife and people throughout the world from toxic contamination.

WWF is calling on governments to ratify the Stockholm POPs Convention within the next year, prior to the World Summit on Sustainable Development in September 2002. The treaty requires ratification by 50 countries before it can enter into force. Finalized last December by a consensus of 122 countries, the new treaty bans or severely restricts a group of 12 pesticides and industrial chemicals, including PCBs, dioxins, and DDT. These dangerous chemicals, known as persistent organic pollutants or POPs, are extremely toxic, accumulate in body fat, do not readily degrade in the enviroment, and have the ability to travel great distances.

"The POPs Convention is critically important to safeguard the health of wildlife and people everywhere on Earth," said Clifton Curtis, director of WWF's Global Toxic Chemicals Initiative. "Governments should waste no time in ratifying the treaty so implementation can proceed as soon as possible."

While most of the targeted chemicals have already been banned in industrialized countries, implementation of the treaty faces some challenges in the developing world due to inadequate resources and regulatory structures, continued use of some of the pesticide POPs, and a need for environmentally sound disposal and destruction of obsolete POPs stockpiles.

"Developing countries will require significant finanical and technical support from the developed world to successfully implement the treaty's provisions," said Brooks Yeager, WWF's vice president for Global Threats. "POPs are a global threat so it is in the best interest of the United States and other wealthy nations to ensure that the treaty's goals are realized." During the negotiations, Yeager was the U.S. government's chief negotiator on the POPs Treaty as the State Department's deputy assistant secretary for the environment.

The Stockholm POPs Convention enjoys wide support from governments, the chemical industry, and environmental groups. Once in force, the treaty will eliminate or severely restrict production and use of this group of pesticides and industrial chemicals; ensure environmentally sound management and chemical transformation of POPs waste; establish a process for screening other chemicals for inclusion in the treaty; and prevent the emergence of new chemicals with POPs-like characteristics.
