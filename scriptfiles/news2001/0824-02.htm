Federal Firefighters Target Communities With Little Fire Risk
Queens, NY Targeted for Federal Fire Prevention Efforts
  WASHINGTON - August 24 - While the West burns, a new government list of communities supposedly threatened by wildfires targets areas with much lower fire risk thousands of miles away.

"Knowing which communities are at risk is crucial to developing an effective strategy to prevent wildfire damage," said Jonathan Oppenheimer, forest expert at Taxpayers for Common Sense, and a leading critic of federal wildfire policy. "Unfortunately, this list will make it virtually impossible to get wildfire prevention resources to the communities where they are most needed."

The list, which was published in the federal registry earlier this month, is intended to provide a guide for where federal fire prevention efforts should be directed.

It will be of little help, according to Oppenheimer. "This list leaves agencies whistling in the dark," he said.

Many of the listed communities are in areas that face little, if any, threat from wildfires. Less than one-third of the communities on the list are in the Interior West, which has experienced the most severe wildfires in recent years.

Some of the areas that were included on the list include: Delaware, Rhode Island, Maryland, Puerto Rico, New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Guam, and the Virgin Islands.

"Many of these areas have never seen severe wildfires and chances are they never will," said Oppenheimer. "A catastrophic wildfire in Queens is about as likely as a hurricane in Montana."

Not knowing which communities are most at risk could complicate implementation of the National Fire Plan, the multibillion funding package adopted by Congress in response to last year's record wildfire season.

The General Accounting Office recently testified to Congress that federal land management agencies have failed to adequately define the criteria by which threatened communities should be identified.

According to the GAO, "the National Fire Plan will become little more than a funding source…and [will] not ensure that federally appropriated funds are being spent in those wildland-urban interface communities at the highest risk of wildland fire."

Of the 545 highest-risk communities identified by the Interior Department, 278 are in Georgia, North Carolina and Tennessee, where severe wildfires do not occur, reported the GAO.

"By including low-risk communities, the government is jeopardizing communities in the West, where there are massive wildfire threats," Oppenheimer continued.

As thousands of acres burn across the western United States, the federal government is preparing to spend billions of dollars to protect areas that are not at risk to wildfire. A recent agreement between the Western Governors' Association and the Bush Administration could cost taxpayers $3 billion per year.

"Before taxpayers spend a nickel to reduce wildfire risks, the federal government must determine where the money is most needed," Oppenheimer concluded. "If the government uses this list as a roadmap for where to reduce wildfire risk, taxpayers are going to get burned."

Taxpayers for Common Sense authored last year's "From the Ashes" report, an analysis of the 2000 wildfire season.
