ABM Treaty and Missile Defense:
Press Breakfast to Discuss Bush Plans, Upcoming Congressional Action
 

WASHINGTON - August 31 - Actions to be taken next year, including the beginning of construction of a missile site at Fort Greely, Alaska, will lead the Bush Administration to withdraw from or violate the 1972 ABM Treaty. Russia, China, and NATO allies want the U.S. to stay within the treaty.

The House and Senate will soon take up the defense authorization for fiscal 2002, including the administration's proposal for $8.3 billion for national missile defense. This $8.3 billion contains money earmarked for construction of the Alaskan missile site. Legislation will be offered in the Senate Armed Services Committee and the House in the next two weeks to prevent any funds from being used for this purpose.

Guest speakers will be Lawrence Korb, assistant secretary of Defense in the Reagan Administration and now at the Council on Foreign Relations, and Philip Coyle director of the Office of Testing and Evaluation in the Clinton Administration and now at the Center for Defense Information

At the press conference, Korb, and Coyle will discuss:

why deployment in Alaska in 2004 is premature
why testing can continue under the ABM Treaty
why the Bush Administration is proceeding on a risky course.
