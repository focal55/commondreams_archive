New IRS Reports Reveal Loopholes in Campaign Finance Law
   WASHINGTON - February 9 - Campaign for America today released an analysis of IRS financial disclosure reports demonstrating that current regulations do little to keep non-federal soft money raised by politicians for their personal PACs (leadership PACs) from seeping into federal campaign activity.

"Politicians are raising so-called non-federal money, avoiding the scrutiny and restrictions imposed by federal election law, and then, either through direct transfers or through indirect subsidizing, using the money to support federal campaign activity," says Cheryl Perrin, executive director of Campaign for America, the only national advocacy group whose sole mission is passage of campaign finance reform.

According to FEC regulations, money raised for non-federal purposes such as state and local election activity must be kept separate. Federal leadership PACs, however, are allowed to allocate non-federal funds for joint fundraising activities and shared operating expenses provided the allocations are disclosed. A law passed in August requiring leadership PACs to disclose non-federal contributions and expenditures sheds new light on how PACs use this minor provision or skirt the provision entirely to subsidize federal election activity.

"The information provided by the new disclosures strongly suggests we need to strengthen the barrier between the federal and non-federal accounts," Perrin says.

The new IRS reports also demonstrate that leadership PACs have become a significant source of political money for many federally elected officials. Financial disclosure forms showed that leadership PACs raised $5.86 million in soft money between August and the end of 2000.

According to the new filings, over half ($3,389,843) was raised by the top five PACs: GOPAC, linked to Rep. David Dreier (R-Calif.); Republican Majority Issues Campaign (RMIC), linked to Tom Delay (R-Texas), the New Democrat Network (NDN) linked to several Democrats; Keep Our Majority PAC (KOMPAC), headed by Speaker Denny Hastert (R-Ill.); and Lone Star Fund, linked to Rep. Martin Frost (D-Texas).

While the Democrats achieved parity in raising soft money for their party for the 2000 election cycle, Democratic leadership PACs raised $2,390,389 in non-federal money, nearly $1.5 million less than the $3,861,198 raised by their GOP counterparts. However, Senate Democrats raised $1,723,576 compared to $269,050 in non-federal money raised by GOP Senate leadership PACs, while GOP House members raised $2,902,635 compared to $666,813 in non-federal money reported by House Democrat leadership PACs.

House Rules Committee Chairman David Dreier's GOPAC topped the list, raising $944,110. GOP Whip Delay's RMIC raised $689,513; Speaker Hastert's KOMPAC raised $669,000; the New Democrat Network raised $645,920 and Democratic Whip Frost's Lone Star Fund received $441,300. The figures reflect only contributions raised from the time the law was enacted in July through the end of the year.

A searchable database of all 527 filers can be found on Campaign for America's website, Campaignforamerica.org. Users can search by name, state or zip code. The site also includes a database of donors, both individuals and corporations.
