Published on Monday, September 9, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Bay Area Marchers Call for Peace
Thousands at Oakland, Marin Rallies Mourn 9/11 Victims, Condemn Attacks
by Charles Burress, Jim Doyle, Chronicle Staff Writers
 

In the countdown to Wednesday's anniversary of Sept. 11, the growing U.S. drumbeat for war was met with protests and vigils in Marin County and Oakland Sunday as people of many creeds and ethnic backgrounds called for peace and tolerance.


We're going in the wrong direction. Violence creates more violence, and hatred creates more hatred.

Jo Button-Tait
Mill Valley
Remembering the victims of last year's attacks on the World Trade Center and Pentagon, participants condemned not only the attacks but also U.S. plans for a possible attack on Iraq, secret detentions by the U.S. government and U.S.-led bombings of Afghanistan.

More than 2,000 people chanted peace slogans and joined hands to form a human chain along major boulevards in Marin County to urge American leaders to show restraint in Iraq.

Passing motorists waved, flashed peace signs and honked their horns in solidarity with demonstrators on Third Street and the Miracle Mile in San Rafael and San Anselmo. Others carried placards with signs such as "Love America, Care for the World." A dozen drummers pounded out a mesmerizing beat.

In Oakland, nearly 250 people with anti-war signs marched halfway around Lake Merritt and attended a memorial gathering with East Bay Muslims at the Islamic Cultural Center of Northern California.

The colorful procession featured flags of the Earth, Ecumenical Peace Institute members singing "Dona Nobis Pacem" (Grant Us Peace), a banner with a war-resistance pledge by the "Not in Our Name" group, and many marchers wearing tags bearing names of people held in secret detention after Sept. 11.

"War is not the answer to the problems we face right now. We need to look for alternatives," said Devi Peri of the Marin Peace and Justice Coalition, which was formed one week after the Sept. 11 attacks.

"We're going in the wrong direction. Violence creates more violence, and hatred creates more hatred," said Jo Button-Tait of Mill Valley, who held a peace sign along Third Street in San Rafael. "Thank God we live in a country where we can do this."

The Oakland march began with Moina Noor, director of American Muslims Intent on Learning and Activism, condemning the Sept. 11 attacks as "an act of criminal and brutal aggression against our nation." The attacks also amounted to "the hijacking of our religion in the name of God, Islam and Muhammad, our beloved prophet."

Carolyn Scarr, a Berkeley Methodist and board member of the Ecumenical Peace Institute, said America is "facing a civil liberties disaster," referring to the arrests of suspects and their secret detention.

"There are hundreds of them," she said. "They've just been scooped up and taken away. Nobody knows where they are. It's just like Argentina." She wore a name tag saying, "Nacer Mustafa, 29, Palestinian, Disappeared in the U.S."

An American Muslim man, Oakland locksmith Carlos Ali, wore a sandwich board saying, "Our grief is not a cry for war," a message displayed on several other signs, stickers and leaflets.

The demonstrators in Marin grew silent shortly before 3 p.m. and held hands, as one organizer described it, to "sense our connection with the whole world. " The marchers later gathered in San Rafael's downtown plaza for a peace rally with music and a "free speech" soapbox.

The peace marchers gathered to mourn the victims of Sept. 11, but also to grieve the deaths of civilians in Afghanistan who were killed by the U.S. bombing campaign there.

"As the current administration prepares for a war against Iraq, our banners rise to oppose war and say yes to a just and peaceful world," the group said in a written statement.

Alameda County Supervisor Keith Carson praised the gathering at the Islamic Cultural Center, saying, "Thank you for stepping forth and boldly saying, 'We too want to remember what has taken place and what is currently taking place around this world.' "

Nearly 20 groups co-sponsored the Oakland event, including United Muslims of America, Berkeley Interfaith Women for Peace, A Jewish Voice for Peace, California Peace Action, the San Francisco Bay chapter of the National Lawyers Guild and Global Exchange.

A featured speaker was Kelly Campbell of Oakland, founding member of the Peaceful Tomorrows advocacy group of families of Sept. 11 victims. One of the victims was Craig Amundson, brother of Campbell's partner, Barry.

"We don't want other people killed in response to Craig's death," she said. "We don't want other families in the world to suffer."
