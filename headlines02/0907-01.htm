Blair and Bush Face Revolt Over Attack on Iraq
by Roland Watson in Washington, Philip Webster and Richard Beeston in London
 

PRESIDENT BUSH and Tony Blair ran into a wall of international opposition to their proposed military action against Iraq yesterday, after world leaders from Paris to Moscow and Beijing urged them to shelve their plans.

On the eve of the Prime Minister’s trip to Camp David for talks with Mr Bush, President Putin of Russia led a diplomatic counter-attack. Mr Bush and Mr Blair telephoned the leaders of the other permanent members of the UN Security Council to convince them of the need for action against Iraq.

But the tactic appeared to backfire when Mr Putin told both men that their strategy was wrong and potentially disastrous. “In the course of the discussions, the President expressed his serious doubts concerning the basis — both in international law, and the global-political sense — of using force against Iraq,” a Kremlin spokesman said. Mr Blair, who will visit Russia next month, was warned of the “serious, negative consequences for the situation in the Gulf region, the Middle East and for the future of the US-led anti-terrorism coalition”.

The Russians were also very concerned about Thursday night’s air raid by British and US aircraft against an Iraqi air defense command post 240 miles west of Baghdad, the latest in a series of strikes.

Mr Bush and Mr Blair also spoke to President Chirac, who emphasized his view that military action could be sanctioned only by the UN Security Council. M Chirac said it was up to the UN, and not the US alone, to decide how to act against Baghdad if Saddam rejected the return of weapons inspectors. The US President met with a similar response when he telephoned Jiang Zemin, the Chinese leader, who echoed M Chirac’s sentiments.

The growing resistance to the Anglo-American push for action is likely to come to a head next week when Mr Bush makes his case before world leaders at the UN General Assembly in New York.

However, evidence emerged yesterday in support of Washington and London’s contention that Iraq is developing weapons of mass destruction. UN experts said recent satellite photographs revealed new construction at sites linked to Baghdad’s development of nuclear weapons. “We see changes on the ground,” Mark Gwozdecky of the International Atomic Energy Agency, the UN’s nuclear monitoring body, said. “But until you get inspectors on the ground you cannot tell what the purpose of the construction is.”

Mr Bush’s spokesman described the pictures as deeply troubling. The evidence is likely to be added to information which Mr Bush and Mr Blair are preparing to make public.

The two will swap notes to ensure that when Mr Blair publishes the Government’s dossier against Saddam, it is in line with Washington’s information. A similar campaign was launched last year to convince world opinion that Osama bin Laden was responsible for the September 11 attacks and to justify the military campaign in Afghanistan.

Jack Straw, the Foreign Secretary, said yesterday that patience with Saddam was running out and that military action had to remain an option.

“Until Saddam co-operates fully with UN weapons inspectors, we have no guarantees that a dictator who has previously shown no restraint in using weapons of mass destruction will not use them again.”
