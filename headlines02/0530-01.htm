Published on Thursday, May 30, 2002 by Agence France Presse
Conflict Spirals on Indo-Pak Border as Militants Strike Again
 

Military conflict escalated along the borders of India and Pakistan, as militants attacked more police posts in Kashmir amid US warnings that "irresponsible elements" in the two countries could spark a war.

Prime Minister Atal Behari Vajpayee, meanwhile, called an unscheduled meeting with his three key advisers on security matters -- Defense Minister George Fernandes, Foreign Minister Jaswant Singh and Home Minister Lal Krishna Advani.

Singh afterwards said "various issues" had been discussed, without elaborating, although the chiefs of the Indian army, navy and air force did not attend.

Fernandes Thursday canceled plans to attend an international conference in Singapore this weekend, his office having earlier said his attendance would be dependent on the prevailing security situation.

A defense spokesman in Jammu, southern Kashmir, said heavy cross-border shelling, mortar and gunfire had raged across the India-Pakistan borders overnight and early Thursday, with the district of Poonch becoming one of the main flashpoints.

At least seven people were killed in the fierce artillery duel that continued deep into the night, Indian police said.

Political analyst Balraj Puri described the current flare up as "serious" as tempers were running high on both sides.

Three separate attacks in Kashmir overnight on police and paramilitary posts, allegedly by Islamic militants, in which four police, two soldiers and a civilian were killed, heightened tension palpably in the disputed state.

In the most dramatic of the attacks, a group of militants stormed into a police station at Doda district, 170 kilometers (105 miles) north of Kashmir's winter capital Jammu, late Wednesday killing the two policemen on duty.

Police around midday (0630 GMT) Thursday said the attackers were still holed up in the building and a gun battle was raging.

Reinforcements had been summoned as other militants hiding in nearby forests and mountains were opening intermittent fire on the police post.

In two other separate incidents, a woman constable and a policeman were killed by suspected militants at a small police post in southern Rajouri district, while two paramilitaries were killed in Udhampur in the north, police said.

The United States, meanwhile, warned India and Pakistan late Wednesday that "irresponsible elements" in the two countries could spark a war, if the two nuclear rivals allow their relations to further deteriorate.

"The climate is very charged and a serious conflagration could ensue if events spiral out of control," State Department spokesman Richard Boucher said in the most explicit US language over the prospect of a new war between the two uneasy neighbors in some days.

"There is a danger that as tensions escalate, the leaders could find themselves in a situation in which irresponsible elements can spark a conflict," he added.

Pakistan's new ambassador to the United Nations, Munir Akram, meanwhile, defended his country's refusal to rule out an aggressive nuclear strike, saying it had to deter India's superior conventional army.

Speaking Wednesday, only one day after he presented his letters of credentials to UN Secretary General Kofi Annan, Akram told a news conference India was "arming itself to the teeth" and Pakistan could not hope to match it.

"We have to rely our on means to deter Indian aggression. We have that means and we will not neutralize it by any doctrine of no first-use."

Tensions between India and Pakistan have risen nearly to flashpoint since a massacre in southern Kashmir on May 14 that India blames on Islamic militants sponsored by Islamabad.
