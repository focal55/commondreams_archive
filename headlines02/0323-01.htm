Published on Saturday, March 23, 2002 by Agence France Presse
More Than Two Million at Rome Demo: Organizers
 
More than two million people, according to an Italian trade union, turned out in a massive protest against government plans to change the labor law and to condemn terrorism following the killing of one of reform's architects.


Italian CGIL labor union flags are waved by demonstrators in Rome's Circo Massimo, Saturday, March 23, 2002. Earlier in March CGIL (Italy's main labor union) called for over one million people in the streets of Rome Saturday to protest against proposed Government labor reforms. (AP Photo/Corrado Giambalvo)
The ancient Circus Maximus, the main rallying point, was already crowded with demonstrators at 11:30 am (1030 GMT) even as six feeder marches were bringing hundreds of thousands of banner-waving protesters from gathering points around Rome.

"I believe that we are more than double the people than what we were in 1994. That's impressive," Sergio Cofferati, who heads the main Italian trade union federation CGIL said.

In November 1994, around one million demonstrators turned out in a demonstration to protest pension reforms, when conservative Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi was briefly in office for the first time.

"This is a unique and extraordinary demonstration," former leftwing prime minister Massimo d'Alema said at the protest, calling it a "symbolic battle" for worker's rights.

The labor reforms Berlusconi envisages would make it easier for companies to fire workers, and they have provoked strong protests among labor unions.

But the demonstration took on a different tone earlier this week, after a key man behind the reforms was assassinated in Bologna, in an attack claimed by the far left Red Brigades.

Marco Biagi, an adviser to the government, was gunned down outside his home in northern Italy on Tuesday.

The Red Brigades, an urban guerrilla group behind a series of deadly attacks and kidnappings in the late 1970s and early 1980s, claimed responsibility for the assassination.

Demonstrators marched with banners such as "terrorism kills freedom" and "Don't touch Article 18", in reference to the clause in the labor law the prime minister wants to change.

The ancient site of Rome's chariot races, the sunken, grassy Circus Maximus is around half a mile long and around 150 meters (yards) wide, the only venue in the capital big enough to hold the one million people which the unions had been expecting.
