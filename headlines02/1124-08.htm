Published on Sunday, November 24, 2002 by the Las Vegas Review-Journal
Papers: Workers Found Defects in Yucca Process
DOE contractor fired one who aired worries
by Keith Rogers
 

Documents obtained by the Review-Journal indicate that Yucca Mountain Project workers registered deep concerns about shortcomings in the process that scientists used to find the site suitable for disposing the nation's highly radioactive waste.

Documents and interviews with project workers reveal that deficiencies in computer modeling, software and data afflicted the project as workers raced to meet looming deadlines.

The project's director of quality assurance, Robert Clark, urged that corrective measures be taken. Clark confirmed he was then transferred and told to "take one for the project."

In addition, one of the workers who had conveyed concerns about the project to the Department of Energy was fired by the DOE's quality assurance contractor, Navarro Research and Engineering.


Jim Mattimoe, a former quality assurance manager for a Yucca Mountain Project contractor, says he was banned from the Energy Department's Hillshire Drive offices after he was fired last year and not allowed to retrieve personal items.
Photo by Jeff Scheid.
A Labor Department investigator later determined that part of the reason Navarro fired its quality assurance manager, Jim Mattimoe, was it had been urged to do so by Lake Barrett, a top DOE official. The inspector deemed Barrett's actions "extraordinarily egregious."

In a June 24 memo to Labor Department investigator Nicholas Sebastian, Navarro employee Kristi Hodges addressed the treatment of Clark and Mattimoe, manager of a staff that audited the project's science and engineering.

"If one connects the dots, the overall theme supports that they were removed because the quality assurance organization was too effective in identifying and pursuing significant deficiencies that were perceived as threatening the Yucca Mountain site recommendation," Hodges wrote.

"As the project schedule became increasingly compressed, cutting corners to meeting project milestones resulted in slippage of corrective action deadlines and creation of new deficiencies," Hodges wrote.

The memo was generated during a Labor Department probe into Mattimoe's claim that he was fired because he blew the whistle on the way project officials investigated workers' concerns.

LABOR DEPARTMENT CASE:

The following are highlights of the Labor Department case of former Yucca Mountain Project contractor employee Jim Mattimoe:
July 1999: Mattimoe, with 28 years experience in quality assurance programs, becomes manager for the program with SAIC, a Yucca Mountain Project contractor.

March 2001: Mattimoe makes claims of wrongdoing in the project's concerns program to Robert Clark, director of quality assurance for the Office of Civilian Radioactive Waste Management.

April 2001: Clark informs Lake Barrett, acting director of the Office of Civilian Radioactive Waste Management, of Mattimoe's concerns. Barrett orders Clark to direct Mattimoe to turn over evidence of wrongdoing in the project's concerns program.

May 2001: Mattimoe becomes quality assurance program manager for Navarro Research and Engineering.

Aug. 6, 2001: Mattimoe is placed on administrative leave and banned from the project's offices.

Aug. 24, 2001: Navarro Research and Engineering terminates Mattimoe.

Aug. 27, 2001: The Department of Energy through a subcontractor settles with quality assurance employee David Mitchell for an undisclosed sum, thus avoiding having corruption allegations heard before an administrative judge.

Sept. 5, 2001: Clark is transferred from director of quality assurance for the Office of Civilian Radioactive Waste Management to a special assignment.

Feb. 20: Mattimoe files a wrongful termination complaint with the Labor Department.

March 14: Barrett confirms he will retire as acting director of the Office of Civilian Radioactive Waste Management, after holding that position for 14 months in his third stint as acting director.

Sept. 13: Labor Department Regional Administrator Frank Strasheim issues a report directing Navarro Research and Engineering to reinstate Mattimoe, clear his record and reimburse his expenses. Susana Navarro, president of the firm, appeals the decision a short time later.

Nov. 18: Mattimoe gives a deposition for Navarro Research and Engineering's appeal of the Labor Department report.
In a Sept. 13 report, the Labor Department ordered Navarro to reinstate Mattimoe, expunge his personnel file and reimburse him for costs incurred.

The report states that Susana Navarro, president of Navarro Research and Engineering, was motivated to fire Mattimoe "at least in part to her fear that she might not receive future extensions or contracts with DOE unless she took this action."

But Navarro is appealing the Labor Department ruling and Mattimoe, who's since been working at the Los Alamos, N.M., national laboratory, was summoned to Las Vegas last week for a deposition.

In an e-mail Thursday, Susana Navarro said an audit by a prominent law firm found "among other things, that Mr. Mattimoe's conduct as a program manager for SAIC (the previous contractor) was inconsistent with a safety conscious work environment."

"I based my decision on the findings of this report, and I really believe that I did the right thing," she wrote.

The Labor Department report, however, says the law firm's audit "is not more than a sophisticated recitation of anonymous charges."

After more than 20 years of political wrangling and scientific study, the DOE earlier this year recommended that more than 77,000 tons of the nation's deadliest nuclear waste be buried at Yucca Mountain, located 100 miles northwest of Las Vegas.

President Bush adopted the recommendation, and Congress approved it over the veto of Gov. Kenny Guinn. The first shipment of nuclear waste could arrive in 2010.

Shortly before DOE issued its recommendation, the presidentially appointed Nuclear Waste Technical Review Board concluded that the "technical basis for DOE's repository performance estimate is weak to moderate."

Specifically, the board was concerned about studies of the way groundwater travels through the ridge, and how thermal conditions from the decaying nuclear fuel will affect the mountain.

DOE officials have defended the science on which the recommendation was based. But documents show that project employees also were concerned about flaws in the process.

A key concern mentioned in Hodges' memo was validation of computer models vital to federal scientists' assertions that nuclear waste can safely be contained in Yucca Mountain for 10,000 years.

"To conduct the Yucca Mountain Project modeling effort with any credibility, a consistent approach was needed. At the time of the (corrective action reports), there was no consistent approach, no governing procedures, and insufficient governing regulations," she wrote.

Mattimoe, 52, said he was fired after he made allegations of wrongdoing and corruption to Barrett. At the time, Barrett was in charge of DOE's Office of Civilian Radioactive Waste Management, which oversaw the Yucca Mountain Project.

In interviews this week, Mattimoe said the allegations involved the manner of investigating concerns that government workers, contract employees and others raised about the project.

According to Hodges' memo, some of the concerns pertained to the "monumental task" of integrating two decades of scientific data with hundreds of computer software codes used by national laboratories, universities and government contractors.

Others involved the defensibility of the "technical products," the models and analyses on which the scientists' conclusions hinge. The DOE must show regulators evidence that data about the planned repository were collected by qualified personnel using appropriate equipment.

Examples of wrongdoing, Mattimoe said, included withholding evidence and attributing statements to people who had never been interviewed about concerns with the project.

"The concerns program, which is much like an internal affairs division in a police department, is chartered to perform unbiased, independent investigations into any type of concern that could impact the safety of the project and the public," he said.

"I identified that the concerns program was corrupt and thereby raised questions about the credibility of all investigations for a period of nearly 10 years," Mattimoe said.

The Labor Department's report states that Barrett "failed to avail himself" to Sebastian, the investigator, during the seven-month investigation until after his findings had been turned in to the Labor Department's regional administrator.

Barrett, who is retired and residing in Rockville, Md., said he tried to make himself available to Sebastian. "When he called I was away on family business," he said last week.

In her memo, Hodges said Clark, the government's quality assurance manager, was transferred to another assignment even though "he had a vision that the science and engineering could be defensible and accomplished with integrity."

"When removed from his position, Mr. Barrett told him to `take one for the project' ... as the deficiencies were reaching too wide of an audience, including the public, and were increasingly reflecting poorly on project management."

Barrett declined to comment on Mattimoe's case and Clark's transfer other than to say, "I'm personally satisfied with the actions that I took."

Both Mattimoe and Clark believe their removal from the project was related to allegations raised by a Colorado-based subcontractor employee, David Mitchell.

DOE documents obtained under the Freedom of Information Act indicate that Mitchell told DOE officials the project's quality assurance program was deeply flawed.

According to the DOE documents, Mitchell alleged that, "This program is corrupt to the point that it may impact licensing application or the entire project. Corruption to this extent may be considered to fall into the `whistle-blowers' act."

A Labor Department report states that Mitchell was fired for "exhibiting hostile behavior towards a supervisor." He filed a complaint claiming he was wrongfully terminated for other reasons.

A Labor Department investigator found no merit to his case and recommended it be dismissed.

But rather than submit to a hearing at which his allegations would have been aired, the DOE subcontractor reached an out-of-court settlement with Mitchell. DOE agreed to reimburse the subcontractor for the settlement. Department officials refused to say how much money was paid Mitchell in the Aug. 27, 2001, settlement.

Prior to the settlement, Mattimoe and Clark had been investigating the allegations raised by Mitchell.

Mattimoe was fired three days before the settlement was reached. Clark was transferred less than two weeks after the settlement.

Clark said he was puzzled when he found out the matter was settled out of court. "I was shocked and amazed," he said in an interview last week.

Clark said he believes he and Mattimoe were targeted for termination or demotion as part of the settlement.
