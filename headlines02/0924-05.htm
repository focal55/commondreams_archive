Published on Tuesday, September 24, 2002 in the Times/UK
Britain Risks Rift with US Over Regime Change
by James Bone in New York
 
BRITAIN has angered the United States by telling other members of the United Nations Security Council that Washington’s policy of regime change had been suspended.

Diplomats said that Sir Jeremy Greenstock, Britain’s UN Ambassador, told the ten non-permanent members of Security Council at a private briefing on Friday that Washington had put regime change “on ice” unless the inspections process came to a grinding halt in the view of Hans Blix, the chief UN inspector.

However, a US official told The Times yesterday that Sir Jeremy’s assertion was absolutely false and insisted that regime change remained official American policy.

The row has exposed a fundamental difference between Britain and the United States just as they are trying to muster support for a new resolution spelling out the terms for the resumption of inspections. While Washington demands that President Saddam Hussein be forced from power, Britain has never publicly committed itself to regime change, which goes beyond existing UN demands.

Tony Blair sidestepped the question in an interview last week with the German newspaper Tagesspiegel. Asked if British policy included ousting Saddam, Mr Blair said: “The regime of Saddam Hussein is loathsome, but the demand from the international community is the entry of weapons inspectors at any time and in any place in Iraq.”

Britain had planned to introduce a draft resolution before the Security Council this week setting terms for inspections and warning of the consequences of non-co-operation.

But the drafting of a text has been delayed by protracted discussions between London and Washington, where hawks are pushing for tougher language. Diplomats say it is now possible that the United States will sponsor its own harshly worded resolution instead.

Alarmed by Iraqi statements in recent days, which seem to impose conditions on the inspectors’ access, hardliners in the Bush Administration — led by Dick Cheney, the Vice-President — are seeking clear commitments from Iraq before the first inspectors go to the country.

Particularly disturbing to the hardliners is the fact that the Iraqi Foreign Minister’s letter last Monday accepting the return of inspectors omitted any promise of “unfettered access” or Iraqi co-operation.

Later in the week Saddam’s own letter to the UN General Assembly seemed to impose veiled conditions by insisting that future inspections respect Iraq’s sovereignty and security. Then, over the weekend, Iraq made clear that it would not accept any change in the 1998 memorandum of understanding limiting access to eight presidential palaces.

Many other Security Council members are wary of the British-US effort to get a new resolution. At Friday’s closed-door meeting at the British mission several of the non-permanent members, including Cameroon, Guinea, Mauritius and Mexico, voiced concern that the proposed resolution was intended as a cover for US military action to oust Saddam.

In a direct challenge to the United States and Britain, France has drafted its own proposal, which omits most of the key provisions that its transatlantic allies want.

The French text, which has not been officially circulated, would require the United States and Britain to return to the Security Council for a second resolution to get authorization for military action in the event of Iraqi non-cooperation. It would also avoid declaring Iraq to be in “material breach” of its UN obligations — language that Britain and the United States interpret as an implicit authorization for the use of force.
