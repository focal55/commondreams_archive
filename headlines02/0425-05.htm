Published on Thursday, April 25, 2002 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Berkeley Suspends Group in April 9 Takeover
Part of Crackdown on Disruptions, Says University
by Tanya Schevitz
 
BERKELEY -- A pro-Palestinian group that took over a building at the University of California at Berkeley this month has been suspended from campus under what officials called a crackdown on academic disruptions.

Campus administrators have also told 41 students arrested when the group Students for Justice in Palestine occupied Wheeler Hall that they may be suspended for as long as a year.

"We never had tolerated disruptions of the academy, but what has changed is that the sanctions have changed," Karen Kenney, director of student activities and services, said yesterday.

Before, she said, "students didn't face some form of suspension for cite- and-release arrests."

The action means the group is barred from holding demonstrations on campus, having an information table or handing out leaflets while the university investigates the April 9 protest, during which 79 people were arrested.

Hoang Phan, 25, one of those arrested, said the group had just been acting out the UC Berkeley tradition of civil disobedience.

"We have a Free Speech Movement Cafe on campus. We have the Mario Savio steps," Phan said. "We were making clear our political message. Our event did not threaten anyone. There was no property destruction. We didn't lock it down -- the police locked down the building."

ZERO WARNING

Students from the pro-Palestinian group said campus officials had declared the zero-tolerance policy right before their protest, and they concluded that it was aimed specifically at them. Kenney, however, said UC Berkeley officials had merely strengthened an existing policy after a series of disruptive incidents.

Students for Justice in Palestine also took over Wheeler Hall last April to push for the university to divest its holdings in Israel, and campus administrators warned them they would face sanctions if they disrupted classes during their latest protest, Kenney said.

The students who were arrested this month have been referred to the Student Conduct Office and will go through a disciplinary process. If the arrested students dispute the charges, a committee of students, faculty and administrators can hold a hearing and recommend sanctions to Kenney.

Other groups have been suspended from campus for protests that disrupted classes, Kenney said. A pro-affirmative action organization, By Any Means Necessary, was suspended for several weeks last year after a rally turned into a near-riot.

"It is standard procedure if a student group violates campus rules," Assistant Chancellor John Cummins said. "The takeover of an academic building is a serious violation."

RALLY PLANNED FOR MAY

The university's action may not keep the group's members from staging more anti-Israel demonstrations. Some members of Students for Justice in Palestine have already signed up with the International Socialist Organization to hold another rally in early May.

"In the end, the university can't stop us from exercising our freedom of speech because there are many more people on campus who feel the way we do," said William Youmans, 24, a law student and a member of Students for Justice in Palestine.

The group, which has chapters on college campuses across the country, has held several protests at Berkeley over the past year, including one in which it erected a mock military checkpoint and herded students into "refugee camps."
