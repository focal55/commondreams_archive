Published on Wednesday, July 17, 2002 by Reuters
Amnesty Says Myanmar Using Forced Labor, Torture
by Andrew Marshall
 

BANGKOK - Myanmar's ruling junta rejected an Amnesty International report on Wednesday that said the military was using forced labor, extortion and torture in a campaign to stamp out separatist insurgencies.

The human rights group said the ethnic Shan, Mon and Karen populations in eastern Myanmar were struggling to survive in the face of army demands on their livelihood.

"The situation for civilians in the east of Myanmar is cause for grave concern," Amnesty said.

"The government needs to show it is serious about human rights improvements throughout the country by taking urgent steps to protect civilians from forced labor, extortion and land confiscation at the hands of its armed forces."

Three significant ethnic minority rebel groups are fighting the junta's rule -- the Shan State Army (SSA), Karen National Union and Karenni National Progressive Party. The military has ruled Myanmar, formerly Burma, for 40 years.

Myanmar's human rights record has been widely condemned by the international community and sanctions have pushed its tottering economy to the brink of collapse.

In a statement, the Myanmar junta said the information for Amnesty's report had come from people associated with rebel groups who wanted to discredit the government.

"It is quite natural for these armed terrorist groups to come up with ridiculous assertions in order for them to gain sympathy and financial support," it said.

SIGNS OF HOPE

Amnesty said human rights had improved in Myanmar in some respects. Pro-democracy leader Aung San Suu Kyi was released from house arrest in May and has been holding confidential talks with the junta for almost two years.

More than 300 political prisoners have been released since the talks began, but hundreds remain behind bars.

Amnesty said despite improvements in some areas, urgent action was needed to save minority groups from abuses.

One Shan villager was quoted as saying her friend, Zaye Seng, had died in December 2001 a year after being severely tortured.

"He told me he was beaten for five nights," the woman was quoted as saying. "When he came back his nose and eyes were dripping in blood from head injuries. They submerged him in water. They accused him of giving rice to the SSA."

Amnesty said civilians were made to do construction work on roads and military camps, work on military farms, and act as porters for soldiers for days or weeks at a time.

Forced labor had been outlawed in Myanmar but the order was being flouted by the military, it said.

"The Myanmar government needs to ensure that the order reaches all levels of the military," it said.

"In addition, they must inform villagers of their rights in languages they understand."

It said the military constantly demanded cash and food, leaving many subsistence farmers with insufficient food to feed themselves and their families.

Earlier this month, the junta rejected allegations that hundreds of ethnic Shan women had been raped by Myanmar troops fighting insurgency. The United States urged Myanmar to investigate the allegations.
