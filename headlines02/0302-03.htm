Published on Saturday, March 2, 2002 by the Knight Ridder News Service
Israel Continues Push Across Refugee Camps, Kills 20 Palestinians
by Daniel Rubin
 
JERUSALEM -- Israeli forces drove deeper into two Palestinian refugee camps Friday, announcing they had recovered scores of bombs, mines and other weapons in a massive two- day anti-terrorism assault that left at least 20 Palestinians dead. Two Israeli soldiers died.

Brig. Gen. Gershon Yitzhak said the most costly suicide attacks waged against Israelis had originated at the Balata and Jenin camps in the West Bank, and Israel acted because the Palestinian Authority had failed to do its job.


Palestinian youth kisses seven-year-old Palestinian boy Mahmoud al-Talalqa, who was killed by a Israeli soldier as he played near his residence in the Bedouin village in the northern Gaza Strip, March 1, 2002. A neighbor watching 20 meters away on his balcony told Reuters that an Israeli tank crewman had beckoned a group of children to approach his vehicle, and Mahmoud was then gunned down by four bullets of the tank's machine gun when the party came near. The boy's body remained on the scene until the tank left the area. REUTERS/Ahmed Jadallah
Criticism of the operation mounted, coming from Palestinians and some Israelis. For the first time since he took office a year ago, Prime Minister Ariel Sharon's approval rating dropped below 50 percent in the weekly poll for the Ma'ariv newspaper. A front page editorial in Yediot Ahronot lambasted the dangerous and high-risk sweep, dubbed "Colorful Journey," saying it would be better named "Casino Sharon" because of the operation's uncertain chance of success.

The action followed a series of suicide bombings and shootings, and came as a Saudi Arabian peace initiative gathered momentum. The Israelis had attacked the camps by air before, but this marked the first time they had stormed them. Backed by helicopter gunships, tanks and paratroopers, they searched house-to- house for weapons and fighters, knocking down walls and cutting electricity. Snipers positioned on rooftops provided cover.

At Jenin, Israelis killed at least five people Friday and demolished the house of the leader of the Al Aqsa Martyrs Brigade. The group, a militant wing of Yasser Arafat's Fatah movement, has threatened to retaliate in Israeli cities if the troops don't withdraw.

"Israel is carrying out dangerous measures on the ground against the Palestinian citizens that could involve the two peoples in a new violent cycle," said Husam Khader, a Palestinian Legislative Council member and Fatah leader in Nablus. "The big loser will be the Israeli citizen ... seeking ... security but still not ready for the peace."

Khader said that the reason more than 30 Al Aqsa activists slipped out of the camps before the troops arrived was that the Palestinian authority refused to provide them with ammunition.

Balata, outside of Nablus, is the largest West Bank refugee camp, with about 22,000 residents packed into an area less than a third of a mile square. Israeli officials say it is a base for Al Aqsa. Israel Radio reported that soldiers discovered a pipe bomb factory there Friday.

Jenin, at the northern edge of the West Bank, contains about 12,000 residents, and Israel says many suicide bombers and gunmen from the militant Hamas and Islamic Jihad have departed from there to launch terror attacks.

Among those killed in Friday's fighting in Jenin was Haled Jamal Nazam, a senior member of Hamas' military wing. Palestinians reported that a 10-year-old girl was killed while standing by a window in her home.

In Gaza, a 7-year-old Palestinian boy was killed by what Palestinian witnesses said was Israeli machine gun fire directed toward a Bedouin camp in the northern part of the strip.

The operation had its supporters. An analysis in the Jerusalem Post credited the assault for demonstrating the Army's ability to maneuver in the crowded, narrow streets of the camps, where few authorities -- Israeli or Palestinian -- had dared to go. "The ... ostensible message to the Palestinians is that there are no sanctuaries, no place is off limits and there is no place to hide," Arieh O'Sullivan wrote.

Israeli Cabinet Minister Tzachi Hanegbi said the assault on the camps would help prevent attacks on Israelis. "This is for the sake of our way of life here, in malls, in cities, in streets," he told Israel Army Radio. "The classic strategy of Israeli military campaigns has been to move the fighting into enemy territory."

Palestinians charged that Israel had stepped up its aggression, and intended to scuttle the Saudi proposal that calls for Arab recognition of Israel, provided Israel withdraws from territory won during the 1967 war so the Palestinians can build their own state.

"By its brutal attacks against the Palestinian refugee camps in Nablus and Jenin, the Israeli government has declared its true intentions," Palestinian Information Minister Yasser Abed Rabbo said in a statement.
