Published on Tuesday, September 17, 2002 by Reuters
France's Bove Wears White Frock to GM Crop Trial
 

FOIX, France - Borrowing a centuries-old symbol of French peasant protest, radical farmer Jose Bove and eight others wore girls' frocks to their trial Tuesday for destroying a field of genetically modified crops.


French radical farmer and anti-globalization activist Jose Bove (C) is surrounded by unidentified supporters as he arrives at court in Foix ,southwestern France, September 17, 2002 for the start of his trial for the destruction of genetically modified (GMO) colza oilseed plants in April 2000. Bove and his supporters wore white gowns in reference to a local conflict from the middle ages between peasants and tax collectors. REUTERS/Jean-Philippe Arles
Bove, a star of the anti-globalization movement, faces up to five years prison and a maximum fine of nearly $15,000 for taking part in an April 2000 attack on a field of GM rapeseed in the southwest town of Gaudies by some 400 anti-GM protesters.

Some 200 years ago, poachers in the region wore white frocks to fool gamekeepers trying to enforce a ban on men entering the forest. They were known as the "demoiselles," or maidens.

"Our battle against GM crops has much in common with that of the demoiselles -- we too are running up against a French justice system opposed to common protest action and deaf to the concerns of the population," Bove told reporters as he entered the court in Foix, 20 miles north of the Spanish border.

Up to 500 supporters cheered Bove and his co-defendants as he entered the court room.

While sharing a widespread European suspicion of GM crops, France has permitted GM experiments on around 100 sites.

Bove says the government is underestimating the risks of cross-pollination with natural crops. He defends the Gaudies and similar attacks by invoking the French legal concept of a "state of necessity" compelling citizen action even if it is unlawful.

The pipe-smoking Bove completed a three-month sentence last month for trashing a restaurant of the U.S. chain McDonalds to protest against U.S. tariffs imposed on French specialty food products and fast-food culture.

The CETIOM public research body which planted the 6.1-acre rapeseed field a year before the attack is pressing charges for destruction and damage to property.

It contends the location and small size of the field meant there was no risk of irreversible impact on the environment.

Center Director Andre Pouzet added in a newspaper interview Sunday that the experiment was unique in Europe and aimed to help the government reach a decision over the issue of GM crops.

The verdict is likely to be deferred to a later date after the one-day hearing. Bove has already been handed a six-month jail sentence for a similar attack in 1999 but has launched an appeal to France's highest court, the Cour de Cassation.
