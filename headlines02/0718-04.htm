Published on Thursday, July 18, 2002 by Agence France Presse
New US Saber-Rattling as Saddam Vows to "Emerge Triumphant"
 

Washington stepped up its saber-rattling against Iraq, saying it is determined to see Saddam Hussein removed from power, as the defiant Iraqi leader vowed that he would never be defeated.

"The Iraqi regime, hostile to the United States and supporting terrorism, is a danger that we cannot afford to live with indefinitely," Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz told reporters here after talks with Turkish leaders.

In Baghdad meanwhile, Saddam had fighting words of his own.

In a televised speech marking the 34th anniversary of the coup that brought his Baath Party to power, Saddam said: "Iraq will emerge eventually triumphant."

Evoking the return of the "July revolution," he said: "You will never defeat me this time. Never! Even if you come together from all over the world, and invite all the devils as well."

The administration of US President George W. Bush has openly threatened to take military action against Iraq and unseat Saddam, whom it accuses of developing weapons of mass destruction.

The prospect of US military action loomed larger after the breakdown of July 4-5 talks between Baghdad and the United Nations on the return of UN weapons inspectors to Iraq -- which has been under crippling sanctions since it invaded Kuwait in August 1990.

Media leaks have revealed an alleged plan calling for a massive US air, land and sea invasion of Iraq from three directions, including the use of bases in Jordan to stage air and commando operations.

The leaks embarrassed the White House and compromised relations with Jordan and other Arab allies, with Amman and Abu Dhabi issuing a statement Wednesday registering their opposition to a US military strike.

They said military action "would have grave repercussions not only in that country but also on regional security and stability."

US Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld, furious over the media leaks, circulated a memo to Pentagon officials ordering them to stop disclosures of classified information.

Meanwhile his deputy Wolfowitz has been in Turkey to sound out Ankara over the US campaign, finding similar skittishness about military action against its neighbor to the south.

The United States has anxiously been watching the political turmoil in Turkey, a key NATO ally and cornerstone of its containment policy against Iraq.

But Turkey, engulfed in a severe political and economic crisis over the past 10 weeks, has little room to manuever, commentators said, although it has pressed Washington to keep it abreast of its plans.

Prime Minister Bulent Ecevit met with Wolfowitz despite a new wave of defections from his party that caused his fractious coalition government to lose its parliamentary majority and led him to call for early elections in November.

Wolfowitz said Washington had not yet specifically sought the support of Turkey, while a Turkish official said Ankara had reiterated its reservations over military moves, fearing "unfavorable" repercussions on the country and the region.

Ankara is wary that an operation against Baghdad may help the Kurds in northern Iraq to set up an independent state, which could have a domino effect on its own Kurds at a time when a bloody Kurdish rebellion in the southeast of the country has abated.

The US official argued however: "When there is a democratic Iraq it will not be only the people of Iraq that will benefit from that, it will benefit the whole world and very much this region."

The mainly Muslim but staunchly pro-Western Turkey is home to a US base that was used to launch strikes against Baghdad in the 1991 Gulf War and which remains in use to enforce a no-fly zone over northern Iraq.

British Prime Minister Tony Blair lent new support to US plans against Iraq, saying: "The issue of weapons of mass destruction and Iraq is an issue we have to deal with. It won't go away."

He added that a new UN resolution would not necessarily be required for any military action against Iraq, but said any such action must be in accordance with international law.
