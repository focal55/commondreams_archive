Ross To Toss Burma Buys
As Unocal Is Ordered to Stand Trial for Rights Abuses in Landmark Case, Clothing Retailer Becomes 31st Company To Refuse Ties to Military Regime
 

NEWARK, CA - June 18 - Ross Stores, the discount clothing giant with 452 stores in 22 states and nearly $3 billion in 2001 sales, has announced it will take steps to cut ties to the Southeast Asian country of Burma, which is ruled by a notoriously brutal military junta. The decision, announced in a letter to the Free Burma Coalition on June 6, 2002 says the company "is taking steps to discontinue the future purchasing of goods originating in Burma”.

The move will make Ross the 31st company to refuse ties to Burma since June 2000. The announcement came just before the California-based Unocal oil company was ordered on June 11th to stand trial by California Superior Court Judge Victoria Chaney for alleged human rights abuses related to its pipeline project in Burma. The trial is seen as a landmark case because Unocal would become the first U.S. corporation compelled to answer foreigners' allegations abuse in American courts.

Aung Din, a director at the Free Burma Coalition who spent five years as a political prisoner in Burma’s infamous prison system where he endured torture and solitary confinement, says that although he is unaware of any explicit connection between Ross’s decision and the Unocal lawsuit, more and more companies are recognizing the pervasiveness of forced labor in Burma:

“We’re encouraged that once Ross was informed of the situation in Burma, it decided to move to end its support for this regime and its brutal human rights abuses. It is simply impossible to sell goods from Burma without supporting the military regime and its brutal repression of human rights.”

The U.S. State Department, United Nations, and Amnesty International have documented serious human rights abuses by Burma’s military regime. The U.S. Department of Labor reports that “millions” of Burmese have been pressed into forced labor in Burma, while the U.S. State Department recently documented connections between the export of clothing and forced labor in its Country Report on Human Rights Practices in Burma, released on March 4, 2002: “Forced labor, including forced child labor, has contributed materially to the construction of industrial parks subsequently used largely to produce manufactured exports including garments.”

Wal-Mart, Hanes, and Gart Sports are among a growing list of companies refusing business with Burma. Heidi Maclean, of the Sacramentans for International Labor Rights, hopes more companies will join the list. "Companies like The Children's Place Retail Stores, that continue to sell large quantities of clothes from Burma, are actually supporting forced labor there," she says.

"Sometimes it is better to have the people of the world on your side than the governments of the world."
- Daw Aung San Suu Kyi, General Secretary, National League for Democracy, Burma
