EPA Says Diesel Fumes Likely Cause Cancer, but White House Waffling on Commitment to Cleaning Up Diesel Trucks and Buses
 
WASHINGTON - September 3 - On the very day that millions of children boarded diesel school buses for their first day of the new school year, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency today released its health assessment for diesel exhaust, for the first time formally classifying the pollutant as a likely human carcinogen at environmental levels of exposure.

"Children riding buses back to school today need stronger protections against the health impacts of diesel exhaust, but the Bush administration is considering rolling back clean air standards for diesel buses and trucks," said U.S. PIRG Clean Air Advocate Emily Figdor.

Early in 2001, EPA issued landmark standards to clean up dirty diesel trucks and buses, comparable to the advent of the catalytic converter on cars several decades ago. The standards will reduce emissions from trucks and buses by more than 90%, the equivalent of taking 13 million of the nation's 14 million trucks and buses off the roads. The Bush administration initially backed the standards after taking office and successfully defended them in court against industry challenges.

"Until recently, the Bush administration appeared committed to ushering in the next generation of diesel vehicles," said Figdor. "In June, however, the administration announced that they might allow diesel engine companies to trade emission-reduction credits rather than produce cleaner trucks and buses."

As part of a plan to reduce pollution from diesel-powered construction and farm equipment, the administration is considering developing an emissions-trading program between the truck and "non-road" sectors. This could enable companies to avoid the tough emission reductions required for trucks and buses and compromise the clean up of non-road diesel engines, which actually release more soot each year than all of the cars, trucks, and buses on the roads.

"In order to reduce the public's exposure to harmful diesel emissions, the Bush administration should honor its commitment to fully implement clean air standards for diesel trucks and buses and should pass equivalent standards for diesel construction and farm equipment," said Figdor.

After more than a decade of scientific review, EPA's diesel health assessment evaluates the public health impacts associated with exposure to diesel exhaust. In addition to concluding that diesel fumes likely cause lung cancer, EPA found that diesel exhaust triggers asthma and other respiratory effects. Part of ambient levels of particulate matter, diesel soot can exacerbate cardiovascular and respiratory conditions and lead to premature death.

U.S. PIRG is the national lobbying office for the state Public Interest Research Groups. State PIRGs are non-profit, non-partisan public interest advocacy organizations.
