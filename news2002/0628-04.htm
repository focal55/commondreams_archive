AFSC Launches Initiative To Help Uplift Voices of Africa
Initiative Designed to Foster Peace, Reconciliation and Economic Development
 
PHILADELPHIA - June 28 - At their quarterly meeting, the American Friends Service Committee (AFSC) Board of Directors approved a multi-year, three million dollar initiative designed to foster peace, reconciliation and economic development on the African continent.

The AFSC Africa Initiative explores new avenues for collaboration and cooperation to help lift the voice of Africans onto a global forum. The Initiative is designed to identify and directly address the most pressing and widespread challenges affecting the basic human rights of African people. Programs will focus on education and policy initiatives in the US and fostering systems of collaboration and empowerment in Central and Southern Africa - with targeted focus in East and West Africa.

Grounded in the principles of the Religious Society of Friends, AFSC works without regard to politics or religious affiliation, recognizing that all people have the basic, inalienable right to self-determination and human dignity.

AFSC employs peace makers from key areas of conflict throughout the world, including Angola, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Rwanda, Burundi and other areas of Southern, Central and Eastern Africa. In its peacebuilding efforts in Africa, as in all its activities, AFSC seeks no easy answers, nor does it offer a predetermined or presupposed solution. Instead, the group offers a safe, impartial forum in which people of all races, creeds, religions, ethnicities, tribes, gender affiliations, abilities, social and economic backgrounds can come together to find common ground. AFSC works to allow regions in conflict to forge their own destinies and find the solutions that are uniquely right for their communities.

The AFSC Africa Initiative provides a forum for the diverse people of the African continent to rebuild a culture of peace and justice that fosters sustainable development. It supports:

• the rights of African people to utilize the continents resources for its own sustenance and development

• holding the US government, corporations and multi-lateral institutions accountable and challenging exploitive methodology, policies and practices

• cultivating a well-informed, diverse constituency that ensures justice and equity on the continent and helps shape US policy toward Africa

A key programmatic thrust will be advocacy campaigns supporting the cancellation of Africa’s financial debt owed to the World Bank and International Monetary Fund (IMF), which has now reached crisis proportions. Additionally, the fight against HIV/AIDS and other critical health problems will be pivotal areas of concentration. Vital social and economic justice projects, including the case for reparations, eliminating child slavery and arms control are also integral parts of the venture.

US-based projects include: The Africa: Peace with Justice Tour, which works to promote organized citizen action that will influence US. policy toward the nations of Africa. The next tour is scheduled for mid-October and will visit various locations in St. Louis, Chicago, Detroit and Dayton, Ohio in the Midwest.

AFSC regional offices in the South, the Great Lakes region, New England, the Pacific Northwest, the Pacific Mountain region and the Midwest will create local initiatives and long-term opportunities to impact US policy and decision making, while offering bridge-building opportunities - particularly among women and youth - in the US and Africa. Efforts will include a ‘people-to-people’ cultural exchange for US and African youth, which is scheduled for September 2002.

Founded in 1917 to provide conscientious objectors with an opportunity to aid civilian victims during World War I, AFSC is grounded in Quaker beliefs respecting the dignity and worth of every person. In 1947, the AFSC and the British Friends Service Council received the Nobel Peace Prize on behalf of the Religious Society of Friends, for humanitarian service, work for reconciliation, and the spirit in which these were carried out.

AFSC has programs in the United States, and in Africa, Asia, Europe, Latin America, and the Middle East that focus on issues related to economic justice, peace-building and demilitarization, social justice, and youth. The group has worked in varying capacities in the countries of Africa since the 1950s.

In addition to the national center in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, and international offices throughout the world, AFSC has regional offices in Atlanta, Georgia; Baltimore, Maryland; Cambridge, Massachusetts; Chicago, Illinois; Des Moines, Iowa; New York City, New York; Pasadena and San Francisco, California; and Seattle, Washington.

The American Friends Service Committee is a Quaker organization that includes people of various faiths who are committed to social justice, peace and humanitarian service. Its work is based on the belief in the worth of every person and faith in the power of love to overcome violence and injustice.

Here are some of the realities of Africa:

• According to the UN, 33 of the world's 40 poorest countries are in Africa. Africa is the only continent on earth where both the number and proportion of people who are hungry is increasing. You cannot get poorer if you try.

• In 2000 life expectancy in Afghanistan was 43 years. Life expectancy in 9 African countries - Botswana, Burundi, Malawi, Mozambique, Rwanda, Sierra Leone, Uganda Zambia, and Zimbabwe - falls well below that. It is within this climate that women and youth fight for basic human rights.

• Africa's colonial, post-colonial and neocolonial history is simple: extraction. Whether it’s human cargo shipped off to the global West - enslaved - to fuel 15th and 16th Century European emergence from feudalism, or minerals to fuel the industrial revolution, Africa’s voice is simply not heard.

• The first tragic stories of AIDS did not come from Africa. However, its finale, played out in massive destruction of African lives, will be staged in Africa.

• Oil and diamonds circulate through Angola’s shipping port without money touching its soil or benefiting its people. The world remains helpless in affecting its 40-year war.

• Seven African countries go to war in Congo - Africa’s first World War and only those who arm the warring factions seem to care.

Ordinary people of the world remain largely oblivious of the facts and realities of Africa. Western media ignored their governments historic roles in the unrest and stood by as nearly a million people in Rwanda were massacred within three months.

US public policy does not serve Africa. Even sympathetic government responses are largely focused on trade beneficial to the US or that offer US government-prescribed solutions. Often these solutions are based on misinformation, misunderstanding and misguided assumptions about Africa, Africans and people of African descent.

AFSC’s Africa Initiative seeks to stand with Africa and its people, fighting for the basic rights to self-determination, economic justice, equity and human dignity.
