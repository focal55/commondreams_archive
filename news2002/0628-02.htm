Federal Bureaucrats Try to Undermine Will of the People
 
WASHINGTON - June 28 - Last week, the Federal Election Commission weakened the soft money ban that Congress enacted last April as part of the McCain-Feingold campaign finance reform law.

The Commission's action came in a rulemaking session to implement the provisions of the soft money ban in the new law. Instead, the Commission rejected numerous parts of the draft regulations proposed by its own general counsel, and in multiple ways adopted dramatically weaker rules that threaten to open major loopholes in the soft money ban.

"Congress banned outright the use of soft money contributions to influence federal elections, and the Commission opened the back door for state parties to do it. That hurts the republic in two ways - it undermines the intent of Congress to stop influence buying by corporations, unions, and the wealthy; and it substitutes the judgment of unelected commissioners for the will of Congress and the President," Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger said.

In a joint statement released by the Congressional sponsors of the law, Senators John McCain (R-AZ), Russell Feingold (D-WI), and Representatives Chris Shays (R-CT), and Marty Meehan (D-MA). "The Federal Election Commission has taken upon itself the task of rewriting the newly passed McCain-Feingold/Shays Meehan law. This is not a role given to the FEC by Congress, or by the Constitution. Congress spent seven years debating complex and difficult policy issues before reaching the judgments contained in the bill. The FEC's job is to implement those judgments, not revisit them - to enforce the law, not undermine it."

The Commission attacked the campaign finance law from several angles.

First, it greatly expanded the ability of state parties to spend soft money on activities that influence federal elections. Congress tried to close this loophole by requiring state parties to spend federally regulated money on "federal election activities," including certain voter registration activities, get-out-the-vote activities in connection with a federal election, and voter identification activities. Spending by state parties on all of these areas has been a major avenue for the flow of soft money into federal campaigns.

The Commission substantially weakened this portion of the law by narrowly defining what activities are included in the term "federal election activities." For instance, the FEC general counsel had recommended that all such activities that occur after January 1 of a federal election year be considered "federal election activities." The Commissioners changed this to include only activities that occur after the filing deadline for a state's primary election. In some states, the deadline is as late as mid-August, which means that all state party activities prior to that date can be funded with soft money, as under the current corrupt system.

Similarly, the general counsel had recommended that voter registration activities include both "encouraging" and "assisting" voters to register. The Commission narrowed this to include only activities that "assist" voters to register, thus excluding much of what state parties do in voter registration drives.

The Commission did the same with "get out the vote" activities, eliminating those state party efforts that "encourage" voters to vote and including only those that "assist" them to vote. It also narrowed the category of "voter identification" activities.

Second, the Commission opened the door to federal officeholders and candidates soliciting soft money once again. A key provision of the law was aimed at shutting down solicitations of soft money by federal officials. The Commission, by its narrow definition of the term "to solicit," has almost certainly created a loophole that will be easy to exploit. The FEC's general counsel had recommended that the Commission define the term to include a "request, suggestion or recommendation" that a donation be made. Instead, a majority of commissioners adopted a much narrower definition that requires a direct and explict "ask" for donations.

Proponents of reform fear that federal officials will be able to stay in the business of soliciting soft money by simply avoiding use of the term "ask" when they request or suggest that large soft money donations be made. The Washington Post noted that this change "potentially opens up a loophole, one in which solicitations can be made eliptically enough to be outside the law yet clearly enough to convey the request to a potential donor."

Third, the Commission's regulations fail to implement the law's provisions that shut down so-called "leadership PACs" as vehicles for the raising and spending of soft money by Members of Congress. Such PAC's have been allowed for years under the Commission's loose definition of "affiliation."

The law addressed this problem by banning Members of Congress from raising or spending soft money, and extended this ban to any entity "directly or indirectly established, financed, maintained or controlled" by the Member. The Commission's rules simply defined this new term to mean the same thing as the old "affiliation" standard, thus continuing the fiction that a Member's "leadership PAC" is not affiliated with, or established by, the Member. This potentially allows Members to continue to control soft money through their "leadership PACs."

Fourth, the Commission created another loophole by allowing Members to solicit soft money at state party fundraising events - precisely where soft money is raised. The law is intended to ban all solicitation of soft money by Members, but to allow them to "appear, speak and be a featured guest" at a state party event. Contrary to the advice of its general counsel, the Commission simply created a blanket exemption for Members to solicit soft money if they do so at a state party fundraiser. One Commissioner called this a "total carve out" from the statute.

Overall, the New York Times concluded that the FEC's "rules and exemptions are nothing less than an abuse of power by unelected bureaucrats pushing a corrupt agenda of favoring special interest money over the voices and votes of citizens."

The congressional sponsors of the legislation - Senator John McCain (R-AZ), Senator Russ Feingold (D-WI), Representative Christopher Shays (R-CT) and Representative Martin Meehan (D-MA) - have announced that they intend to seek congressional invalidation of the new regulations under the Congressional Review Act, a law that provides special procedures for Congress to review agency regulations. They have also said that they intend to challenge the new FEC rules in court. The Americans for Reform coalition that helped pass McCain-Feingold are gearing up grassroots efforts to ensure that the campaign finance law does not get rewritten out of existence.
