ActionAid Report: IMF Macro-Economic Conditionality Plays Role in Malawi Deaths and Violated the Right to Food
 
WASHINGTON - June 10 - News Advisory:

WHO: This week ActionAid releases a policy brief based on a recently completed substantive independent report that traces the policy cycle and political economy that led to the recent food related disaster in Malawi. The policy brief isolates policy elements that show aid conditionalities and bad directives from the IMF to the government of Malawi played a role in famine and death at the beginning of this year.

WHAT: "The food crisis in Malawi in early 2002 resulted in several hundred hunger-related deaths -- perhaps several thousand. These deaths make this the worst famine in living memory, certainly worse than the drought of 1991/92, and worse even than the Nyasaland famine of 1949." Quotation from the report.

WHEN: 1:30 p.m., Thursday, June 13

WHERE: National Press Club, The Murrow Room, 14th and F Streets, NW, Washington, D.C.

WHY: Understanding of the process and conditions that led to the deaths of up to 1,000 men, women and children in Malawi this year has been clouded by the public trading of blame by the Government of Malawi and the International Monetary Fund. In the United States, recent USAID statements have further complicated understanding that there has not yet been a famine. The USAID Administrator is scheduled to testify to Congress on Thursday morning on this very issue.

This independent report and brief by one of the largest development, relief and rights NGO in Malawi is the first independent NGO assessments of the famine. The report persuasively argues that the famine was caused by negative synergies between government and donor policies and practices. It outlines the following AND MORE:

-- The IMF instructed the Government of Malawi to sell the Strategic Grain Reserve, to repay a debt of MK1 billion incurred by the National Food Reserve Agency when it was set up as a quasi-independent agency.

-- Private traders profiteered from the sale of the grain reserve, buying maize cheaply and hoarding it until prices rose before reselling it for exorbitant profits.

-- Donor-government relations were strained at the crucial time, because of allegations by the donors of economic mismanagement and governance failures. The donors delayed responding to the food crisis because they were obsessed with finding out what had happened to the Strategic Grain Reserve.

The release of the Policy Brief and Report in Washington occurs during the week that hundreds gather in Rome for the World Food Summit and one month from an IMF Board meeting to receive staff reports on the situation in Malawi.

For further information on the press conference contact Denise Hughes at 202-321-8923.

ActionAid believes that poor people's right to food in 2002 is directly threatened by global trade agreements that are dominated by rich nations and profit-driven multinationals. International corporations and institutions such as the World Trade Organisation, IMF and World Bank have a huge impact on poor people's daily lives. ActionAid works to influence these institutions and national governments in thirty countries where it works, including Malawi and an international level.
