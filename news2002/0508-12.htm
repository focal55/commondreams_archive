Legislation Proposed to Overturn Damaging New Clean Water Act Rule Adopted by the Bush Administration
Representatives to Introduce Bill to Protect Streams and Rivers from Waste Fills
 
WASHINGTON - May 8 - Members of Congress today announced a proposal to overturn the Bush administration's recent assault on laws that protect the nation's waters -- specifically, the adoption last Friday by EPA and the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers of a new rule to allow the Corps to permit wastes to fill and destroy streams, rivers, wetlands and other waterways. Reps. Chris Shays (R-Conn.), Frank Pallone, Jr. (D-N.J.), and a bipartisan group of House members announced at a Capitol Hill press conference that they will introduce legislation to counteract this destructive rule change. Earthjustice supports the legislation and expressed its appreciation for the Representatives' efforts to protect water quality.

"Nothing is more inconsistent with the Clean Water Act than allowing the nation's waterways to be filled and destroyed by industrial wastes -- yet that is precisely what the Bush administration's new regulation is intended to permit," said Joan Mulhern, senior legislative counsel for Earthjustice. "While we believe the Clean Water Act and its regulations already prohibit the obliteration of the country's precious water resources in the manner promoted by the Bush administration, this legislation will overturn the new rule and make it even clearer that dumping coal mining waste and other masses of waste into waters violates the law."

"We greatly appreciate the efforts of all of the Representatives who are supporting this legislation for their leadership in protecting our nation's waters from attempts by the coal industry and the Bush administration to undermine our environmental laws," said Mulhern.

The legislation announced today would amend the Clean Water Act to enact into law the key portion of a regulation, first adopted in 1977, that prohibits the Corps from permitting industries -- including coal mining companies -- from filling waters with waste material. It is this 'waste exclusion' that the EPA and Corps removed from long-standing clean water regulations with the new rule they adopted last Friday.

According to Earthjustice, the Bush administration rushed through the regulatory process late last week to finalize this rule change in an attempt to minimize Congressional and public scrutiny. "The Office of Management and Budget, which supposedly gives close scrutiny to new regulations, officially received and then signed off on this major Clean Water Act rule in about 36 hours. Ordinarily, that is a process that takes at least a month, often much longer," said Mulhern. "That says something about the administration's urge to finalize this rule."

"The bipartisan opposition to this waste dumping rule is significant and has been growing as Representatives and Senators have learned about the threat it poses to waters in their districts," added Mulhern. "I think that worried the administration."

"While this rule change was motivated by the administration's desire to legalize the illegal waste dumping practices of the coal companies that engage in mountaintop removal mining, its effects will be nationwide," Mulhern said. "All streams, wetlands, rivers, and lakes in the country will be placed at risk of becoming a dumping ground for coal and hardrock mining wastes, construction debris, plastic refuse, and other wastes. Every member of Congress and every member of the public concerned about clean water should take a stand against this destructive change in water protection rules."

In March, a dozen senior House Republicans, led by Shays, wrote to President Bush, urging him to reconsider "this ill-advised and dangerous rulemaking" to allow waste disposal in waters.

They were joined on April 30 by fifty-seven Members of the House of Representatives, led by Pallone, who sent a letter to EPA Administrator Whitman conveying their "strong opposition" to the proposed rule. "This rule change is a clear attempt to legalize the destructive practice of mountaintop removal coal mining, where coal companies literally blow the tops off of mountains and dump the waste into nearby valleys and streams," stated the letter.

On May 1, Sens. James Jeffords (I-Vt.) and Joseph Lieberman (D-Conn.) sent a letter to President Bush asking him to stop immediately his administration's efforts to overturn this important Clean Water Act rule. The letter from the Environment and Public Works Committee Chair and the Wetlands Subcommittee Chair, respectively, stated, "the proposed rule would jeopardize the health of the nation's streams, wetlands, lakes, rivers and other waters."

Copies of the Congressional letters are available by contacting Ken Goldman.

Earthjustice is the nonprofit law firm for the environment representing-without charge-hundreds of public interest clients, large and small. Earthjustice works through the courts to safeguard public lands, national forests, parks and wilderness areas; to reduce air and water pollution; to prevent toxic contamination; to preserve endangered species and wildlife habitat; and to achieve environmental justice.
