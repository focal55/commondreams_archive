PSR Applauds New Nuclear Arms Reductions, Urges Bush and Putin to Destroy, Not Store Deadly Weapons
 
WASHINGTON - May 13 - Members of Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR) applauded today's announcement by President Bush that he will co-sign a treaty with Russian President Putin to reduce U.S./Russian nuclear arsenals later this month. Although the finer details have not been released, the agreement will allow for some weapons to be stored, and possibly reactivated.

"This agreement is an important step but we need to see the actual language of the treaty," said Robert K. Musil, PhD, MPH, executive director and CEO of Physicians for Social Responsibility. "We are still concerned about placing U.S. nuclear weapons in storage instead of permanently putting them out of commission. And we are concerned that this treaty will be a footnote on the way to President Bush's long-sought flexible arsenal of useable war-fighting offensive and defensive nuclear weapons."

Since Bush and Putin agreed in November in principle to reductions of up to 67 percent in current arsenals, Russia has pushed for a formal agreement that would codify formal cuts, while Bush has leaned toward an informal agreement with minimal restrictions. Negotiations have continued since then, and a compromise reached this week allowed for a formal treaty to be created, but preserved the Bush Administration's dangerous desire to be able to reverse arms cuts by re-deploying weapons.

"The big question here is whether these nuclear weapons will be reserved to use another day, or taken permanently out of commission," said Musil. "Our position is that arms control treaties should reduce nuclear dangers with real cuts."

Putin and Bush will meet in St. Petersburg May 23-26 to sign the new agreement and to discuss principles for the new relationship between the two countries.

"We're happy that the two presidents plan to take thousands more nuclear weapons off their dangerous, high-alert status, but we urge Mr. Bush to truly make the world a safer place by committing to truly destroy these weapons and cease development of new, dangerous, war-fighting nuclear arms," said PSR Nuclear/Security Program Director Martin Butcher. "There are already too many skeletons in the United States nuclear closet. These weapons are far too dangerous to be maintaining a shadow arsenal ready to deploy at a moment's notice."
