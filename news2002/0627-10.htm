Physicians For Social Responsibility Applauds Senate EPW Committee's Action in Support of Cleaning Up Power Plant Pollution
 
WASHINGTON - June 27 - In a letter sent to Senators this morning, over 1,300 doctors, nurses and health professionals called for support of legislation that would dramatically cut emissions of nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulfur dioxide (SO2), mercury, and carbon dioxide from power plants. Hearing the call of health professionals around the nation, the Senate Environment and Public Works Committee passed the "Clean Power Act of 2001," (S.556) introduced by Senator Jeffords (I-Vt.), that requires emission reductions of these four pollutants.

"Power plants are the leading source of the harmful air pollution that is taking its toll on America's health," the letter states, and "we urge you to take advantage of the opportunity before you to clean up America's air."

"The EPW Committee's action today represents a huge leap towards cleaner air and improved health. The Clean Power Act is the most effective way to protect public health from air pollution and we call on the President to support this plan that will require stringent cuts of these four pollutants," said, Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., M.P.H., executive director and CEO of Physicians for Social Responsibility.

Scores of new studies each year demonstrate that air pollution can be harmful to human health and that children, the elderly, and those with respiratory diseases are most susceptible. Power plants are the source of 67 percent of total U.S. emissions of SO2, more than a quarter of the nation's NOx, one third of the nation's mercury pollution, and the source of approximately 40 percent of carbon dioxide emissions.

Studies indicate that exposure to SO2 and NOx can result in reduced lung function, increased asthma attacks, more visits to doctor's offices and emergency rooms, increased hospitalization rates, and even death. The effects of mercury can range from subtle losses of sensory or cognitive ability and developmental delays, to birth defects, to death. Children and pregnant women or women of childbearing age are most at risk.

Carbon dioxide contributes to the blanketing effect of greenhouse gases that traps excess heat in our atmosphere. This forces global average temperatures to rise and the climate to change. Higher temperatures take their toll on human health, leading to an increase in cases of heat-related illnesses, water- and vector- borne disease, and exacerbating respiratory illnesses by increasing smog levels.

In their letter, health professionals also spoke out against the Administration's proposed changes to current Clean Air Act -- New Source Review programs, calling for the full enforcement and support of programs that result in fewer emissions.
