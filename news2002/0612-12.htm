Global Business Council on HIV/AIDS Awards Gala
Ploy to Detract from Inaction on Global AIDS Disaster, say AIDS Activists
 
NEW YORK - June 12 - AIDS activists leading efforts to secure treatment for people dying with AIDS are targeting the world's largest companies today at the Gala Awards ceremony of the Global Business Council on HIV/AIDS. The ceremony, attended by Bill Clinton and UN Secretary General Kofi Annan, is to be held at Pier 60 of the Chelsea Piers June 12, 2002.

"Multinational companies have no laurels to rest on," said ACT UP New York co-founder and HIV-positive activist Eric Sawyer. "The most prominent members of the Global Business Council -- Coca-Cola and Anglo American -- have plainly reneged on their promises to provide antiretroviral treatment and care to their workers and their families. They have nothing to be proud of, and nothing to be congratulated for."

According to activists, representatives to the awards ceremony from corporations with large African employee bases should stop celebrating and take substantive steps to provide treatment to people with HIV and AIDS in the developing world. "The massive corporations that are members of the Global Business Council have not done enough to sit in a room and congratulate each other on their good work. They have skirted their most fundamental obligation, which is to pay for treatment for their workers," said Sharonann Lynch of ACT UP New York.

"Richard Holbrooke, the United Nations and multinational corporations have promised to bring life and hope to millions of people with HIV in the developing world by providing antiretroviral treatment. Instead, we've lost ground over the past year, as Coca-Cola and Anglo-American have broken their promises," added Lynch.

At Coca-Cola's annual meeting activists launched a campaign singling out the beverage company, the largest sole private sector employer in Africa, for their refusal to pay for HIV treatment for workers in their bottling and distribution facilities. Coca-Cola has refused, choosing instead to offer little more than billboard and truck space to desperately poor African country governments for anti-AIDS advertisements and safe sex materials.

"Crumbs from Coke while thousands of workers face a needless death sentence are unacceptable. Coke must commit to paying for the full course of antiretroviral treatment for all of their infected workers and their families," said Amanda Lugg of ACT UP New York. "With over 100,000 employees, Coke is the largest private employer in Africa. Africa is Coke¹s largest growth market. The company's inaction is inexcusable."

"Last year, Coca-Cola and Anglo-American grabbed the headlines with big announcements about workplace initiatives. Anglo-American has reneged on its treatment policy; Coca-Cola has failed to treat its workers and their families," said Eustacia Smith of ACT UP New York. "Meanwhile there are 14,000 new HIV infections every day."
