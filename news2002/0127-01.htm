Sept. 11 Victim's Families Return from Afghanistan Trip Press Conference Monday, January 28, 10 a.m. 777 U.N.Plaza
After meeting with victim's families of U.S. bombing in Afghanistan, Sept. 11 victim’s families call for compensation fund for innocent Afghans
 
NEW YORK, NEW YORK - January 27 - Sept. 11 victim's families returning from a 9-day visit to Afghanistan will hold a press conference on Monday, January 28 at 10 a.m. at 777 United Nations Plaza, 12th Floor (corner of 1st and 44th, entrance on 44th).

They will speak about their cathartic meetings with Afghans who lost family members in the recent U.S. bombing campaign and their visits with hospital workers, relief agency officials, and internally displaced refugees. They will also call for the establishment of a compensation fund for Afghan victims of the U.S .bombing campaign, similar to the fund that was set up for Americans whose family members were killed in the attacks of Sept. 11.

"We’ve met with people who have lost loved ones to the U.S. bombing We have looked at pictures of their families, they have looked at pictures of ours, we have talked with them, we feel the same grief," said Kelly Campbell, whose brother-in-law Craig Amundson was killed in the Pentagon attack, in an interview from Afghanistan with Agence France Presse. "The United States government needs to take responsibility for the direct effect on these people's lives," she added. "We owe it to them to do what we can to help them rebuild their homes and give their children health care and an education so they can get on with their lives."

"Monetary compensation can never erase the sorrow of loss. But establishing a compensation fund would represent a significant step toward building the bonds of friendship that are the true foundations of peace," said Medea Benjamin, founding director of Global Exchange, the group that organized the trip. "Whether one supported the bombing or not, shouldn’t our hearts and helping hands go out to those people who were every bit as innocent as the victims of the September 11 attack?"

The delegation to Afghanistan included four Americans whose family members were victims of the Sept. 11 attacks:

Rita Lasar is a 70-year-old retired small businesswoman from New York City. Rita lost her brother, Abe Zelmanowitz, at the World Trade Center. Abe was on the 27th floor of 1 World Trade Center when the first plane hit. Although he could have gotten out of the building, he chose instead to stay with his friend, a quadriplegic who could not get out.

Kelly Campbell is a 29-year-old environmental campaign coordinator in Oakland California. Her brother-in-law, 28 year-old Craig Amundson, was killed in the Pentagon attack, leaving behind a young wife and two small children. Kelly is traveling on behalf of Craig’s widow, Amber Amundson, who has been an outspoken voice for reconciliation.

Eva Rupp is a 28-year-old woman who works in Washington, DC at the Department of Commerces National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration. Eva is the step-sister of Deora Bodley, and the two of them were close from the time Deora was five.

Derrill Bodley is a 56-year-old professor of music at Sacramento City College and the University of the Pacific in Stockton, California. Derrill lost his daughter, Deora, on United Airlines Flight 93, which crashed in Pennsylvania.

Before he left for Afghanistan, Derrill Bodley said: "For me, this trip is about respect and love for all human beings by all human beings, regardless of where they come from. I’m going on this journey to show my concern for those innocent Afghans who have died or are suffering now. By embracing our common humanity and sharing our sorrow perhaps we will be able to avoid other loss in the future." While in Afghanistan, Bodley met and cried with a man who had also lost his daughter in the aftermath of Sept. 11a man whose 5-year-old daughter was killed by a U.S. bomb.

Also participating in the press conference will be Masuda Sultan, a young Afghan-American woman who lost 19 members of her extended family when the US bombed their farm outside Kandahar just over a month ago.

Although a full accounting of civilians killed during the U.S. bombing of Afghanistan has not yet occurred, two studies of civilian casualties put the number at between 1,000 and 4,000 people. The studies were conducted by the Center for Defense Alternatives (http://www.comw.org/pda/) and by a University of New Hampshire Economics Professor (http://pubpages.unh.edu/~mwherold).

The victims-to-victims delegation was organized by Global Exchange, an international human rights organization whose motto is "building people-to-people ties." Global Exchange say the journey had a profound impact in both countries by showing that individual Afghans and Americans suffer alike. Afghans saw that U.S. citizens do care about their well being. The U.S. visitors gained a deeper understanding of Afghans as real people. And people around the world who learned of the exchange through the extensive press coverage it received witnessed the common humanity of all persons. The press conference of the delegation participants is co-sponsored by the American Friends Service Committee.
