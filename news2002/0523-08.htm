NWLC Disappointed Over D. Brooks Smith Vote
 
WASHINGTON - May 23 - The National Women's Law Center is profoundly disappointed that a majority of the Senate Judiciary Committee has voted to approve the nomination of D. Brooks Smith to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit. Judge Smith, who currently serves on a federal district court in Western Pennsylvania, has a troubling record on issues of particular concern to women, including his continued membership in a club that excludes women despite his promise to the Senate Judiciary Committee that he would resign.

"We call on the full Senate to reject this nomination," said Marcia D. Greenberger, NWLC Co-President. "Judge Smith's refusal to resign from a discriminatory club, especially when viewed in combination with other troubling statements and rulings in his record, demonstrates that he does not belong on the Court of Appeals."

Judge Smith was for many years a member of a club that bars women from membership. Membership in a club that discriminates on the basis of sex, race, national origin or religion is prohibited by the code of judicial conduct because it can be viewed as a judge's tacit endorsement of the discriminatory practices. Moreover, during his 1988 confirmation hearing for the district court seat he now holds, Judge Smith assured the Senate Judiciary Committee that he would resign from the club if he could not get it to change its rules within a year. But he did not resign until 1999 -- over ten years later -- when the seat he now seeks on the Third Circuit became vacant.

Judge Smith has also espoused extreme views on Congress' authority to enact important protections for women like the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA). In a 1993 speech to the Federalist Society, he stated an extremely narrow view of Congress' authority to protect women from violence and disregarded the extensive Congressional findings that supported VAWA.

Judge Smith's decisions as a district court judge include some that reflect a disregard for established legal principles protecting against sex discrimination. For example, he struck down an affirmative action policy for female firefighters, relying on an incorrect and overly stringent standard of review. In another case, he upheld a family leave provision that applied to mothers but not fathers, seemingly assuming that only mothers raise children; a higher court reversed the decision.

For more information, please view NWLC's special report, "The Nomination of D. Brooks Smith to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit" at http://www.nwlc.org/pdf/DBrooksSmithReport.pdf
