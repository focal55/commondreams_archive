Beware Of Media Spin After Debates
by John Nichols
 
BOSTON -- In October 1984, as a young reporter just four months into my first job writing for a big-city newspaper, I was dispatched to Kansas City to cover the second presidential debate between Republican Ronald Reagan and Democrat Walter Mondale.

The debate, it was said, would be a high-stakes affair. Former Vice President Mondale was broadly viewed as having won the first debate with a sharp, issue-oriented challenge to the seemingly less prepared and certainly less sharp Reagan, a man in his mid-70s who was clearly showing his age. Indeed, the sitting president's performance had been so inept that it is now referenced as a possible first public manifestation of his Alzheimer's disease.

The story of how the media saved Reagan with its coverage of that second 1984 debate points up everything that is wrong with how these campaign face-offs are staged and reported. It also offers a cautionary warning to viewers of tonight's much-hyped encounter between Democrat Al Gore and Republican George W. Bush.

The Gore-Bush debate comes at a moment that is, if anything, even more critical than that second 1984 debate. Polls show that this year's candidates are evenly matched in their popular appeal -- and those numbers are just the latest measure of a constantly shifting contest where support for both contenders is so soft that minor missteps can have big meaning.

But the question of how the media will measure those missteps is even more up in the air than the question of who will win in November. And the Orwellian lessons from the 1984 Mondale-Reagan debate are not encouraging.

Sitting in the audience in Kansas City that night, I watched as Ronald Reagan lost it. In a rambling closing statement, the incumbent president went down the Pacific Coast Highway and never came back. It appeared that Reagan was attempting to expand on the "morning in America'' theme of his campaign's television commercials. But what the audience heard was a unfocused, seemingly unending rumination on California cruising that went, precisely, nowhere.

Finally, mercifully, moderator Edwin Newman interrupted Reagan, explaining to the leader of the free world that, while he had made no point whatsoever, he had run well beyond his allotted time. And on the basis of one of the strangest and most disconcerting performances ever in a presidential debate, it seemed that Reagan's time was, indeed, up.

Until the media started "spinning'' the story.

By the time the television talk shows got done with the "instant analysis'' and the morning papers rolled off their presses, a truly tragic presidential performance had been transformed into a triumphal "knockout punch'' because Reagan had thrown off a line about not wanting to exploit Mondale's youth and inexperience. Scripted to address rising concerns about his age and mental agility, the quip was delivered perfunctorily by a man who had spent most of his adult life in Hollywood.

But how could a throwaway line erase the reality of a dismal debate performance?

Because, for all the hype, debates are usually reduced to a couple of sound bites that are repeated again and again in the post-debate coverage that ends up defining winners and losers. Television could reproduce the "youth and inexperience'' clip a lot more easily and a lot less controversially than it could the more revealing closing-statement mishap.

So that's what television did. And the rest was history -- or, rather, historical revision.

Reagan lost the second debate in 1984 badly. But the media reports made him a "winner'' -- and that "win'' virtually guaranteed his re-election.So, as viewers watch tonight's debate, they would be well advised to dismiss the spin and believe their own eyes. Because, as that final 1984 debate proved, reality often gets washed away in a media spin cycle that opts for the quick and inoffensive one-liner over the complex and disturbing reality.
