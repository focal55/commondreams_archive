Published on Sunday, December 17, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Nation Has Dismissed Precious Florida Votes
Registering to vote took courage for blacks in Panhandle
by Wendel Brunner
 
LET ME tell you why I believe that every vote in Florida should count.

In 1965, as a 19-year-old college student, I was part of a mid-20th century children's crusade, the second Freedom Summer. Hundreds of white students headed South to join local civil rights activists to register black voters, integrate the schools and desegregate public accommodations.

Today's younger voters cannot begin to imagine what some people had to go through to cast a ballot, or how important a vote was to their lives. Thirty- five years ago, the Florida Panhandle surrounding Tallahassee belonged to the Deep South. In some places, blacks had not voted since Reconstruction. Many high school students didn't know that blacks had ever voted in the Deep South.

Few Southern whites welcomed our arrival. The summer before, members of the Klan, in collusion with local sheriffs, had killed three civil rights workers in Mississippi and buried their bodies in an earthen dam. The names Andrew Goodman, James Cheney and Michael Schwerner were added to those of Medgar Evers and all the other civil rights workers murdered in the South. We had no idea what lay in store for us.

I ended up in Gadston County, west of Tallahassee, working with the Congress On Racial Equality, or CORE. Blacks were in the majority in that county, and several had registered to vote as early as 1962. In some ways, registration was relatively easy. There was no literacy test and no poll tax, as was the case in other Southern states. All you had to do was go to the registrar's office at the county courthouse, which was open only Tuesday afternoons. You filled out a form, and in front of God and everyone, signed your name.

But people who registered lost their jobs. Their families were threatened. Nightriders fired bullets into their homes. They couldn't get credit to plant their crops.

Meanwhile, we CORE workers knocked on doors, spoke in churches, held meetings, passed out flyers and organized car pools. And every Tuesday afternoon a small line of folks formed at the county courthouse, where they dared to register to vote.

Gadston provided a stark vision of what happens to people whose votes don't count. The economy of the county was based on plantations which grew shade tobacco. Many black workers lived in isolation in company-owned houses. Their bosses paid them in company scrip, good only at the company store. They rarely saw real money, and they never were out of debt.

These conditions existed in other parts of the South, but in Gadston they were even more oppressive. If workers left the plantation Sheriff O. C. Wade arrested them and brought them back to work off their debt.

This kind of coerced labor still exists in some parts of the world, and pops up occasionally even in the United States, usually in some sweatshop stuffed with illegal immigrants.

There is a precise term for it. It is called slavery.

Violence and intimidation pervaded the culture in the Florida Panhandle. After six weeks, another CORE member and I transferred to Jefferson County, east of Tallahassee, where we stayed in a farmhouse with a family of sharecroppers.

As CORE workers, all of us, black and white, embraced Rev. Martin Luther King Jr.'s philosophy of non-violence. In some ways this was an easy stance for us. The mother of the family slept with a pistol under her pillow to protect us, and her three small children, from nightriders enraged at the thought that blacks might vote.

During the daytime, however, we were on our own. More than once, armed men in pickup trucks chased us down the red-dirt roads, past sharecropper shacks with no electricity or running water, over earthen levees in the swamps, past children whose legs were bowed with rickets.

At the end of the summer, I returned home to Berkeley, finished college, then graduate school and medical school, and eventually became a public health physician. I never knew what happened to those people who remained in Florida.

But the stories coming out of the state over the last month are haunting. African Americans purged from voter lists. Old Votomatic machines jammed with chads in predominantly black precincts. Police roadblocks in African American communities on election day.

All this brings back memories of a time I had wished was long gone but realize is still with us in different ways.

I still see those proud, courageous black people lining up to register and now, in the aftermath of this election, I stare at the U.S. Supreme Court's words with disbelief, the majority babbling about equal protection, and I cannot believe that every vote was not counted.

Wendel Brunner works as the director of public health in Contra Costa County.
