Published on Monday, February 7, 2000 in the St Paul Pioneer Press
Earned Income Tax Credit Should Be Expanded Even Further
by  Max Sawicky
 

We hear a lot about the heavy yoke of the federal income tax, with blather about capital gains, the ``death tax'' (look out -- it's Darth Vader!), AMTs and BVDs.

But according to Bill Gale of the Brookings Institution, for more than 70 percent of taxpayers the payroll tax takes a larger bite. The payroll tax finances Social Security and Medicare, so it will not be reduced any time soon. But it is easy to reduce its burden for low-income families with the Earned Income Tax Credit.

Do you qualify for the EITC?

It depends on two things: the number of children in your family and your wage level. I'll give you a quick summary, but for the gory details you need Publication 596 from the IRS. Applying is not so hard: The IRS will do the calculations for you. Nonprofit groups are helping, too. And there's always H&R Block.

The credit is refundable. That means the government mails you a check, even if you don't owe any income taxes. This is real money.

If you have no children, you get a rather small tax credit -- no more than $347. As wages increase beyond $5,700, the credit shrinks. By the time wages reach $10,200, the credit phases out altogether.

If you have one child, the credit expands considerably, to as much as $2,312. That's more like it! You keep this amount until your wages exceed $12,500, at which point the benefit begins to shrink. The vanishing point for benefits is $26,900.

With two or more kids, the credit expands yet again. The maximum is $3,816 for wages between $9,500 and $12,500. Families with two or more children and wages below $30,850 could qualify.

Enter President Clinton, who expanded the credit in 1993 and proposes to do so again. First, the maximum credit for families with three or more children would rise by $500.

Second, for married two-earner couples the phase-out point is raised.

Third, Clinton would increase the maximum level of wages that qualify for a credit.

Fourth, some families who do not qualify now because their income is too high would be able to get the tax credit if they put money into a 401(k) savings plan. Fifth, Clinton would make the Dependent Care Credit refundable, which helps low-income parents who pay for child care.

The president's proposals are endorsed by Democrats but resisted by Republicans in Congress because Clinton heartlessly neglects the rich.

In the end, the GOP will fold on this one. More important is that Clinton's proposal could go much further. There is plenty of money available to raise the maximum credit level and extend the phase-out range beyond what the president proposes.

Given the drive to place welfare recipients in jobs that are likely to be low-paying, making work pay more would be a splendid kickoff to this year's budget debate.

Three basic principles for expanding the credit are:

  • Offset all income and payroll tax for individuals and families in poverty.

  • Nobody who works full-time should be in poverty.

  • The climb from poverty to the middle class should be facilitated.

    We could start by expanding the benefit for low-paid single persons or couples with no children. Right now the credit begins to phase out at $5,700, well below the poverty line. Better to eliminate the entire payroll tax burden for all poor households.

    What about families with children? Consider a family with income between $31,000 and $40,000. They do not qualify for the EITC. They will not owe much income tax, but they could pay more than $6,000 in payroll tax. In addition, their income will bump them out of eligibility for health insurance under the Medicaid program.

    There are few more worthy uses of the federal government's burgeoning budget surpluses. On an annual basis, these are projected in the hundreds of billions.

    Clinton proposes to use $2 billion for the EITC expansion. Here's hoping for a ``bottom-up'' approach to tax cuts this year.
