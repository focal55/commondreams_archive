Beware The Radical Right's Silence
by Dave Zweifel
 
Where's the normally obstreperous Christian Coalition this election campaign?

It's almost as though Pat Robertson, Jerry Falwell and Ralph Reed fell off the face of the Earth. They haven't uttered a sound in several months now.

But never fear. They're just around the corner waiting for Nov. 7 to roll around and crossing their fingers that George W. is going to be elected. Then they'll be back in all their glory.

Russell Hemenway, the director of the National Committee for an Effective Congress, recently wrote:

"Last spring, when Bush hired Ralph Reed, the former head of the Christian Coalition, he bought himself an insurance policy.

"Reed did his job well. He and Pat Robertson convinced the leaders of the 40 organizations that make up the core of the radical right that winning was paramount.

"They must allow Bush to campaign as a moderate. Reed's message was simple. Keep a low profile and, if elected, George W. Bush was going to be their boy.''

In other words, this whole business about being a compassionate conservative doesn't hold much water as far as Hemenway is concerned.

Lying in the weeds, obviously, is good strategy for the likes of Robertson and Falwell. They have become a lightning rod for many Americans who are repelled by the coalition's attempts to impose its ideas of morality on the rest of the country. Even some longtime Republicans have become so alarmed at the Christian Right's agenda -- forced prayer in the schools, no abortion even if the mother's life is at stake, strict condemnation of homosexuals -- that they've either voted for the Democrat or simply stayed home on Election Day.

By agreeing to stay in the background, Robertson et al. have allowed Bush to campaign as a "new'' moderate without the baggage that hindered his father in 1992 and Bob Dole in 1996.

In another year, Falwell, Robertson and Reed would have been at Bush's throat for the waffling he did on the abortion question in the first debate. Yet there wasn't a peep from them even though Bush danced around the issue of whether he would appoint Supreme Court justices based on their anti-abortion stands.

As Hemenway says, "It's eerie.''

The unanswered question, of course, is just how much has Bush promised these people for their silence?
