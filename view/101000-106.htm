Published on Tuesday, October 10, 2000
Presidential Debates and My Three Daughters
by Fran Shor
 
Unlike the bland and boring debates that marked this last week, the discussions between friends and family about whether to vote for Gore or Nader continue at an intense level. For those still frightened by the possibility of a victory for Bush and the right-wing goblins and ghouls gathered around his campaign, not to vote for Gore, especially in states where the race is tight, is tantamount to treason to progressive causes. For supporters of Nader, Gore and the Democrats have betrayed those causes so many times that not voting for Nader is tantamount to enabling an abusive spouse.

Although I have made an unshakable commitment to the Green Party and the Nader/LaDuke ticket, my three daughters reflect, I believe, the variety of positions adopted by those wrestling with the impact of the Nader campaign. To review each of their situations and their perspectives may shed some light on the divisions that underscore the Gore/Nader dilemma.

My oldest daughter, Molly, resides in Michigan - a key “toss-up” state. As a young professional with feminist convictions, she is horrified at the thought of what a Bush Administration would do to the right to choose. Although I have pointed out that this is a right that women will still have to struggle to retain, irrespective of Supreme Court appointments, she cannot bring herself to “waste” her vote on Nader. When I’ve countered that a “wasted” vote is one that is squandered on the lesser of two evils, she argues that she could not bear the thought that her vote would help elect Bush.

My middle daughter, Miriam, is an actress living in New York City. With equal amounts of realism and idealism, she understands that voting for Nader will not cost Gore a victory in the safe state of New York. While not convinced that Nader is the best Presidential candidate on all of the issues that concern her, she values his integrity against the highly scripted and opportunistic Gore.

My youngest daughter, Emma, is a freshman at the University of California at Berkeley. That in itself is an indication of where she comes down on the Presidential question. With a sticker attached to her dorm room saying “Gore and Bush make me want to Ralph!,” she reflects the incredible passion with which young students have embraced the Nader campaign. Also, as an Ani DiFranco-loving, vegan-proselytizing, and righteous-declaiming teenager, she represents one wing of a growing Green orientation around this country.

I am proud of all my three daughters. I deeply respect their intelligence and choices they have made and continue to make in their lives. Even if they don’t share all of the values I have absorbed and articulate in my life, they have a wonderful capacity to re-shape these values according to their own consciences. For that I am thankful, even if there are still certain disagreements, whether political or not.

On the other hand, the great fortune and privilege that we all have does not extend to other young woman and girls. Under other circumstances, I could be a father of a young teenage daughter in Vietnam suffering from the toxic residues left behind by the American military (a military that both Gore and Bush supported and continue to support in aggressive interventions). Now working in a Nike factory under near slave conditions with slave wages, the life of this teenage girl would not be made better under either a Gore or Bush Presidency.

If I were a father in Iraq whose young daughter came down with dysentery because of the destruction of the water sewage system overseen by Washington policymakers during the Gulf War (a war supported by Gore and Bush) would a Bush or Gore Presidency change the policies that led to this destruction and the continuing blockade of simple chlorine tablets and other medicines necessary to prevent the deaths of thousands of daughters and sons? Where would the relief come from?

And if I were a father in Gaza, running away with my young daughter from Apache helicopter gunships piloted by Israeli soldiers but supplied by the political support of politicians like Gore and Bush, what vote would help me and my family to live in peace in my own country?

Yes. My three daughters and I are very fortunate. We have the luxury of arguing and debating about our choices for President. We also are not threatened in a direct way by the ravages of corporate globalization or imperialist policies. However, isn’t it incumbent upon all of us to recognize that voting for Gore and Bush and everything they represent is a threat to the well-being of so many other children around the world. If we are only fathers to our daughters, parents to our own children, and not the protectors of others, who are we? If not now for Nader and alternatives to politics as usual, when?

Fran Shor teaches at Wayne State University. He is an active member of the Metro Detroit Greens and a coordinator for “Labor for Nader.” His e-mail address is: f.shor@wayne.edu.
