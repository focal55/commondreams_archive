Trade Policy Is Still The Great Lost Issue Of The Presidential Campaign
by  John Nichols
 

MANCHESTER, N.H. -- A few days before the high-stakes New Hampshire presidential vote, Paul Wellstone and I were careening across the first-primary state on our way to another of the dozens of campaign pep rallies where the Minnesota senator waved the flag for Bill Bradley's moderately insurgent Democratic presidential candidacy.

No one campaigned harder for Bradley in Iowa and New Hampshire than Wellstone. No one -- including Bradley -- campaigned more effectively. And no one seemed to enjoy the political fray more than did Wellstone, who had to ditch his own presidential hopes because of a bad back.

As our Jeep rolled into Manchester, Wellstone and I got to talking about the big "what if.'' What if his back hadn't been so bad? What if he could have mounted a serious campaign for the Democratic presidential nomination?

At first, Wellstone was resistant. He didn't want to take the focus off Bradley, and he certainly didn't want to take the discussion to the logical conclusion: that, in every sense, he would have been a better challenger to Al Gore than Bradley has been.

But Wellstone's natural exuberance soon took over, and he admitted, "I wouldn't mind being in some of these debates.''

What issue would Wellstone have brought to the debates that Bradley and Vice President Gore have failed to address?

"Seattle!'' says the Minnesota senator, employing the city name that has become a universal shorthand for opposition to corporate-driven "globalization'' of the economy and the "free trade'' agenda of dark economic alchemists who would have us all eating genetically modified foods produced by multinational corporations that jump from country to country in order to depress wages, avoid regulation and otherwise undermine community and civil society.

Wellstone understands what Bradley and the other major candidates do not: that the issues raised during last fall's World Trade Organization sessions in Seattle matter a lot more than the minutiae that has tended to define Democratic and Republican presidential debates this year.

In late November and early December, the Seattle gathering of trade ministers and their minions was disrupted by tens of thousands of trade unionists, farmers, safe-food activists, environmentalists and others who objected to the WTO's efforts to eliminate the ability of the United States and other nations to control corporations. Polls show that close to 60 percent of Americans share the concerns of the protesters in Seattle about "free trade'' policies designed to benefit corporations while cutting wages, gutting environmental protections and forcing farmers off the land.

Yet, none of the major presidential candidates has even mentioned Seattle -- let alone the issues that came into focus there: corporate responsibility, deindustrialization, the collapse of family farming, food safety, environmental decay, the ongoing attack on public services.

In the two months after marches, rallies, teach-ins and riots in Seattle exposed the depth of America's discomfort with free trade, most of this country's political "leaders'' have worked to sweep the issues back under the table. But Wellstone says that is a fool's mission, as the American people are not going to stop asking tough questions about corporate misbehavior and the government policies that permit it.

"A lot of what was captured in Seattle -- the questions about the changing face of the global economy -- is not going away,'' says Wellstone. "These are issues that are going to have to be addressed. They can't be avoided forever.''

Eventually, says Wellstone, the coalitions that came together to challenge the corporate agenda in Seattle will develop into a powerful political movement.

"A hundred years ago, the challenge was to civilize the national economy. There were powerful interests working to maintain the status quo -- as there are today. But a great populist and progressive movement sprang up, and that movement gave us anti-trust laws and safe food regulations, child labor laws, the 40-hour work week and so many of the other policies we take for granted today,'' says Wellstone. "Now what you have is another transformation -- a transition to a global economy. Now, the demand is to civilize the global economy. And, just as it was at the start of the last century, that demand is coming from the people. It is only a matter of time before the political leaders have to respond.''

The lack of a response this year is frustrating, however. Despite Wellstone's close alliance with him, Bradley remains firmly ensconced on the wrong side of the trade debate. He may actually be worse than Gore on the issue. And, of course, Republican front-runners George W. Bush and John McCain are pathetic apologists for the economic agenda of the corporate special interests.

So there is no debate.

And Wellstone's absence from the platform is severely missed.

What of the fall? No coupling of the major-party front-runners would yield a serious dialogue on these issues -- Gore vs. McCain is no better than McCain vs. Bradley is no better than Bush vs. Bradley is no better than Gore vs. Bush. So the two out of three voters who are troubled by the direction of the United States on global economic issues have little choice but to look to the margins.

Former Republican Pat Buchanan, who is seeking the Reform Party nod, has developed into an able critic of the free trade agenda, and his criticisms of corporate greed and irresponsibility are trenchant and well delivered. Yet Buchanan rates so low in the polls as of now that organizers of this fall's presidential debates will have little problem keeping him off the stage.

Consumer activist Ralph Nader, who is preparing a run for the presidency on the Green ticket, offers the U.S. trade agenda and the broader crisis created by an economy that is regulated only by the corporations that control it. Nader's long record of activism on consumer and economic issues has given him a genuine following across the United States. As one of the most broadly respected figures in the country, he might have the name recognition and the following to force his way into the debates, but Nader has yet to prove himself as a serious candidate.

All of this leaves us with the reality of presidential politics that are devoid of serious discussion regarding a seminal issue, and with the very real prospect that it may remain so through this most critical election year.

Oddly enough, the one prominent political figure operating at the national level who really does seem to be listening to the complaints of the public in the post-Seattle era is the one person who won't be involved in any fall presidential campaign debates.

William Jefferson Clinton, the soon-to-be ex-president, has been the most militantly pro-free trade chief executive in the nation's history. He pushed the North American Free Trade Agreement and the General Agreement of Trade and Tariffs through Congress, brought the WTO session to the United States and now is jetting around the world to attend gatherings of the planet's corporate overlords.

Yet, unlike the Gore-Bradley-McCain-Bush axis, Clinton is starting to ask serious questions about whether the process of corporations-can-do-no-wrong globalization has, perhaps, gone too far.

At the World Economic Forum at Davos, Switzerland, last month, Clinton delivered a speech that displayed a startling awareness of the post-Seattle shift in public sentiment regarding trade and economic issues. "Those who heard a wake-up call in Seattle got the right message,'' said Clinton. "I do not agree with those who view with contempt these new forces seeking to be heard in the global dialogue.''

Clinton said it was wrong for corporate elites and their political pawns to view trade unions, environmental groups and other activist organizations as nuisances. Rather, he suggested, the free trade agenda will be truly successful only when it has addressed concerns about the effect of a changing global economy on worker rights, environmental protections and consumer safety.

Don't get Clinton wrong; he's still wrong on most trade and economic issues. But at least he recognizes that, in a democracy, voices of broad-based dissent deserve not just a hearing but a response that seeks to incorporate their legitimate concerns into national policy-making.

Clinton is a perfectly imperfect player. But at least on issues of trade and economics he understands that U.S. policy is out of touch with U.S. public sentiment, which is more than can be said for Bradley, Gore, McCain and Bush.

As the 2000 presidential campaign proceeds, it is possible that Clinton will push his candidate, Gore, to adopt more reasonable positions, just as Wellstone may get his candidate, Bradley, to hear the wake-up call from Seattle.

Until that happens, however, the most important issue of the 2000 presidential campaign will be debated by everyone but the major candidates for president.

That's not just an undemocratic circumstance, it's an unreasonable circumstance. And until it is rectified -- until trade and economic issues are at the center of the debate -- the 2000 presidential campaign will serve as nothing so much as a reminder of how very badly warped our political system has become.
