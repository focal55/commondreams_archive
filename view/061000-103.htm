Published on Saturday, June 10, 2000 by The Progressive
War Criminals? Who Us?
by Matthew Rothschild
 
Amnesty International announced on June 7 that it had come to the conclusion that NATO had committed war crimes in its war against Yugoslavia last year.

It cited, in particular, the bombing of Serb radio and TV headquarters on April 23, 1999, according to Steven Erlanger of The New York Times. The Amnesty report stated: "NATO deliberately attacked a civilian object, killing sixteen civilians, for the purpose of disrupting Serb television broadcasts."

This was an explicit violation of international law, which, as Erlanger pointed out, "forbids direct attacks on civilians and civilian targets and requires all feasible precautions to prevent civilian deaths."

So where is the outcry in the United States over this?

There was little last April. In fact, I recall hearing George Will on ABC's "This Week" clamoring for the bombing of the Serb TV headquarters. And the so-called liberal commentators weren't any better.

The two loudest voices of protest at the time were from former Attorney General Ramsey Clark and from Dennis Kucinich, Democrat of Ohio, and the mainstream media all but ignored them. And the grassroots protests were similarly undercovered.

The idea that the United States and NATO may have committed a war crime is beyond the realm of the possible.

Who us?

We're the guys in the white hats.

Part of the problem is that this was a Democratic war, one which Madeleine Albright insisted on, and one which most Democratic politicians rallied around, the better to inoculate themselves against the charge of being soft on the military.

So the nearly extinct Democratic doves were almost impossible to find.

They're on the endangered species list.

And part of the problem is that the mainstream media demonized the Serbs, just as they demonized the Iraqis, so that an attack on Serb civilians did not pluck at our conscience.

But it should have--and so should the Amnesty report.

Instead, the Amnesty report will probably give the Clinton administration further pause about agreeing to an expanded World Court.

Clinton, Albright, and NATO commander Wesley Clark don't want to be arrested on a war crime now, do they?

Fortunately for them, the head of the war crimes tribunal for Yugoslavia at the Hague exonerated them, saying only that "some mistakes were made by NATO."

Had that report agreed with Amnesty's, our own elected officials could have been in the dock.

It's a consummation devoutly to be wished.

But it's not likely to happen while Washington calls the tune.
