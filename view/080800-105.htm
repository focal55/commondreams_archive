Published on Tuesday, August 8, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
A Decent Conservative
Editorial
 
Trailing in the polls and struggling to counter George W. Bush's "new kind of Republican'' appeal, Al Gore has chosen a novel approach to the vice presidential selection process. He has tapped as his running mate a man who is the closest thing the Democratic Party has to a Republican.

This is not to say that Connecticut U.S. Sen. Joseph Lieberman is a right-wing fanatic, an evil player or some sort of fifth columnist sneaking into the Democratic fold. No, not at all. Joe Lieberman is a principled man who has shown some courage over the years, displayed an admirable commitment to his Orthodox Jewish faith and earned a reputation as a thoughtful legislator.

But he is a conservative.

In 12 years in the Senate, Lieberman has compiled a record that places him well to the right of his own party, and even to the right of moderate Republicans. He has supported every aspect of Wall Street's free trade agenda. He backs Star Wars nuclear missile initiatives. He votes to spend more on the military and less on social welfare programs. He beats the drum for media morality in rhythm with former Reagan appointee William Bennett. And he backs tuition vouchers that would funnel taxpayer dollars to private schools.

Lieberman's ratings from progressive groups that monitor Congress have frequently been dismal. In 1993, the Council for a Livable World, which backs arms control, gave him a rating of 15 percent -- the worst for a Senate Democrat. In 1997, his rating from the AFL-CIO on votes of concern to working people was just 29 percent -- less than half that of some Republicans.

Lieberman does not always rate that poorly on votes of concern to progressives. And on some issues, such as abortion rights, he is a champion.

But as chair of the Democratic Leadership Council, a corporate-funded group that seeks to make the Democratic Party even more slavishly pro-business, Lieberman has been in the forefront of efforts to blur differences between the Democratic and Republican parties. And he has succeeded to such an extent that, in his first U.S. Senate campaign, he ran with the support of conservative columnist William F. Buckley.

Gore's selection of Lieberman is being hailed as a bold move by the usual coterie of pundits and politicos who put style ahead of substance. That's because, in 1998, the Connecticut senator criticized Bill Clinton's affair with Monica Lewinsky as "immoral.'' In the end, however, Lieberman backed Clinton throughout the president's impeachment fight -- unlike U.S. Sen. Russ Feingold, D-Wis., who never engaged in preachy moralizing, but did recognize the significance of the constitutional questions that were in play.

Lieberman is a "bold'' pick only from a political standpoint. Selecting Lieberman signals that Gore is unconcerned by complaints from progressives, trade unionists and disenchanted voters about the Democratic Party's drift to the right.

At a time when, in key states such as California and Wisconsin, Green Party candidate Ralph Nader is drawing unprecedented support from traditional Democrats, the selection of Lieberman indicates that Gore remains more concerned about appealing to centrist "swing'' voters than to Democrats who might be lost to Nader.

Terry Fritter, a good union man who works at Madison's Oscar Mayer plant, puts it well when he says, "Picking Lieberman plays right into Nader's hands. I just can't figure out why Gore didn't go with Feingold.''

Good question.

If Gore was looking for someone with a reputation for ethics, Feingold would have been a far stronger choice -- especially considering the national reputation that Feingold has acquired as one-half of the McCain-Feingold campaign finance reform team. In addition, Feingold has a solid record with trade unionists, environmentalists and human rights activists -- precisely the people the Gore campaign is losing to Nader.

If Al Gore had wanted to energize his campaign and put it back on the winning track, he would have picked Russ Feingold. Instead, he picked a decent conservative who, ultimately, brings less to the Democratic ticket than the lamentable Dick Cheney did to the Republicans.
