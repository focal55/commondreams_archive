Published on Thursday, July 6, 2000 in the Buffalo News
End Corporate Welfare To Achieve True Reform
by Rod Watson
 
It's too bad welfare reform has just about become a non-issue as Congress spends the summer writing the 2001 budget.

Oh sure, every now and then some people still talk about reform. But in the era of the projected surplus - $1.9 trillion, and climbing - what are the odds Washington will complete the job?

After getting rid of Aid to Families with Dependent Children, what are the odds it will eliminate that other form of AFDC: Aid to Finance Dependent Corporations?

"Corporate welfare" still slides by well under the political radar screen while costing taxpayers anywhere from $75 billion to $200 billion per year, depending on who's counting.

Hard-liners would wipe out everything from corporate subsidies to tax breaks to pet projects - all the gifts of public money to those who do some giving of their own at campaign time. They'd even eliminate research subsidies, like the kind other nations fund for ground-level work that could eventually benefit the public but that has no immediate profit in it.

Aaron Taylor, spokesman for Citizens Against Government Waste, said the high-tech sector now fueling the U.S. economy "came out of nowhere" without such taxpayer help, which proves such subsidies aren't really needed.

That's no doubt an overstatement; government has some role to play. But it shouldn't play nearly as big a one as it's playing now in a game determined more by political connections than any concern for America's future. Big Business needs a dose of the same tough love that has helped other welfare recipients turn their lives around.

Yet welfare reform has failed to take hold in the corporate sector. To wit:

The Market Access Program still exists. That's the one in which Joe Taxpayer helped corporations like Burger King, Dole and Sunkist advertise their products overseas, as if marketing shouldn't be the company's responsibility.

The Overseas Private Investment Corp. still exists, subsidizing businesses and guaranteeing loans to get companies to invest in countries where good business sense tells them not to. Taxpayers could save an estimated $1.5 billion over five years on that program alone.

Agricultural subsidies like those given to sugar growers still exist. Subsidizing sugar costs taxpayers $90 million per year in higher prices for government-supported food programs, the CAGW estimates, and costs consumers another $1.2 billion.

Yet there is no outrage about the recipients of this kind of public largess driving Cadillacs or using their taxpayer subsidies to support their children.

In fact, few politicians seem to be talking about it at all. And those who do don't get very far. It couldn't propel Sen. John McCain past George W. Bush, and corporate critic Ralph Nader can't even get enough traction on the issue to make it into the televised presidential debates.

Now Rep. Joe Hoeffel, D-Pa., wants to try. He's pushing a bill to create a commission to target corporate welfare and recommend a package of cuts in the same way such commissions targeted unneeded military bases.

There would be one big difference: While Congress had to accept or reject the base closures as a package, Hoeffel's bill would allow amendments.

However, they couldn't be made in some obscure committee hearing. Any lawmaker who wanted to amend the list and protect a favorite subsidy would have to stand up in the House and do it "in a very public way and make a case why this isn't corporate welfare," said Joshua Shapiro, Hoeffel's chief of staff.

Whether you could shame corporate welfare supporters in this way is an open question. But it's worth a try, and the bill finally got a hearing last month.

As for its future, the question remains: If Congress wouldn't cut corporate welfare when there were huge deficits and pressure to save every dollar, why would it bring itself to do so now when the country is rolling in surpluses?

"People are more focused now than ever before on retiring the (national) debt," Shapiro said optimistically.

"It seems like a dim prospect," countered the CAGW's Taylor, more realistically. "It's going to be very, very difficult."

That's particularly true because corporate welfare has become such an ingrained culture, being passed down from one generation of stockholders to the next. In fact, the conservative Cato Institute notes that, while families now face time limits and can be kicked off of welfare after a few years, corporations still can stay on the dole forever.

Welfare reform won't truly be considered a success until Big Business is told it will have to upgrade its skills, go into the marketplace and compete on its own merits.

Sure, that's a scary prospect for those used to dependency; but it can be done. In fact, I can't wait to see the Rose Garden ceremony at which the president can introduce a business that has successfully made the transition from welfare to work.
