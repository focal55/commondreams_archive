In Politics, Still a Man's World
by Anita Hill
 

BOSTON -- In 1873, the Supreme Court declared in Bradwell v. Illinois that the state was justified in denying Myra Bradwell a license to practice law on the basis of her gender. The court informed Bradwell that despite her education and skills, as a woman her best roles were wife and mother.

More than 100 years later, New Yorkers may give Hillary Clinton that same message.

The court's decision in Bradwell gave legal recognition to the idea that men and women operated in "separate spheres" -- ideology that permeated upper- and middle-class society at the time. According to the court, "God designed the sexes to occupy different spheres of action," and "it belonged to men to make, apply and execute the laws." The "domestic sphere" was women's proper domain.

Such thinking seems quaint today. Or does it?

Hillary Clinton's approval rating as first lady was at its lowest when she tackled health reform, rose steadily when she confined her activity to the traditional role as White House hostess, and was at its highest when she stoically stood by her husband despite his marital infidelity.

Current reactions to Hillary Clinton's Senate campaign suggest that although many approved of her performance in the private sphere, this approval won't necessarily help her win in the public sphere.

Look at the case of Elizabeth Dole. When her husband was running for president in 1996, she was recognized as a great campaigner. There was even talk about whether she should have been the Republican nominee. But when she declared her own presidential bid in 1999, everything changed. True, the political landscape was not the same, but does that completely explain why her champions in 1996 suddenly disappeared? Her own husband undermined her candidacy when he said he wanted to send a donation to John McCain. Elizabeth Dole, like Hillary Clinton, was only popular when she was seen as a wife.

Historically, women have crossed the line from wife of elected official to being elected themselves. But in those cases, women typically became surrogates for husbands who died in office or were ineligible to run.

It is true that the United States has made incremental advances over the years. Today, women make up some 20 percent of elected officials. But the experience of Hillary Clinton shows that these gains aren't complete. Female candidates must deal not only with their own personal inadequacies (as every candidate must), but also with the larger question of whether their roles as wives and mothers would hurt their role as policymakers.

Indeed, questions from the media further consign Hillary Clinton to the domestic realm. Will she and Bill Clinton stay married? Was she ever unfaithful to him? How does she feel about Monica Lewinsky? Putting aside the debate over whether such questions are appropriate in any circumstance, it is hard to imagine such deeply personal questions being asked of Rudolph Giuliani.

Of course, Hillary Clinton has made her own mistakes. For instance, when she announced her candidacy this week, she proudly stated that she made wonderful salads and omelets. And she has certainly misinterpreted her popularity if she thought it could be automatically converted to a position of public leadership. But if Hillary Clinton can make the issues the focus of the campaign, the gender barrier might come down for good.

Anita F. Hill is a professor of social policy, law and women's studies at Brandeis University.
