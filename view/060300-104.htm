Published on Saturday, June 3, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
Social Security Doesn't Need Bush's Gamble
by Dave Zweifel
 
R.C. Longworth of the Chicago Tribune blames the educational system for younger Americans' belief that Social Security won't be around for them.

I think it's simpler than that. The problem is that too many today rely on radio and television's superficial news coverage. Take that incomplete information, add to it the flood of misinformation generated daily by talk show hosts around the country, and you wind up with a perception about Social Security that is far, far from the truth.

As Longworth pointed out in a major piece on the nation's safety net for older Americans, "No matter what you hear: Social Security is not in crisis. It has some problems but nothing that can't be solved with a little tinkering. Root-and-branch reform is not needed. And while it's better to act now, we really have 30 years to do this.''

Remember now, Longworth is a senior writer for the Chicago Trib, a paper not exactly known for its liberal or Democratic leanings. Yet he is appalled at George W. Bush's plan to "reform'' the Social Security system by privatizing some of the contributions.

"(It's) an unneeded solution to a nonproblem, a reform that will only make things worse, a rescue that could kill its victim,'' he wrote.

"The real debate is not about Social Security itself but about the size of benefits. Privatizers say retirees would be better off it we put Social Security's money into the stock market. Anti-privatizers say this may be true but it also may not be true and, at any rate, it's a gamble.

"Do we really want to roll the dice with the national nest egg for the elderly?'' he asked.

Precisely. Too many today view Social Security solely as a pension system. It is so much more than that. It takes care of widowed mothers and their families. It pays kids who have lost a parent a monthly benefit until they graduate from college. It is insurance for the permanently disabled.

Take all that out of today's high-flying 401(k)s and then let's see just how large the growth rate is.

Those like Bush who tout the market as somehow a savior for the system are oblivious to history. There was a day in this country when millions of Americans did rely on the market for their future. Most of them lost it all in the Great Depression, winding up penniless in their old age.

There was also a day when older Americans lived in abject poverty, virtually having to beg for food and shelter because they had no income. Our counties set up "homes'' to take care of the more destitute.

But then Franklin D. Roosevelt, with the help of several progressive Wisconsinites, devised the Old Age and Survivors' Benefit plan -- Social Security -- that turned that sorry chapter in our history completely around.

Today, thanks to the base of income that Social Security provides, our parents and grandparents can live out their lives with dignity.

As Longworth wrote, "We're talking about Social Security here, not Social Maximum Return. And there's more to Social Security than the monthly check, including a compact between generations and Americans' sense of themselves as one nation, united by something more than the profit motive.''

George Bush's plan to take billions of Social Security dollars and invest them in the market would be a tremendous boon to Wall Street and many of the Texas governor's big-time contributors.

But what about the ordinary American people?

Dave Zweifel is the editor of The Capital Times.
