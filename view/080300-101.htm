Published on Thursday, August 3, 2000 in the Philadelphia Inquirer
Out Of The Shadows:
Other Convention Offers Substance On Issues
Editorial
 
Too bad organizers of the Shadow Convention over in West Philadelphia couldn't offer the media the same free food and big parties made available by the Republican National Convention and its many corporate sponsors.

Had the Shadow Convention done so, perhaps more of the 15,000 media types in town would have journeyed beyond the First Union Center and Center City hotels to offer their audiences something beyond cynical deconstruction of speeches they consider to be little more than well-tailored propaganda.

The Shadow Convention, ending today, was a well-intentioned effort to prompt serious discussion of issues largely ignored by the GOP conventioneers this week: the dreadful toll of the unsuccessful decades-long "war on drugs"; the wealth gap between the upper 10 percent of Americans and everyone else; campaign-finance reform; and the role of the media in encouraging - or discouraging - social change.

The participants ranged from evangelical Christian organizations worried about economic fairness to the folks from NORML, who want to legalize marijuana. Given that enforced unity was not the goal of a group so diverse, the Shadow Convention often moved beyond easy sound bites or easy solutions.

For example, on Tuesday, Gary Johnson, the Republican governor of New Mexico, made an eloquent argument against the bipartisan war on drugs, a multi-decade effort that has neither reduced the cost or availability of illegal drugs, but has contributed mightily to more than doubling America's prison population over the last 15 years.

Two million Americans are locked up - meaning America has more of its citizens in jail than communist China. Yet the price of marijuana, cocaine, heroin, etc. has decreased over the past decade while the availability and potency of the drugs has increased. Who says so? The Office of National Drug Control Policy.

Mr. Johnson, a self-made businessman, triathlete and teetotaler, is no supporter of drug use. But he asks why this country is so intent on spending billions of dollars to arrest and jail thousands of Americans who have committed no violent crime and are not drug kingpins. (The FBI say that 79 percent of drug arrests in 1998 were for possession, not sale or manufacture.)

It's also a war unconscionably aimed at Americans of color. While government statistics say white and black Americans use drugs at about the same rate, African Americans make up somewhere between 40 and 60 percent of those behind bars for drug offenses.

Isn't there a better way? Mr. Johnson asks. And isn't that kind of straight talk a better story?
