Published on Thursday, September 14, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
Dems Must Get Progressives To Believe In Them
by John Nichols
 
U.S. Rep. Barney Frank, D-Mass., came to Madison last Sunday to tell progressives they should get excited about electing Al Gore president.

It was a tough sell.

Frank, one of the most identifiable liberal Democrats in the country, has emerged as the leading Democratic critic of Ralph Nader's Green Party challenge to Gore. Like a number of Wisconsin liberals, he fears Nader's candidacy could draw enough progressive votes away from the Democratic column to tip the election to Republican George W. Bush.

Frank makes the argument passionately, but he undermines himself when he claims Gore's positions are actually better than Nader's. From any progressive perspective, that's a laughable assertion. On labor rights, the environment, equal access to justice, gay and lesbian rights, the death penalty, military spending, international intervention and, above all, on the question of corporate influence over American politics, Nader's stances are monumentally superior to those advocated by Gore.

The more intriguing argument advanced by Frank was this: that the Democratic Party, bad as it may be, is the only truly viable vehicle for progressives. Here, he has some ground to stand on, since it cannot be denied that most of organized labor, most major environmental groups, most African-American and Latino voters, and progressives such as Paul Wellstone, Jesse Jackson Jr., Tom Hayden, Tammy Baldwin and Dennis Kucinich seem to be committed to stay the Democratic course. Whether these groups and individuals are right to do so, their adherence to the Democratic line makes it difficult to determine where a new party of the left will build a base from which to compete for power.

Frank argues that progressives in general, and Nader backers in particular, ought to move into the Democratic Party and take it over. "Join us! Help us!'' he said to Nader supporters.

The problem with Frank's argument for entryism is that the Democratic Party has not proven particularly welcoming to progressives, even when they play by the rules and present it with an opportunity to win. Witness the treatment of the Rev. Jesse Jackson who, after running a far better race for the presidency in 1988 than did Al Gore, was never given serious consideration for the vice presidency in 1992.

Better yet, consider the circumstance of Ed Garvey in 1998. After years of working harder than just about anyone else in Wisconsin to bring progressives into the Democratic Party, Garvey won the gubernatorial nomination with overwhelming support from across the state. Yet, though he was standing proudly on the Democratic platform in a race against conservative Republican Tommy Thompson, Garvey was abandoned by top Democratic givers and provided with only tepid support from many leaders of organized labor.

The fact that Garvey ran better than past Democratic nominees for governor only rubbed salt in the wound -- emphasizing to rank-and-file labor, farm and peace and social justice community activists that even when they play by the rules and produce a strong candidate, they cannot necessarily count on the party establishment.

The Garvey lesson lingers in Wisconsin. It is no wonder that some of the 1998 Democratic nominee's ablest backers are this year supporting Nader, or simply sitting out the presidential race. It is no wonder that Garvey, though he remains a Democrat, has joined Jesse Jackson Jr., Russ Feingold and other sincere progressives in advocating for the Green's inclusion in the debates -- a position Garvey will advance Wednesday when he appears at Nader's Orpheum Theatre rally in Madison.

If Barney Frank and other Democratic leaders want progressives to see the Democratic Party as a natural political home, they have to offer more than an invitation. They have to make real the promise that progressives who pour their human energy into the Democratic Party will not see their efforts trumped by the powerful economic interests that use their resources to warp the party's message. For progressives, that is, and should be, the true test of whether they want to identify as Democrats.
