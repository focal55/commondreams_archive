Published on Monday, March 13, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Death Penalty: The Speech California's Governor Davis Should Make
by Jeff Gillenkirk
 

GOOD MORNING. As everyone in this state should know, I was elected governor of California as a strong advocate of law and order, and an equally strong proponent of our death penalty statute. A society as complex and diverse as ours can function only by the common acceptance, and adherence to, an easily understood code of behavior. A foundation of that code, spelled out clearly by the Legislature and upheld in numerous court decisions, states clearly that those who without conscience, remorse or mercy take the life of another forfeit the right to theirs.

I have carried out the mandate of California's death penalty on two occasions and stand prepared to do so again. As a lawyer and this state's chief executive, however, I recognize that the death penalty can work only when it is impartially invoked, and infallibly enforced. There is evidence that has come to my attention that shows these fundamental conditions are not always met.

All of us are aware of the steps taken by my colleague, Gov. George Ryan of Illinois. Following exoneration of 13 prisoners on death row, and evidence of police, judicial and prosecutorial misconduct and inadequate legal representation, Gov. Ryan halted all executions pending a comprehensive study of his state's use of capital punishment. I raise this not to compare our states on the issue of the death penalty -- we are vastly dissimilar. Our system is marked by caution, thoroughness and conscientiousness, theirs admittedly less so. Nevertheless, I have asked for, and received, a preliminary report on our death penalty system in California. A summary of that report is in the packet before you. In it, you will find that as thorough, fair and effective as our death penalty system is, it is not without problems.

At least five men convicted of capital murder in California have been found to be wrongly accused and imprisoned since the death penalty was reinstated. The most recent was Dwayne McKinney, who was released from Lancaster State Prison on January 28 of this year after serving 18 years for a murder he did not commit. ``If jurors in 1982 had voted to execute Dwayne McKinney, as the prosecutor in that case had wanted,'' the Orange County Register wrote, ``he probably would be dead by now.'' Rather than immunity from the possibility of executing the innocent, the recent Rampart Division scandal in the Los Angeles Police Department shows that our state may in fact be especially susceptible to it. That scandal in our state's largest city has led to the release of at least 99 inmates who were framed by officers who conducted false arrests, witness tampering, evidence planting and perjury in conspiring to put innocent people in jail. In my investigations, I have learned that the U.S. Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals has rejected a request to `'fast track'' death penalty cases in California. The court ruled that our state provides neither adequate compensation for counsel, nor adequate resources for constitutional issues to be raised in death penalty cases. Approximately 180 prisoners on California's death row today -- almost one-third of all those condemned to die -- have no legal representation whatsoever. This is unconscionable.

I have learned that California has no standardized guidelines for prosecutors to use in determining when a case should be tried as a death penalty case. Each district attorney is left on his or her own to pursue the death penalty for whatever reasons they choose -- and do. This is unacceptable.

I have learned that whereas DNA testing has proven to be an effective means of establishing the guilt or innocence of the accused, a Los Angeles Times investigation has found that prosecutors in California routinely deny prisoners the right to DNA tests even in cases in which innocence is strongly suggested. This is unjust. Many critics of the death penalty point to new opinion polls that show public support for capital punishment now below 50 percent. But opposition to the death penalty should not be based on opinion polls, any more than support should be. The death penalty should be supported -- or not -- because it is impartial, fair and effective -- or not.

I have concluded that at this moment in California, it is not. Imposition of the death penalty is marked by arbitrariness in which race, ethnic origin and economic status are key determinants. Minorities, the poor and those without the means to mount an adequate defense are disproportionately represented on death row.

Therefore, I am announcing the appointment of a bipartisan task force to investigate whether the death penalty in our state can be administered without posing the risk of executing the innocent. In the meantime, I am suspending all executions statewide and calling upon district attorneys in each of our 59 counties to cease seeking the death penalty in capital cases, and pursue instead life sentence without possibility of parole.

I want to assure all Californians that this moratorium presents no danger to themselves or their families. More than 2,300 convicted murderers have been given life sentence without parole in our state since 1977 -- not one has been released. Besides protecting the public's safety, this sentence removes any possibility that our state will execute an innocent person.

Today, 563 men and women are on California's death row, the most in the nation. If just one of them were to be innocent and die at our hand, we will have failed them all -- and failed ourselves. During this moratorium, we will use this time to study whether the death penalty can work as we hoped it could. If it can't, together we will discover what can.

Jeff Gillenkirk was speechwriter for former New York Gov. Mario Cuomo. He is a board member of Death Penalty Focus, a California- based organization working for abolition of the death penalty.
