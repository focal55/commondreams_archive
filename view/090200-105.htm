Published on Saturday, September 2, 2000
Warning Signs Over Al Gore's Claim To Be A Real Campaign Finance Reformer
by Micah Sifry
 
Which is worse: a presidential candidate opposed to cleaning up big money’s role in politics, or one who offers the illusion of wanting to do something about it without demonstrating that he’s really serious?

On the one hand, George W. Bush is big money incarnate. His presidential campaign has broken all records for fundraising, topping $100 million. Bush, the self-proclaimed "Reformer With Results," says he favors banning soft money from corporations and unions, but wants to allow individuals to keep giving those large, unregulated contributions—which means he favors no meaningful reform at all.

By contrast, Gore says that he has learned from the fundraising scandals perpetuated by the 1996 Clinton-Gore campaign. He says he wants to get "all special interest money" out of politics, and has proposed creating a Democracy Endowment to provide full public financing for candidates’ general election campaigns. Many observers were impressed by his acceptance speech at the Democratic National Convention, when he promised to make the McCain-Feingold bill banning soft money the first priority of his administration.

Gore’s declarations sound sincere. That is, until you recall what the current version of McCain-Feingold actually says. Yes, it shuts down all federal soft money donations. But it no longer covers "issue ads," which means that it does nothing about the millions of unregulated dollars being spent on slyly worded commercials that imply support for candidates without actually saying so. The words "McCain-Feingold" have become such a mantra that few people have stopped to consider how modest a first step the actual bill has become.

The truth is that Gore is stuck on the horns of a dilemma. To show that he is serious about really changing how big money dominates politics—and not just talking about the issue as a way of attracting independent voters and McCain supporters—he has to do a lot more to build a popular movement capable of forcing Congress to take real action. For starters, that would mean aligning himself with the strongest advocates for reform within his own party. And it would mean advocating full public financing for candidates in general elections and primaries to free them from dependence on big money donors, as already exists in several states. But such moves would put him in direct conflict with powerful forces within his own Democratic ranks

The story of what happened to Wisconsin Senator Russell Feingold at the Democratic convention is indicative. Feingold, John McCain’s crusader-in-arms, spent the week in a state of high moral outrage. Speaking at the opening evening of the Shadow Convention, he made a perfunctory declaration of support for his party and then launched into a passionate condemnation of "what has happened to our conventions, to our government and to our democracy."

"I’m sorry to say it, but the big story at the Democratic Convention is really influence buying and peddling….The story is not whether to have a fund-raiser at the Playboy Mansion, but the obscenity of unlimited special interest soft money contributions. The story is not whether the President is upstaging the Vice President by holding fundraisers in L.A. this week; it’s the staggering size of the contributions required to get into the darn thing. The story is not the elaborate security arrangements to protect the convention participants at the fund-raisers, it’s that people feel so shut out of the process that they feel they must take to the streets."

Feingold’s attack on "our brave new corporate democracy" won him a standing ovation at the Shadow Convention, which was organized by the campaign finance reform groups Public Campaign and Common Cause, among others. But his honesty and forthrightness did not win him any friends at the Democratic convention a few blocks away.

Indeed, two days later Feingold was scheduled to give a three-minute speech to the delegates at the Staples Center. But after DNC officials got wind of what Feingold was planning to say, they pushed up his speaking time by an hour—to insure a tiny audience—and then chucked his speech down the memory hole. Several checks at the DNC press table inside the convention center turned up no copies of the transcript of Feingold’s address, and while aides there suggested that it could be found on the official convention website, the Monday after the convention there was nothing to be found there either. Transcripts, as well as audio and video recordings, could be obtained for many other Democratic elected officials, including such notables as the mayor of Minneapolis and several first-termers in the House. But nothing from the Senate’s leading Democrat pushing campaign reform.

Feingold’s thought-crime? Before endorsing Gore and praising his commitment to shut down soft money, he expressed his dismay at all the soft money fundraising going on at the convention and called on DNC officials to prohibit it at future conventions. This, apparently, was too much for Democratic poobahs and powerbrokers, who wanted to enjoy their days in the sky-boxes and nights limo-ing from one Beverly Hills party to another without being reminded of their own complicity in corrupting American democracy.

It was only a few hours after Gore promised to get all special interest money out of politics that he was off partying at a Barbra Streisand concert-fundraiser that netted the Democrats more than $5 million. A Gore spokesperson claimed there was no contradiction, pointing out that this was "hard money" raised at the maximum of $1000 per contributor. But when you remember that less than one-tenth of one percent of all Americans make a $1000 hard money contribution to any federal candidate, and that this donor class is much whiter, wealthier, older, more male and more conservative than the average voter, with economic interests often intertwined with the companies they either own or manager, you realize that this is a distinction without a difference.

The sad truth is that both parties are now little more than foot-stools for corporate fatcats. Changing that reality is the real work of reform.

Micah L. Sifry is Public Campaign’s senior analyst.
