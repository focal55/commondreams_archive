Published on Thursday, August 10, 2000 in Newsday
Bail for Protesters in Philly Is Way Out of Line
by Larry Frankel
 
ONLY IN Philadelphia can someone who is accused of blocking traffic and disrupting rush hour be held in jail on bail higher than that usually set for violent crimes. But that's what is happening to a number of people who took to the streets during the Republican convention to express their dissatisfaction with the status quo.

On Aug. 1, several hundred people were arrested during protest activities that got out of hand. A few were arrested for causing property damage, turning over dumpsters or injuring police officers. A smaller number were arrested for being "anarchist ringleaders." Seventy people were arrested because they happened to be at a warehouse where chicken wire and arts and crafts materials for a puppet display were confiscated by police as "dangerous implements." Many others were arrested because they had sat in traffic intersections and joined arms and legs.

The district attorney of Philadelphia initially sought high bail for quite a few defendants. Bail for John Sellers, who was charged with eight misdemeanors, was initially set at $1 million at the request of the prosecutor.

By the Monday after the conclusion of the Republican convention, the district attorney had conceded that this eye-popping figure was excessive and asked that bail be lowered to $200,000.

The judge, who had a better grasp of what -and who -was really before her, lowered the bail to $100,000. Even that amount appears to be disproportionate to the allegations against Sellers, who has family in the Philadelphia area.

At first, it seemed that bail had been set so high to keep some of these defendants in prison until the Republicans left town. Then it looked as though the intent was to keep folks locked up through the Democratic convention next week. Now we have every reason to believe that the prosecutors intend to try to hold some of the defendants through the time of trial to make sure that they are punished no matter what the ultimate outcome of those cases. All of these tactics violate basic rights and are a misuse of the bail process.

The purpose of bail is to ensure that a defendant who is released from prison shows up for trial. In setting bail, the factors that can lawfully be considered are the nature of the crime, the background of the defendant and the defendant's ties to the community.

Bail is not to be used as punishment or to hold persons in confinement until their trial date. Certainly, bail is not intended as a means for preventing people from expressing their dissenting views.

Much has been made of the fact that many of the people arrested have refused to identify themselves to authorities. But the reality is that many others have either willingly identified themselves to authorities or have been easily identified by the police.

Other defendants, whose identities are known, are being held on bail that runs into the six or seven figures. In some cases, these defendants are charged with misdemeanors even though they have no known record of failing to appear in court.

The American Civil Liberties Union does not condone the unlawful acts that occurred in Philadelphia last week. But we don't believe that those acts warrant this kind of overreaction by requiring excessive bail.

Sure, vandalizing property, turning over trash cans, assaulting persons and shutting down the city by blocking traffic are not activities protected under the First Amendment. But they are not the kind of crimes that justify treating these defendants as if they were a dangerous urban mob.

Requiring excessively high bail to keep speakers from expressing their views turns a tool of law enforcement into a means of repression. You can't call it free speech when the price tag is $1 million.

Larry Frankel is the executive director of the American Civil Liberties Union of Pennsylvania.
