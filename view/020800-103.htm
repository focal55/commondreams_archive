Published on Tuesday, February 8, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
'Cradle Will Rock' Profoundly Important Film
by  John Nichols
 

 It is not giving away too much to report that Tim Robbins' remarkably adept new film, "Cradle Will Rock,'' ends with a haunting juxtaposition of the engaged, activist theater scene of the 1930s and the sterile, apolitical commercialism of contemporary "entertainment'' -- as illustrated by the vapid offerings of theater and film billboards surrounding the new "spruced-up'' Times Square.

Never has the glittering intersection of commerce and art seemed so entirely empty of meaning as it does in this brief portrayal by Robbins.

And rarely has a filmmaker delivered such a direct blow to the zeitgeist.

Critics have been split as regards "Cradle Will Rock'' -- which is showing this week at Madison's Hilldale Theatre. For some of the men and women who earn their keep by pondering the relative merits of "Scream 3'' and "I Know What You Did Last Summer,'' Robbins' project is simply "too political.''

But that, of course, is the point.

As with a previous Robbins film, "Bob Roberts'' -- the finest send-up of American politics since Robert Redford's "The Candidate'' -- "Cradle Will Rock'' seeks to make a statement that is at once blunt, controversial and indisputably accurate. With his latest film, Robbins recalls the brief shining moment in which New Deal era arts programs stretched the boundaries of American theater and raised the prospect that a medium of entertainment might also be a vehicle for informing, energizing and provoking the citizenry to act against economic and social injustice.

Needless to say, the Federal Theater Project, which gave jobs to thousands of out-of-work writers, directors, actors, actresses, set designers, stagehands and musicians, was not popular with conservative forces in the theater and politics. Art with a purpose frightened the defenders of the status quo then as now -- when right-wing efforts to cut National Endowment for the Arts grants mirror the crusade of obscure congressmen to shut down 1930s theater groups that were supposedly soft on communism.

Robbins deftly interweaves related tales of Orson Welles (Angus MacFadyen) directing Marc Blitzstein's (Hank Azaria) play about striking steelworkers, the struggle between young Nelson Rockefeller (John Cusack) and muralist Diego Rivera (Ruben Blades) over the radicalism of Rivera's painting in the lobby of a Rockefeller building, and the investigation of the theater project by the communist-hunting House Un-American Activities Committee.

The end result is a chilling statement about the power of money to shape, direct and -- when the funds are directed toward the unworthy but uncontroversial -- warp and undermine artistic expression.

In one scene, Rockefeller, William Randolph Hearst and their contemporaries are conspiring, after the Diego Rivera fiasco, to fund only that modern art that is of a distinctly apolitical bent. Only today -- when art is so very modern but not so very threatening -- do we begin to recognize the success they made of their scheme.

The message that comes through loud and clear from "Cradle Will Rock'' is that significant amounts of "no strings attached'' federal, state and local government arts funding is needed in order to break the stranglehold on theater and other artistic endeavors that has resulted from the essential privatization of arts spending in this country.

Just as Tim Robbins did a brilliant job of exposing the dark truths of our thoroughly corrupted politics with the wickedly satirical but never-far-from-the-truth "Bob Roberts,'' he has now turned his muckraking camera toward an exploration of what happened to the arts in America.

"Cradle Will Rock'' is an imperfect film in some senses -- translating a stage musical to the screen is no easy task -- and the none-too-inspired critics among us have wasted no time in pointing out the weaknesses. But those imperfections are far outweighed by tremendous displays of soul, passion and political savvy that make "Cradle Will Rock'' one of the finest films of recent years.
