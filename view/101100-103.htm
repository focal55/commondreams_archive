Published on Wednesday, October 11, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
Let's Debate Israel Policy
Editorial
 
Maybe now that Israelis and Palestinians are engaged in the deadliest Middle East conflict since the Persian Gulf War, perhaps Jim Lehrer will decide the topic merits comment by Al Gore and George W. Bush.

In an uninspired first debate that had the Democratic and Republican presidential nominees arguing like low-level government accountants rather than potential leaders of the world's only superpower, Lehrer's lame questioning set precisely the wrong tone. While the PBS persona found plenty of time for picayune inquiries about theoretical crises, Lehrer didn't insert a single question about the very real crisis that has swept outward from Jerusalem to the entire region.

Now that close to 100 people are dead and thousands have been injured, it is difficult to dismiss the burgeoning crisis in the Middle East. Even Lehrer is likely to make sure that the candidates address the issue.

But will he ask the right questions? And will Bush and Gore get anywhere near the heart of the matter?

The safest bet is that the answers to both questions are the same: "No.''

American policy in the Middle East has too frequently been misguided, biased and damaging to the prospects of peace. And Bush and Gore need to explain exactly what policies and approaches they intend to embrace.

The can start by answering several fundamental questions:

  • Do they share the position of the clear majority of Israelis and the overwhelming majority of Palestinians that the route to peace in the region is a two-state solution where a secure Israel would border a secure Palestine?

  • Do they share the position of most serious international observers, academics and analysts, who argue that a system of shared control of Jerusalem will be an essential feature of any final peace plan?

  • Do they favor a restructuring of U.S. aid priorities in the region so that money goes to fund economic development and renewal projects, as opposed to military buildups by Israel and Egypt?

  • Do they understand that moving the U.S. Embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem -- a step the State Department has long resisted but which a growing number of politicians endorse -- would be read by Palestinians as an act of provocation?

  • Do they recognize that the Middle East peace process, to which the United States has provided assistance over the years, has been harmed by the U.S. failure to send clear, unequivocal signals about our commitment to serve the cause of peace -- as opposed to serving the purposes of military contractors, oil lobbyists or domestic political groups?

These are tough questions that force candidates to think outside the box created by their campaign consultants. But they are also the questions that the next president will have to answer.

No region in the world demands more attention from the United States than the tiny patch of land that is Israel. The next president will devote a substantial portion of his tenure to sorting through his beliefs regarding the appropriate approach to the crises that continually arise in this part of the world.

Don't the American people have a right to some serious insight into those beliefs before they make their choice on Nov. 7?
