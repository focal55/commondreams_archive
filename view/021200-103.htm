Genetically Modified Foods Need Labeling
Editorial
 

Stories about genetically modified food in such disparate locations as Brazil and Japan recently appeared back-to-back on the front page of the Wall Street Journal. When it's on Page 1 of the nation's leading financial newspaper, you can be sure it's an issue that has implications for American businesses and consumers. Similar front-page stories have appeared with increasing frequency in the New York Times as well.

But now the subject -- specifically labeling foods that contain genetically modified (GM) ingredients -- popped up this week in a column by Marian Burros, the Times' widely read and respected food editor. Genetically modified food refers to splicing the genes of two species together. If Burros is tackling this subject, you can be sure it's mainstream.

Consumers -- especially those in Western Europe -- are already saying no to such products by requiring labeling of all GM foods at the retail level. In the United States, on the other hand, we've been eating GM foods for more than a decade. For example, almost all canola oil is made from GM rapeseed. Consumers have been unwitting guinea pigs because, as Burros points out, so few studies have been conducted on these new products that almost nothing is known about their long-term impact on human health.

Two weeks ago the United States signed a trade agreement with 130 nations that will allow any of them to prohibit the import of GM foods -- even without conclusive scientific evidence they're harmful. It's widely seen here as the first step in this country toward labeling and regulations that are common in Europe.

Few Americans realize that major companies, like Kellogg's, Kraft Foods, McDonald's, Nestle USA, Quaker Oats and Frito-Lay, sell GM foods domestically but not abroad. Where consumers have demanded safe foods, businesses have complied. Now that message may be hitting home.

Burros notes that last month, Frito-Lay told the farmers who grow about 85 precent of the corn used in Doritos, Tostitos and Fritos not to use GM seed for this year's planting. Next year, the company plans to stop using GM potatoes in its Lay's and Ruffles potato chips.

Until there's reliable proof that GM crops harm neither humans nor the environment, consumers need a way to help them make informed choices. Whether businesses prefer the method to be retail labeling or entirely dropping GM ingredients, the end result is a genuine victory for American consumers, whose safety concerns deserve the same attention as those of their European counterparts.
