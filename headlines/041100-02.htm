Published on Tuesday, April 11, 2000 in the Chicago Tribune
Albright Says That Pollution Is Threat To US Interests
Half Of World's Rivers, Lakes 'Seriously Sick'
by Michael Kilian
 
Warning that increases in global water pollution and water shortages pose a major national security threat to the U.S., Secretary of State Madeleine Albright on Monday proposed the creation of a world alliance devoted to preserving water resources.

"Environmental threats should be seen for what they are," she said. "Namely, threats to our security."

Albright told a joint session of the World Resources Institute and U.S. National Defense University that the competition for water and the economic and social consequences of unsafe water are contributing to political extremism and civil strife.

"Today, around the world, more than 1 billion people lack access to safe drinking water," she said. "More than 2 billion live in countries experiencing some kind of water stress. At least 5 million people die every year from water-related illness--more than the population of Maryland."

The secretary said this means trouble for stability and U.S. interests in any region where a struggle for water resources exists.

"Studies show that the squeeze on water resources will tighten as populations grow, demand increases, pollution continues and global climate change accelerates," she said. "As competition for water intensifies, further disagreements over access and use are likely to erupt. Unless properly managed, water scarcity can be a major source of strife, as well as a roadblock to economic and social progress."

She called for the U.S. to lead the proposed alliance, despite reported resentment of America's dominance over other countries as the world's only superpower. Developing nations are now responsible for about half the pollution on the planet, Albright said, and the U.S. should take a leadership role in curbing contamination of the environment because it has long been a major polluter.

"The United States has the world's largest economy," she said. "Our scientists have designed the best environmental technology. Our society is by far the largest emitter of the gases that cause global climate change. So we have both the capacity and the obligation to lead."

The secretary said she will begin meetings this summer with other industrial powers on finding ways to help developing nations with serious water problems, provided the countries are willing to take action to solve them.

She noted that 130 nations sent representatives last month to a World Water Forum conference at The Hague, which adopted a declaration that "every person, everywhere, should have access to enough safe water at an affordable cost."

But reality is far different, she said.

"Of every two major rivers and lakes on the planet, one is seriously sick," Albright said. "On every continent, fresh-water ecosystems have been harmed. And half the world's wetlands have disappeared."

Part of the problem is inefficiency, she said. Some 70 percent of all water usage in the world goes for agriculture, but as many as three in every five gallons pumped for irrigation are wasted, she said.

Improved irrigation technology developed by the U.S. can reduce the waste, she said.

Albright said that, despite continuing tensions and strife, countries in the Middle East are beginning cooperative efforts to improve mutual "water security" there.

She said that during a visit next week to central Asia she will confer with leaders of former Soviet republics about water quality problems, which she said were caused in part by practices implemented under communism. "The two river systems of the Aral Sea basin are sorely degraded," she said.

The secretary complained that "pollution, poorly placed dams and flooding" have threatened the Mekong River basin in Southeast Asia, which supports 250 million people.

She also said international cooperation by adjoining nations has made considerable progress in improving the quality of the Nile, the world's longest river. But the rest of Africa is in serious trouble, Albright said.

"More Africans lack access to safe water now than a decade ago," she said. "Almost half the people on the continent suffer from water-related disease. The result is economically crippling and, from a humanitarian standpoint, flat-out unacceptable."
