Published on Saturday, February 19, 2000 in the Beirut Daily Star
Lebanese Students Turn Their Rage On 'Biased' CNN
by Munira Khayyat
 

BEIRUT - More than 10,000 angry demonstrators took to the streets on Friday for a third day of protests against perceived US bias toward Israel, with some students and activists laying siege to the offices of the American television network CNN.

Riot police and troops blockaded the streets leading to CNN’s office in Verdun and beat back some 500 furious demonstrators with tear gas, batons and water cannons. The demonstration came a day after a march on the US Embassy in Awkar also ended in violence.

The protest began peacefully in the afternoon, with thousands marching through Basta to assemble in front of UN House in Riad Solh. The General Labor Confederation, Hizbullah, Amal, the Communist Party and the Syrian Social Nationalist Party joined students from many universities for the protest. Even the Nijmeh and Ansar soccer teams participated in the march.

The crowds waved flags and banners blasting America and Israel. Amid traditional chants of “Death to Israel, Death to America” were more studied insults: “You old woman, take back your owl-faced ambassador,” yelled a group of protesters referring to US Secretary of State Madeleine Albright and US Ambassador David Satterfield.

The procession, which extended for about 2 kilometers, rippled with banners: “This land is ours,” (in Hebrew), “America and Israel are two faces of the same enemy,” “Albright, we are now All-dark,” “Israel and America, our enemies forever,” “and “Barak is a good terrorist.” The Israeli premier was also portrayed with donkey’s ears and burned in effigy.

Youngsters leaving school joined the march, as did motorists. “We’ve reached the end of our patience,” said one man as he parked his Mercedes. “We can’t take American-Israeli aggression any longer.”

A group of 500 students raced through the streets toward Ras Beirut ahead of ISF troopers  toward the CNN offices in the Concorde complex. Police managed to cut them off less than 50 meters away from the building.

CNN has been accused of pro-Israeli bias in its coverage of the recent Israeli air strikes subsequent fighting. Although CNN’s Brent Sadler broadcast live from the Jamhour power station in the aftermath of the air raids, subsequent footage dwelt more on the residents of northern Israel in their bomb shelters and the Israeli government’s perspective  rather than the Lebanese view.

The crowd ran straight into the helmeted and masked policemen, who beat them back with batons, tear gas and water cannons. Two students were injured.

An elderly woman, originally from the south, whose house overlooked the scene applauded the students and threw them onions, lemons and shredded bed sheets, antidotes to the tear gas.

The protesters then stood at the crossroads chanting: “We have to tell the truth: We hate you America,” and “Resistance is our right,” while holding up banners parodying CNN that read: “Q and A: Where is the other side of the story?” and “CNN where is your Insight?”

When a CNN cameraman appeared on the roof of the building to film the photographers, the crowd shouted “Zionist Journalism,” and directed obscene finger gestures at him.

Sadler could not be reached for comment on Friday.

The demonstrators then proceeded toward the home of Prime Minister Salim Hoss in Aisha Bakar. Hoss, with a small police escort, came out of his house to meet the excited crowd. “We’re all resistance,” he replied to the cheers of the crowd. “We will not surrender.”

Raghid Adhami, an Arab University student activist, noted that “it’s ironic that there were more policemen guarding the CNN offices than there were guarding the prime minister.
