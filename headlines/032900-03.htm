Published on Wednesday, March 29, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
'Environmental Heroes' Lost At Sea
Off The Baja Coast
by Stacy Finz, Chuck Squatriglia, Jaxon Van Derbeken
 

A scientific research expedition to the Mexican coast by 20 University of California at Davis students, scholars and environmentalists turned tragic when one of their boats capsized, leaving at least three people dead and two missing.

Authorities said yesterday that the research party was returning to the remote fishing village of Bahia de los Angeles late Monday when its 22-foot inflatable boat capsized off the coast of Baja California about 300 miles south of the U.S. border.

Four survivors clung to the overturned boat for two hours, then spent three hours swimming through swells reaching six feet before reaching two small islands late Monday. They were rescued yesterday.

UC Davis administrators identified one of the dead as Michael Rose, a 28-year-old researcher at the university's Department of Environmental Science and Policy. He also was a project director for Earthwatch Institute, an international environmental group that works closely with UC Davis researchers in Mexico.

University officials withheld the name of the second victim, a visiting Japanese scholar, pending notification of relatives. A third victim, whose name was not released, was found later yesterday.

SPIDER EXPERT MISSING

One of those missing was Gary Polis, the 53-year-old chairman of the Department of Environmental Science and Policy. He is considered one of the world's foremost authorities on spiders. His work has appeared in forums ranging from national television to children's books, and Polis himself was the topic of the 1994 book ``Scorpion Man.''

``He's a renowned expert in the field,'' said Dr. Charles Griswold of the California Academy of Sciences in San Francisco. ``If he is lost, it will be a huge loss to the field.''

The identity the other person who remained missing was not released.

The researchers were returning to Bahia de los Angeles from Isla Cabeza de Caballo, where they were studying spiders and scorpions. They boarded two inflatable Zodiac boats at midday for the four-mile trip back to the Baja peninsula.

WAVES FILLED BOATS WITH WATER

Winds gusting to 30 mph churned the sea, kicking up four- foot to six-foot swells that caused one of the boats to fill with water and capsize.

``It all happened within a matter of minutes. There was a big wave every two minutes that pounded us,'' said survivor Gary Huxel, 38, a post-doctorate ecology researcher at Davis.

After clinging to the capsized vessel for two hours, Huxel and two others set off for shore with life jackets and seat cushions. Huxel said Polis was still hanging onto the boat when he swam off.

Those who left the boat were soon separated by the punishing waves; Huxel drifted to one nearby island while the others wound up on another.

``It was a long, brutal swim,'' said Rob German, whose girlfriend, Rebecca Lawson, survived the ordeal and recounted it to him in a phone call yesterday. ``(Rebecca) was pretty beat up.''

The other rescued survivors are UC Davis students Sarah Ratay and Ralph Haygood. All were found sunburned and exhausted but otherwise uninjured.

SEARCH TO RESUME TODAY

Mexican naval authorities and the Coast Guard called off their search last night but planned to resume this morning..

``It's impossible to speculate what has happened to them,'' said Shannon Knight, a Coast Guard spokeswoman. ``The good news is that they're young and probably healthy and could have possibly swum to land. The other good thing is the weather has been calm.''

Water temperatures in the area ranged from 60 to 65 degrees. An average-sized person could last up to eight hours in those conditions, Coast Guard officials said. A person with more body fat and in good health could last longer.

Friends and colleagues remained hopeful last night that the missing would turn up safely.

``For all we know they could be holed up on an island somewhere,'' said Roger V.D. Bergen, president and CEO of Earthwatch Institute.

Six members of Earthwatch were on the ill-fated expedition but occupied the boat that made it back.

HELICOPTER SENT TO SEARCH

The Mexican Navy had been searching for Polis and two others since daybreak yesterday. A body was found later in the day but no identification was made public. A Coast Guard helicopter arrived at the scene from San Diego at about 4 p.m. to assist in finding the missing pair but returned to Southern California as darkness fell on the Sea of Cortez.

Polis' work has appeared in publications such as Nature, Smithsonian Magazine, and National Geographic Explorer. He has been hailed by conservationists as ``a hero to the environment.''

He came to UC Davis two years ago from Vanderbilt University in Nashville, Tenn., where he taught from 1979 until 1998.

Colleagues called Polis a warm, outgoing man who loved cooking Italian meals. His fascination with scorpions began in 1972, when he spotted one of the venomous creatures during a camping trip in the Santa Monica Mountains.

Polis began his research in Baja in the late 1980s and travelled there three or four times a year, university officials said. This time the researchers concentrated on an island off the coast of Bahia de los Angeles, a tourist spot and popular destination for sports fishermen and wildlife biologists drawn by the region's rich array of wildlife.

Bahia de los Angeles is so remote the town has just one telephone and electrical service is shut off at 10 p.m. each night.

Rose, who died in the mishap, had helped Polis in the laboratory and in the field. Colleagues said Rose enjoyed playing soccer and possessed a keen sense of humor.

``He was a great guy to be around,'' said Paul Sabatier, vice chairman of the Department of Environmental Science and Policy at UC Davis.

Bergen said Rose had gone to Mexico early to prepare for the expedition because of his organizational skills.

``He was a very detail-oriented person,'' he said. ``We had tremendous confidence in him -- this is the last thing anybody would have thought would have happened. We just felt this guy had everything down pat.''
