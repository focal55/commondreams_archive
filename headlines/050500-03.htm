Published on Friday, May 5, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Environmentalists Who Were Pepper-Sprayed by Police Will Get New Trial
by Bill Wallace
 
A federal appeals court yesterday ordered a new trial for environmental activists who sued the Humboldt County Sheriff's Department for swabbing pepper spray on their eyes during a series of 1997 protests, saying the trial judge had been wrong to rule that the chemical's use could not be considered excessive force.

In a 28-page decision, the U.S. Court of Appeals in San Francisco said U.S. District Court Judge Vaughn Walker had been wrong to issue a verdict against the demonstrators in 1998 after the jury panel hearing the case had reached an impasse in its deliberations.

The ruling, issued by Judges Myron Bright, Harry Pregerson and William A. Fletcher, said the jury had failed to reach a conclusion about the protesters' central complaint -- that Humboldt County sheriff's deputies and Eureka city police had used cotton swabs dipped in pepper spray to paint the eyes of unarmed and nonviolent demonstrators during a peaceful protest.

Walker had erred in substituting his own judgment for that of the panel, they said.

In fact, the appellate judges said the demonstrators had provided ample evidence that the use of pepper spray was excessive force outlawed by the U.S. Constitution.

``The district court's conclusion that the officers did not use excessive force to effect the arrests of the protesters as a matter of law is untenable given the evidence presented at trial,'' the opinion said.

``It is clear to us that a `fair-minded jury could return a verdict for the plaintiff(s) on the evidence presented.'

``Accordingly, we reverse the district court's decision to enter judgment,'' the judges ruled. ``We remand this action for a new trial.''

In a separate but concurring opinion, Bright said he hoped that the two sides would resolve the lawsuit through a negotiated settlement now that the appellate decision ``has established that the use of pepper spray in the eyes and on the faces of nonviolent, passive protesters may amount to an unreasonable use of force.''

In its decision, the appeals court made it clear that it was not drawing a legal precedent on whether the use of pepper spray violates constitutional rights. ``We are only to decide whether the district court erred in directing a verdict for the defendants in light of the evidence in the record,'' the judges wrote.

At the same time, there is little doubt that the ruling will cause law enforcement agencies to re-examine their rules for the use of chemical agents, particularly against political protesters who are passively resisting arrest while involved in demonstrations.

The suit stemmed from three demonstrations against the logging practices of Pacific Lumber Co. in September and October 1997. During those protests, anti-logging activists engaged in nonviolent sit-ins. County and city law enforcement officers sprayed aerosol pepper spray into some demonstrators' faces from inches away, and swabbed other protesters' eyes with pepper spray while removing and arresting them.

The anti-logging activists filed suit against the county and city in October 1997, charging that pepper spray is a chemical weapon designed to be used against violent suspects who are resisting arrests, not nonviolent demonstrators engaging in protected speech.

The case went to trial in August 1998.

At trial, Macon Cowles, an attorney for the protesters, said the two departments had ``run wild'' by using the spray in an experimental fashion that is not condoned by other law enforcement agencies.

``These sheriff's deputies and police officers were working off the books,'' he said. ``They've got to be reined in.''

Lawyers for the city and county countered that the spray had been used properly and that no serious injuries had resulted from the application of the chemical.

``No one is before you with a physical injury of any kind,'' said Nancy Delaney, an attorney representing the city and county. ``No one required medical treatment.''

After only seven hours of deliberation, the jury reported to Judge Walker that it was hopelessly deadlocked. The judge initially declared a mistrial, but later dismissed the suit after ruling that no reasonable juror could find that the officers involved had acted improperly.
