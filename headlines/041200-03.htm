Published on Wednesday, April 12, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
Judge Says Tuna Must Remain `Dolphin Safe' Ruling in S.F. blocks attempt by White House to lower standard
by Harriet Chiang
 
A federal judge in San Francisco yesterday rebuffed an effort by the Clinton administration to relax standards for ``dolphin safe'' labeling on tuna.

Several environmental groups had filed suit in August after Commerce Secretary William Daley proposed a change in the standard for labeling tuna ``dolphin safe,'' which had been reserved for tuna caught without any chasing and netting of dolphins.

Daley ruled in April 1999 that there was insufficient evidence that dolphin populations are adversely effected by large encircling nets used by tuna fisherman. Environmentalists have long objected to the use of such nets, which tend to harass and ensnare dolphins, which are swimming above and feeding on tuna.

In ruling against the administration yesterday, U.S. District Judge Thelton Henderson said Daley ``acted contrary to the law and abused his discretion when he triggered a change in the `dolphin safe' label standard.''

David Phillips, director of the international Marine Mammal Project at Earth Island Institute, one of the environmental groups, called the ruling a ``tremendous rebuke'' of the administration and other officials ``who sold out dolphin protections to accommodate a handful of foreign fishing companies.''

A spokesperson for the Commerce Department could not be reached to respond to the ruling.

Environmental groups have waged a long battle against fishermen in the eastern, tropical Pacific who use the encircling nets, known as purse seine nets, to capture tuna swimming below dolphins.

The environmentalists claimed that Daley's decision to loosen the standards was politically motivated to allow fishing fleets in Mexico and other Latin American countries to sell tuna that has been banned in the United States since 1990, when the ``dolphin safe'' label was adopted.

In yesterday's ruling, Henderson found that Daley abused his discretion because he failed to provide research data to support a change in the labeling standard. A law passed by Congress requires the Commerce Department to back up labeling changes with evidence that the use of nets was not harmful to the dolphin population.

The Commerce Department responded that it could not take samples from dead dolphins because of a lack of cooperation from the foreign tuna fleets.

But Henderson did not buy that argument. ``The record, however, makes it clear that Mexico was more than willing to cooperate in the necropsy research,'' the judge wrote in his decision.
