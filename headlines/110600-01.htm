Published on Monday, November 6, 2000 by Reuters
DC Super Rally:
Green's Nader Stomps in U.S. Capital
by Joseph Boris
 
WASHINGTON -- Nearing the end of his longshot campaign for the U.S. presidency, Ralph Nader on Sunday joined thousands of Green Party supporters for a boisterous rally in ''the last American colony.''


A supporter displays signs as Green Party presidential candidate Ralph Nader delivers his keynote speech during a Green Party rally at the MCI Center, Sunday, Nov 5, 2000, in Washington. (AP Photo/Amy E. Conn)
More than 12,000 people gathered in the heart of the nation's capital for four hours of progressive politics, cheering as Nader and his celebrity supporters dismissed arguments that a vote for Nader is a vote for Republican presidential nominee George W. Bush.

``If you don't win on Tuesday, Mr. Gore, it won't be because of Ralph Nader,'' said Michael Moore, the liberal filmmaker. ``It will be because of you, Mr. Gore.''

Moore argued that with 35 years experience as an activist, Nader was inherently more qualified than either Bush or Gore, a theme echoed by former TV host Phil Donahue, actor Danny Glover and rock musician Patti Smith.

They also minimized the differences between the major-party candidates, filling the MCI Center with repeated denunciations of the ``Republicrats.''

``A vote for Nader means the buck stops here ... that we're not willing to play the game of lesser of two evils,'' actor Danny Glover said, quoting extensively from Langston Hughes, the great Harlem Renaissance poet.

With Nader hovering at about 5 percent in most national polls, but drawing significant support in Oregon, Michigan and other toss-up states, his rag-tag campaign has increasingly worried associates of Democrat Al Gore.

In an address that went on for more than an hour, Nader regaled the mostly white, 20-something crowd with some of his favorite themes, arguing for an increase in the minimum wage, stricter consumer and environmental regulations, universal health care and an end to the war on drugs.

But, as he has been prone to do, Nader seized the opportunity to criticize the power and influence of American businesses.

``Corporations were designed to be our servants, not our masters,'' Nader said, surrounded by ads for the giant corporations that bankroll the local Washington Wizards basketball team and Capitals hockey team.

``We can and must stop this corporate welfare that is coming out of Washington, a bustling bazaar of accounts receivable for these corporations,'' Nader said.

The rally also served as a national platform for local activists working to secure statehood for the District of Columbia, a city of more than 500,000 that depends on Congressional largesse for many of its operations, yet lacks a voting representative in either legislative chamber.
