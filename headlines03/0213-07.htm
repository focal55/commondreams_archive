Published on Thursday, February 13, 2003 by Reuters
Western Peace Activists Invite Mandela to Iraq
 

BAGHDAD, Feb 13 - A group of Western peace activists said on Thursday they had written to former South African President Nelson Mandela asking him to come to Iraq to help them prevent war, but he said he needed a U.N. mandate.

Mandela, a Nobel Peace Laureate, has accused U.S. President George W. Bush and British Prime Minister Tony Blair of running roughshod over the United Nations in their bid to oust Iraqi President Saddam Hussein.


Canadian peace activist Roberta Taman talks to reporters at the information ministry in Baghdad, February 13, 2003. Taman, who recently arrived with 14 other Western peace activists to the Iraqi capital, said her group had written to South African statesman Nelson Mandela asking him to come to Iraq to help them avert war. REUTERS/Suhaib Salem
"He has not responded yet, but we believe that Mr Mandela would come," said Roberta Taman, spokeswomen for 15 activists from Canada, Italy and other European countries who recently arrived in Iraq.

Mandela's spokeswoman Zelda LaGrange said Mandela would need a clear mandate from United Nations Secretary General Kofi Annan before embarking on any trip to Baghdad.

"He has received a number of requests to go to Iraq, but he is adamant that he will not go unless he is requested by the United Nations," LaGrange told Reuters in Johannesburg.

Mandela, a recipient of Bush's Presidential Medal of Freedom last year, launched a blistering personal attack against the U.S. president last month, questioning whether he would treat the United Nations the same way if it wasn't headed by a black man.

"If the U.N. kindly requests him to go, then he will go," LaGrange said.

In Baghdad, members of the group of activists -- one of several around the world involved in a variety of solidarity visits to Iraq -- said they would sit outside Iraqi power plants and hospitals. It was unclear if they would remain there under bombing.

"We have a responsibility to say no to this war. There is no justification," Taman told a news conference.

Millions of people in cities across the globe are expected to demonstrate on Saturday against any U.S.-led invasion of Iraq and organisers say it could be the world's biggest anti-war protest.

Hundreds of thousands of protesters are expected in London and Rome, with smaller events planned from Antarctica to Reykjavik. In South Africa, where President Thabo Mbeki shares Mandela's anti-war position, demonstrations are planned in Johannesburg, Cape Town and other major cities.

A group of about 50 peace activists said in Turkey this week they had got visas to enter Iraq where they plan to disperse to populated areas of Baghdad and other parts of the country, acting as "human shields".

They hope their presence and the possibility of Western casualties will encourage U.S. political leaders and military planners to rethink any plans to bomb Iraq over its alleged chemical, biological and nuclear weapons.

The Iraq Peace Team, another Western group, has been in the country since September 2002 and pledges to stay with Iraqi families if war erupts.

Neither Washington nor London, preparing for a possible war over Saddam's alleged weapons of mass destruction, have said if the presence of Western human shields would affect their plans.

In the Muslim world, the main rallying point for would-be human shields is in Jordan. There, a campaign led by leftist parties and civic bodies is seeking 100,000 volunteers.
