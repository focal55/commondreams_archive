Published on Wednesday, May 7, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Keeping the Faith
Iraq War Strengthens Family's Passion for Peace
by Peter Fimrite
 

The pounding rain soaked the large peace sign hanging on the front of Phil Meymand's and Alice Cannan's Oakland house, tearing off one hand-drawn letter at a time.

When the rain stopped, all that was left was a smeared, soggy remnant.

It was a symbolic moment for the couple and their 4- and 6-year-old daughters, who had marched together at numerous anti-war rallies in the Bay Area and in the state capital only to see their hopes for a peaceful solution in Iraq crushed under a wave of war.


Phil Meymand kneels behind wife Alice Cannan, holding her daughter Leila Meymand on her lap during a hearing on schools. Chronicle photo by Katy Raddatz
Like a hard rain, the swamping of Saddam Hussein's army has washed away peace signs all over the Bay Area and left the anti-war movement in tatters.

But to Meymand and Cannan, the end of the war is just the beginning of the battle against what they see as a growing conviction in America that it's all right to invade a country that you think needs a new government.

"Sure, peace signs are coming down, but I don't think it's a lost cause," said Meymand, a 41-year-old engineer. "Now more than ever it is important to work for peace. One thing we've learned from this is that by the time polices are set in motion, it is tremendously difficult to change their course. If people think they can go out and march a few weekends and change America's course, they are mistaken. It is a long-term process."

That means Meymand and Cannan will continue questioning what they see as a national policy of spending billions bombing a country and then billions more rebuilding what was destroyed.

"My question is, why not skip the bombing and killing part and go directly to the aid part," Meymand said. "The only thing our government has proven so far is that the military can pound the hell out of anybody."

And, he said, if Americans aren't careful, our military may soon be trying to pound countries like North Korea, Iran and Syria.

"Our government has designs to transform the Middle East, to influence the political landscape of all the countries in that region," Meymand said. "I think the war in Iraq is really the first battle in that foreign policy goal."

Meymand and Cannan have been stung by criticism of the anti-war movement, especially since they believe the ramifications of the war in Iraq could last for years.

If America's quick victory in Iraq means protesters were wrong, they ask, would a devastating terrorist attack in a month or a year mean the war was wrong?

"We don't know what would have happened if we didn't invade Iraq," said Cannan, 39, a women's health practitioner. "Who's to say what outcome is better when you can't test one against the other? Right now, people are still being fired upon. There is anti-American feeling. The big question is, does the end justify the means?"

And there may be no end in sight, according to Meymand. He said the Israeli- Palestinian conflict continues to rage while anti-American sentiment in Arab and Muslim countries grows. Meanwhile, he said, the United Nations is divided, and many of America's traditional European allies have turned against Bush administration policies.

But Meymand and Cannan also believe this is "a crossroads" in history for the Middle East, if not the entire world.

"All these countries in the Middle East are poised at a moment of opportunity now with the new Palestinian prime minister," Meymand said. "The U. S. has an opportunity to boldly support a truly equitable agreement. But if they stay with this idea of unilateral threat -- it's our way or the highway -- it won't be successful."

This is why Meymand and Cannan believe it is important to continue to actively oppose the Bush administration.

Ultimately, what they are most concerned about are the lessons their children learn. Cannan said it is difficult teaching children to use words to solve conflicts when their government is doing the exact opposite.

But she hopes that taking them to the demonstrations at least taught them it is all right to speak out when they see injustice.

In many ways, it was a lesson the parents learned. Both are now more involved in local issues, and Cannan has recently become immersed in the politically charged issue of improving Oakland schools.

"Our objective in marching against the war wasn't to stop it," Meymand said.

"It was to voice our objection to it. It shows the world that not all Americans are of the cowboy mentality. So protesting is never futile. It's part of a long-term process of working for peace and justice."

For the children, it's all about contrasts.

"Do you know what the opposite of war is?" asked 4-year-old Leila one recent day as the last of the Iraqi forces were being driven out of Baghdad.

"Peace," said Cannan.

"And the opposite of peace, is war," said a smiling Leila, grasping for perhaps the first time what all the recent fuss was about.
