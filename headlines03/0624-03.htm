Published on Tuesday, June 24, 2003 by the Daily News Tribune (Waltham, Mass)
Protesters Send a Message to Cheney
by Cathy Flynn
 

HOPKINTON -- While an elegantly dressed, conservative crowd gathered yesterday at Richard Egan's home to listen in rapt attention to Vice President Dick Cheney, a different crowd stood at the busy corner of West Main and West Elm streets to send him a message.

About 50 protesters, who called their gathering the "We the People's reception for Dick Cheney," stood in front of the Community Covenant Church and held signs and banners decrying President George W. Bush's foreign and domestic policies.

Members of peace groups from around Greater Boston and as far west as Worcester, they held signs that read, "The world is not for profit, Dick." "George Bush's policies leave 12 million children behind," and other sentiments.


Ridgley Fuller, right, of Sherborn, Mass. wearing a Vice President Dick Cheney mask, protests Monday, June 23, 2003, in Hopkinton, Mass., against Cheney's private fund-raising visit to Hopkinton. (AP Photo/Chitose Suzuki)
Holocaust survivor Stella Penzer, wearing a red sequined beret decorated with anti-war buttons, cradled a sign that read, "U.S. -- addicted to violence and war."

"My whole family was wiped out in the Holocaust, and I am a passionate believer in peace," said Penzer, who came to the protest from Newton Center. "There is no good war, and no bad peace."

The Cheney motorcade passed the protesters twice, at around 5:45 p.m. and about an hour later, after the vice president's stop at the Egan residence, where he spoke to about 600 supporters.

"I am sure that he saw us," said organizer Louise Coleman. "It was chilling to see so many motorcycles and state police cars with him, and I'm amazed at how little time he spent here."

Coleman sent e-mails to peace group members last week and asked them to join yesterday's protest.

A statement sent by Coleman to the media said, "The truth is that Americans are suffering because of this administration's policies...none more so than our fellow Americans, our soldiers, being killed in Iraq in a war that is a dishonest travesty....Also of particular concern is Dick Cheney's role in covering up the administration's machinations before and after the tragedy of September 11."

Plenty of motorists slowed down to read the signs yesterday, and Hopkinton Police Chief Tom Irvin said the late afternoon traffic near that busy intersection was more brutal than usual.

Hopkinton Police contacted Community Covenant Church to arrange a place for the protesters and provided extra personnel to keep traffic moving.

Protester Alice Copeland Brown of Canton, dressed as a pilgrim woman in homage to her ancestor Joan Tilley, who Brown said came over on the Mayflower. Brown claimed that Bush was a distant cousin.

When asked what brought her out that afternoon to protest against a relative, Brown answered, "How do I count the ways? When they stole the election? If they steal one more election, I will follow Joan's example and emigrate to Canada."

Brown said that an uninformed electorate is responsible for Bush being in office.

"We're savages, we're undereducated and we're easily brainwashed," she said.

A few feet away, Isabel Jancowitz of Weston was continuing a 40-year commitment to the peace movement.

"This war is a slaughter," she said. "Some vets are calling it a turkey shoot....It's immoral."

Hopkinton veterinarian Margo Roman was among the protesters, along with a group of Teamsters who arrived in a bus decorated with their logo.

As the Egan guests began heading west towards Gassett Road, where signs directed them to the party site, some protesters began calling out to them.

"Cheney is a war criminal," shouted one woman as a sleek black car passed. And a man called out, "All millionaires, turn right!"

But most of the crowd held their signs quietly, unwavering in their commitment to the cause of peace. Hopkinton resident W. Al Brown said, "I wouldn't be anywhere else right now."
