Published on Wednesday, March 12, 2003 by Reuters
Supreme Court Grants Last-Minute Stay of Execution
 

HUNTSVILLE, Texas - Minutes before Texas was to carry out its 300th execution since resuming capital punishment 21 years ago, the U.S. Supreme Court on Wednesday stopped Delma Banks' death by lethal injection.


Sporting a tattoo reading 'Delma Jr.', Dakinya Jefferson celebrates word of the stay of execution for her father Delma Banks just minutes before his scheduled death Wednesday, March 12, 2003, just outside the death house in Huntsville, Texas. Jefferson is surrounded by death penalty opponents. (AP Photo/Pat Sullivan)
Banks, a black man with no prior criminal record, was sentenced to die for robbing and shooting to death Richard Whitehead, a white 16-year-old, on April 14, 1980, in the east Texas town of Nash. Banks was 21 at the time.

Supporters said he was wrongly convicted, his right to a fair trial marred by alleged prosecutorial misconduct, ineffective legal assistance and the deliberate selection of an all-white jury.

"I just thank the Lord. Give Jesus all the credit," Banks said. The nation's highest court acted just moments before he was to be strapped to the gurney in the Texas death house.

He had already eaten his last meal of two double cheeseburgers, a double order of french fries and orange juice, which he told prison officials was good.

As word of the stay spread, supporters and relatives, including his adult daughter, cheered and jumped for joy.

Banks' lawyer, George Kendall of the NAACP's Legal Defense Fund, was waiting to enter the death chamber when he heard.

"There is no execution today. I think it means the court is seriously thinking about taking this case," he told Reuters.

Kendall said he tried to see Banks, but prison officials were preparing to return him to death row in Livingston, Texas, about 50 miles away.

Bowie County prosecutor James Elliott, who helped win the conviction, said it was too early to draw any conclusion other than that the Supreme Court needs more time.

"I wish we could have brought it to a conclusion today, but I have been here for 23 years and I'm prepared to stay here to see it through," said Elliott, who was in his office in Texarkana awaiting news of the execution.

Kenneth Clay, scheduled to die on March 20, is next in line to be Texas' 300th execution. The state has carried out more than a third of all executions since the Supreme Court lifted a ban in 1976. Texas resumed executions six years later.

UNUSUAL INTERVENTION

Unusually, three former federal judges, including former FBI director William Sessions, who is from Texas, filed a brief urging the Supreme Court to hear the case.

In a one-paragraph order, the high court said the stay would remain in effect until the justices decide whether to hear one of the pending appeals filed by Banks' lawyers.

If the appeal is rejected, the stay of execution will end, the court said. If the justices decide to hear the case, it will continue until the court issues a decision.

Banks had shown that from 1975 through 1980, Bowie County prosecutors accepted 80 percent of qualified white jurors and eliminated 90 percent of blacks in felony cases. They used a code, writing "b" or "n" by the names of blacks on jury lists.

Prosecutors argued they had race-neutral reasons to remove the four potential black jurors in Banks' pool. Banks' lawyer never objected during the trial.

The Supreme Court ruled last month that another Texas death row inmate, Thomas Miller-El, should get a hearing to present similar evidence of racial bias in his jury selection.
