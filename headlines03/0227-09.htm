Published on Thursday, February 27, 2003 by Reuters
Germans Turn Carnival Revelry into Huge Peace Demo
by David Crossland
 

COLOGNE, Germany - Germany's carnival is turning into a week-long peace protest this year with up to 10 million revelers set to oppose war on Iraq dressed as clowns and Friesian cows, organizers say.

Better known for its beer-guzzling crowds and symbolic castrations, the annual carnival is a religion to many in the Catholic Rhineland and brings large parts of Germany to a standstill in the six days leading up to Ash Wednesday.

Organizers expect this year's revelry to reflect overwhelming public support for Chancellor Gerhard Schroeder's opposition to U.S.-led military action against Iraq.

"It's going to be the biggest peace demonstration imaginable," Franz Wolf, president of the federation of German carnival associations, told Reuters. "I think there's hardly anyone in Germany who wants this war to take place."

Wolf expects up to 10 million people to join the processions this year and rejects calls to have the event cancelled as it was during the 1991 Gulf War.

The revelry entered its final week on Thursday and culminates on "Rose Monday", March 3, when millions are expected to throng the streets of Cologne, Duesseldorf, Mainz and practically every village in the Rhineland.

Revelers often wear costumes -- the Friesian cow being among the most popular. Among rituals that may startle a stranger is one in which women symbolically castrate men by cutting off their ties.

"MAKE ALAAF NOT WAR"

The bizarre rallying cry "Alaaf", roughly equivalent to the exclamation "Ole!", echoes through ballrooms and streets. One regional politician wanted this year's motto to be "Make Alaaf not War".

"Given the massive threat of war against Iraq the carnivalists should make a statement," said Michael Vesper of the pacifist Greens who share power with Schroeder's Social Democrats in Berlin.

In Cologne, a band has scored a hit with its version of the anti-war classic "Where Have All the Flowers Gone" which contrasts with usual carnival fare that has titles like "My Pig is Gone" or "Do You Have a Pizza, Pizza Wunderbar."

The carnival has its roots in a Roman spring festival and ancient Germanic fertility rites, later adopted by Christians as a way to usher in the Lent fasting period before Easter.

It also has a tradition of poking fun at authority. Brigades of carnival "soldiers" in mock 18th century uniforms invade town halls in carefully staged uprisings, usually cheered on by revelers Beer and sausages are always at hand.

The ritual storming of town halls offers a clue to a long tradition of opposition to military occupiers in the Rhineland, dating back to the French and Prussians in the 18th century.

As U.S.-German tensions rise, poking fun at U.S. President George W. Bush has become increasingly popular with audiences at carnival events.

"I took a long Bush joke into my repertoire about three weeks ago," said Guido Cantz, a Cologne comedian who performs at major carnival balls each year.

"At first the applause was a bit hesitant but in the last 10 days I've almost been getting standing ovations. You can really tell that people just don't want a war."
