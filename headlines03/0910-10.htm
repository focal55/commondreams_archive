Published on Wednesday, September 10, 2003 by Reuters
Senate Opposes Bush on Overtime Change
by Thomas Ferraro
 

WASHINGTON - A divided U.S. Senate defied a White House veto threat on Wednesday and voted to block proposed federal work rule changes that foes say could deny millions of Americans pay for overtime work.
The White House said last week that President Bush's advisors would recommend he veto the entire spending bill if the amendment was attached to it.

The Republican-led chamber, on a 54-45 vote, approved a Democratic amendment that would derail a proposed expansion of overtime exemptions for white-collar workers under the 1938 Fair Labor Standards Act.

Yet the final fate of the Bush administration proposal remained unclear, as it still faces a number of procedural and possible legal twists and turns.

"This was perhaps the most important victory that we have had for working families in some time," said Senate Democratic Leader Tom Daschle of South Dakota.

But Daschle added, "The president has threatened to veto this legislation, so our victory today is only the first step."

The administration, along with the business community, has argued that its proposed changes are needed to update antiquated work rules and aimed at middle- and upper-level white-collar employees.

Opponents, who include labor unions, say the changes could unfairly strip workers, including police and firefighters, of overtime protection and allow them to be made to work longer than a 40-hour week without additional compensation.

The Senate vote came on a Democratic amendment that would deny any funding for these proposed changes. The amendment was attached to a broader $138 billion spending bill for U.S. health, labor and education programs.

The White House said last week that President Bush's advisors would recommend he veto the entire spending bill if the amendment was attached to it.

PARTY LINES

The vote was mostly along party lines, although six Republicans, including Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Ted Stevens of Alaska, joined 47 Democrats and one independent in approving the amendment. One Democrat, Zell Miller of Georgia, joined 44 Republicans in opposing it.

Before the measure could become law, the Republican-led House of Representatives would have to concur, and both chambers would have to override any presidential veto with a two-thirds vote.

The House narrowly defeated a similar amendment earlier this year, but proponents are hopeful that the Senate action will give the measure new momentum.

There is plenty of confusion and debate over how many and what type of workers would be affected by the proposed changes.

The Labor Department estimates no more than 644,000 employees would lose overtime protection. But critics put the figure at 6 million to more than 10 million.

In addition, the Labor Department contends the changes would affect only white-collar employees and not manual workers or firefighters, police officers or nurses. Yet critics have argued the proposed changes could be interpreted to strip many of them of overtime protection, too.

The Fair Labor Standards Act created the 40-hour work week by guaranteeing overtime pay, at time and a half, for each hour worked over 40. The law allows for the exemption of administrative, professional and executive workers.

Under the Labor Department's proposal, more employees could be reclassified as exempt administrators, professionals or executives -- provided they meet certain criteria.

In addition, employees earning more than $65,000 a year could be exempt if they meet one or more of the duties for an executive, administrative or professional employee.

Sen. Judd Gregg, a New Hampshire Republican and chairman of the Senate Health, Education and Labor Committee, argued that the proposal was still being reviewed by the administration and it would be wrong to "short circuit it."
