Published on Wednesday, October 1, 2003 by Agence France Presse
Council of Europe Presses US, Japan on Abolishing Death Penalty
 

The Council of Europe lamented the lack of progress in efforts to persuade the United States and Japan to abolish the death penalty and again warned that their observer status at the pan-European rights body was at risk.

The council's parliamentary assembly called for greater efforts to hold a dialogue with both the United States and Japan over getting rid of capital punishment, according to a statement from the 45-nation organization.

While talks with Japan were "fruitful and ongoing," the assembly said dialogue had "largely failed with the US."

The assembly, which has asked Japan and the United States to put a moratorium on executions with a view to abolishing the death penalty altogether, said it was becoming increasingly difficult for the council "to accept observer states which carried out executions."

The Council of Europe claims to be a "death penalty free zone" and says no executions have been recorded in its 45 member states since 1997.

Armenia, a member since 2001, confirmed on Monday it had abolished the death penalty by ratifying a council protocol on capital punishment.

Two Japanese parliamentarians, addressing the assembly here, said it would be difficult for Japan to abolish the death penalty because of strong popular opinion.

Some 80 percent of Japanese people supported keeping the death penalty, according to a survey, Katsuyuki Hikasa said.

Since June 2001, Japan has carried out five executions, while 137 death penalties have been carried out in the United States, the assembly said.

Two years ago, the assembly asked the US and Japan to take steps towards abolition or risk having their observer status called into question.

But Renate Wohlwend, a member of the assembly in charge of the issue, said Wednesday it was too early to issue threats.

"We have to establish the dialogue first," she said.

But while talks had been held with Japanese parliamentarians, no members of the US Congress had taken part in a conference on the subject in Washington in April, she said.
