Published on Wednesday, May 21, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Senate debates ban on small warheads
'Is Half a Hiroshima OK?' Losing Democrats Ask
by James Sterngold
 

Senate Democrats launched an impassioned but ultimately futile effort Tuesday to prevent the Bush administration from lifting a 10-year-old ban on the development of smaller, more usable nuclear warheads and dramatically shifting the nation's defense policies.

The procedural vote went 51-43 against the Democratic effort, which pushes the proposed repeal of the ban -- and a potentially historic resumption of nuclear weapons development -- to the House.

The full House is expected to vote later this week on the issue, which both sides say could mark a critical turn in the country's security policy.

The Democratic stance was put in the most graphic terms Tuesday by Sen. Edward Kennedy of Massachusetts, who with Sen. Dianne Feinstein of California led the effort to retain the weapons ban. The new, low-yield warheads, Kennedy contended, would be easier to use and thus make nuclear conflict more likely, not less so.

"Is half a Hiroshima OK? Is a quarter Hiroshima OK? Is a little mushroom cloud OK?" he asked on the Senate floor. "That's absurd. The issue is too important. If we build it, we'll use it."

The Bush administration's efforts to repeal the decade-old prohibition is part of its overall $400.5 billion military budget, but it is probably the most contentious provision in the bill. The Senate Democrats were trying to remove that provision from the budget.

'BUNKER BUSTERS'

In the Senate on Tuesday, Republicans stressed that the new types of warheads -- which would be the first developed since the Cold War -- were needed because of the extraordinary threats the country now faces. The warheads are intended as "bunker busters," to destroy underground stashes of prohibited weapons.

"I believe this country has a moral responsibility to lead in this world, and we will not be an effective leader unless we maintain our leadership in all areas, including nuclear weaponry," said Sen. Jeff Sessions, R-Ala.

Sen. Lindsey Graham, R-S.C., said it would be illogical for the United States not to develop a weapon that could prevent an attack by destroying a foe's buried caches of weapons.

But Democrats insisted that the low-yield weapons, with a force of five kilotons or less -- a third the force of the bomb that destroyed Hiroshima in 1945 -- would make the nation less secure because they would only provoke potential foes to hurriedly build up their own arsenals as a deterrent, and lead to a destructive proliferation of nuclear weapons.

NUCLEAR ARMS RACE

"I can't believe that I have witnessed in my time on Capitol Hill a more historic debate than what we are undertaking at this moment," said Sen. Richard Durbin, D-Ill. "We are literally talking about whether or not the United States will initiate a nuclear arms race again. Nothing that I can think of meets this in terms of gravity and its impact on the future of the world.'

Added Feinstein: "I deeply believe this bill places America at a crossroads in the conduct of foreign policy, and how we determine nuclear weapons policy will go a long way to determining whether we control nuclear proliferation or expand it. This bill will expand it. Let there be no doubt."

The House Armed Services Committee came up with a compromise version of the provision last week that permits design work to proceed on the new types of low-yield warheads while still prohibiting production.

If that version passes the full House, the issue would then be decided in a conference between the House and the Senate.

Sen. Jack Reed, D-R.I., later offered an amendment that was similar to the House version of the bill, and that is expected to be voted on today. The administration has pushed for repeal of the law, known as the Spratt-Furse Amendment, because of what it says are the extraordinary threats the nation confronts.

ONLY RESEARCH

At the same time, it has said it has no plans to build the new bombs, only conduct research. That was the tack taken Tuesday by Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld.

"It is a study. It is nothing more and nothing less," Rumsfeld said. "And it is not pursuing. And it is not developing. It is not building. It is not manufacturing. And it's not deploying. And it is not using."

Feinstein dismissed his comments.

"Just a study? Baloney. Does anyone really believe that?" she asked.

Later, she added: "I cannot think of a single issue that should more define the political agenda today than whether the United States should go back into the nuclear business again, and repeal of Spratt-Furse is the first step in that direction."

Near the end of the Cold War, the United States not only negotiated a series of large reductions in its strategic nuclear weapons with Russia, but it decided to withdraw nearly all of its smaller tactical nuclear warheads, feeling they were no longer needed.

Congress passed the Spratt-Furse Amendment in 1993 in response to that withdrawal. It was an effort to prevent those smaller weapons from returning, in large part because they are considered more usable, and thus more dangerous.

The Bush administration has adopted an aggressive nuclear policy, calling for a repeal of that low-yield ban and an approach that would permit the first use of nuclear weapons to destroy dangerous weapons caches in enemy hands.
