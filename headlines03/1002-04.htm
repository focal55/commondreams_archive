Art Shows Fear of Profiling and U.S. Anti-Terror Act
by Grant McCool
 

NEWARK - Stark paintings of young black men holding their hands up in resignation, frustration and fear are part of a new exhibit that takes aim at racial profiling in America and the "war on terrorism."

In New Jersey, a state that became synonymous with racial profiling during a 1990s police highway patrol scandal, artist Eric Brown asks whether the hotly debated anti-terrorism USA Patriot Act and profiling of minorities are "one and the same."


Eric Brown discusses his paintings for an art show titled 'Homeland An American Profile' in Newark, New Jersey October 1, 2003. Stark paintings of young black men holding their hands up in resignation, frustration and fear are part of the exhibit that takes aim at racial profiling in America and the 'war on terrorism.' Photo by Chip East/Reuters
The paintings of silhouettes against brick walls, arms raised and fists curled or clenched, are dominated by the red, white and blue colors of the U.S. flag.

The exhibit, "Homeland Security ... An American Profile" opened on Wednesday and runs through Nov. 7 in this run-down city struggling to revive itself.

Brown, 30, gave his works titles such as "Homeland Security," "Empty Streets," and "Day of Absence."

He said in an interview at his "SleepWalker's Art" gallery he wanted to convey the fear that under the Patriot Act "anyone can be rounded up, it's not just black people or Muslims, it's just Americans."

"With the empty street I wanted to pose the question: what happened to the people? Was it fear from gangs? Was it everyone rounded up and put in a camp like the Afghans in Cuba?"

He was referring to the U.S.-run Guantanamo Bay military camp where suspected militant Islamists of the former ruling Taliban in Afghanistan are held with members of al Qaeda. The group headed by Osama bin Laden was blamed for the Sept. 11, 2001, attacks on America that sparked the Bush administration's declared war on terrorism.

The U.S. Congress passed the Patriot Act in Oct. 2001, which has been criticized by liberals and conservatives for infringing on civil liberties and constitutional rights.

GANGS AND DRUGS

Brown said he also wanted his exhibit to reveal the scourge of drug trafficking and inner-city gangs like the Bloods and Crips on urban black communities across the United States.

"I feel African-Americans are placed in a position of subservience where we have our hands up in the air, be it out of frustration, being pulled over by police or getting robbed," Brown said.

He believes many people share his fear that in the post-Sept. 11 era "profiling has taken on a different name as a means to find acceptance with the majority as a reason for losing their rights without too much opposition."

The faces of the men each have the number 7 painted on them, a religious symbol of God's instruction in the Bible to rest on the seventh day, according to Brown, who is a Christian.
