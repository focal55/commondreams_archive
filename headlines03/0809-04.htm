Published on Saturday, August 9, 2003 by Bloomberg News
French Meeting to Protest Globalization Turns Away Supporters
 

Aug. 9 (Bloomberg) -- The Larzac anti-globalization meeting in south-central France asked local authorities to turn supporters away from the event because it was running out of space and water amid a record turnout.

``We asked police to block access so that the people who are already here can have a proper discussion,'' Jose Bove, one of the organizers, said on Canal Satellite's all-news channel. Bove estimated that more than 150,000 people attended, though local police put the number between 100,000 and 150,000, Agence France- Presse said.


French anti-globalization icon Jose Bove, head of the Peasants Confederation, addresses tens of thousands at the Larzac 2003 rally. (AFP/Eric Cabanis)
Bove, freed from jail a week ago, called on the French government to initiate a national debate on the Cancun round of World Trade Organization negotiations that take place next month. Larzac, an annual gathering that lasts all weekend and uses the slogan ``The World Is Not For Sale,'' was the site of Bove's first protest 30 years ago.

Bove also called for French citizens to strike and protest against government pension and welfare reforms again in September to build on a movement that began this spring. His 10-month term for destroying genetically modified crops was cut short by a judge after only six weeks on condition he does community service.

``September should not just be hot but burning,'' Bove said, according to Le Monde newspaper.

`Not For Sale'

Bove went to jail when helicopter-borne police seized him in a dawn raid on his farmhouse on June 22. A court sentenced him two years ago for destroying genetically modified crops in attacks on an experimental site owned by Novartis AG and a laboratory in Montpellier in 1998 and 1999.

In 1973, Bove, then 20, led Larzac sheep farmers proclaiming ``We Are Not for Sale'' in successful protests to stop the French army turning the plateau into a firing range that would have destroyed their holdings. Those sit-ins and marches started a movement that developed into the ecologist Greens party which shared power with former Prime Minister Lionel Jospin's Socialist Party from 1997 until last year.


French anti-globalization activists arrive at the 'Larzarc 2003' meeting on the Larzarc plateau, southern France, Friday Aug. 8, 2003. Tens of thousands of anti-globalization activists are expected to gather for a three-day rally taking aim at the upcoming World Trade Organization meeting in Cancun, Mexico, next month. (AP Photo/Bob Edme)
A producer of ewes' milk for Roquefort cheese, Bove gained international notoriety by leading the dismantling of a McDonald's Corp. restaurant that was under construction near his home in 1999.

He served two months in jail for the McDonald's operation last year. On that occasion, pipe-smoking Bove, distinctive for his walrus moustache, went to prison on his own tractor, turning the morning's drive into an event that was covered live by French television news channels.

Bove said he attacked the McDonald's in August 1999 to protest increased U.S. import duties on French foods, including Roquefort, imposed in retaliation for a European Union ban on U.S. beef produced with hormones.

The Frenchman said he plans to stop acting as a spokesman for the movement in France next year, AFP reported. He added that he doesn't plan to run for office.
