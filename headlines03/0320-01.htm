Published on Thursday, March 20, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
As War Begins, Protesters March
Today, huge demonstrations planned in downtown S.F.
by Joe Garofoli, Kevin Fagan, Anastasia Hendrix
 

As cruise missiles struck Iraq on Wednesday night, thousands of peace activists marched through the streets of San Francisco and blocked a major intersections in a glimpse of the huge demonstrations planned for today.

Frustrated that they'd proved powerless to stop a war some demonstrators did not realize had already begun, many activists vowed Wednesday to do everything they could to end it quickly.

"Now our goal is to get it stopped as soon as possible," said LeiLani Dowell of International Answer, which coordinated Wednesday night's generally peaceful demonstration. "We're angry. And we've got to keep fighting."

Police did not provide a crowd estimate, but observers said it looked like the demonstration drew from 2,000 to 3,000 participants. Protests also took place Wednesday in New York, Boston, Detroit and other cities.

Today, protest groups expect a lot more people to descend on the Financial District and other locations citywide, beginning at 7 a.m., in a campaign to shut down the city through civil disobedience.

Similar campaigns are slated for 49 other cities nationwide, and smaller demonstrations such as rallies and vigils are expected in as many as 500 communities, activists said.

"Bush, brace yourself," Gopal Dayaneni of Direct Action to Stop the War, told a roaring crowd gathered at Powell and Market streets before the cruise missile strike began. "We are here. And we are going to stop this war."

The demonstration was orderly, police said, until about 7:30 p.m., when thousands of people briefly blocked the intersection at 24th and Mission streets. But there were no arrests by 8:30 p.m.

Similar demonstrations took place from Los Angeles to Santa Rosa and beyond.

Most were peaceful, but police in West Los Angeles arrested 35 protesters after they refused to disperse during an afternoon demonstration one block from the Federal Building, authorities said.

The San Francisco protest, which began as the 5 p.m. deadline for Saddam Hussein to step down passed unheeded, was marked by a sense of frustration not seen at far larger demonstrations held in recent weeks.

"At the rallies and protests in January and February, there was more of a sense of hope that we might be able to stop the war," said Dan Fahey, a Navy veteran of the 1991 Gulf War. "At this point, it's disgust and anger."

Others expressed fear -- for U.S. soldiers, for the Iraqis and for our own homeland security.

"We're here to protest the war because we got letters from kids in Baghdad, " said Sheena Castrejon, a seventh-grader from Oakland. "They are really scared and so are we. It made me think about how they're suffering. I could imagine myself in their shoes."

Police led the rain-soaked crowd, led by actor Danny Glover, down Market Street toward the Mission District. At one point, Glover climbed onto a flatbed truck to address the crowd.

"We're all here to let our voice be heard, to say we are against this illegal, unilateral war against the people of Iraq," Glover said. "It's not just about Saddam Hussein, it's about the women and the children, it's about the people."

The crowd was dotted by placards and signs decrying the war and the Bush administration, and many in the crowd had harsh words for the president.

Monica Neff of Fremont came with her husband and clutched her 22-month-old son in her arms. An American flag was draped around her shoulders, a black swastika emblazoned across its stripes.

"Bush wants to be in control of the world," Neff said.

Moments later, Sue Harden, a fourth-grade teacher with two adult sons, commented on Neff's get-up.

"I told her I would have never agreed with that before today," Harden said. "In other wars, we were attacked, like Pearl Harbor, but now we are the ones doing the attacking. Never in 225 years have we done anything like this."

If there were any Bush supporters along the march route, most of them kept quiet.

"I'm not for war, but I'll support the troops," said David Loudermilk, a 17- year-old high school student from Hayward. "I really don't think our country can survive if it's divided."

Of the marchers filing past him, he said, "I respect their decision. I don't agree with it, but I respect it."

Police and security officials braced for civil disobedience or violence. Scores of police, many with riot gear in hand, climbed off three buses near the Old Mint before the demonstration started.

As the 5 p.m. deadline crept closer, a security guard at the San Francisco Shopping Center was overheard on his walkie-talkie telling someone, "Are you ready for this? It's showtime. We're going to knock heads."

But police, who followed the demonstrators on foot, bicycle and in patrol cars, said the crowd was well-behaved.

"Very peaceful," San Francisco police Capt. Bill Davenport said Wednesday. "They even changed their route, and we accommodated them. We're hoping things go like this tomorrow."

Chronicle staff writers Stacy Finz, Tyche Hendricks, Matthew B. Stannard and Chuck Squatriglia and the Associated Press contributed to this report.
