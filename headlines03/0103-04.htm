Published on Friday, January 3, 2003 by the San Francisco Chronicle
Activists Sue Over Relaxed Tuna-Label Rules
by Bob Egelko
 

Hours after the Bush administration acted to loosen labeling standards for "dolphin-safe" tuna, pro-dolphin groups returned to the same San Francisco federal court that had blocked similar government efforts in the past.

In a lawsuit filed late Tuesday and made public Thursday, Earth Island Institute and other conservation organizations said the Commerce Department's announcement ignored the government's own evidence that tuna fleets are harming dolphins by chasing and netting them.

"The administration's own scientists have shown that the fishing practice (newly reclassified as dolphin-safe) is a disaster for dolphins," said David Phillips of Earth Island Institute in San Francisco. "This is no more than a political gift to Mexico at the expense of dolphin lives."

The National Marine Fisheries Service ruled Tuesday that imported tuna caught with huge nets that encircle dolphins can be sold to Americans as "dolphin safe," as long as onboard observers certify that no dolphins were killed or injured.

The change will primarily affect tuna from Mexico, a major exporter that has been kept out of the U.S. market for more than a decade because its tuna boats drop nets on dolphins. Dolphins swim above schools of tuna in the eastern Pacific and were dying in the hundreds of thousands each year before U. S. conservation groups led a campaign to change fishing practices.

Fisheries Service director Bill Hogarth said he had determined that the netting does not have a "significant adverse impact" on the dolphin population.

"We've gone from 700,000 mortalities down to around 1,600 killed last year, " Hogarth said Thursday. With the observer program and other safeguards, including a requirement that each net contain an escape route for dolphins, "we can assure our American consumers that they still have dolphin-safe tuna," he said.

But the lawsuit said some depleted dolphin species are not recovering, and cited a report to Congress by Hogarth's agency in August that found that netting by tuna boats was the most likely cause. Onboard observers have underreported dolphin kills and have failed to account for deaths caused by the stress of repeated nettings and the separation of baby dolphins from their mothers, the suit said.

The battle over dolphins and tuna dates back more than 30 years. Congress prohibited U.S. fishing boats in 1972 from dropping nets on dolphins to trap tuna, imposed the same standard for foreign tuna imports in the 1980s and in 1990 prescribed the dolphin-safe label for tuna caught without netting dolphins.

Major U.S. brands sell only tuna that carries that label. Conservation groups said, and Hogarth confirmed, that brands including StarKist, Bumble Bee and Chicken of the Sea have promised not to buy tuna caught by netting dolphins, even if the U.S. government now considers it dolphin-safe.

The current dispute stems from a 1997 law that allowed the government to relax standards for dolphin-safe tuna based on evidence that a nation's fishing practices were not harming dolphins.

Relying on that law, President Bill Clinton's Commerce Department was prepared to certify Mexican tuna as dolphin-safe in April 2000, but was stopped by a court order obtained by the same conservation groups.

U.S. District Judge Thelton Henderson of San Francisco, who is likely to be assigned the latest lawsuit, ruled in 2000 that the government had dragged its feet on studies to determine whether dolphins were harmed by being repeatedly captured and released.

Hogarth said Thursday the studies have now been conducted but are inconclusive.
