Unfit to Serve: The National Rifle Association Shouldn’t Have the Power to Name Our Next Attorney General
Statement of Physicians for Social Responsibility
Executive Director and CEO Robert K. Musil, Ph.D
 
WASHINGTON - January 9 - George Bush continues to rub salt in the wounds of American voters who supported Al Gore, the winner of the popular vote for President. Remember soccer moms, moderation, the center, the suburbs? Those of us who opposed Governor Bush were aware of the National Rifle Association’s boast that if he won they would have an office in the White House. But no one could have imagined that the NRA’s cozy new home would be the office of the United States Attorney General.

Bush’s nominee for the office of the nation’s chief law enforcement official is John Ashcroft. The recently defeated Missouri Senator’s ultra-conservative credentials would lead someone hearing President-elect Bush’s ‘uniter not divider’ rhetoric to scratch their heads. Ashcroft’s single-minded and dogged fight to ensure that African American Judge Ronnie White not be confirmed as a District Court Judge, as well as his devotion to such causes as the segregationist Bob Jones University and the neo-Confederate Southern Partisan Magazine would give anyone pause. But his slavish devotion to the National Rifle Association — including recording ads for the NRA endorsing a doomed law that would have granted child molesters and stalkers the right to carry concealed weapons — is what particularly concerns Physicians for Social Responsibility and its 22,000 members nationwide.

Ashcroft’s commitment to ensuring freedom for weapons was a major factor in his departure from the Senate and underscores the growing tide of opinion against the NRA. He lost the election to the late Mel Carnahan despite receiving independent expenditures (issue advertising support) totaling nearly $340,000 from the NRA. Even worse, the radio spots Ashcroft personally recorded for the NRA were part of a $4 million blitz the pro-gun group assembled to ensure the right of almost every variety of felon to carry a concealed weapon. That piece of legislation would have represented the capstone of Ashcroft’s long Missouri career serving the National Rifle Association.

Ashcroft’s NRA legacy scars Missouri to this day. Ashcroft served as the state’s Attorney General and then Governor from 1976 to 1985. The result? Missouri now ranks 15th in the nation for the weakest gun control laws, observing only the few common-sense gun regulations which came to the state as Federal law. Women suffer particularly in Ashcroft’s armed Missouri — 50% of all female homicide victims die from firearms. And a well-armed Missouri has contributed to another grim statistic: the lion’s share of gun deaths in the state are suicides; a tribute, numerous health studies show, to the risks of having guns in the home.

As a Senator, Ashcroft continued to ignore the undeniable epidemic of gun injuries and deaths in America. Ignoring the nearly 800 gun deaths in his home state and over 30,000 gun deaths nationwide, Ashcroft cast vote after vote — against closing the gun show loophole, against punishing adults who make it easy for kids to get their hands on guns — in line with the NRA and the gun lobby.

Other citizens will rightly oppose the Ashcroft nomination because of his links to racial extremists, his disregard for Federal law protecting reproductive rights. But physicians also will not support an activist from the extremist NRA as America’s top cop. Mr. Bush, check your guns and the Ashcroft nomination at the White House door.

Robert K. Musil, Ph.D. is Executive Director and CEO of Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR), a national membership organization in Washington, DC. It shared the 1985 Nobel Prize for Peace with International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War and supports sensible gun laws to reduce firearms injuries and deaths.
