Making Sense of Defense: Two War Strategy Is...
 
WASHINGTON - April 23 - Recently there have been press reports that Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld's review of the military will call for an end to two war strategy, a strategy that calls for America's armed forces to fight and win two major theater wars simultaneously with little help from our allies. If Rumsfeld does call for canceling the two war scenario, this will be an important step towards redesigning America's armed forces to face the threats of the future. The following are some arguments against the two war strategy from the Council for a Livable World Education Fund's upcoming military spending briefing book.

Unnecessary. There has been no major war in the last century where the U.S. has fought without allies. And we have never fought two major wars at the same time. Even in World War II, the U.S. performed a holding action in the Pacific while pressing the war in Europe. Plus, the U.S. had massive allied support in that war. Thus, the two war scenario as envisioned by current strategy is an illusion.

Additionally, U.S. forces are vastly superior in quality, and usually quantity, to any potential enemy. Few countries then, if any, will seek to fight a traditional war against the U.S.

Dangerous. Few military experts believe that future conflicts will be large-scale land battles. Instead, the far more likely threats are terrorism, ethnic unrest, peacekeeping, or urban warfare. However, these are not the conflicts for which the Pentagon is prepared. The two-war strategy "hamstrings the Pentagon,"says Michael O'Hanlon of the Brookings Institution. "It hurts them in research and development and readiness." The National Defense Panel, a Congressionally mandated panel tasked with critiquing the Pentagon's Quadrennial Defense Review, warned that the strategy is "an inhibitor to reaching the capabilities we need." The Panel concluded that, "a significant share of the Defense Department's resources is focused on the unlikely contingency that two major wars will break out at once, putting greater risk on our long-term strategy."

Redundant. The Pentagon's minimum group of forces it believes will be necessary to win one theater war is 4-5 Army divisions (60,000-100,000 troops), 4-5 Marine brigades (12,000-25,000), 10 Air Force fighter wings (720 planes), 100 Air Force bombers, and 4-5 Navy carrier groups. However, this "building block" does not reflect allied support, nor allied military superiority over North Korea and Iraq. In the 1999 war in Yugoslavia, the U.S. did not even use ground troops, nor did we use 100 bombers or four carrier groups. Thus, the size of these building blocks are grossly overstated, even assuming that two major wars do break out. The U.S. could fight and win two wars with a smaller force structure.

In fact, the National Defense Panel's final report to Congress criticized the two-war strategy as "a force- protection mechanism—a means of justifying the current force structure." In other words, the two-war strategy is little more than a way to secure additional funding for the military.
