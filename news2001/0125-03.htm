Christmas In January:
Joint Chiefs Lobby for Extra $10 billion
 
WASHINGTON - January 25 - The Joint Chiefs of Staff got an early start to Christmas 2001 when they dispatched representatives to Capitol Hill on Tuesday to lobby the Senate Armed Services Committee for supplemental appropriations expected to total $10 billion. The move angered not only newly confirmed Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld, who immediately ordered a stop to such lobbying efforts, but also Appropriations Committee members in Congress who have jurisdiction over the mid-year spending bills.

The request for "emergency" funds would be added to the defense budget total for fiscal year 2001 which is already authorized at about $310 billion -- a $20 billion dollar increase from the previous year. The move came as a surprise to the Bush Administration, which has not figured out its budget plans.

"The Chiefs are end-running the new Administration," argued John Isaacs, President of the defense reform group Council for a Livable World, whose recently released report "No Increase Necessary" recommends limiting "rogue operations" run by the Chiefs -- frequently in collusion with key politicians.

The report advises "reigning in the Pentagon's Christmas list." In recent years, in response to request from key committees, the services have submitted "wish lists" directly to Congress without going through the normal budget processes within the Pentagon or the Office of Management and Budget.

"Only in the Pentagon could you ask for a $10 billion raise without first checking with your boss," said Isaacs.

The Council report also recommends addressing the financial and accounting problems that bedevil the Pentagon before giving the military new handouts. According to the Council, "accounting at DoD is horrific." The group cites a Pentagon Inspector General report that found $2.3 trillion in unsupported accounting entries and a General Accounting Office report that describes billions of dollars in missing items.

Accusing Pentagon planners of being "frozen in Cold War thinking," the report recommends canceling weapons such as the Crusader mobile howitzer, the F-22 tactical aircraft and the V-22 Osprey. Such cancellations would assist President Bush in following through on his campaign pledge to "skip a generation" of weapons now in development, according to the Council report.

Council also criticizes Congress for using defense spending bills as vehicles for adding billions in pork-barrel projects that waste taxpayer dollars. According to Sen. John McCain (R-AZ), the Senate's top pork buster, Congress added $7 billion in unrequested spending to the fiscal 2001 defense spending bill. The report points out that those funds would "easily pay for the readiness shortfalls" often cited by the Pentagon.
