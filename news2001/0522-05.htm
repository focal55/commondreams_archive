Rejection of Biological Weapons Convention Protocol Latest Sign of U.S. Unilateralism
 
WASHINGTON - May 22 - An internal Bush administration review is taking aim at a verification and monitoring protocol to the 1972 Biological Weapons Convention, which prohibits the development, production, stockpiling, or use of biological weapons. The review reportedly concludes that since verification of the treaty will be extremely difficult, the protocol will jeopardize U.S. secrets while doing nothing to stop cheaters from developing biological weapons.

While some of the review's concerns may be legitimate, its wholesale rejection of the protocol is the latest sign of a growing U.S. unilateralism. In its first few months in office, the Bush administration backed out of the Kyoto protocol on global warming, suspended missile negotiations with North Korea, cut funds for Energy Department nonproliferation programs that reduce the threat of Russian nuclear weapon and fissile material stockpiles, appointed a notorious arms control opponent to the top arms control post in the State Department, and signaled its readiness to abandon the 1972 Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty in order to deploy a costly and controversial national anti-missile system.

"Arms control agreements such as the protocol to the Biological Weapons Convention may not be perfect, but that doesn't mean we should abandon them," said John Isaacs, President of Council for a Livable World. "If they are flawed, then the U.S. should take the lead in strengthening and improving them. Instead, this administration seems determined to shrug off its international commitments," continued Isaacs.

The Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, which the U.S. Senate rejected in 1999, has also been criticized as unverifiable, and President Bush cited verification concerns in refusing to continue negotiations with North Korea on eliminating its development and export of ballistic missiles.

"The most effective nonproliferation tools have been multilateral agreements, quiet diplomacy, and threat reduction programs," added Council nonproliferation specialist Steve LaMontagne, "Unfortunately the Bush administration would rather just spend money on building more weapons, which will only exacerbate the weapons proliferation problem."

"This Administration is putting its head in the sand like an ostrich. Instead of proactively dealing with weapons proliferation, the Administration prefers to foolishly and dangerously hide behind more weapons, particularly its ineffective missile defense," added Isaacs.
