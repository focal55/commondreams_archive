Bush Promise to Help Families Must Include all Families, Including Low-Income Americans
 
WASHINGTON - February 7 - As President Bush continued to push his tax cut plan today with families he said would gain from it, the National Women's Law Center criticized the Administration's proposal for leaving low-income families out of its potential benefits. If there is to be a tax cut, any proposed "marriage penalty fix" should be extended to include low-income couples and the child tax credit and the child and dependent care tax credit should be made refundable. These measures would allow families who do not earn enough income to pay taxes to also benefit, said NWLC.

In responding to President Bush's comment that he wants to dismantle the "toll booth on the road to the middle class," Nancy Duff Campbell, NWLC Co-President, said, "Unfortunately this plan has ignored the many roadblocks that remain for low-income families, so many of whom are headed by women."

For example, Campbell praised Bush's approach to fixing the marriage penalty -- which currently causes some married couples to pay higher taxes than they would if they were single -- by allowing them to deduct from their taxable income up to $3,000 of the second earner's pay. By structuring the marriage penalty fix in this way, the plan targets middle-income couples with two earners. However, low-income couples that claim the Earned Income Tax Credit are hit with the most substantial marriage penalties, but get little or no relief under Bush's plan. "President Bush's second-earner deduction approach works for many middle-income couples, but fails to recognize that low-income couples are also penalized when they marry. It's only fair that they are included in the marriage penalty fix," said Campbell.

Campbell also expressed support for the President's move to increase the child tax credit from $500 to $1,000 per child, because it recognizes the greater expenses of families with children, but criticized the proposal for not making the child tax credit refundable so that low-income families, who are most in need of help in meeting these expenses, would benefit.

In addition, the Administration's proposal fails to recognize that one of the largest expenses for working families is child care. Yet, the child and dependent care tax credit, which helps families pay for these expenses, has not even been increased for inflation since 1981. For this reason, bipartisan legislation has been introduced in Congress to increase the credit and make it refundable so that the lowest income families can get help with their child care costs. "Real tax relief for families would address one of the largest expenses for families -- child care," said Campbell.

Campbell reiterated that the Administration's plan, as a whole, is unfairly skewed toward tax breaks for the wealthiest Americans. In a statement issued Tuesday, Campbell criticized the President's move to eliminate the estate tax because it would benefit only the top five percent of tax filers and do nothing for the overwhelming majority of women and their families who do not stand to gain from inherited wealth. In a statement issued Monday, Campbell said that the size of the proposed tax cut poses a serious threat to Social Security, Medicare and other safety net programs, including the need to make increased investments in areas critical to women and their families such as education, child care and health care.

Campbell has over 30 years' experience in tax law and policy, especially as it pertains to women and their families. She serves on the board of the Community Tax Law Report and chaired the national Coalition on Women and Taxes during the 1986 tax reform debates.

The National Women's Law Center is a non-partisan, non-profit organization that has been working since 1972 to advance and protect women's legal rights. NWLC focuses on major policy areas of importance to women and their families including economic security, education, employment and health, with special attention given to the concerns of low-income women.
