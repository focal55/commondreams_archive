It's Christmas in Taiwan
Taiwan made a list, Bush checked it twice
 
WASHINGTON - April 24 - President Bush today announced the sale of a comprehensive $4 billion arms package to Taiwan. Bush's first major arms deal perpetuates a U.S. policy that contributes to a dangerous arms race in one of the world's most sensitive regions. The annual ritual of the President of the United States reviewing Taiwan's wish list like Santa is the wrong approach to building a constructive relationship between Taiwan and China. President Bush should substitute this lazy approach to U.S. foreign policy in the region with a pro-active, long-term policy that cultivates reconciliation and stability.

Like Christmas, the annual review of Taiwan's arms request provides a measure of predictability to an unstable situation. Taiwan submits a weapons wish list, the administration reviews the request, Beijing howls with anger, Congress increases its rhetoric, and the President decides which goodies to put under Taiwan's tree. The U.S. feels compelled to respond to China's arms purchases from Russia by providing Taiwan with sophisticated weapons; this pushes China to enhance its military capabilities even further. Unfortunately, this arms race has no end in sight.

The over-arching goal of U.S. policy in the region must be to foster an atmosphere of reconciliation between China and Taiwan. The U.S. should negotiate with other weapons suppliers to establish technological and numerical limits on arms sales to the region. These limits must include a commitment not to sell any advanced power-projection weaponry and not to sell any weapons that further upset the balance of power in the region. Additionally, the U.S. must focus on those weapons already delivered to the region. The U.S. should strongly encourage efforts by both China and Taiwan to enter into negotiations to remove Chinese missiles in the Taiwan Straits in exchange for restricting the acquisition of anti-missile systems by Taiwan.

China's weapons purchases from 1992-1999 total over $20 billion. Taiwan has received arms equal to that value in the same time period. As a result, two adversaries armed to the teeth with modern military technology are facing each other separated by a mere 94 miles. Current U.S. policy, while predictable, does nothing to rectify this situation. Rather, it facilitates a dangerous arms race, which eventually could lead to disaster. Despite economic interests and political pressures, this risk should be enough of an impetus to replace our reactive policy with a pro- active approach to curb the build-up of sophisticated weapons and promote real stability. Until then, see you next year.
