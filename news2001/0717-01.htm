Faith-Based Organizations Representing Millions of Americans Join AIDS, Health Groups to Call for Debt Cancellation to Stop AIDS
 
GENOA, ITALY - July 17 - Today a transnational alliance modeled after Jubilee 2000, the Global AIDS Alliance (GAA), challenged the G7 governments meeting in Genoa, Italy this weekend to take real action to stop global AIDS. The club of seven wealthiest nations, meeting on the Italian Riviera, are expected to discuss the Global AIDS and Health Fund, the AIDS epidemic and the status of debt relief efforts.

"The fate of millions of lives in Africa and other areas rides on this meeting," commented David Bryden, spokesperson for the Global AIDS Alliance. "People in impoverished, indebted nations, struggling to contain the AIDS crisis, cannot afford more empty rhetoric. The leaders at the Summit will discuss the state of the industrialized nations' economies -- but what about the economic impact on countries in Africa of AIDS and the still ridiculous levels of debt payments to the IMF and the World Bank? That should be a primary focus, the fate of over a billion people in impoverished countries. But will they have time to give this serious attention as they sip their Italian espressos?"

On Saturday, July 21, the Global AIDS Alliance will take part in a peaceful and non-violent march and rally sponsored by Drop the Debt. GAA and many other AIDS organizations are calling for deeper and broader debt relief as a means of freeing up scarce resources to fight the epidemic. Over 30 major AIDS, health care, development and religious organizations, including the Christian Children's Fund, have written to President Bush appealing to him to use the Genoa meeting to mandate full cancellation of debts owed the IMF and World Bank by the poorest nations.

"In Genoa we'll see if the G7 leaders accept distortions by the World Bank and the IMF about their ability to drop the debt," stated Bryden. "As elected leaders they must exercise their responsibility and mandate these institutions to contribute their fair share."

The World Bank and IMF are the biggest remaining creditors to the poorest countries and independent accountants have confirmed they could write off 100 per cent of these debts without impeding their ability to function.

The GAA also challenged the G7 leaders to contribute billions more to fight the epidemic, to open the Global Fund design process to full, open participation, and to make sure recipients have the option of purchasing AIDS medicines from generic manufacturers.

"President Bush and Members of Congress from both parties support billions of dollars in emergency US funding for purchasing armaments, while blocking Congressional efforts to provide billions to fight AIDS" noted Bryden. "We hope the Genoa meeting will be a turning point where the US will match its rhetoric about AIDS with action that reflects our tremendous wealth. AIDS needs real money, not pennies."

The Global AIDS Alliance, founded earlier this year, is a broad, international coalition of religious, humanitarian, consumer and social justice groups. Founding members include African Services Committee, Physicians for Human Rights, AFRICARE, Jubilee USA Network, Health GAP Coalition, American Jewish Congress, the National Council of Churches and the National Black Leadership Roundtable.
