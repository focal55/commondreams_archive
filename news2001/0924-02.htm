Physicians Group Calls on President to Maintain Congressional Role in Arms Sales, Sanctions
 
WASHINGTON - September 24 - A Nobel Prize-winning physicians group today called on President Bush to not undermine years of human rights and arms control work by waiving Congressionally-mandated sanctions on rogue nations and nuclear proliferators.

"We at Physicians for Social Responsibility and our colleagues have worked to restrain America's role as the world's largest arms dealer for years," said PSR Executive Director and CEO Robert K. Musil, Ph.D M.P.H. "President Bush is attempting to cloak this dangerous move in the guise of a necessary anti-terrorism tactic. That simply isn't true."

PSR sees key flaws in the Administration's proposal. First, the very heart of our form of government, the system of checks and balances, would be voided. The White House could trade weapons for information with the least savory international characters and nations threatening all of the world with nascent nuclear weapons programs. It is the duty of Congress to authorize expenditures and set legal restrictions on U.S. trade. Their work would be muted if this proposal is accepted.

A second danger is the unknown. Our intelligence can not see into the future. Too many times, America's participation in the world commerce of war has revealed itself as a disastrous decision. Arming future revolutions, civil wars, and hostile invasive forces should not be America's strategy as we fight international terrorism.

"Our intelligence apparatus is the best in the world but still couldn't possibly know all the players in any White House-initiated arms deal," said Musil. "How would we know if we weren't arming a friend one day, and an enemy the next?"

The final, and most deadly flaw in the proposal would be the undoing of world non-proliferation. For 40 years, Physicians for Social Responsibility has advanced the world's slow march to what we see as the only final point: nuclear abolition. A vital tool in this struggle has been U.S. sanctions against nations that have demonstrated that they are building a nuclear capability. The U.S. government has already lifted sanctions on India and Pakistan, who gained entry into the nuclear club when they detonated weapons in May 1998.

"When there is no meaningful penalty for bringing the world one perilous step closer to a nuclear end, how can non-proliferation be accomplished?" said Musil. "The U.S. must protect our ability to rise above the terror and our current war-fever by protecting the very human rights -- and human lives -- that this proposal would compromise."

Physicians for Social Responsibility is the United States Affiliate of International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War. IPPNW received the Nobel Prize for Peace in 1985 for the work of the organization and its affiliates opposing global nuclear proliferation.
