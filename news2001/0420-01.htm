Senators James Inhofe, John Breaux
The Clean Air 'Villains Of The Month'
 
WASHINGTON - April 20 - The nonprofit Clean Air Trust today awarded its clean air "Villain of the Month" award for April to Senators James Inhofe (R-OK) and John Breaux (D-LA).

The two senators earned this dubious distinction by urging Vice President Dick Cheney to suspend and weaken a key enforcement tool in the Clean Air Act. This enforcement tool, known as "new source review," is designed to make sure that big polluters upgrade pollution controls when they make major changes to expand or extend the life of existing dirty factories or electric power plants.

Many companies apparently have been flagrantly disregarding the law. Virginia Electric Power Company (VEPCO), for example, rebuilt the boilers at several plants and also doubled the height of the smokestack at one plant to 730 feet to ensure that air pollution from the facility did not fall on nearby communities. The result is that much of the plant's emissions now drift hundreds of miles to downwind states such as Maryland and New York.

Finally, lawsuits were brought against power companies by several environmental groups, New York State Attorney General Eliot Spitzer, and the Clinton Administration's Justice Department and Environmental Protection Agency.

Because of these enforcement efforts, major power companies (including VEPCO), refineries and paper mills have all agreed to massive cleanups. As Spitzer told a Senate subcommittee earlier this month, "These lawsuits will have absolutely no detrimental effect on our energy supply."

But Inhofe and Breaux have seized on California's energy problems -- which, as EPA Administrator Christie Whitman has noted, were not caused by the Clean Air Act -- as an excuse to roll back clean air protections. "Our nation is currently facing a potential energy crisis similar to what is happening in California," Inhofe asserted in calling for a suspension of the clean air enforcement.

It is suspected that Cheney will urge some rollback in this enforcement effort as part of his upcoming energy plan. We note with alarm that the Bush Administration is proposing a nearly 10 percent cut in federal environmental enforcement. (Historical note: in ancient Rome, when the legions got rebellious, the leaders sometimes killed one soldier in 10 as a way to keep the rest in line. Thus the origin of the word "decimate" -- and perhaps the thinking behind the proposed enforcement cut.)

Fortunately, there is bipartisan opposition to the misguided Inhofe-Breaux effort to let big polluters off the hook. Senators John Kerry (D-MA) and Susan Collins (R-ME) told Cheney that they "strenuously object" to the "invitation to disrupt enforcement of the Clean Air Act." They noted "there is broad agreement, reflected in Congressional testimony and elsewhere, that air pollution laws are not causing electricity or fuel supply shortages."

Even so, those interested in clean air ought to be on the alert against the sort of dirty-air con job promoted by Inhofe and Breaux.
