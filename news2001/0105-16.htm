LCV Applauds Clinton’s New Forest Plan
Warns That Bush Administration Could Turn Back Clock on Resource Protection
 
WASHINGTON - January 5 - The League of Conservation Voters (LCV) today applauded President Clinton’s ban on new roadbuilding on 58.5 million acres of national forests in 38 states, including 9 million acres in Alaska’s Tongass National Forest.  The initiative, which Clinton signed today, will take effect in 60 days.

"Today’s announcement further demonstrates President Clinton’s dedication to leaving a livable legacy for future generations and to ensuring that important natural treasures like the Tongass Forest are protected as some of our last remaining pristine spaces," said LCV President Deb Callahan. 

According to the League, both Congress and the new Bush administration appear poised to overturn some of President Clinton’s many environmental initiatives.  While campaigning for president, Bush promised to reverse, if possible, President Clinton’s roadbuilding proposal, which at the time did not include the Tongass.  In addition, Vice President-elect Dick Cheney has vowed to review and possibly rescind some of President Clinton’s new national monument designations.  Representative James Hansen (R-Utah), who was named House Resources chairman yesterday, has already proposed working with the Bush administration to dismantle some of the Clinton administration’s most prominent natural resource conservation efforts.

Bush’s choices of former Michigan Senator Spencer Abraham for Energy secretary and former Colorado Attorney General Gale Norton for Interior secretary are also clear signs that increased resource extraction on public lands will be a priority for the Bush administration.  Both Abraham and Norton support oil drilling in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge.  Abraham, who was named to LCV’s 2000 Dirty Dozen list of anti-environment congressional candidates, had a failing lifetime environmental rating of five percent for his votes on key environmental legislation while in office.  Norton worked for the Mountain States Legal Foundation (MSLF), which at the time was headed by controversial Reagan Interior Secretary James Watt.  MSLF is a conservative organization that strongly supports "takings" legislation and logging and mining on the nation’s public lands.  As Colorado attorney general she was instrumental in creating the state’s "self audit" program, which allows companies to avoid penalties when they violate environmental laws if they report the violations themselves.  Environmentalists oppose this type of program, calling it a "get out of jail free" card for polluters. 

"While the Clinton administration in its final days continues to make forward-thinking progress on environmental protection, it appears that the Bush administration -- based on the nominations of Norton and Abraham to key environmental cabinet positions -- may be ready to turn back the clock on natural resource protection," Callahan said.  "The majority of Americans support stronger environmental protections. Clean air, clean water and our public lands need not be sacrificed in order to have successful energy and natural resource policies."

LCV is the political voice for the national environmental community. LCV’s Scorecard is published each session of Congress. Members of Congress are assigned a percentage score based on their votes on key environmental legislation. A 100 percent score indicates the strongest environmental commitment, while a zero percent shows a consistent voting pattern against conservation and public health protections. LCV is working full-time to monitor administration appointments and actions that affect the environment.  Information on LCV’s efforts pertaining to the administration will be made available to reporters and over the Internet at www.lcv.org.
