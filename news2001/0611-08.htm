President's Plan on Global Warming Still 10 Years Behind the Times
 
WASHINGTON - June 11 - Jennifer Morgan, director of World Wildlife Fund's climate change campaign, issued the following statement Monday in response to President Bush's latest remarks on global warming:

"President Bush's call for more research into climate change -- even as his energy plan would increase the carbon emissions that cause global warming -- is a grossly inadequate and irresponsible response to one of the most urgent environmental crises of our time.

"The industrialized world realized almost a decade ago that any solution to the looming problem of global warming would require binding targets for the reduction of carbon dioxide emissions. From that political and scientific consensus, the Kyoto Protocol was born. But while other nations have moved ahead, the United States has slipped behind.

"In calling for more research into what he belatedly concedes is a serious problem, President Bush is catching up to U.S. policy as it existed 10 years ago. The rest of the world has moved forward in that time; so must the U.S.

"Indeed, only last week, the National Academy of Sciences affirmed that the IPCC Third Assessment Report accurately reflects the current thinking of the scientific community. This report finds that we are already experiencing the initial impacts of global warming and that warming over the next century may be catastrophic for both human communities and biodiversity around the globe. Other countries have heard this wake up call and are moving to ratify the Kyoto Protocol and reduce emissions even as the Bush Administration is preparing to increase them.

"Just last month, the Administration introduced an energy plan that would increase emissions of global warming pollution by 35 percent or more over the next 20 years. While European countries are moving forward to develop the clean energy technologies of the future, the Bush Administration appears content to continue to rely on the dirty technologies of the past. If the Administration implements their energy plan and their climate change proposal, the US will dramatically increase emissions at the same time it is spending billions of dollars to study the problem its emissions are creating.

"We don't need a technology initiative to achieve our Kyoto targets, we need leadership. A 1998 WWF study showed that by implementing responsible energy policies using existing technologies, the U.S. could have achieved deeper reductions than required by the Kyoto Protocol, while increasing jobs by nearly 900,000, with a net benefit to the economy.

"It is misleading to continue to use developing country emissions as an excuse for inaction. With only four percent of the world's population, the U.S. is the world' leading emitter of greenhouse gases on both a national and per capita basis. While President Bush complains about the Kyoto targets unfairly impacting the U.S., some developing countries are actually taking action to reduce their emissions. Since 1995 China has reduced its emissions by 17 percent, while US emissions grew by about 7 percent.

"The Bush proposal is so far behind the current thinking on climate change that it is hard to imagine how the Administration will engage in climate change negotiations in Bonn, Germany, this July. If he is unwilling to lead, President Bush should step aside and allow the other nations of the world to implement the mandatory pollution reductions contained in the Kyoto Protocol."
