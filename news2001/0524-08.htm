Drivers Ask President Bush: Save Us Money on Gas by Raising Fuel Economy Standards
Sierra Club kicks off Memorial Day holiday with call for presidential action
 
WASHINGTON - May 24 - As Americans take to the highways this Memorial Day weekend, the Sierra Club is calling on President Bush to raise automobile fuel economy standards. With gas in much of America costing $2.00 a gallon, an average car owner would save $340 a year in gasoline costs and an average SUV owner $558 a year if their autos met a new auto fuel economy standard of 40 mpg.

"Fuel economy standards were set back in 1975, when today's technology was just an engineer's dream," Sierra Club Executive Director Carl Pope noted. "The beginning of summer driving season is the perfect time for President Bush to show he is serious about saving oil and saving consumers money at gas pump."

Raising fuel economy standards would save Americans money: * A car at the current Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standard of 27.5 mpg car uses $872 of gasoline each year at $2.00 per gallon. If the same car got 45 mpg it would save $340 per year. (Based upon the average driving distance of 12,000 miles a year.) * The driver of an SUV like the Ford Explorer only gets 19 mpg SUV and spends $1263 for fuel a year at $2.00 per gal. If the same SUV got 34 mpg the driver would save $558. (Based upon the average driving distance of 12,000 miles a year.)

Higher fuel economy standards will also slash the amount of carbon pollution emitted from cars and light trucks, which contributes to global warming. The average car emits 6 tons of carbon dioxide pollution every year, but it would emit less than 4 tons if standards were raised. A more efficient SUV would emit 4 tons less global warming pollution on average than today's SUVs.

"The biggest single step we can take to curb global warming is to make cars and light trucks go further on a gallon of gas," continued Pope.

President Bush's Energy Plan recognizes that fuel economy has fallen to a 20-year low, yet calls for a study, not action. With today's technologies more efficient engines, transmissions, hybrid gasoline-electric systems, and better aerodynamics the automakers could make cars and light trucks that go further on a gallon of gas. Congress set the current 27.5 mpg fuel economy standard for cars in 1975 and has not changed the standard since.

All savings indicated represent a 40 miles per gallon standard for cars, SUVs and light trucks. Automakers would meet this standard by building car fleets averaging 45 miles per gallon and light truck fleets averaging 34 miles per gallon.
