California Study Shows Energy Crisis Driven by Campaign Contributions from Energy Industry
 
WASHINGTON - April 18 - Common Cause released an assessment of a Capitol Research Group report yesterday which links the California energy crisis to campaign contributions from the utility industry and energy providers. It is widely recognized that energy industry money had a profound impact on shaping the failed deregulation legislation of 1996.

“California has some of the most lenient campaign finance laws in the country, leaving big industry an easy opportunity to buy influence from lawmakers,” said National Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger. “This is a call to speak up and fight for campaign finance laws on the state-level and to press your U.S. Representatives as the fight for McCain-Feingold moves to the House with the Shays-Meehan bill.”

“Consumers have cause to question whose interest will be in the minds of lawmakers as they craft a legislative response to the crisis – the public interest or the special interests of well-financed energy industry players,” said California Common Cause Executive Director Jim Knox.

The report finds that utility companies, power generators and marketers, contributed over $600,000 to Governor Davis during the last election cycle. They gave over $3.3 million to state lawmakers, $820,514 to other statewide officers and $339,900 to political parties. In the past two years alone, energy special interests spent $16 million on political contributions and lobbying expenses.

In Washington, the fight for effective campaign finance reform continues. The McCain-Feingold bill passed in the U.S. Senate 2 weeks ago and awaits debate in the House.

Utilities lobbied hard during crisis; Legislators received millions, study shows By Rick DelVecchio, Chronicle Staff Writer

Electric utilities blanketed California politicians with campaign and lobbying money at a time when the state was rolling downhill toward the current power crisis, Common Cause said yesterday.

The government watchdog group detailed $11.6 million in political spending by the state's major utilities in the 1999-2000 legislative and election cycle and characterized the record as a case study in the ability of powerful interests to influence policy makers through money.

"The energy crisis story in California raises a national issue, not only because of the challenge of meeting the crisis itself but because it's a classic example of the influence, or the potential influence, of money in politics," Common Cause President Scott Harshbarger said yesterday.

Pacific Gas & Electric Co., Edison International, which owns Southern California Edison, and Sempra, the holding company for San Diego Gas & Electric, dished out $4.2 million in campaign contributions to state elected officials and $1.5 million in contributions to local officials during the 1999-2000 period. They also spent $5.9 million on lobbying at the state level.

Gov. Gray Davis was the top recipient of campaign contributions from utilities, with $348,000, followed by Senate President Pro Tem John Burton, D-San Francisco, with $172,000, and Assembly Speaker Robert Hertzberg, D-Los Angeles, with $165,000.

'NO INFLUENCE'

Davis denied that the contributions had any influence on his decisions during the energy crisis, saying he had stopped taking money from any involved groups in October.

"That was well before the crisis developed," Davis said yesterday. "I ceased taking any money from them. I cancelled some events that some of the folks were going to have for me in December."

Burton staunchly believes the utilities have no impact on his decisions on energy policy. "If you take a look at who's leading the fight on behalf of the ratepayers, it's been me," he said yesterday. "I'm happy. Whether (the utilities) are happy, I can't tell you."

All but one of the 118 state legislators holding office or elected in the period received campaign contributions from the utilities. The lone holdout was Assemblywoman Gloria Negrete McLeod, D-Chino.

"As the whole energy crisis evolved last summer, she made a conscious decision it was something she wanted to stay away from," said Don Wilcox, McLeod's chief of staff. "As things were borne out, it was a wise decision."

Wilcox said he did not believe that energy industry money had influenced energy policy. "If money bought influence, the energy crisis would have been bailed out in the very beginning," he said.

RETURN TO SENDER

Hertzberg, as the crisis broke open late last year, refused further contributions from the energy industry. He also returned industry contributions made to him during the last three months of last year, said spokesman Paul Hefner.

"The support he receives from a wide variety of folks plays no role whatsoever in the policy positions he takes," Hefner said.

At the end of last year, financially struggling PG&E temporarily suspended its political contributions in order to conserve cash. Spokesman Ron Low said there was nothing questionable about the firm's past political activities.

"We contribute to candidates who we believe are most qualified to serve the public," he said.

But Common Cause's Harshbarger believes those dollars swayed policy decisions in favor of the utilities. "The special interests who pay to play get not only major benefits from their contributions to the leading elected officials in terms of defining the market, but also in terms of who was given priority to resolve the crisis financially," he said. "The consistent loser was the average person."
