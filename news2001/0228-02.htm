Sierra Club Reaction to President Bush's Address to Congress
 
WASHINGTON - February 28 - The Sierra Club today noted that while President George W. Bush's address to Congress did mention environmental issues, it included proposals that could have serious impacts on environmental protection.

"President Bush's address to Congress last night shed little additional light on his administration's environmental policies," said Sierra Club Executive Director Carl Pope. "While on the face of it, President Bush declares a desire to protect the environment, it is the details of his plans that will truly show whether this administration will preserve our natural treasures or exploit resources for the short term benefit of industry."

"When it comes to President Bush's environmental policy, the devil will be in the details," continued Pope.

Bush continued to push an energy policy which he has said will include drilling for oil in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge. Bush's continued emphasis on increased production over conservation is fundamentally flawed because there is too little supply of oil in the U.S. to affect the energy situation and the environmental cost is far too high. Bush's plan would do nothing to solve America's energy problems but would increase global warming pollution and destroy one of the last unique and unspoiled natural places left on the earth. True energy solutions set America on the path to a sustainable, clean, and affordable energy future. We must increase efficiency, develop renewable energy, and replace older power plants with more efficient, less polluting power plants.

While Bush's tax cut plan does not explicitly mention the environment, the overly large and fundamentally unfair cut could have serious implications for environmental protection in this country. Financing the President's tax plan could trade vital funds for preserving clean air, clean water, and special places in exchange for a tax benefit for the wealthiest Americans.

While the Sierra Club was pleased that President Bush fulfilled his campaign promise to fully fund the Land and Water Conservation Fund and pledged $4.9 billion for the maintenance of National Parks, the real test of his commitment to environmental protection will be whether he funds the full range of environmental programs that protect our clean water, clean air, and unique, natural places.

"If Bush truly wanted to protect the environment and save money, he could cut below cost timber sales, subsidies to the oil industry, and pork barrel Army Corps of Engineers projects," said Pope. "These are the kind of details on which we will judge the Bush budget."
