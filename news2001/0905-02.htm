Brady Campaign, Brady Center Submit Comments on Proposed Change to Instant Criminal Background Check System
 
WASHINGTON - September 5 - The nation's leading gun violence prevention organizations submitted comments yesterday to the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ), opposing Attorney General John Ashcroft's proposal to reduce the amount of time that National Instant Criminal System (NICS) background check records are kept from 90 days to one day. On July 6, Ashcroft proposed to destroy these critical records just one business day after a gun is sold, despite law enforcement officials saying they need to retain the records for at least 90 days to ensure the integrity of the background check system. The 60-day public comment period on the rule closed yesterday.

"As the saying goes, 'If it ain't broke, don't fix it,'" said Michael Barnes, president of the Brady Campaign and the Brady Center to Prevent Gun Violence. "Since the Brady Law went into effect in 1994, background checks have stopped more than 680,000 felons, fugitives and other prohibited purchasers from buying guns from licensed dealers. The NICS is a key component of the Brady Law and shortening the time that background check records are kept will seriously undermine law enforcement's efforts to keep guns out of the wrong hands."

In its six pages of comments, the Brady Campaign and the Brady Center made several arguments against shortening the record-keeping period. They argued that the change will:

  • Undermine the core mission of the Brady Law and the NICS -- preventing prohibited purchasers from obtaining firearms;
  • Cripple the FBI's ability to perform routine checks that ensure the accuracy and integrity of the NICS;
  • Hamper the FBI's ability to identify instances in which the NICS is used for unauthorized purposes, such as gun dealers running background checks on people other than actual gun buyers, and protect against the invasions of privacy that would result from such misuse;
  • Hinder the FBI's ability to determine whether potential handgun purchasers have stolen the identity of innocent and unsuspecting individuals or otherwise submitted false identification information in order to thwart the system.

In its comments, the Brady Campaign/Center also points out that the DOJ has already concluded that 90 days was the "shortest practicable period of time for retaining records of allowed transfers that would permit the performance of basic security audits of the NICS," after a lengthy and comprehensive rule-making procedure that began in March 1999. The 90-day rule went into effect on July 3 of this year, yet just three days later, Attorney General Ashcroft proposed changing it once again.

"It defies common sense for the Attorney General to now suggest that a dramatic shortening of the record retention period -- from 90 days to one -- suddenly becomes possible and desirable," said Barnes. "Mr. Ashcroft's desire to change a rule that has already been thoroughly vetted can only be seen as more of his pandering to the gun lobby."

"This is not simply a procedural change -- there are lives at stake," he continued. "The main beneficiaries of the proposed rule will be criminals and corrupt firearms dealers seeking to subvert federal gun laws. Furthermore, the proposed rule will jeopardize the privacy rights of all Americans -- by allowing corrupt dealers to conduct background checks of whomever they choose and by enabling criminals to engage in identity theft, both without fear of being detected."

To see the full text of the comments submitted to the Department of Justice, visit the Brady Campaign's Web site at www.bradycampaign.org.
