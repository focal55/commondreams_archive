Bush Decision on Roadless Areas Another Step Backwards
 
WASHINGTON - May 4 - The following is a statement by Kathryn S. Fuller, president of World Wildlife Fund-U.S., on today's decision by the Bush Administration to re-open the rule-making process on the Roadless Area Conservation Rule:

"Today's decision by the Bush Administration to reopen the rule-making process on the Roadless Area Conservation Rule, and thus rehash the past decade's rancorous debates over national forest policy, is a disappointing step backward.

"While ostensibly leaving in place the policy that protects the last intact 30 percent of our national forests, the Administration plans to amend the rule to provide logging and oil and gas companies more influence at the local level in the management of roadless areas in national forests. This signals a return to the days before the roadless policy was promulgated, when local officials, extractive industries and environmentalists battled each other on every national forest, paralyzing the U.S. Forest Service, interfering with the orderly management of natural resources, and, more often than not, opening up more and more pristine forests to the axe and the bulldozer.

"The Roadless Area Conservation Rule is a carefully wrought compromise. It provides protection for some intact forests, limits (but does not eliminate) road building, logging and oil and gas exploration within them, and leaves over half the national forest land in the country open to roading and extractive industry. The rule was the result of ten years of study and dialogue, 600 public meetings across the country, and benefited from the input of 1.6 million Americans, the greatest number of comments ever received by the Federal government in response to a proposed rule. The comments were over 95 percent in favor. Today's decision to reevaluate roadless area protection on each national forest sweeps all that aside, disregarding years of hard work and the concerns of millions of Americans.

"Roadless areas in our national forests provide refuge for wildlife, reservoirs for plant life, and protect the freshwater supplies of local communities. They are vital resources that must be protected. On behalf of WWF's 1.2 million members in the U.S., I urge President Bush to reconsider this decision and implement the Roadless Area Conservation Rule as it now stands."
