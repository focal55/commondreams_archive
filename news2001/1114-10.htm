Launch of New Trade Round Demonstrates Why 'Fast Track' is the Wrong Track
 
WASHINGTON - November 14 - Defenders of Wildlife today sharply criticized the Bush administration's decision to support a new round of trade negotiations within the World Trade Organization, warning that the agenda for the negotiations promises to exacerbate the conflict between environmental protection and global trade rules.

The WTO ministers, meeting in Qatar, declared their conviction that international trade and environmental protection "can and must be mutually supportive." But according to Carroll Muffett, Defenders' director of international programs, the agenda for the new round reflects a different reality.

"What the WTO gives with one hand, it takes away with another," said Muffett. "It agrees to negotiate on environmentally harmful subsidies, like the huge subsidies that drive over-fishing. But it puts environmentally beneficial support programs on the table at the same time. While U.S. agricultural subsidies are major drivers of environmental degradation, these negotiations could also affect agricultural conservation programs, like the Conservation Reserve Program, that do exactly the opposite."

Far more serious, according to Defenders, is the WTO's decision to negotiate global investment rules like those that have plagued the NAFTA parties.

"The NAFTA parties have been trying to contain the damage from Chapter 11, which allows foreign investors to sue governments for expropriation any time they face a new environmental rule or lose a court case. The WTO could have avoided this mistake, but decided instead to repeat it on a global scale. We think that's a bad idea."

Defenders warned that the administration's decision to support this agenda sends a clear message to Congress about the risks of granting the president "fast-track" trade negotiating authority.

"The administration has put everything from environmental protection to anti-dumping laws on the table at the WTO," said Muffett. "Fast Track would give trade negotiators free reign to bargain away these laws with no input from Congress. If policies of such critical national importance need to be amended, it should be Congress that amends them."

Defenders of Wildlife is a leading nonprofit conservation organization recognized as one of North America's most progressive advocates for wildlife and its habitat, and played a leading role in efforts to reintroduce wolves into Yellowstone National Park. With more than 480,000 members and supporters, Defenders of Wildlife is an effective leader on environmental issues.
