Illegal EPA Action Jeopardizes Public Health
EPA Exempts Factory Farms in Central Valley From Clean Air Requirements
 
SAN FRANCISCO - December 3 - The Bush Administration today bowed to pressure from giant factory farmers and turned its back on California's breathing public. Rather than requiring the largest California farms to play by the same rules as every other industry, the Environmental Protection Agency ("EPA") allowed the state to continue exempting all farming operations from clean air permitting requirements. This means that air pollution from the Central Valley's largest farms - from such sources as giant diesel irrigation pumps - will continue to spew, unmonitored and without permits into the Valley's air. Because of the influence industrial agriculture has on the Administration, they will continue to enjoy immunity from clean air requirements, while identical pollution sources on neighboring oil fields (or any other industry) have to obtain permits which limit the amount of pollution they can emit.

Medical professionals who treat thousands of Valley residents suffering from pollution-related respiratory problems were shocked. Dr. David Pepper of the Medical Alliance for Healthy Air said, "This is an outrage. Our patients are suffering. The EPA should to come to my clinic, visit with an asthmatic child, and then explain to the parents why they are letting air quality worsen and favoring giant factory farms over the health of children."

Earthjustice lawyer, Bruce Nilles, vowed to investigate all legal options to overturn this decision. "The Administration is playing special interest politics, putting the health of California residents at great risk. This is patently illegal. We are consulting with medical professionals and community groups about the appropriate legal response," said Nilles.

Federal law provides that EPA's decision may be appealed in the Federal Court of Appeals within sixty days. Earthjustice is currently representing a coalition of medical, community and environmental groups in another lawsuit against EPA for its multiple failures to take action to improve air quality in the San Joaquin Valley.

BACKGROUND ON TITLE V

* Today EPA granted final approval to the Title V operating permits program for all 35 California Air Districts despite a state law exemption under state law for "any equipment used in agricultural operations in the growing of crops or the raising of fowl or animals" Cal. Health & Safety Code §42310(e).

* EPA is action is illegal. The Act explicitly states that "the Administrator may not exempt any major source from such [permitting] requirements." 42 U.S.C. § 7661a(a). This clear requirement admits no exception and EPA's notice fails to provide a legal justification for creating one.

* For the past five years EPA had consistently maintained the position that the State of California must remove its statutory exemption for agriculture before EPA can grant full approval to the State's Title V program. See, e.g., 59 Fed. Reg. 60939, 60942-43 (Nov. 29, 1994) ("In order for this program to receive full approval (and avoid a disapproval upon the expiration of this interim approval), the California Legislature must revise the Health and Safety Code to eliminate the exemption of agricultural sources from the requirement to obtain a permit.")

* In a March 2001 legal opinion about the adequacy of the state's permitting program, Attorney General Bill Lockyer agreed that the agricultural exemption is inconsistent with Title V.

[I]t is my opinion that the laws of California provide adequate legal authority to carry out all aspects of the [Title V permit operating] program. . . with one exception... The one exception to the state's authority to issue permits to all air pollution sources concerns 'equipment used in agricultural operations in the growing of crops or the raising of fowl or animals.' Section 42310(e) of the HSC [Health and Safety Code] currently exempts these potential Title V sources from the permit requirement. In the event the legislature remedies this provision to eliminate the exemption for agricultural equipment that emits sufficient quantities of air contaminant to be a Title V source, we will amend this opinion accordingly.

Excerpts from letter by California Attorney General Lockyer to U.S. EPA Administrator Whitman, March 21, 2001:

* Title V of the Clean Air Act requires "major sources" of air pollution to obtain a permit that details all the environmental protections that must be installed on a pollution source, as well as the monitoring obligations necessary to ensure the facility is in compliance with applicable air quality standards. The permitting process also provides an opportunity for public review and comment before a permit is issued.

Implications for San Joaquin Valley

1. Under the San Joaquin Valley's new "severe" ozone nonattainment status and its current "serious" PM-10 nonattainment status (the worst possible PM-10 status), a "major" source required to obtain a permit is defined as any source (or combination of sources located in a contiguous area) emitting more than (1) 25 tons per year of NOx or VOCs; (2) 70 tons per year of PM-10 or PM-10 precursors; (3) 10 tons per year of a hazardous air pollutant; or (4) more than 25 tons per year of any combination of identified hazardous air pollutants".

2. Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) and Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) combine to form Ozone (smog) pollution. Two months ago the San Joaquin Air District ranked the top 25 sources of NOx and VOCs for the Valley in 1999. Farm equipment topped the list as the largest source of NOx emissions, even higher than heavy duty diesel trucks. Agricultural sources of VOCs ranked second (livestock waste), fourth (pesticides/fertilizers) and fifth (agricultural and prescribed burning).

3. In a 1996 report, the San Joaquin Valley Air District determined there were approximately 2,830 diesel irrigation engines in the Valley, that the average engine was 161 horsepower, and each engine was operated for approximately 2,298 hours over a 12-month period, for a total of 369,978 horsepower hours. With a NOx emission rate of approximately 10 grams of NOx emitted per horsepower hour, each of these 161 horsepower diesel engines each emitted more than four tons of NOx annually. The report also concluded that the largest diesel engines (450 horsepower) were operated for approximately 8,280 hours each year and thus emitted 41 tons of NOx in a twelve-month period. Thus, individual diesel irrigation engines or clusters of such engines located in a contiguous area emit substantially more than 25 tons of NOx per year. Without the agricultural exemption, operators of these engines would be required to obtain a Title V permit. In any other industry, these same engines are currently required to obtain such a permit.
