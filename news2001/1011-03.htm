Americans Rally to Protect Nation's Treasured Places
Sierra Club Volunteers Go Door to Door to Protect National Forests and Local Gems
 
SAN FRANCISCO - October 11 - From coast to coast, this weekend thousands of Sierra Club volunteers will rally their neighbors to protect America's National Forests and local treasures closer to home. In 40 cities volunteers will hand out 400,000 postcards, talk to neighbors door-to-door, speak at festivals and lead community tours of threatened parks and steams to celebrate and protect America's beautiful lands. Sierra Club Chapters are urging local officials to protect threatened areas that include:

· In Oregon, the Tillamook and Clatsop state forests, which are endangered by increased logging; · In Michigan, the Great Lakes, which are polluted by animal waste from factory farms; · In South Dakota, the Missouri River, which is shrinking due to dam building, wetlands destruction and alteration of natural water flows.

In addition to reaching out personally to their neighbors, Sierra Club chapters are running radio ads in 20 cities, calling attention to the threats facing their local gems.

"Americans face a choice ? to safeguard our natural heritage or lose it forever," said Sierra Club President Jennifer Ferenstein. "Our nation has the opportunity to protect the places our communities love. The Sierra Club stands ready to offer real solutions that will help people protect America's natural treasures."

Sierra Club volunteers are also urging public officials to protect our last wild, unspoiled National Forest areas.

"We are calling on our public officials to protect the last fragments of pristine National Forest. These forests provide essential sources of clean drinking water, wildlife habitat, and special places to hike, hunt camp and fish. Our last wild forests are national treasures, and are important places to rejuvenate and draw inspiration," Ferenstein said. "Local communities overwhelmingly recognize the value of these forests and our officials should act to protect America's environment for our families, for our future."

The popular wild forest protection policy, which would protect nearly 60 million pristine acres of National Forests from logging, mining and other destruction, was finalized by the U.S. Forest Service in January after the largest public involvement process in the history of federal rulemakings. To date, the Forest Service has received more than 2 million comments calling for full protection of our last wild forests. The Forest Service is considering weakening the rule and opening the door to logging and roadbuilding in our last wild forests.
