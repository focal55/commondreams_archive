V-22 Osprey: Technical Flaws Should Call for Cancellation, not Delay
 
WASHINGTON - April 18 - In response to a Pentagon panel report recommending further delays in the Marine's V-22 Osprey program, Council for a Livable World today called for the program's cancellation.

The panel, which consisted of retired military officers and former defense contractor employees, has found the program, as panel member Normal Augustine said, "not ready today . . . not close to it."

"Vice President Dick Cheney was right a decade ago when he tried to kill the Osprey," said John Isaacs, the Council's president.

The cost of an Osprey has ballooned to $83 million a copy and the tilt rotor technology has proved difficult to master. A November 30, 2000 report from Philip Coyle, then the Pentagon's Director of Operational Test and Evaluation, found that the Osprey will be more difficult to maintain than its predecessors, the CH-46E and the CH-53D helicopters.

"After spending $12 billion and more than fourteen years trying to perfect the technology, losing 23 lives in the past year and a half and admitting falsified maintenance records," continued Isaacs, "the Osprey's numerous technical problems show that nothing short of a complete redesign of the aircraft, which is prohibitively expensive, will fix these problems. The Osprey should be killed."

"The Pentagon should not throw good money -- and more brave airmen's lives -- after bad," concluded Isaacs.
