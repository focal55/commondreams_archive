NCRC Calls Upon Federal Banking Agencies to Strengthen America's Communities by Strengthening the Community Reinvestment Act
 
WASHINGTON - October 17 - The National Community Reinvestment Coalition (NCRC) joins with Representatives Thomas Barrett, Luis Gutierrez, and other key members of Congress in urging the federal banking agencies to shore up America's communities by updating the Community Reinvestment Act (CRA).

John Taylor, President and CEO of NCRC, declares, "As our nation faces uncertain economic conditions following September 11, the federal banking agencies will provide a shot in the arm for our communities if they update the Community Reinvestment Act (CRA)."

Passed in 1977, CRA mandates that banks and thrifts meet the credit needs of all the communities in which they are chartered and from which they take deposits. Federal officials examine banks and issue them CRA ratings based upon their number of loans, investments, and services offered to residents of low- and moderate-income communities. The federal banking agencies are now in the process of asking for public comments on whether the CRA regulations and examination procedures should be changed.

Taylor continues, "CRA has leveraged a tremendous increase in home mortgage lending for America's working class and minority communities during the 1990s. Families realizing the American dream of homeownership now face the threat of layoffs or wage freezes. Down on their luck, these families are most vulnerable to the slick promises of predatory lenders. A stronger CRA is needed to protect our communities as well as resume the progress in lending and investing."

In 1990, the federal banking agencies made CRA evaluations and ratings public. The agencies also improved the CRA regulations in 1995 by basing a bank's rating on its performance, instead of its process, for making loans, investments, and services.

As a result, the Department of Treasury documents that home mortgage lending to low- and moderate-income communities is higher in areas in which banks undergo CRA exams and in areas in which they have CRA agreements and partnerships with community groups. In 1990, banks made 18 percent of their home mortgage loans to low- and moderate-income borrowers. By 2000, banks made 29 percent of their mortgage loans to these borrowers. In CRA agreements with community groups, banks have pledged more than $1 trillion dollars in loans and investments for working class and minority communities, according to NCRC's calculations.

Now, however, the storm clouds of economic uncertainty and predatory lending threaten the gains in reinvestment. On a national level, the Department of Housing and Urban Development found that African-American neighborhoods are five times more likely to receive subprime refinance loans than white neighborhoods. Neighborhood residents face fewer choices in loan products and become more vulnerable to predatory lenders when subprime lenders dominate lending in their community.

"Archaic CRA examination procedures are making neighborhoods vulnerable to predatory lending. Banks are becoming national in scope and lending far beyond their branches. But the CRA regulations confine CRA exams to assessing performance to only areas where banks locate their branches. The Treasury study concludes that bank prime lending is higher in areas where they undergo CRA exams. In order for CRA to continue leveraging more prime lending, it must be updated to examine banks in areas where they make the great majority of their loans, not just areas where their branches are located," exclaims NCRC's Taylor.

Another method by which CRA can increase prime lender presence is by explicitly examining lending to minority communities as well as to low- and moderate-income communities. The CRA statute mandates that banks must be responsive to credit needs in all of their communities, including minority ones. Furthermore, since the current CRA regulation awards banks for being innovative and flexible in response to credit needs, NCRC believes that banks should earn more points for prime lending than higher interest subprime lending on their CRA exams.

Taylor explains, "Subprime refinance and home equity lenders are responding to pent up demand in minority communities that is not being met by traditional lenders. There is no better way to protect minority communities in these uncertain times than by encouraging CRA-covered institutions to take market share away from loosely regulated subprime lenders."

All of the recommendations in NCRC's detailed comment letter (available via NCRC's website of http://www.ncrc.org) can be implemented by the federal banking agencies under authority granted to them by the CRA statute. In addition, NCRC reiterates our strong support for the CRA Modernization Act, H.R. 865, co-sponsored by Representatives Barrett, Gutierrez, and 34 other members of Congress. The CRA Modernization Act would update CRA in several critical ways such as extending CRA to independent mortgage companies, insurance firms, and securities companies. H.R. 865 would also mandate enhancements to data disclosure that the Federal Reserve has proposed but not yet acted upon. These are improving HMDA (Home Mortgage Disclosure Act) to include information on interest rates and loan terms and conditions, and enhancing CRA small business data to include the race and gender of the small business owner.

Taylor concludes, "Twice as many community groups as banks have sent in comment letters to the federal agencies and have made their voices heard regarding the need for a strong CRA. The relatively low number of bank comments suggest that they do not mind CRA that much, and could be persuaded to support changes to CRA that help them compete against subprime lenders. NCRC stands proudly today with key members of Congress who also believe that the regulatory agencies must act to strengthen CRA so as to strengthen our communities."

The National Community Reinvestment Coalition is a national not-for-profit with over 800 membership organizations with constituents in every state in the country. The Coalition has spearheaded a proactive community reinvestment movement with the goal of ending discriminatory banking practices and increasing the flow of private capital and credit into traditionally underserved communities.
