Questions Challenge Mojave Water Grab: Company Seeks to Profit by Selling Controversial Water
 
LOS ANGELES - January 17 - The following was released today by the National Parks Conservation Association, the Sierra Club and the Western Environmental Law Center:

Metropolitan Water District (MWD) met Tuesday to discuss its financial agreement with Cadiz, Inc., regarding its proposal to sell water from a groundwater aquifer that underlies Mojave National Preserve and several federally protected wildernesses. Public interest organizations decry the deal, saying it would be disastrous for southern California water users and could destroy extraordinary public lands in the California desert.

Cadiz, Inc., a private agricultural corporation based in Santa Monica, proposes to pump out native groundwater from an aquifer and pump in water from the Colorado River for additional withdrawal.

Project critics protest the apparent haste to close a deal between the agency and corporation. Last week, the MWD directors were given only a day to review and vote on the drafted agreement. Protesting directors asked that the vote be delayed until Feb. 13. Tuesday's meeting gave directors a chance to express their concerns about the project.

"Metropolitan Water District must act on behalf of the best interests of its customers in Los Angeles and San Diego. They have a public responsibility to take the time needed to better understand the commitment they are being asked to make, instead of acting like this is a shotgun wedding," reflects Elden Hughes of the Sierra Club.

Cadiz, Inc. grows fruit in the California desert and has suffered financial declines over the past two years. The potential water project has propped up the faith of their investors and creditors. "Cadiz already has an 84 percent debt to 16 percent equity ratio. This is not a sound business partner. The MWD shouldn't allow itself or its customers to be pressured into this deal. The MWD is responsible for providing customers with a safe, affordable and reliable water supply, not corporate bail-out," states Simeon Herskovits, senior attorney at the Western Environmental Law Center. (Further information on Cadiz, Inc.'s financial status can be read at http://www.axelsabyss.com/cadiz10q.html)

"We can't stand by and watch the MWD ignore this opportunity to carefully consider its alternatives, including more efficient use of currently available water and facilities. If the MWD signs this agreement, how are they going to explain to the public who fought for the California Desert Protection Act to protect these precious and extraordinary lands that depend upon this aquifer?" asks Steven Krefting, Pacific regional director of the National Parks Conservation Association.

Controversy has beset the Cadiz project for the past year on a number of fronts: criticism of the inflated estimates of available water, alarm over potentially carcinogenic chromium 6 in the water supply, questions about the company's legal rights to the water, concern that draining the aquifer will devastate federally protected desert lands and cause massive dust storms, and the threat of environmental litigation to stop the project.

The coalition of environmental organizations that wrote comments critical of the project include Sierra Club, National Parks Conservation Association, Defenders of Wildlife, Wilderness Society, California Wilderness Coalition, Desert Survivors, Desert Citizens Against Pollution, California Citizens Against Toxics, Southern California Watershed Alliance, California Watershed Network, Butte Environmental Council, Valley Water Protection Association, Center for Biological Diversity, Center for Community Action and Environmental Justice, and Citizens for the Chuckwalla Valley. The coalition's comments can found at http://www.axelsabyss.com/comments2.html

The Metropolitan Water District is expected to vote on the agreement on Feb. 13, 2001 at the Metropolitan Water District Headquarters in Los Angeles.
