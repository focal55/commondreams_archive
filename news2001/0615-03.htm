Bush Administration Gives Final OK for Sale of F-16s to Chile
Congress Formally Notified of $714 Million Jet Deal
 
WASHINGTON - June 15 - Wednesday, the Bush administration notified Congress of the sale of 10 advanced F-16 fighter jets and two KC-135 mid-air refueling tankers to the government of Chile. The notification represents the practical end to a ban on sophisticated weaponry to Latin America since the Carter administration. It's official, the Bush administration has opened the store, and all South American countries with the cash are invited to The Arms Bazaar of the Americas!

The Pentagon's news release makes several false claims. What follows are excerpts from the release and our rebuttal.

"This proposed sale will contribute to the foreign policy and national security of the United States by helping to improve the security of a friendly country . . ."

In fact, the sale will work against the foreign policy and national security aims of the United States, by decreasing Chile's security. While Chile may have more sophisticated weaponry in its arsenal, the F-16 buy will spur other countries in the region to purchase similar weapons.

". . . which has been and will continue to be an important force for political stability and economic progress in South America."

Chile's political and economic development will not be helped in any way by the $714 million purchase of F-16s. Rather, resources devoted to military modernization will reduce funding for pressing needs identified by the heads of state at the most recent Summit of the Americas - "access to education, poverty alleviation, strengthening human rights and democracy and economic integration."

"The proposed sale of this equipment and support will not affect the basic military balance in the region."

This sale will fundamentally alter the basic military balance in the region. No other country in the region can defend against Chile's proposed F-16s, which have advanced, power projection capability. Chile's neighbors will be forced to match Santiago's purchase of jets that can strike deep and accurately into their territory.

The United States' first priority should be to contribute to the political, social and economic development of the countries in South America. This sale does exactly the opposite, reinforcing a militaristic approach to U.S. foreign policy in a region where it is not needed, and is potentially dangerous.
