Popular Hybrid Cars Top New Fuel-Economy List
Gas-Guzzling SUVs Scrape Bottom as Americans Want to Use Less Oil
 
WASHINGTON - October 9 - Two hybrid cars top the new Model Year 2002 Fuel Economy Guide, with a fleet of gas-guzzling light-trucks bringing up the rear. The government's annual report, released today, catalogues the parking-lot full of hyper-guzzling SUVs automakers are building, with two super-efficient hybrid cars leading a new direction for American drivers.

"The winners for 2002 are the gasoline-electric hybrids -- the 64 mpg Honda Insight and the 48 mpg Toyota Prius, which top the chart," said Ann Mesnikoff, Washington Representative of Sierra Club's Global Warming and Energy Program. "The big losers are the monstrous SUVs, like the Ford Expedition, Lincoln Navigator and Cadillac Escalade which guzzle 14 mpg or less."

"Americans have long wanted to reduce our dependence on oil," Mesnikoff continued. "We know that automakers have the capability to stop making gas-guzzlers and can give Americans what they want: cars and SUV's that go further on a gallon of gas. It's time they started. Today's SUV's will be on the road for 10 years or more, driving up our need for oil with every mile."

Published by the U.S. Department of Energy and the Environmental Protection Agency, the report provides consumers with fuel economy information on every 2002 car and light truck. If recent trends continue, Americans can expect nearly half of all vehicles sold in 2002 to be SUVs and other light trucks. The fuel economy of 2001 vehicles continued a downward spiral, plummeting to the lowest point since 1980, according to the 2001 Light Duty Automotive Technology and Fuel Economy Trends Report, issued by the EPA last week.

"This report provides consumers with information on individual cars and trucks, but when you add it up we get increasing demand for oil," Mesnikoff added. "We are dismayed that in 2002 the auto industry is offering consumers light trucks with an average fuel economy virtually the same as those sold in 1980. Gas guzzling costs drivers money at the pump, drives up global warming pollution and keeps America dependent on oil. The auto industry should put technology to work so Americans can reduce our oil dependence, spend less at the pump and curb global warming."
