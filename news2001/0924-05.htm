Blanket Weapons Export Exemption Goes Too Far
U.S. Must its Maintain Principles in Fight Against Those Who Undermine Them
 
WASHINGTON- September 24 - Today, the Washington Post reported that the Bush administration has asked Congress to lift all restrictions on U.S. weapons exports and military assistance through 2007. This short- sighted and dangerous proposal goes beyond providing assistance to those countries on the front lines of the new fight against terrorism, and gives the President free reign to discard laws and policies carefully crafted over the last fifty years to keep weapons out of the hands of governments and terrorists who may threaten U.S. national security. Congress should not write the President a blank check and abdicate its responsibility to regulate the export U.S. military weapons and supplies.

History has taught the United States that the indiscriminate export of U.S. weaponry has resulted in a decrease in U.S. security. At one time, Iran and Iraq, countries currently on the U.S. state-sponsored terrorism list, were close allies and major U.S. arms clients. U.S.-made Stinger anti-aircraft weapons that it gave to the Afghan resistance in the 1980s may also pose a threat to U.S. military personnel in Afghanistan. The administration should not repeat past mistakes by indiscriminately exporting weapons for short term goals.

The Bush administration's proposal to lift all restrictions on U.S. military aid, weapons and training is reckless in its scope. The proposed legislation reads: "The President may provide assistance or take any other action, sell or authorize the export of defense articles or defense services, or issue credit, credit guarantees or extend other financial assistance ... if to do so is important to United States efforts to respond to, deter or prevent acts of international terrorism or other actions threatening international peace and security." [emphasis added]

Instead of creating a blanket weapons export authority for the President, Congress should make specific changes to U.S. law where it hinders the President's ability to provide allies with the equipment or assistance they need to address the terrorist threat. U.S. weapons exports should continue to support the underlying values of U.S. foreign policy - democracy, free market economies and respect for human rights.

The events of September 11 demonstrate that the U.S. must take steps to protect the country and bring to justice those responsible for the attacks. Lifting all restrictions on U.S. arms exports to any country willing to provide rhetorical support for the "war against terrorism" may provide human rights violators engaged in internal or regional disputes the equipment to imprison, torture or kill their own people. In an effort to fight the terrorist threat, the U.S. should not adopt policies which will lead to an erosion of the very principles of freedom, democracy and a respect for civilian life that are traditional American values. As the President has said, the tragic events of September 11 have changed our world, but they have not undermined our determination to uphold our basic principals.
