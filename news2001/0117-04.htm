Clinton Administration Attempting to Gut 1992 Water Law
 

WASHINGTON - January 17 - In the 11th hour of the Clinton Administration, Department of Interior (DOI) officials are attempting to gut a landmark California water reform law, guaranteeing that taxpayers will continue to subsidize water for agriculture interests for the next 25 years at bargain basement prices.

Before they leave office, Administration officials are poised to sign the first 50 of 200 water contracts that will guarantee that agricultural interests in the Central Valley will continue to receive cheap, federally subsidized water - making it virtually impossible to pay back the billions they owe the federal taxpayers. The first 50 water contracts are being subsidized to the tune of $290 million by the federal taxpayer, according to an analysis by Taxpayers for Common Sense, a national budget watchdog organization.

"The failure of the Clinton Administration to fully implement these pricing reforms will mean hundreds of millions of taxpayer dollars will be wasted," said Jonathan Randall, coordinator of the California Water Project at Taxpayers for Common Sense (TCS).

The Central Valley Project Improvement Act (CVPIA), which has been hailed by many as "the most sweeping reform of California water policy in a half century," was signed into law days before the 1992 elections. Senator Bill Bradley, D-NJ, and Rep. George Miller, D-Martinez, co-sponsors of the bill, consider this one of their greatest achievements.

The fundamental taxpayer protections of the CVPIA were water pricing reform and shorter-term contracts. These provisions were intended to reduce the federal subsidy, encourage water conservation, and allow for more frequent public review to address California's changing water needs. As the proposed contracts are currently written, however, water pricing reforms will be ineffective, and the contracts will all but guarantee an additional 25-year extension, effectively making them 50-year contracts.

In a recent letter to Interior Secretary Bruce Babbitt, nine taxpayer and environmental advocacy groups argued, "the policy that was intended to encourage the wise use of water and/or raise funds for environmental remediation will be rendered all but meaningless in the proposed round of contracts."

"In recognition of the constantly evolving needs of California's diverse set of water users, it seems unwise to simply guarantee contract renewal without clear accountability on the part of the water contractor, a rigorous public review process, and thorough analysis of California's water needs in the future," the activist letter continued.

The Central Valley Project (CVP) is the largest federal water project in the United States, delivering enough water to supply every household in the state of New York for a year. Ninety percent of the water from CVP's 20 dams and reservoirs, 11 power plants, and 500 miles of major canals and aqueducts is allocated to agriculture. The CVP has cost federal taxpayers over $3.6 billion dollars since the late 1930's. Water users have paid back less than14% of that sum.

"By continuing to receive dirt cheap water contacts, it is virtually impossible for water users to pay back what they owe taxpayers," continued Randall, "They get cheap water, and taxpayers continue to foot the bill."

The first 50 water contracts will also set a terrible precedent for the more than 150 contracts left to be renewed. If the remaining contractors receive the same kind of deal, taxpayers will be liable for about $1.5 billion in interest subsidies alone, the TCS analysis found.

Without raising the water rates charged to users, the federal government will be unable to recoup the $2 billion in outstanding debt still owed to the federal government by the 2030 deadline.

"These contract renewals represent an opportunity for meaningful and lasting water reform. However, as it stands, we may just lock ourselves into 50 more years of wasteful water use and mismanagement," said Randall.

Taxpayers for Common Sense (TCS) is a non-partisan voice for American taxpayers. TCS is dedicated to cutting wasteful spending and subsidies in order to achieve a responsible and efficient government that lives within its means.
