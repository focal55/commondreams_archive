Bush Budget Plan Turns Its Back On A Sensible Energy Policy
 
WASHINGTON - April 9 - Today, President George W. Bush unveiled his first budget, signaling his administration's intent to turn its back on decades of progress in energy policy. Bush's Fiscal Year 2002 budget slashed the Department of Energy's ("DOE") energy efficiency research programs budget by 30 percent and renewable energy programs by 40 percent. Some programs were eliminated altogether. This is at a time when the country's energy situation demands a fuller commitment to technologies that can be brought online quickly, produce new energy for the electrical grid without emitting greenhouse gases, provide a safe energy supply, improve national security by decreasing the country's reliance on foreign oil imports, and help stabilize the economy by creating jobs.

"To say we are disappointed by this budget is an understatement," said Susanna Drayne, coordinator of the Sustainable Energy Coalition. "With soaring energy prices and an energy crisis that is likely to affect a broader segment of the country this spring and summer, the Bush budget is patently irresponsible. A comprehensive and balanced approach to addressing the nation's energy crises should include incentives to stimulate energy efficiency improvements as well as incentives to facilitate expansion of renewable energy."

In addition to the deep cuts in energy efficiency and renewable energy research and development, the Department's budget request both maintains and increases funding for several expensive and unproductive research and development programs, such as "clean coal" and nuclear power, that support the fully mature nuclear and coal industries. The budget also contains an increase of over $1.5 billion for the design and development of new nuclear weapons. The new Administration's appointees obviously feel that new kinds of nuclear weapons are more important than lower electric bills, fewer energy imports, and a cleaner environment.

DOE research, development and deployment programs conceive and nurture new clean energy technologies from the laboratory to useful application in the marketplace, which is a critical need right now. Consumers and businesses are facing heating bills two or three times higher than those last winter. California is facing an electricity reliability crisis that could cripple the state economy and spread throughout the west, with states competing with each other for scarce electricity. Power plants, transmissions lines, and natural gas pipelines are stretched to their limit in many states. And U.S. oil imports surged to around $120 billion last year. The Administration clearly should be requesting additional funds for energy efficiency and renewable energy at this time, not requesting cuts.

"These draconian cuts will have significant consequences for consumers," Drayne said. "For example, energy efficiency and renewable energy activities that currently save consumers more than $30 billion a year will perish. Research and development will decrease for super-efficient cars, appliances, heating and cooling systems, windows, and lighting products. And efforts to improve and make renewable energy sources such as solar energy, geothermal generation, and wind power more affordable will be severely limited."

The Bush administration's focus on building new power plants and drilling for more oil will not solve the nation's energy problems alone. "Both building and drilling take years until they yield an energy supply," Drayne noted. "Both also contribute significantly to the degradation of the environment. Given the nation's current immediate energy needs and worsening global warming, now is not the time to cut funding for energy efficiency and renewable energy programs. Nor is it the time to cut consumer and community options on how they can immediately address the rolling brownouts and electricity price spikes that are creating budget hardships."

The Sustainable Energy Coalition brings together more than 30 national business, environmental, consumer, and energy policy organizations. Founded in 1992, the Coalition promotes increased federal support for energy efficiency and renewable energy technologies and reduced federal support for unsafe or polluting energy resources. Coalition members advocate federal energy policies that will lead to a cleaner environment, safe reliable energy technologies, and a secure, prosperous future for all Americans.
