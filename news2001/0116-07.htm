Universal Health Care, Health Disparities, Resident Work Hour Reform Top Med Student Legislative Agenda For 2001
 
RESTON, VIRGINIA - January 16 - Today's most pressing health care crises will top the 2001 legislative agenda of the American Medical Student Association (AMSA), according to the organization's president Sindhu Srinivas, MD. Medical students will challenge legislators on Capitol Hill to address universal health care, health disparities and medical errors committed by overtired physicians. Founded in 1950, AMSA (http://www.amsa.org) is the nation's largest, independent medical student organization with more than 30,000 members.

Universal health care will be the focus of numerous legislative vehicles in early 2001, and AMSA will work to educate members of Congress and its own physicians-in-training on the dire need for radical and immediate reform of the health care delivery system in this country.

"It is unconscionable that 45 million Americans struggle without health insurance, while it is criminal that 25 cents of every dollar spent of health in this country is wasted in administrative overhead," Srinivas said. "Years have been wasted fighting for minor reforms in managed care plans while studies have proven that for-profit managed care has failed. Organizations across the political spectrum propose conflicting solutions to the problems-solutions designed to support their constituencies, yet are doomed to failure."

AMSA believes immediate changes must be made and the organization will support congressional legislation sponsored by Sen. Paul Wellstone (D-Minn.), and Rep. John Tierney (D-Mass.) and Jim McDermott (D-Wash.). AMSA maintains that McDermott's H.R. 1200, the American Health Security Act, contains the most comprehensive universal health plan under consideration. AMSA has prepared a comparison chart outlining several major proposals for universal health care and has made that chart available at http://www.amsa.org/lad/uhcinitiative.html.

The pervasive disparity between the health care of the underprivileged and minorities and wealthier Americans has become a top issue for AMSA. AMSA's legislative and programming efforts will continue to support congressional action to close the gap, including economic development in depressed inner cities and increasing the number of scholarships awarded by the National Health Service Corps.

"We don't think there's a silver bullet to defeat health disparities in this country," said Srinivas. "But removing a toxic waste dump from an inner-city community and improving the ratio of doctors to patients in rural areas are concrete steps that must be taken." AMSA has prepared additional resources on this issue, online at http://www.amsa.org/div/. AMSA's national convention scheduled for late March in Anaheim, Calif., will feature keynote speakers, plenary discussions and workshops designed to address how medical students can work toward the goal of zero health disparities. Learn more about AMSA's 51st Annual Convention online at http://www.amsa.org/news/conv/convention.htm.

A reformation of the resident-physician work hour system is expected to see movement in the new Congress. Residents-new doctors completing their training in hospitals-are routinely forced to work 36-hour shifts and 100-hour workweeks. Studies have shown that persons awake for more than 24 hours perform as if legally drunk, and AMSA believes that fatigue caused by such long hours logically leads to errors committed on patients. A 1999 Institute of Medicine report showed that as many as 100,000 Americans die each year in hospitals due to preventable errors. AMSA hopes to work with congressional supporters to introduce legislation to require hospitals to curtail resident work-hours or face stiff penalties. AMSA's resources on this issue can be found at http://www.amsa.org/lad/reswork.html.

Hundreds of medical students participated in a variety of 'lobby days' last year on these issues of critical importance, including a major rally last March on the steps of the U.S. Capitol, in conjunction with a celebration of the organization's 50th anniversary. AMSA's full-time legislative affairs director, a medical student taking a year away from training, directs the efforts of the independent, wholly student-run organization's legislative advocacy efforts in Washington. In addition to the organization's work in Washington, the organization regularly undertakes national letter-writing campaigns and local activities such as candlelight vigils to raise public awareness on these issues and others.
