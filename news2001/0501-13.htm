Nation's Scientists Urge Bush Administration To Uphold Critical Forest Protection Rule
 
WASHINGTON - May 1 - Nearly 300 of the nation's most pre-eminent scientists joined World Wildlife Fund and other leading conservation groups today in calling on the Bush Administration to support and implement the Roadless Area Conservation Rule.

In a letter sent to the President, the scientists warned of incalculable damage to nature from the "continuing loss and fragmentation of the nation's forests." America's remaining undeveloped national forests play a key role in helping nature to "withstand the effects of global climate change and...as refugia for sensitive wildlife," the letter signed by 291 biologists, ecologists and geologists said.

Following more than a decade of study and more than 600 public hearings over a two-year period, President Clinton promulgated the Roadless Area Conservation Rule to protect the nation's remaining intact forests before he left office last January. During the exhaustive public debate, more than 1.6 million public comments were received -- the overwhelming majority of them in favor of the new rule. Nevertheless, the Bush Administration, siding with the mining, logging and oil industries, has indicated it wants to delay the rule and scrap most of it.

"The roadless rule reflects good science and good policy and was crafted with record-breaking public participation," said Kathryn Fuller, president of World Wildlife Fund. "We have called upon President Bush to heed the millions of Americans who want intact national forests to remain refuges for wildlife."

The letter sent to President Bush represents the scientific consensus of some of the nation's best known biologists, ecologists and conservationists including Gordon Orians of the University of Washington, John Terborgh of Duke University, Stuart L. Pimm of Columbia University and George M. Woodwell, director of the Woods Hole Research Center.

"Time and again, the Bush Administration has claimed that its stewardship of America's natural resources will be based on good science," noted WWF Vice President William M. Eichbaum. "Today nearly three hundred leading scientists sent the President a clear message: the roadless area policy is good for the health America's forests, wildlife, and people."

More than two-thirds of America's original forest cover has already been wiped out and all but two percent of what remains is spider-webbed with 380,000 miles of roads -- enough to wrap around the Earth 15 times.

The relatively few roadless areas that remain provide crucial refuge to reclusive and threatened wildlife such as bears, wolverines, lynx, martens and salmon. They also play an indispensable role in the function of watersheds and aquatic ecosystems, reducing flood threats and providing millions of Americans with clean sources of drinking water.

"The biological, economic, social and recreational benefits of roadless areas are enormous," Fuller said. "We will all pay a dear price -- and our children and grandchildren the greatest -- unless we act now to protect what little intact forest we have left."
