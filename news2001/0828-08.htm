U.S. Civil and Human Rights Leaders Attending World Conference Against Racism Strongly Urge Bush Administration to Participate
 

DURBAN, South Africa - August 28 - A diverse group of U.S. civil and human rights leaders attending the World Conference Against Racism (WCAR) in Durban, South Africa issued the following statement today regarding the United States government's participation at the WCAR set to begin Friday.

"The WCAR represents a historic opportunity for hundreds of nations around the world to acknowledge, discuss and combat issues of racism, racial discrimination, xenophobia, and related intolerance. The United States government should participate in the global dialogue taking place in Durban and should do so at a level that reflects its responsibilities as a world leader.

The core principles upon which our nation was founded -- equality of opportunity, freedom of expression, and individual liberty -- are the same principles that the WCAR participants are working to make a reality for all people around the world.

The U.S. should join the discussion to celebrate the progress we have made and share the lessons we have learned, while at the same time, using this historic opportunity, to renew our commitment to further advance equality and justice at home and throughout the world.

No nation comes to this conference with entirely clean hands: racism and intolerance are global issues that affect every nation. We strongly believe that no one country should be singled out for special contempt, nor exempt from a review of its civil and human rights practices.

In attending the WCAR, nations agree to participate in a full and open debate, one that acknowledges the historic effects of racism, xenophobia, and intolerance while simultaneously working to develop strategies to eradicate these evils in the future.

As a vigorous defender of free speech, it would be ironic for the U.S. to refuse the opportunity to present its views in the marketplace of ideas that the WCAR provides. To the extent that it disagrees with the views of other nations, the U.S. should engage the WCAR to help shape the Conference's Declaration and Plan of Action.

The WCAR will be tackling a wide array of significant domestic and international issues that are important to all Americans and others around the world. Educational quality, economic and employment opportunity, access to health care, criminal justice, voting rights, migration and refugees, discrimination against women, environmental justice, persons with disabilities, and sexual orientation are among the many issues that will be discussed. If the U.S. fails to participate in the conference at a meaningful level, the Bush Administration would be sending a message that the U.S. has further relinquished its place as a world leader on civil and human rights."
