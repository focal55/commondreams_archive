Value of State Dept. Arms Export Licenses Doubles in Two Years
U.S. is a Major Supplier of Small Arms to Latin America
 
WASHINGTON - September 5 - According to a new government report obtained by the Arms Trade Oversight Project, licenses for weapons, spare parts and arms manufacturing assistance totaled $55 billion in fiscal year 2000. This is a 100 percent increase over licenses granted in 1998. Combined with the Pentagon's $12.1 billion in foreign military sales in 2000, the U.S. authorized more than $67 billion in arms sales last year. This surge in license authorizations to over 140 countries solidifies the U.S. as the world's dominant arms merchant.

The Project's analysis of the report (www.clw.org/atop/655analysis.html) shows that the U.S. government approved licenses worth tens of millions of dollars for small arms and related materials to nearly every country in Latin America. In 2000, thirty-one of thirty-eight countries in Latin America received some small arms related export licenses from the United States. Licenses for weapons to Latin America included: 53,099 pistols and revolvers valued at over $53 million, nearly 20,000 non-military rifles valued at over $8 million, and over 195 million small caliber ammunition cartridges valued at over $18 million. These numbers show why the U.S. doggedly fought efforts to restrain the trade in small arms and light weapons at the recent conference UN conference on small arms.

The State Department also granted licenses to countries in Latin America that it cited in its previous human rights reports for human rights violations. State granted licenses for 2,372 pistols and revolvers for the Dominican Republic, 7,775 pistols and revolvers for El Salvador, and 3,006 non-military rifles for Guatemala. Notably, Costa Rica, which has no military, may receive over 24 million small caliber ammunition cartridges, 5,987 pistols and revolvers and 2,247 non-military rifles. Evidently over half of Costa Rica's 8,400 paramilitary forces will have a new side arm next year.

Remarkably, the U.S. granted licenses worth over $30.3 billion for manufacturing and technical assistance in 2000, a figure that exceeds the value of all licenses only two years before. According to the report, the rise in manufacturing and technical assistance agreements between the U.S. and foreign countries reflects "the growing complexity of commercial defense trade as international joint ventures, co-production, licensed manufacturing and offset arrangements involving offshore assembly or procurement increasingly characterize major sales." In other words, the U.S. government is rapidly providing foreign countries the materials and expertise necessary to build their own weapons. Over the long term, this will lead to a proliferation of weapons and manufacturing capabilities, leaving the U.S. with less control over its weapons and manufacturing expertise.

The figures are part of the annual "655 report," the primary public disclosure of annual U.S. arms export licenses granted by the State Department. However, these figures do not represent the total amount of weapons sold or delivered through the State Department's arms export system, since not all licenses result in signed contracts or deliveries.
