Environmental Rollback Would Open Taxpayers to $1 Billion Liability from Mining Pollution, New Report Says
 
WASHINGTON - April 26 - A new report finds that American taxpayers will be liable for between $250 million and $1.1 billion in potential clean up costs at active U.S. metals mines if the Bush Administration rolls back current mining regulations. The finding does not include the estimated $32-72 billion price tag to clean up already abandoned mines. The report, Passing the Buck, released today by the Mineral Policy Center and Taxpayers for Common Sense, uses federal and state government data and bonding formulas to determine the liability to taxpayers.

"A Bush reversal would force taxpayers to write a blank check for mining cleanups," said Jill Lancelot, Legislative Director of Taxpayers for Common Sense. "This is corporate welfare at its worst."

Companies are required to post bonds with the federal government before mining can begin on public lands. These bonds cover clean up costs if a company lacks the money to do it themselves. Passing the Buck illustrates how the weak bonding requirements in old regulations that the Bush administration is actively seeking to reinstate allow companies to pass their cleanup costs onto taxpayers.

"If the Bush Administration rolls back these hard fought improvements to mining regulations, they are saying that taxpayers, rather than mining companies, should pay for cleanup costs," continued Lancelot.

On March 23, the Department of the Interior announced that it wanted to revoke current rules governing mining on public lands, the product of an extensive four-year rulemaking process that ended November 21, 2000. The current rules require companies to post adequate financial bonds for cleanup before mining commences, closing the loophole that had allowed mining companies to paying for environmental protection and remediation at mines.

"Mining companies routinely go bankrupt just as they extract the last ounce of ore from their mines leaving little or no money for clean up," said Stephen D'Esposito, President of Mineral Policy Center. "Bonding is the most important protection taxpayers have against being left holding the bag when a mining company goes under and leaves behind a mountains of toxic mining waste that can cost hundreds of millions of dollars to clean up."

Copies of Passing the Buck are available online at www.taxpayer.net and www.mineralpolicy.org
