Missile Test Just One Piece of The Puzzle
 
WASHINGTON - July 13 - Tomorrow's test of the Pentagon's national missile defense system is only the fourth of 21 scheduled tests. Single test results provide only modest indications about the future effectiveness of missile defense.

"This missile defense test should be treated as one of a long series of evaluations to determine if the system works; it should not be overblown," said Dudley Herschbach, a Nobel laureate and professor of science at Harvard University who serves as the chair of the Council for a Livable World Education Fund. "Tomorrow's results do not mean the U.S. can build an effective defense. We are a very long way from deploying even a white elephant system," he said.

Herschbach added, "This project has nothing to do with genuine defense, but rather with defending a political stance."

"If you look at the Administration's overall strategy, the test almost seems irrelevant," said Jerome Grossman, chairman of the Council for a Livable World. "The Administration is pushing ahead furiously to deploy a missile defense system whether it works or not — despite questions about cost, the technology, the nature of the threat, and the possibility of violating the ABM Treaty. It appears determined to proceed at any and all costs."

There is much skepticism about missile defense in advance of this latest test. The system now being developed has flunked two of the three tests in which the performance of major components was evaluated.

The limitations of the missile defense testing program have been noted by Pentagon officials. Philip Coyle, formerly director of Operational Testing and Evaluation for the Pentagon, said the tests lack "operational realism...While a successful intercept during any future flight test will be a significant achievement," it should be viewed in the context of built in "limitations."

Among those limitations, Coyle said, is the reduction in the number of decoys used in testing from nine to one. The use of decoys is intended to make it difficult for the missile defense system sensors to identify the actual war head. Critics have accused the Pentagon of carefully scripting the tests to increase the chances for a successful intercept.

Coyle has pointed out that determining the effectiveness of a missile defense system in real world conditions will take years, and deployment at least a decade.
