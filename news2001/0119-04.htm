Arrested Development:
Troubled Osprey Should be Canceled
 
WASHINGTON - January 19 - After suffering two tragic accidents last year, the MarineCorps' vaunted MV-22 Osprey program has again come under fire. Federal investigators raided the North Carolina headquarters of the only Osprey squadron yesterday relieving the commander. Lt. Col. Odin F. Leberman has been charged with falsifying maintenance records in an effort to aid the troubled program as it moved toward full-scale production.

"It will be difficult for the Osprey to recover from this," said John Isaacs, President of the Council for a Livable World, a group that opposes production of the V-22, "The program has always been of questionable military value, but its strong congressional backing made it the Teflon weapon — no criticism could stick. But now that political cover is coming off."

During his tenure as Defense Secretary, Dick Cheney attempted to kill the Osprey, a troop transporter that takes off like a helicopter and flies like a plane, but the program was saved by congressional supporters. The cost of an Osprey has ballooned to $83 million a copy and much of technology is still not ready. A November 30, 2000 report from Philip Coyle, the Pentagon's Director of Operational Test and Evaluation, claimed the Osprey will be more difficult to maintain than its predecessors, the CH-46E and the CH-53D helicopters, and an August 16, 2000 Inspector General report described 22 "major deficiencies" within the program. Moreover, despite the Osprey's high speed transport capabilities it cannot carry heavy equipment. Consequently, troops delivered by the Osprey will often have to wait for heavy equipment to arrive from the traditional helicopters.

"This is a wake up call," claimed Isaacs, "These allegations of fraud illustrate the severity of the Osprey's many technical problems and the Pentagon's flawed procurement process. Clearly, the Osprey should be canceled, but other programs need to be questioned as well."

Coincidentally, the General Accounting Office released a report on Wednesday accusing the Pentagon of using "high risk acquisition strategies such as acquiring weapons based on optimistic assumptions about the maturity and availability of enabling technologies." One example cited by the GAO is the Army's plan to begin producing the Comanche helicopter before starting initial operational testing.

"This is not just about the Osprey," added Isaacs, "The Pentagon is planning to spend billions on unneeded, unproven, Cold War era weapons such as the F-22, the New Attack Submarine, and the Crusader howitzer."
