Amnesty International Urges Bush Administration To Use State Department's Human Rights Report In Developing Foreign Policy
 
WASHINGTON - February 26 - Amnesty International USA (AIUSA) today welcomed the U.S. State Department's comprehensive report on human rights documenting the status of human rights around the world. The human rights organization urged the Bush Administration to use this information in the formulation of foreign policy.

"Information is important, but action on human rights affects people's lives," said William F. Schulz, AIUSA executive director. "A critical test for the Bush Administration is whether this information on human rights violations will be considered in foreign policy decisions."

Earlier in the day, Amnesty International USA released Stopping the Torture Trade, a new report documenting that U.S.-manufactured products become weapons of torture in many of the same countries outlined in the State Department report. Amnesty International's report found that U.S. companies were more involved than any other country in the manufacture, marketing and export of the type of weapons and other equipment used in torture.

"It is unconscionable that while the U.S. State Department promotes human rights, the U.S. Department of Commerce has approved export licenses to countries that our own government documents as committing torture," Schulz said.

In response to the State Department report, AIUSA provided comment on countries in which human rights practices have been of particular concern:

China: The State Department assessment that the human rights situation in China has "worsened" mirrors Amnesty International's own report, Torture: A Growing Scourge in China -- Time for Action, released earlier this month. Amnesty International's report found that a growing range of officials, from judges to family planning officers, are committing torture in China and that their circle of victims is expanding.

"The decision of the Bush Administration to sponsor a resolution censuring China due to human rights violations is a strong first step in setting the tone for U.S.-China relations," said Schulz. "But given China's deteriorating human rights record, the U.S. must continue to demand that the Chinese government improve its record on human rights."

Colombia: Given the documented links between the Colombian Army and other security forces with the paramilitaries, AIUSA again questioned the U.S. commitment to a huge financial aid package to Colombia. AIUSA is particularly concerned that reports by a Colombian government official documenting connections between paramilitary groups and Colombian military brigades in Putumayo are not included in the State Department report.

Israel and the Occupied Territories: AIUSA noted an increased candor from previous years in the State Department report on Israel and Occupied Territories, but that there still exists a marked difference in tone and tenor with how similar violations are addressed in sections on other countries. For instance, the State Department frequently reports Israeli government claims, without independent verification. Amnesty International reiterated its recommendation that the U.S. government conduct investigations into whether the continuing flow of military hardware to Israel violates U.S. law governing arms transfers. That call was included in a recent Amnesty International report documenting Israel's targeted killing of Palestinians.

Russia: AIUSA urged that U.S. policy toward Russia take into account the continuing and well-documented human rights violations in Chechnya.

Sierra Leone: Given that the State Department documents the murderous rampage by the Revolutionary United Front in Sierra Leone, AIUSA urged the Bush Administration to support the "Clean Diamonds" legislation proposed by Representatives Tony Hall (D-Ohio), Tom Wolf (R-Va.) and Cynthia McKinney (D-Ga.).

For copies of Stopping the Torture Trade, Torture: A Growing Scourge in China -- Time for Action, or the Israel Human Rights Report visit the AIUSA Web site at http://www.amnestyusa.org.
