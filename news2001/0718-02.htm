Americans Say Helping Our Environment Creates More Jobs
As House Debates Gas Mileage, New Poll Shows Harmony Between Environment, Economy
 
WASHINGTON - July 18 - Americans believe stronger environmental protections lead to economic growth and new jobs, according to a new poll conducted by Lake, Snell, Perry and Associates, Inc. The Sierra Club released the research today as a Congressional committee considers curbing global warming and saving energy by raising gas mileage standards for cars, SUVs and other light trucks to 40 miles per gallon. Although the auto industry claims higher gas mileage standards would hurt the U.S. economy, most voters surveyed believe stronger environmental policies help the economy.

"Americans see environmental protection and economic growth as two sides of the same coin," said Debbie Sease, the Sierra Club's legislative director. "When we improve fuel economy standards, we'll create high-tech and manufacturing jobs building cars and SUVs that don't guzzle gas. Making our cars and light trucks go farther on a gallon of gas is the single biggest step we can take to curb global warming, save consumers money at the gas pump and cut our dependence on foreign oil. Better gas mileage is a win-win for our environment and for our economy. Automakers may claim gas mileage hurts our economy, but Americans know better -- when we guzzle gas, we only help OPEC economies."

"Despite the assertions of the Bush Administration, the public believes a strong economy and a healthy environment go hand-in-hand -- even though they have increasing concerns about the stability of the economy," said Celinda Lake, President of Lake, Snell, Perry and Associates, Inc. "Voters not only reject the notion that our nation must choose between a strong economy and a healthy environment, but they believe improving the environment can actually create jobs and strengthen the economy. By a margin of more than three-to-one, people thought improving the environment creates jobs and strengthens the economy."

When asked about the relationship between the economy and the environment, a strong majority of voters believe improving the environment can create jobs and help the national economy. Fifty-six percent of voters say improving the environment creates more jobs and helps the economy, compared to only 17 percent who say improving the quality of the environment slows down economic growth and job creation. Moreover, across the board, Republicans, Independents and Democrats all say improving the environment creates more jobs and helps the economy, over statements that environmental protections have no effect or a negative effect on the economy. Union members are even more likely than voters overall to believe that improving the environment helps the economy; 64 percent of union members agree with that statement, compared to 17 percent who say improving the environment hurts the economy.

Today, Representative Edward Markey (D-MA) will offer an amendment to the Energy Advancement and Conservation act to raise Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards to 40 miles per gallon for cars, SUVs and other light trucks. Markup on the amendment begins this afternoon in the House Energy and Commerce Committee.

Despite conventional wisdom and the assertions of the Bush/Cheney Administration, the public believes a strong economy and a health environment go hand-in-hand -- even though they have increasing concerns about the stability of the economy.

A recent national survey of registered voters The survey reached 1,000 registered voters nationwide, ages 18 or older. The survey was conducted between June 3-5, 2001. The total margin of error for this survey is +/- 3.1%.

A recent national survey of registered voters, conducted by Lake Snell Perry & Associates for The Sierra Club, found that voters not only reject the notion that our nation must choose between a strong economy and a healthy environment, but they believe improving the environment can actually create jobs and strengthen the economy. (The survey reached 1,000 registered voters nationwide, ages 18 or older. The survey was conducted between June 3-5, 2001. The total margin of error for this survey is +/- 3.1%.)

This is despite the fact that voters see the economy declining and are divided over whether the economy today is personally characterized by increasing opportunity or increasing instability. Forty-five percent of voters say they see increasing instability when thinking about the economy, compared to 41 percent who say they see increasing opportunity. Notably, women voters are more likely to associate the economy with instability than are men (48 percent to 42 percent, respectively), while men are more likely than women to associate the economy with opportunity (44 percent to 38 percent, respectively).

There are differences by political affiliation as well, with Democrats and Independents seeing increasing instability (49 percent and 51 percent, respectively) and Republicans seeing increasing opportunity in our economy (51 percent). Additionally, voters ages 18 to 34 are the most likely to believe the economy holds increasing opportunity (51 percent), while baby boomers ages 45 to 64 are the most likely to see increasing instability (51 percent).

When asked about the relationship between the economy and the environment, a strong majority of voters believe improving the environment can create jobs and help the national economy. Fifty-six percent of voters say improving the environment creates more jobs and helps the economy compared to only 17 percent who say improving the quality of the environment slows down economic growth and job creation. An additional 20 percent say there is no relationship between the environment and the economy and only 8 percent say none of the above or they are not sure about the relationship between the two. Interestingly, there is no gender gap with 56 percent of both men and women say improving the environment creates jobs and helps the economy, although men are slightly more likely to say improving the environment hurts the economy than women (19 percent to 16 percent, respectively). In fact, every demographic and geographic subgroup are more likely to believe improving the environment helps economic growth and job creation.

Moreover, across the board, Republicans, Independents, and Democrats all say improving the environment creates more jobs and helps the economy over the other two statements. Democrats are the most likely to believe that improving the environment helps the economy (69 percent) followed by Independents (54 percent). However, a plurality of Republicans also believe a better environment means more jobs and a better economy (43 percent), although they are the most likely to say that improving the environment slows job growth and hurts the economy (22 percent , compared to 14 percent for Democrats and 17 percent for Independents). Additionally, there is a gender gap among Republicans with Republican women 10 points more likely than Republican men to believe improving the environment helps our economy (48 percent to 38 percent, respectively).

Overall, most likely to believe a good environment means a good economy are voters with no religious affiliation (75 percent), Liberals (71 percent), voters ages18 to 24 (70 percent), Democrats (69 percent), voters who disapprove of Bush's job performance (69 percent), minority male voters (65 percent), union members (64 percent), unmarried men (64 percent), younger non-college educated voters (61 percent), Catholic voters (61 percent), and voters ages 25 to 29 (61 percent). Many of these sub-groups are key target voters for the 2002 elections.

In contrast, not surprisingly, those most likely to believe improving the environment slows economic growth are Republican (22 percent) -- especially Republican men (26 percent) and older Republicans (24 percent), voters living in the South Central region (25 percent), voters ages 30 to 39 (23 percent), younger Independents (22 percent), and white upscale suburban voters (22 percent). However, among all of these groups, by a 2 to 1 margin, voters still believe the economy is helped more than hurt by strong environmental policies.

Furthermore, after hearing both sides of the debate, a majority of voters believe stronger environmental policies mean a stronger economy. Fifty-three percent of voters choose a statement that strong environmental policies create new jobs in technology and environmentally safe products which strengthens our economy and our quality of life over a statement that tough environmental laws cost us jobs and hurt our economy by forcing businesses to cut their workforce or go out of business (35 percent). Just 10 percent say both, neither or not sure. While majorities of both men and women say the statement that stronger environmental policies means a stronger economy, there is a slight gender gap with women choosing the stronger environmental policies means a stronger economy statement by 21 points and men by 15 points.
