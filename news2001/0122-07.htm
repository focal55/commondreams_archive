WRI Urges Bush to Resume Climate Talks As New Report Warns of Devastating Global Warming
 
WASHINGTON - January 22 - As the world's leading climate change scientists envision a potentially more devastating global warming in this century, the World Resources Institute (WRI) urged President George W. Bush to rapidly move for the ratification of the Kyoto Protocol and to resume the stalled talks to implement the international climate protection treaty.

The latest scientific report, Third Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), was approved today by climate change scientists from more than 100 countries in Shanghai, China. It presents new and stronger evidence that most of the global warming in the last 50 years was caused by human activities. Global warming temperatures are also projected to increase by 2.52 degrees F - 10.44 degrees F (1.4 degrees C -- 5.8 degrees C) from 1990 to 2100, which is higher than the estimates in the last IPCC assessment in 1995.

"As the biggest emitter of carbon dioxide, the U.S. has an historic opportunity and a moral responsibility to jump-start the stalled negotiations for the Kyoto Protocol," said Jonathan Lash, WRI president. "Scientists have presented compelling evidence and politicians must move boldly to mitigate the impacts of climate change before it is too late."

The Third Assessment Report of the IPCC also reveals:

-- In the Northern Hemisphere, the 1990s were the warmest decade. 1998 was the warmest year since 1861.

-- Snow cover has decreased by 10 percent since the 1960s in the mid- and high altitudes of the Northern Hemisphere. In the recent decades, the thickness of the ice in the Arctic Sea has declined by as much as 40 percent.

-- Warm episodes of El Nino phenomena have been more frequent, persistent, and intense since the 1970s.

-- In parts of Asia and Africa, the frequency and intensity of droughts have increased.

-- Sea levels are expected to rise by 0.88 meters by 2100.

"The Third Assessment Report of the IPCC is another warning bell." said Dr. Anthony C. Janetos, senior vice president of WRI and co-chair of a separate report, Climate Change Impacts on the United States, released by the U.S. government late last year. "You just need to look around the country to see what the rest of the world is already experiencing in terms of climate change."

The U.S. report warns climate change and the resulting rise in sea level are likely to worsen threats to buildings, roads, and powerlines in low-lying coastlines and the permafrost regions of Alaska.

It also says that certain ecosystems, like the alpine meadows and some barrier islands, are likely to disappear. Other ecosystems, like the forests of the Southeast, may break up or their composition will change. "The goods and services lost through the disappearance or fragmentation of certain ecosystems are likely costly or impossible to replace," said Dr. Janetos.

Dr. Nancy Kete, director of WRI's climate, energy and pollution program, says that globally, climate change will have the greatest impact on those with the least resources to adapt, especially developing countries. For citizens of island nations and low-lying countries like Bangladesh, there may simply be no place to retreat as sea level rises.

"One could argue that the U.S. and other industrialized countries have imposed potentially catastrophic environmental changes in the developing world," said Dr. Kete. "Although this problem was created inadvertently, its consequences are now quite clear. The responsibility to reduce carbon emissions -- either through energy conservation or new, low-carbon energy sources -- lies with industrialized countries."

While industrialized countries account for as much as 80 percent of the build-up of heat-trapping greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, none of them have ratified the Kyoto Protocol. Negotiations in The Hague last November collapsed as the U.S. and the European Union could not agree on how they can reduce their emissions of greenhouse gases.

"The political impasse in the climate negotiations over relatively insignificant differences was like Nero fiddling while Rome burned," Dr. Kete said. She hopes that the Third Assessment Report of the IPCC will motivate the U.S. and leading industrial countries to go back to the negotiating table and finish what they started in Kyoto, Japan in 1997.
