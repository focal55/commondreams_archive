Interfaith Alliance Submits Testimony Urging Elimination of 'Charitable Choice' Provisions in Drug Abuse Act
 
WASHINGTON - June 6 - At the first Senate hearing on the controversial "charitable choice" provision, The Interfaith Alliance (TIA) submitted testimony calling upon senators to protect the religious freedom of those seeking government services, to prevent taxpayer funded religious discrimination, and to uphold the integrity of religious autonomy by eliminating the faith-based solutions component of the Drug Abuse Education, Prevention, and Treatment Act of 2001, S. 304, found in Section 701 titled "Assistance by Religious Organizations Under Government Programs Funded Under This Act."

"Today's public hearing marks the first opportunity for Senate Judiciary Committee members to hear concerns and examine the far-reaching implications of government funding pervasively religious ministries," said the Rev. Dr. C. Welton Gaddy, executive director of TIA. "We will learn from the questions whether lawmakers in the Senate understand the proper relationship between religion and government in arguably the most religiously diverse nation in the world."

Yesterday at a Habitat for Humanity building site in Florida, President Bush continued his ongoing campaign to build public support for his faith-based initiatives, calling for the expansion of "charitable choice" legislation.

"It is unfortunate that the president in promoting his faith-based initiatives has confused the fundamental difference between non-financial partnerships and contractual employment that comes with rules, regulations and risks," said the Rev. Gaddy. "I hope and pray that the Senate Judiciary Committee will resist the political temptation to obscure the real consequences of government funding pervasively religious programs."

Follwing are excerpts from the testimony submitted by The Interfaith Alliance to the Senate Judiciary Committee:

"In no uncertain terms, we believe that this legislation raises serious concerns about the prophetic voice of faith, the integrity of religious autonomy, excessive government entanglement in the affairs of religious institutions, and the overarching temptation to abuse religion and manipulate faith to achieve political power....

"Whenever elected leaders craft public policy that impacts the relationship between the institutions of religion and government, the potential for equating a policy position with a litmus test of faith, is a real concern. Unfortunately, that concern often leads to silence as public officials fear being unfairly labeled by those who promote an agenda that endangers religious liberty and tolerance. In the face of uncivil attacks, we ask Senators to demonstrate courage and protect the religious liberty of all people of faith and goodwill....

"While it is well-intentioned, we believe this 'charitable choice' legislation is severely misguided....The Interfaith Alliance is opposed to so-called 'charitable choice' provisions because we believe this legislation raises serious issues that can irreparably harm religious life in America....

"The Interfaith Alliance does believe that religion and government can, and should, work together. There is no debate among us about the ability of faith to have an impact on and create change in our nation. The controversy arises around strikingly different opinions on the government's responsibility to fully fund efforts to serve those in need. We believe the reasonable compromise between pervasively religious ministries receiving government funds for which the president and supporters of 'charitable choice' are calling, and religious ministries and organizations receiving no funds at all, is embracing and enhancing the current levels of partnership which exist already. This will facilitate the organization of houses of worship and religious ministries under today's statutes and protections."

Since the 2000 presidential campaign, TIA has voiced concerns about "charitable choice" legislation. In an effort to educate local religious and civic leaders about the dangers of government funding religion and the potential for civil rights violations against those in need, TIA launched a grassroots campaign to host public forums, bringing together local community leaders to discuss the implications of "charitable choice" and President Bush's faith-based initiatives. Forums have already taken place in Oklahoma, Kansas, Missouri, Florida and Virginia, with others scheduled in the future. To obtain information about this grassroots effort and transcripts from events, contact the Communications Department at 202-639-6370.

To increase understanding among religious leaders about the ethical and legal ramifications of becoming government contractors, The Interfaith Alliance Foundation and the Baptist Joint Committee on Public Affairs published Keeping the Faith: The Promise of Cooperation, The Perils of Government Funding: A Guide for Houses of Worship. This 15-page, easy-to-read guide has been distributed to more than 30,000 religious leaders and houses of worship throughout the country. To obtain a free copy of this booklet, please contact the Religious Outreach Department at 202-639-6370. To view this document online, visit www.interfaithalliance.org/Initiatives/ktf.pdf.

Founded in 1994, The Interfaith Alliance (TIA) is a non-partisan clergy-led grassroots organization dedicated to promoting the positive and healing role of religion in the life of the nation and challenging those who manipulate religion to promote intolerance. With more than 130,000 members drawn from over 50 faith traditions, clergy-led local Alliances in 38 states, and a national network of activists in every state, TIA promotes civility, mutual respect, and cooperation in our increasingly diverse society.
