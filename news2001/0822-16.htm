Pentagon Declares War on Social Programs:
Adjusted Surplus Pits Defense Versus Domestic
 
WASHINGTON - August 22 - The Bush Administration released its revised budget surplus estimates today. The Office of Management and Budget projects a non-Social Security budget surplus of only $1 billion for both fiscal years 2001 and 2002. That leaves no money for the $18.4 billion Pentagon budget amendment without cutting domestic programs or tapping the Medicare or Social Security surplus. Given that the U.S. has no significant enemies, there is no justification for raiding popular social programs in order to fund the already bloated Pentagon.

"Raiding these vital programs to fund a Department of Defense that cannot balance its books and has no significant enemies is absurd, especially when public opinion polls consistently show that military spending is a low priority compared to domestic programs," said Dan Koslofsky, analyst with the pro-arms control organization Council for a Livable World Education Fund.

Even the administration's optimistic budget numbers show that providing the $18.4 billion Pentagon increase will require tapping either Social Security or Medicare. The total increase requested for the 2002 Pentagon budget is $33 billion (10 percent over 2001) raising total defense spending to over $343 billion. That amount of military spending will likely be more than all the other nations in the world combined and will actually top the average Pentagon budget during the five decades of Cold War spending.

Language in the budget resolution allows the budget committee chairmen to increase the cap for defense spending only if it does not tap into the Social Security and Medicare trust. Senate Budget Committee Chairman Kent Conrad (D-ND) said he will "personally oppose" the defense increase. His counterpart in the House Rep. Jim Nussle (R-IA) has consistently stated that he will not touch either trust fund.

This dynamic sets up a major political battle in which supporters of increased defense spending will likely try to bypass the committees or seek cuts in other discretionary domestic programs. Considering the Administration's request already cuts funding for the Departments of Interior, Agriculture, Energy, Justice, Labor and Transportation along with the EPA and the Federal Emergency Management Agency, additional offsets will be difficult to find.

"The debate is simple: either freeze defense spending or cut popular domestic programs," added Koslofsky.
