Nation's Leading Interfaith Organization Tells President: Now is Not the Time to Advance the Divisive Faith-Based Initiative
 
WASHINGTON - November 8 - Today, The Interfaith Alliance (TIA), the nation's leading interfaith advocacy organization, and 27 religious organizations, including Jewish, Christian, Muslim, Sikh and Jain groups, urged President Bush to hold off on pushing the U.S. Senate into taking action on the deeply divisive issue of the faith-based initiative this year. (See letter attached).

"Furthermore, The Interfaith Alliance is concerned that with national attention being placed on a lack of donations to charities not connected with September 11 relief efforts, we will see the president put pressure on the Senate to pass his faith-based initiative, otherwise known as the 'Armies of Compassion' Bill," said the Rev. Dr. C. Welton Gaddy, executive director of TIA.

Today, The Washington Post cited the president as saying, "It is now time for America to stand by her charities as they suffer from the economic consequences of September 11." The Rev. Gaddy believes that this statement points to the Administration's strategy to put direct pressure on Congress to pass faith-based legislation.

"The faith-based initiative puts our civil and religious liberties at risk," said the Rev. Gaddy. "For the last few months, we have heard calls from members of Congress and the White House to not use the terrorist attacks to promote special interest projects. It seems to me that is precisely what the president is doing."

It is important to note that the $1 billion dollars raised on behalf of September 11 relief efforts, equals only one-half of one percent of the $203.45 billion in philanthropic giving that took place in this country last year. (Miller and Rollins Fund Raising)

-- Letter to president is attached (letter was delivered to the White House late Thursday afternoon, November 8, 2001)

November 8, 2001
President George W. Bush
The White House
1600 Pennsylvania Avenue, NW
Washington, D.C. 20500

Dear President Bush,

As we reflect on how the lamentable and egregious assault on our nation has impacted the religious landscape in America, we, the undersigned religious organizations, write to you on behalf of hundreds of thousands of people of diverse faiths to urge you to reconsider any legislative action on the charitable choice provisions in your Faith-Based Initiative at this time.

Mr. President, many of us support the major tax incentives you have proposed as part of your Faith-Based Initiative. We believe that such tax incentives would continue - and even further - the recent outpouring of contributions and individual giving with which the American people have blessed and empowered charities and religious organizations since our national tragedy.

While the organizations listed below can agree to disagree about whether your expanded charitable choice proposal is a step forward or a step backwards for religious liberty, as people who affirm the critical role of faith as a healing source in our society, we all agree that now is not the time to advance such a religiously divisive and controversial legislative provision.

Mr. President, any effort by the White House to advance charitable choice in the foreseeable future has the potential to turn the national sentiment away from feelings of unity and patriotism and back to the contentiousness and partisanship that were so evident before September 11. As people of faith, we are particularly concerned about the following unintended consequences of having a debate on charitable choice at this time:

First, since you announced this initiative during the opening days of your administration, organizations and faith groups from across the ideological and theological spectrum have expressed numerous objections to charitable choice, including the selection process, during which political appointees and bureaucrats would decide which faith-based organizations would receive funding and which ones would not.

This debate subsequently sparked a spirited national dialogue about criteria that would be used in the government's decisions about which religions would receive federal funds. During this dialogue, it became increasingly clear that many political appointees and elected officials do not have an adequate understanding - or appreciation - of minority religions.

A reopened debate at this time about what qualifies as an acceptable religion will become all the more contentious in the wake of recent events as numerous hate crimes have been directed at our nation's Muslim, Hindu, Jain and Sikh citizens out of misunderstanding and unfortunate ignorance. As people of faith, we fear that these communities would only be further ostracized in the funding and selection process due to misinformation, false stereotyping and retaliation. While we wish this not to be the case, recently reported inflammatory remarks by a Member of Congress toward Arab-Americans make our fear well founded.

Second, in the aftermath of September 11, we have joined you in calling for an increase in civic duty through volunteerism and personal giving. The result of this has been an outpouring of compassion matched with generous giving by the American people. Were we to have a debate about charitable choice at this time, we fear that the public's assumption that it is the role of government - not the people - to fund the good works of houses of worship would blunt the sense of personal responsibility that has been so apparent and lower the sacrificial level of giving that has been so helpful. Mr. President, we do not need any action that lessens a sense of personal responsibility and reduces levels of generosity toward those in need.

Finally, as organizations representing the breadth of religious life in this nation, we believe that any action on this issue during this time of increased sensitivities toward the minority faith community will sadly divert the country's attention away from the positive effects of interfaith cooperation and leadership that have been so apparent and focus on the differences that divide America's rich religious landscape.

Mr. President, as a person of deep faith yourself, we hope you will listen to our counsel and consider our point of view as we all work with you to try to heal a nation that is torn by grief, sorrow and hurt, but united by a deep sense of patriotism, personal faith and conviction.

Sincerely,
