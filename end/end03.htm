Ed Mirmak
Irvine, California
12/13/00 10:30 PM

We have been constantly bombarded with propaganda for 20 years or more, with the simple but persistent message "Government bad, private business/ownership good". Repeated often enough, the message sinks into the collective national subconscious, and the citizenry eventually accepts it as a law of nature, hence not subject to question. After that, it's much easier to begin dismantling the government programs and agencies that were established to carry out the goal in the Preamble, "to promote the general welfare". In parallel, the propaganda machine has been busy around the world, selling the concept of "privatization" to all who will buy.

Look at what's going on in public transportation in the UK. In a recent article in the Guardian, a British government spokesman was quoted as saying that privatization was the only option to raise enough capital to rebuild the air traffic control system.

Well, here's a novel concept -- if something is needed for the public good, why not impose a tax of some sort to pay for it, then put together a quality product that will be of use to the public for a long time? How about Interstate Highways and federal gasoline taxes, as an example? A great system, and the revenue is collected at the gas pump.

One of the major contributors to the Florida fiasco was the private corporation hired by the state to "cleanse" the voter registration rolls, and their list of 85,000 wrongly classified felons. Why was such an important civic action entrusted to a non-governmental agency in the first place?

For the past few decades we have been witness to the equivalent of the Enclosure of the Commons in England many centuries ago. Put public property into private hands, so that a few may profit immensely from it. Consider the spectrum auctions that have been held for the past several years by the FCC. This is reportedly tied to the Federal Budget Reconciliation Act of 1996. How much money has this brought into the Treasury, and how much value can be placed on that which has been sold?

What is needed to begin turning things around in this country is to open a new public dialogue on the proper role of government in our civic life. Perhaps Common Dreams can offer the forum.

A parting thought -- in a short span of roughly four years, from about 1933 to 1937, Americans started and completed the Golden Gate Bridge, the San Francisco Bay Bridge, and the Hetch-Hetchy dam and aqueduct. The last one might ruffle some feathers, but it's the Northern California equivalent of Mulholland's Southern California water system.

Folks, these were all public works! All the bonds were paid off long ago. Americans once dreamed big, and built on those dreams. We can do so once more.

(P.S. I voted for Nader, for the second time in four years.)

3. Imagine that the self-declared winner's 'victory' turned on disputed votes cast in a province governed by his brother.

8. Imagine that the self-declared winner and his political party opposed a more careful by-hand inspection and re-counting of the ballots in the disputed province or in its most hotly disputed district.
