Rick Rozoff
Chicago, Illinois
12/13/00 2:29 PM

First of all my profound appreciation to Common Dreams for providing this forum as well as having consistently brought principle and clarity to the presidential election debate from the beginning of the party primaries to this decisive day.

What has encouraged me greatly, and is reason for optimism in the upcoming weeks, is that the real constituencies supporting the Gore campaign - often in spite of Gore himself - namely labor and the African-American community, have demonstrated a level of commitment andcombativeness during the past five weeks that surpased that of the election season proper. This is no doubt attributable to their having a nobler, more meaningful cause than the mere election of a lesser evil.

When the real struggle commences, which means this moment, added to these two key forces must be the equally vital peace and solidarity movements, both of whom had little enough reason to support Gore but both of whom should be gearing up for a preemptive fight to defeat the machinations of Mssrs Bush, Cheney, Rice and company. The peace/disarmament/solidarity component, always in the forefront of sacrifice, daring and creativity, will also help bridge the gaps between the all too frequently isolated American progressive movement and the rest of the world's advanced democratic forces.

And don't write off honest if politically hidebound people who may have voted Republican because of lack of imagination and information, and because of outdated habits. Many of them can be won away from the GOP and toward independent, popular politics, especially if the campaign to do so begins immediately.

Dave DeRosa
Washington DC
12/13/00 1:26 PM

My truest hope after this fresh debacle of an election is that some Americans have noticed, or have seen their concern grow. I worked on the Nader campaign here in DC, and I know how we excited some people around the country that cared about politics already--but weren't ready to care about electoral politics, because they'd decided early in life that little of worth could be accomplished there. They were right, of course, but it didn't have to be so dismal--just because cynicism is correct doesn't mean it should be. Yes, the election was stolen--like always, but more clearly--and all so Scalia can be the new chief justice? The one thing Ralph got wrong was that, indeed, this election was ALL about the Supreme Court. But he was right on the bigger point--we can't trust the Court to safeguard any right--we have them, inalienably, only when we demand them.

As for all the other issues, I hope that this current congress moved on only one issue that Americans can agree on: electoral reform. Bring voting methods into at least the 20th century, and ideally the 21st--with optical scanned ballots nationwide that also are trackable. We need universal suffrage--never mind this Motor Voter nonsense, any eligible citizen should be able to decide on election day that they want to vote, show up, and do so. This is the true need established under the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

Election Day should be a paid national holiday (keep it on a Tuesday, though, or people will make a three day weekend out of it), as the AFL-CIO has recognized--if it's a problem to make another holiday, let's get rid of the anachronistic Columbus Day. Of course, Election Day even for Congressional races is only every two years--perhpas we should make it Citizenship Day, so that on off-years we can look at what we've got coming and perhaps organize a national plebiscite on what we want to see addressed.

Nader had most of the rest of it right--a People's Debate Commission, meaningful campaign finance reform, instant runoff voting so that voters can express their true wish without seeming "naive", and a reinjection of issues people care about into these races. Let's get folks to want to vote again.

As for Bush--yes, I'm worried about all the damage he can do to the environment, human rights, military spending, and our national skid into irrelevance--but this is likely to be a reign by the stupidest president we've had since Rutherford Hayes, and the most corrupt since Ulysses Grant. So, if we keep our eyes open, I'm guessing we'll be able to catch them in some doozies. And I hope that, once we begin to accomplish the above, we can end political dynasty-making that both Bush and Gore represent...

Not that having a famous dad is always bad--
I say, Jesse Jackson Jr. for President in 2004!
(if not him, I'm going Green again, which I'm certain to do in 2002)

One would think the Constitution is made of blotter acid. The Supreme Court decision stands the concept of Constitutional Equal Protection on its head. One group whose vote might be counted because their intent can be discerned and a group whose vote won't be counted because their intent cannot be discerned. So? More legitimate votes would have been counted than not without the Supreme Court decision.. A decision utterly without integrity.

More importantly, Ralph Nader's campaign was fueled by the corruption and anti-democratic tendencies in our system. This election, particularly the involvement of the Supreme Court including two judges who should of recused themselves because of conflicts of interest, proves his point. If Nader supporters and Greens through-out the country can make common cause with African-Americans to demand an end to the institutional racism that disenfranchised Florida's voters, to the common end of building a true progressive movement, this election can be turned into a significant victory.

Only those who for the first time, experience injustice wrapped up and packaged by supposedly democratic institutions, may be surprised by the"Theft of Florida". When was this kind of fraud not possible in this country? Think of Treaties with Native Americans or the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution and other legislation, court decisions and executive orders enabling not just the theft of an election but the deaths of tens of millions of human beings...all nice and legal. There are so many other instances of this same seemingly inevitable dialectic that point to the distinction between law and right.

There are too many others to think that these, like last night's are abberations. What the cold light of morning must reveal is that naked power bulks up under the Emperor's constitutional dressings. And,we should remember that only power may successfully confront power. When ever and where ever justice and human need have succeeded in this struggle, it has been through the independent activity of people in motion in their own interest. When law helps right it is only because it has no other choice.

Will the Supreme Court, augmented by Bush appointees, abbrogate abortion rights? Probably? Will that dismal decision end abortion rights? No. It will take struggle, demonstrations, defeating some and supporting other congresspeople, but we will re-gain those lost rights. Yes, I know that lives will be lost during that time to illegal abortions and even, I can envision, police brutality so I profouldly wish I could imagine other ways for history to move. I can not.

Last night, the slow progress of humanity from the grip of injustice was again thwarted. Today my perspective was simply put by that greatest of organizers, Mother Jones -- "Don't Mourn -- Organize!" Come out. The bell has rung for round number who knows what round --- but the fight goes on ---pick your particular issue and organize, organize, organize.
