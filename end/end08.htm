The Un-president

Over the last couple days, I’ve heard lots of people saying “Bush will not be MY president.” I might say this too, but only in the same narrow sense that, since I voted for Nader (and would not have voted for Gore even if Ralph himself had held a gun to my head and commanded me to), I would also have said “Gore” (had he ended up in the White House) “will not be my president.”

No, in any broad and meaningful sense of the term, Bush is not going to be anyone’s president. He won’t be the president at all.

I’m not disputing any obvious facts in making this assertion. Sure, I acknowledge who’s going to be living at 1600 Pennsylvania Ave, flying around in Air Force One, speaking in front of the Presidential Seal, and all of that. I’m not delusional.

What I’m concerned about is the proper and meaningful use of words in our republic. And in our civic discourse.

A president is, by definition, someone who has won a presidential election. Bush didn't do that. You don't get to be President of the United States just because local vote certifiers or state Heads of Elections or special session State Legislators or national Electors or Supreme Court Judges--or even your conceding opponents--announce that you are. (Let’s agree not even to discuss the irrelevancy of announcements from a mainstream national media whose subservience to the dollar puts Disney to shame.) The legitimacy of an announcement that“So-and-so is the next President of the United States” is subject not just to the existence of a process by which certain office-holders and broadcast-personalities are authorized to speak on the subject. Genuine legitimacy depends upon a candidate actually winning a fair and complete count of the votes.

What “President-elect” Bush won was a fraudulent and incomplete--and so decisively inaccurate--vote count. As soon as The Miami Herald’s team wraps up its own re-count, we’ll have the final numbers on exactly how inaccurate. Specifying the degree of fraudulency will take longer, but I’m confident that some crack investigative journalists are going to give us damn thorough reports about that within the year.

So I will not be using the term of office “President” and the proper name “George W. Bush” in the same phrase. And I’ll refuse to do so for exactly the same reason that principled voters in Peru and Yugoslavia refused to acknowledge the illegitimate office-seizures of the persons they vowed not to describe as “President Fujimori” or “President Milosevic.”

I’m happy, on the other hand, to call things as I see them. “Coup-meister Bush” is fine by me, for example. Since I’m partial to historical precedent, I wouldn’t object to a revival of “Your Fraudulency.” And I’ll gladly join along in singing “Hail to the Thief.”

But I’m not trying to lay down the verbal law for anyone else. Use your own imagination. (We’re going to need a LOT of imagination to overcome the real-life surrealism of the absurdities we’ve just endured--and of those we'll face in the years to come.)

Just be sure that if you slip and say “President Bush,” you make up with air quotes.

The Problem With Blaming Nader

The fallacy here is that people who claim that Nader cost Gore the election are making an assumption that Nader folks were basically Democratic party members who would come back into the fold if an emergency arose. Not true. Many of us have deep political disagreements with the Democratic Party leadership. A lot of us were insulted and angry at the assumption that we would come back if called. I think Nader could have run a more thoughtful and analytical campaign, instead of constantly harping on the fact that the two major party's are identical. This isn't really true, although it is true that neither stands for progressive social change. And he may have been able to run a more thoughtful campaign if he'd had media coverage or been included in the debates. Many progressives have decided to make strategic alliances with Democrats in the past in spite of profound disagreements with them. This year a substantial number of us decided not to, for a range of reasons. I don't know what the future holds here. But I do think that Democrats should stop venting anger at Greens. They are more legitimately angry at the Democratic voters who went for Bush.

Here's a letter to the editor that I submitted to the New York Times. Needless to say, you won't see it in the "newspaper of record."

"To the Editor:

Comes now the predictable call for "healing", for rallying around George W. Bush. Well, count me out. To all attempts by the media, by mainstream politicians and talking heads of all political stripes, indeed, even by Al Gore himself, to paper over what has transpired in the interests of "stability", "the rule of law" etc., my ears are deaf. Why? Because what the US Supreme Court did in it's 5-4 decision was a cynical and lawless act, one which, I believe, may very well mark a turning point in American history. In effect, Al Gore was mugged and robbed in plain view of the entire world by a Court that has abandoned all pretense of careful, impartial judgment and fashioned itself into an instrument of the extreme right. Did I say Al Gore was mugged? No, that's incorrect. As much as even Gore himself, in his stunningly over-praised concession, sought to personalize what transpired, falling utterly silent with regard to the principles he had claimed for five weeks to be seeking to uphold, it was, in fact, the American people who were mugged by the Court.

This decision undermines the fundamental values upon which (so we're told as children in civics class) the government of this country rests. It must not be abided - - it must be resisted. Perhaps, as a result of this sordid episode, Americans are waking up to the fact that our bedrock liberties are not as durable as we had believed. If so, some good may eventually come of it. Or perhaps, cowed by incessant talk of the need to rally around the flag, and by Bush's cynical inclusion of a couple of right wing Democrats in second-tier cabinet positions, most people will simply go back to sleep. If so, the next time they awaken, they may find themselves with no real liberties at all. Come to think of it, maybe that day has already arrived."
