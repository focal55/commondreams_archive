When I joined others in wholeheartedly supporting Ralph Nader's candidacy, I understood George Bush could win the election -- not because of Ralph Nader, particularly, but because of the lackluster and confusing campaign run by Al Gore. I mean, was he for the people or for major corporations? For the environment or for "free trade" and special interests? Was he a Democrat or a Republican? It was hard to tell most times.

He promised us a "blanket of blue" around communities, increasing the numbers of police -- all the while we have a dropping crime rate and all too many nonviolent drug offenders -- mostly minority -- in our increasingly privatized prisons, as well as an escalation of racial profiling and police and prison brutality.

He promised in a speech in May "quicker interventions" and "less diplomacy." This following a most brutal eight years of the Clinton-Gore administration: 70-plus days of "humanitarian" bombing in Yugoslavia; the starving and withholding of medicine from Iraqi civilians, causing over a million deaths so far, over half a million of them children under 5 (I guess the little ones haven't figured out how to take out Saddam); spearheading through the United Nations phony reparations bills on Iraq amounting to hundreds of billions of dollars -- debts that will effectively keep Iraq from rebuilding and repairing for decades the tens of billions of dollars of devastation caused by Desert Storm; "accidentally" bombing the major pharmaceutical plant in the Sudan (which had produced 90% of the antibiotics used for malaria, the leading cause of death there -- but note, the US has no obligation to pay reparations for this unfortunate mistake in military aggression); military aid to Colombia's right-wing military in the name of the "drug war" but really on behalf of special interests here and there; selling far more arms abroad than the previous Bush administration (during Clinton's first year in office, U.S. arms sales more than doubled) -- the list goes on and on.

As Ramsey Clark, former US Attorney General says, "The greatest crime since World War II has been US foreign policy."

Now, it would be at least something if things were better in this country. Instead -- more people without health insurance, declining quality of health care, a bigger divide between the rich and the poor, housing costs skyrocketing, and many many in the so-called middle class, struggling just to maintain their lives where they were a decade ago.

Welfare reform -- argued against by all Clinton advisors as a disastrous idea, even in times of "prosperity," but catastrophic if there is an economic downturn. Oh, I forgot, one advisor did advise Clinton to sign the bill: Albert Gore.

Environmentally -- well, we are worse off now than we were four years ago -- particularly in the areas of global warming and environmental racism, but actually in all areas. The EPA -- which I believe was part of Al's bailiwick -- is now usually the defendant in lawsuits, instead of the plaintiff.

So you see, we haven't lost much with Al's loss, never mind the crooked way it happened. I hope the Democrats wake up, take this opportunity, with a Republican in office, to find their backbone and remember who they represent and what kind of party they are supposed to be. I doubt they will, they are too bought and paid for, most of them, and it is those interests they represent. But please, please, don't grieve too long for Al Gore. Bush's presidency may be just what is needed to foster more awareness, more activism, and more determination.

We have a lot to do and a lot to fight for. Let's get going.

Thank you for hosting this message board which is the first I've encountered where participants actually have something thoughtful to contribute.

I'd like to share a quote from an spiritual teacher of great wisdom from India, Sri Nisargadatta -

"At every moment, whatever comes to you unwanted comes from God and will surely help you if make the fullest use of it. It is only what you strive for out of your own imagination and desire that gives you trouble. Be not afraid of freedom from desire and fear. It enables you to live a life so different from all you know, so much more intense and interesting, that surely by losing all, you gain all."

I certainly was swept up in the drama of these past few weeks and regret the outcome mightily. Yet I find it impossible to join those who prophesies catastrophe at the hands of Republicans. Many things will come from this moment, ranging no doubt from increased apathy for some, radicalization and confrontational activism for others, increased mobilization efforts by those engaged in mainstream politics, to continuing indifference for the many who sat out this last election. The invitation offered by Nisargadatta is to bring awareness to whatever you do as a response to this moment. Being trapped by fear and desire brings only pain. Liberation comes with releasing one's attachment to outcome. However you respond to this brief episode in our country's long history, this moment in your much shorter life, do it with mindfulness and joy. Bitterness and anger offer nothing of lasting value to anyone.
