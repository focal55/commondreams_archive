Published on Monday, May 22, 2000 by the Associated Press
Nader Tries To Woo Environmentalists From Gore Camp
 
TRUCKEE, Calif. (AP) Though national environmental groups are  embracing Al Gore, some grass-roots conservationists are  contemplating a protest vote for Ralph Nader - a gesture that could  end up helping George W. Bush in Western states.  

Nader, a Green Party candidate, is winning support from people  who normally vote Democratic by pressing hardline environmental  proposals on forest policy and other subjects.  

For example, during a campaign stop here along the Sierra  Nevada's eastern front, he won applause when he said, "I don't  believe there should be logging in federal forests, period." He  also advocated sweeping mine reform.  

The consumer advocate, who got his start taking on the auto  industry, increasingly is taking aim at Gore's environmental record  - an area thought to be a Gore strength.  

"He wrote a book in 1992 (Earth in the Balance), that he  completely turned his back on," Nader said during a speech at the  University of Nevada, Reno.  

The message seems to be resonating with some environmental  liberals who view President Clinton and his vice president as  dealmakers too quick to compromise.  

"Most of my folks are going to vote for Nader. They will never  vote for Al Gore again," said Tim Hermach, executive director of  the Native Forest Council in Eugene, Ore.  

Sixteen percent of the old-growth forest that existed when  Clinton imposed his Northwest forest plan has now been logged "on  the way to clearcutting 55 percent of the old-growth we had when he  started," Hermach said.  

"I think the worst, dumbest Republican is better for us because  we don't perceive him to be our friend."  

While it's hard to measure Nader's impact, a poll last month in  Oregon - a state that has backed the Democratic presidential  candidate in the past three elections - had Bush at 40 percent,  Gore at 37 and Nader at 7, with a margin of error of plus or minus  4.5 points. Gore and Bush are tied in Washington, another  environmentally conscious state that has voted Democratic in the  past three presidential elections.  

The Sierra Club and the League of Conservation Voters haven't  decided whether to endorse Gore. But they are leaning that way and  doing their best to defend him against detractors.  

Among the largest national groups, only Friends of the Earth,  which endorsed Bill Bradley in the Democratic primary, appears to  be realistically weighing a Nader endorsement.  

A national poll by Zogby International last month found Nader drawing 6 percent - slightly more than Pat Buchanan's 4 percent - in a field with Bush at 43 percent and Gore at 39. The poll had a  margin of error of plus or minus 3.5 percent.  

One of Nader's goals is to pull 5 percent or more of the  national vote, which would qualify him for millions in federal  campaign funds.  

The Gore campaign and its mainstream environmental allies argue  that a vote for Nader is ultimately a vote for Bush, whose record  as Texas governor hardly endears him to conservationists.  

In addition to Bush's ties to the oil industry, critics point to  Texas' bad air quality, and particularly Houston's crown as the  nation's smog capital of 1999. The League of Conservation Voters  argues that Bush opposed the Kyoto treaty on global warming, worked  to make some Texas air pollution regulations voluntary and  generally advocates a reduced role in environmental enforcement and  regulation.  

"I'm not sure Ralph Nader has been paying too much attention,"  said Doug Hattaway, a Gore spokesman. "The most productive thing  for an environmentalist to do is to support Al Gore."  

But some activists say they've been taken for granted by a  Clinton-Gore administration too ready to compromise away old-growth  forests and other habitat for endangered wildlife.  

Environmentalists in the Sierra Nevada, organized under the  national group American Lands, don't endorse candidates. But  leaders say many will vote for Nader.  

Brian Vincent, California organizer for American Lands, was  among those who had trumpeted Clinton's proposal to protect  roadless areas in national forests. But he's changed his tune.  

"We think the administration is talking out of both sides of  its mouth," he said.  

Clinton unveiled the roadless initiative in October not long  after Friends of the Earth endorsed Bradley.  

"I think it is pretty obvious it is an election-year ploy to  get environmental endorsements," said Fraser Shilling, a Sierra  Club member who coordinates a research program on land use at the  University of California-Davis.  

The Sierra Club's national board has identified Bush as the  enemy but hasn't decided whether to endorse Gore over Nader.  

"If they both take us in the right direction, the question  becomes who can excite people and compel people to vote?"  executive director Carl Pope said from San Francisco.  

Deb Callahan, executive director of the League of Conservation Voters, said Gore might have been the victim of inflated  expectations.  

"Sometimes, advocates who don't really understand the stakes in  some of these policy fights don't acknowledge the role a politician  has to play in forging something that can be passed by Congress,"  she said.
