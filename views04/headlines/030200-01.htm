Published on Thursday, March 2,2000 by Agence France-Presse
Britain Tells Pinochet He's Free To Go Home
 

LONDON - Chile's former dictator Augusto Pinochet was free to go home Thursday as Britain halted extradition proceedings after more than 16 months of legal wrangling.

Britain's Home Secretary Jack Straw said he had decided to release Pinochet, 84, wanted in Spain to face torture charges relating to his 1973-1990 military regime, as he was mentally unfit to stand trial.

Within two hours of Straw's ruling, a six-vehicle convoy led by two police motorcycles left the luxury residence southwest of London where Pinochet has been under house arrest.

Police confirmed Pinochet and his entourage were in the convoy, but refused to disclose his destination.

Straw said independent medical tests had shown Pinochet was unfit mentally to stand trial, and last-minute representations from Spain and three other countries seeking his extradition had failed to convince him otherwise.

Extradition requests from Belgium, France and Switzerland would not be allowed to proceed, he said.

As the decision was announced, supporters reacted with delight and opponents with anger, while human rights groups said the case had at least made the world a more uncomfortable place for rights abusers.

Madrid immediately signalled that it would not appeal Straw's decision, while Britain's Crown Prosecution Service said Pinochet would not be tried here.

In a written parliamentary statement, Straw said: "The principle that an accused person should be mentally capable of following the proceedings, instructing his lawyers and giving coherent evidence is fundamental to the idea of a fair trial.

"An attempted trial of an accused in the condition diagnosed in Senator Pinochet would not be a fair trial in any country and would violate Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights."

Protesters gathered outside the luxury mansion where Pinochet had been in detention since his arrest in October 1998 on a Spanish warrant.

Journalists and protesters were also camped outside RAF Brize Norton, an air base in Oxfordshire, southern England, where a Chilean air force plane had been on standby for the past month to whisk Pinochet home.

But the plane left the air base shortly after 5:00 a.m. (0500 GMT) Thursday for an unknown destination.

A defence ministry spokesman said: "The plane will be leaving the UK from somewhere else. I cannot tell you from where for security reasons."

Henrika Harkko, a 24-year-old student from Santiago, studying in London, who took part in a candlelit vigil outside Pinochet's villa overnight, said: "We have demonstrated in vain and now he shall never be brought to trial for the torture and murder of our people.

"It's a terrible disappointment for Pinochet's thousands of victims that he will not face trial in Spain," said Reed Brody of Human Rights Watch.

But he added: "The fact that four countries have sought his extradition and that his claim of immunity has been rejected has made the world a smaller place for people who commit atrocities."
