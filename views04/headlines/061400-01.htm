Going Backwards:
Global Spending On Weapons Rose In 1999
From Tribune News Services
 
STOCKHOLM, SWEDEN - World military expenditure rose 2.1 percent in real terms in 1999 after a long period of decline, the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute said Wednesday.

The United States, Russia, China and France accounted for most of the global increase but countries spending relatively the most on weapons are the emerging economies, the institute said.

Arms spending totaled about $780 billion in 1999.

"While this is almost one-third less than 10 years earlier, it still represents a significant share of world economic resources: 2.6 percent of world gross national product," the annual report said.

Russia's spending rose 24 percent in real terms in 1999 but was still 53 percent lower than in 1992, and most other developed countries have cut military expenditures by about one-third over the 1990s.

"The countries with the heaviest economic burden of military expenditure are generally poor countries involved in armed conflict and/or located in areas of tension," the report said. Armed conflicts in Africa have led to an increase in military spending since 1997.

The amount spent on weapons in Africa is hard to calculate because it can involve the legal or illegal appropriation of national resources outside a government's official defense budget. The institute figured the total international arms trade to be worth between $35 billion and $49 billion.
