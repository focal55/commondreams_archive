Published on Friday, February 4, 2000 in the Madison Capital Times
Clinton Is Considering Sen. Russ Feingold's Request For A Moratorium On Federal Executions
by Aaron Nathans
 
President Clinton is considering Sen. Russ Feingold's request for a moratorium on federal executions, the White House said.

The request is being reviewed by White House counsel, spokesman Joe Lockhart said. But he said that does not guarantee that Clinton would act on the matter soon.

A spokesman said Clinton is "certainly concerned'' about Illinois' decision to halt executions and evaluate how that state handles the death penalty.

"The president was certainly concerned by the issues raised by the governor of Illinois,'' Lockhart said. "If there are legitimate concerns that are brought to us, we will look at the concerns. But I can't predict anything beyond that until we've had a chance to study the issue.''

Feingold, D-Wisconsin, a longtime opponent of the death penalty, wrote letters to Clinton and Attorney General Janet Reno dated Wednesday.

Feingold wrote the letters after Illinois Gov. George Ryan halted executions earlier this week. Ryan cited the high number of death-row inmates later found innocent in his decision to stop executions pending a review of whether the death penalty is being administered fairly.

"We'll see how far we get,'' Feingold said in an interview Friday. "It makes me ill to think we have had innocent people on death row in this country. I think it's a great opportunity for us to not make a horrible mistake at the federal level.''

The last federal execution took place in 1963, but 21 inmates sit on federal death row and another eight are on military death row, Feingold said.

Among the federally condemned is Oklahoma City bomber Timothy McVeigh.

Another, Juan Raul Garza, has exhausted his appeals and could become the first federally executed prisoner in more than 35 years. Garza, head of a marijuana-trafficking ring in Brownsville, Texas, was sentenced to die for the murders of three men he suspected of being police informants.

Feingold introduced a bill in November to abolish the federal death penalty and called on all states that impose the death penalty to cease the practice.

"All my life I have felt there's something fundamentally wrong with the death penalty. It's an exciting development that people are starting to question this,'' Feingold said.

In his letter, Feingold said the government must determine whether the death penalty is applied in a fair manner, free of racial bias.

"Mr. President, in light of the serious questions raised in Illinois and elsewhere, I respectfully urge you to suspend federal executions and undertake a thorough review of the administration of the federal death penalty,'' Feingold wrote.

Since 1976, Illinois has executed 12 prisoners. But 13 death row inmates have been exonerated since 1987 through appeals, DNA evidence or, in a few cases, persistent investigation by college journalism students.

Nationally, 85 people have been freed from death row since 1973, according to the Death Penalty Information Center.

Feingold said that although Clinton has been a supporter of the death penalty, he may choose to weigh other factors in considering a moratorium.

"The president is, in my view, fair-minded and concerned about the administration of our system of justice. I think he's also sensitive to issues of racial bias, which are among the many issues that are involved with the death penalty,'' Feingold said.

Local leaders were energized by the development.

Former Mayor Paul Soglin said it was "a courageous act by both Senator Feingold and the president. I hope the president exercises the moratorium.''

Mayor Sue Bauman said through her spokesman, Ryan Mulcahy: "I oppose the death penalty and would be happy to see a moratorium on the death penalty.''

The Associated Press contributed to this report.
