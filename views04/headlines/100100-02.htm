Published on Sunday, October 1, 2000 in The Jordan Times
Condemnations Pour In Over Israeli Violence
 
CONDEMNATION POURED out from around the Middle East Saturday over what Saudi Arabia called Israel's “brutal aggression” during clashes this week in which 22 Palestinians were killed and nearly 750 injured.

Many focused their condemnation on Israeli right-wing leader Ariel Sharon, whose visit Thursday to the compound of holy mosques in Jerusalem sparked the days of riots.

The area, known as Haram Al Sharif, is the third most sacred site in Islam. Israelis call the site Temple Mount.


The sister of Nizar Ede, 14, killed by Israeli soldiers during clashes Saturday Sept. 30, 2000, cries at her brother's funeral in the West Bank town of Ramallah. (AP Photo/Nasser Nasser)
A Saudi government spokesman said Sharon's visit was “an affront to the feelings of the Palestinian people and the Islamic nation and a violation of the sacred nature of the Islamic places” in Jerusalem.

In Amman, Foreign Minister Abdul Ilah Khatib denounced the “escalation (of violence) and the provocative acts at the sacred Islamic sites of Jerusalem that have infuriated the Palestinians and brought about the confrontations of the past two days.”

In a statement carried by the Jordan News Agency, Petra, he called on the Israeli government to “take the necessary steps to avoid the resumption of the provocative acts that complicate the situation even more” during the decisive juncture through which the peace process is passing.

The Iranian government, which does not recognise Israel and is opposed to the peace process, also reacted harshly.

A foreign ministry spokesman called the latest incidents Israel's “new crime,” and lambasted what he said was “an attack on the faithful, praying and defenceless, martyring several of them, with the sole objective of Judaising Al Qods (the Arabic name for Jerusalem).”

Hamid-Reza Assefi went on to say that the “permanent crimes of this regime are in complete contradiction with the claims of (its) leaders, who pretend they want peace.”

The Arab League meanwhile tried to mobilise support for the Palestinians.

League Secretary General Esmat Abdel Meguib met with Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat in Cairo and said they discused the “situation in the region, particularly the bloody incidents at Haram Al Sharif after the visit by Ariel Sharon, which profaned the Muslim holy sites.”

Abdel Meguid added that Arab ambassadors had been summoned to discuss the crisis at League headquarters here on Sunday under the chairmanship of Palestinian information minister Yasser Abed Rabbo.

Later Saturday, Meguid said the League would consider calling a special summit to discuss the bloodshed in east Jerusalem.

Asked on the Qatari-based satellite television channel Al Jazeera about a possible summit, Abdel Meguid said: “That is effectively what we are hoping. This question will no doubt be discussed during the special meeting of the Arab League.”

“We are going to carry out all the necessary contacts with the head of the UN Security Council and the secretary general of the United Nations. That is what we decided during the tripartite meeting held today,” Abdel Meguid told the television channel.

“It is the visit of the criminal Sharon which is at the origin of the butchery” in the Palestinian territories, which has cost 22 lives in the past two days, he said.

“Sharon, with his dark past, is the criminal of Sabra and Shatila. Today, he sullies the sanctuaries of Islam,” Abdel Meguid said.

Sudanese Foreign Minister Mustafa Ismail reacted similarly, calling Sharon's visit “provocative” and voicing his country's support for the Palestinian cause.

Earlier, the Syrian official newspaper Tishrin called Friday's events a “massacre,” claiming that Israeli leaders “systematically use massacres and assassinations to carry out their expanionist plans.

“It appears as if the failure of Israeli negotiators to persuade the Palestinians to abandon Jerusalem has caused Israel's leaders to lose their heads,” the newspaper continued.

Beirut newspapers took a similar line, considering the “massacre” a declaration of war against the peace process, and condemning Sharon, who led Israel's 1982 full-scale invasion of Lebanon.

The front-page editorial of the leading An-Nahar newspaper said Sharon's visit “was not just to send messages...it was like the bullet that he fired as a declaration of war against the peace process and against all those promoting it in Israel and abroad.”

Lebanon's Palestinians protest killings


Palestinian youth clash with Israeli soldiers in the West Bank city of Hebron Saturday, Sept. 30, 2000. Clashes erupted across the West Bank, Gaza Strip and Arab neighborhoods of Jerusalem in what Palestinian leaders said was a religious war over the disputed Jerusalem hilltop known to Jews as the Temple Mount compound, and known to Muslims as Haram as-Sharif (Noble Sanctuary), in the Old City. (AP Photo/Nasser Shiyouki)
Thousands of Palestinian refugees demonstrated in south Lebanon on Saturday against the killing of Palestinians in three days of clashes with Israeli troops and police.

Lebanon's Prime Minister Selim Al Hoss urged the international community to “act immediately and stop the continuous Israeli aggressions.”

In Lebanon's biggest Palestinian refugee camp, Ain El Hilweh, protesters marched in the streets, chanting anti-Israeli slogans and burning an effigy of rightwing Israeli politician Ariel Sharon.

“Sharon, we will never forget your massacres in Sabra and Shatila. The Aqsa Mosque is Arab and would remain Arab,” read one banner.

Thousands of Palestinians also demonstrated in Bourj Al Shimali camp near the port city of Tyre, burning US and Israeli flags.

“We are ready to sacrifice our souls and blood for Jerusalem,” chanted the protesters in Bourj Al Shimali, carrying pictures of Palestinian President Yasser Arafat.

Fathi Abu Al Audat, a representative of the main Palestinian Fatah group at Ain El Hilweh near the southern Lebanese city of Sidon, urged Arab countries to support the Palestinians.

“We urge our brothers to stand by the Palestinian people in their battle and help them set up their independent state,” he said in a speech.

Thousands of Alexandria students protest

In Alexandria, around 5,000 Egyptian university students on Saturday protested the Israeli massacre of Palestinians, an AFP correspondent reported.

The protesters, responding to calls from the banned but partly tolerated Muslim Brotherhood, marched at three university locations in the Mediterranean city of Alexandria.

The police did not intervene.

Students called on Arab countries to act rapidly after denouncing what it called the Israeli army's “massacre at Al Aqsa.”

Demonstrations are forbidden in Egypt, but are tolerated inside university grounds and are kept under police surveillance.

In Tehran, a leader of the Palestinian Islamic resistance group Hamas on Saturday called for all Muslims to rise up in defence of Islamic holy sites in Jerusalem.

Khaled Mishaal, head of the Hamas political bureau, said “the situation is very grave. It means all the children of Islam must unite to save Al Qods and Al Aqsa (the Jerusalem mosque which is Islam's third holiest site).”

“All the Muslims of the world should rise up to strengthen their control over the sacred sites of Al Qods,” Mishaal told journalists on arriving in Tehran for a visit.

“The situation in Palestine is extremely grave,” he added, referring to clashes with Israeli security forces this week that have left eight Palestinians dead and hundreds injured.

US State Department criticises Sharon

On Friday, the US State Department on criticised Sharon for visiting Al Haram Al Sharif and said the Palestinians also should avoid actions that create tension.

The State Department, which on Thursday called in general terms for a lowering of tensions, on Friday got specific and pointed an accusing finger at Sharon, a former general and Cabinet minister.

“We were quite concerned that the visit by Sharon to this site risked creating tensions,” spokesman Richard Boucher said. “And, in fact, it did.”

“We think it's incumbent upon people on both sides to avoid actions that inflame the situation and create tension,” he said.

As for Palestinian demonstrations, Boucher said “certainly we don't approve of any violence. We don't condone it.”

In fact, he said, “the two sides should be actively working to stop the violence. But also, in the meantime, we need to remember how important it is to actively work to reach an agreement.”
