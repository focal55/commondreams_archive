Published on Tuesday, October 24, 2000 in the San Francisco Chronicle
A Potent, Illegal Weapon Against Unions
Employers know it costs them little to fire organizers
by Steven Greenhouse
 
MIAMI -- Marie Sylvain, a longtime nursing home worker, was so upset about her low wages and workload -- feeding, washing, dressing and changing linens for 14 patients each shift -- that she decided to lead a unionization drive.

But her employer soon fired her, and the organizing drive quickly fizzled as her 70 co-workers feared they would also be dismissed if they pushed for a union.

That was 4 1/2 years ago. Today, Sylvain voices intense frustration that, because of extended legal appeals, she has still not been reinstated even though a judge ruled nearly three years ago that her firing violated the National Labor Relations Act, which prohibits employers from firing or otherwise penalizing workers for supporting a union.

Sylvain is one of thousands of workers who are dismissed, demoted or otherwise punished each year for backing a union.

A recent study of National Labor Relations Board data showed that from 1992 to 1997, employers fired or otherwise punished more than 125,000 workers for supporting a union.

Numerous academic studies show that it often took three, four, even five years for fired workers to be reinstated, delays that union leaders say often cause organizing drives to die at a time when the labor movement is seeking to add hundreds of thousands of members and reverse its decades-long decline.

``U.S. labor law is stacked against employees who wish to organize a union, allowing employers to intimidate and coerce workers not to unionize,'' said John J. Sweeney, president of the AFL-CIO.

But some experts said unions are having troubles attracting more members not because of firings, threats and delays by the labor board but because of economic changes, especially the shift from manufacturing to services.

``Job growth is coming where unions are historically weak,'' said Leo Troy, a labor economist at Rutgers University. ``The economy is leaving unions behind. Employer behavior is virtually irrelevant to labor's decline. Employer behavior is an easy scapegoat.''

Labor's concern about obstacles to organizing is one reason labor is supporting Vice President Al Gore in the presidential race despite differences with him over trade.

In appearances at union conventions, Gore has vowed to push for changes that would make it easier for unions to organize workers.

``Vice President Gore has been the most outspoken champion of the freedom to join a union since Franklin Delano Roosevelt,'' Sweeney said.

A recent report by Human Rights Watch concluded that because of systematic violations by employers and long delays in obtaining legal redress, the United States was violating international agreements guaranteeing the rights of workers to form unions.

``Most people view these issues as just a labor-management dispute,'' said Lance Compa, a Cornell University labor relations professor who wrote the report by Human Rights Watch, ``but we think the focus should be that there is a right of freedom of association recognized under international law, and that right is being widely denied in the United States.''

In the 1950s, several hundred workers suffered reprisals each year for backing a union, according to NLRB data. But Charles Morris, a professor emeritus at Southern Methodist University, said that by the late 1990s, employers carried out reprisals against more than 20,000 union supporters each year. He also found that one out of 18 workers involved in unionization drives faces some type of punishment for supporting the union.

Sylvain said her firing frightened other workers from backing a union at Palm Gardens nursing home.

``They say, `Look what happened to Marie,' '' said Sylvain, who said she was paid $6.50 an hour, so little that she needed food stamps and Medicaid to support herself and her two children. ``The law gives you something with one hand, and then it takes it away with the other.''

But business groups argue that after eight years under President Clinton, the nation's enforcement is stacked against management, not unions.

``Back in the 1950s, unions were fat and happy and represented 35 percent of the labor force,'' said Randy Johnson, vice president for labor policy at the U.S. Chamber of Commerce. ``Now that they represent just 9 percent of private-sector workers, they're blaming the law for their problems.''

Many labor experts say the penalties that employers face for firing union supporters are so modest that it makes economic sense for companies to flout the law.

When the NLRB ruled that Ernest Duval and six other fired workers should be reinstated five years after a nursing home in Palm Beach fired them for leading a unionization drive, the labor board concluded that he was due $1,800 in back pay. The award was modest because federal law requires employers to pay the wages workers missed since their dismissal, minus whatever they earned in other jobs they took after being fired.

The Human Rights Watch report said: ``An employer determined to get rid of a union activist knows that all that awaits, after years of litigation if the employer persists in appeals, is a reinstatement order the worker is likely to decline and a modest back-pay award. For many employers, it is a small price to pay to destroy a workers' organizing effort by firing its leaders.''
