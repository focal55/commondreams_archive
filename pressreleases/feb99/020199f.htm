U.S. PIRG Gives President’s Budget Request Mixed Reviews
 
WASHINGTON - February 1 - The President’s FY 2000 budget got mixed reviews from a national public interest watchdog organization when it was submitted to Congress today.  While the U.S. Public Interest Research Group (PIRG), applauded the President’s request for increases in certain environmental protection, clean energy and education programs, as well as new environmental initiatives, the PIRGs condemned the Administration’s continued support of “polluter pork” programs that fund environmentally-harmful programs, and blasted the Administration for short-changing student aid programs.

“When it comes to protecting public health and the planet, we applaud several of the land acquisition initiatives but are extremely disappointed that President Clinton continues to support polluter pork subsidies to multi-billion dollar corporate polluters,” said Anna Aurilio, U.S. PIRG staff scientist.

Energy and Climate Change Programs:  PIRG applauds the Administration’s efforts to respond to the urgent problem of  global warming by requesting a 28% increase in cleaner renewable energy and energy efficiency technologies.  Initiatives that help states and municipalities make verifiable reductions in their greenhouse gas emissions are also a positive new development.

However, PIRG called the President’s request for an overall increase in the Coal and Oil Research and Development budget and funding for the oxymoronic Clean Coal Technology Program “outrageous.”  The Administration has bowed to the radioactive lobby by asking for an increase in unnecessary nuclear power research programs, according to PIRG.

Environmental Preservation: U.S. PIRG applauds several initiatives to provide funding for open spaces but condemned the Forest Service’s continued subsidization of destructive logging.

The Administration’s Land Legacy is an important first step to protect our wilderness and wildlife and preserve our open spaces.  However, permanent funding is necessary if we are to achieve permanent protection for these places.  PIRG called on the Administration to oppose the current legislative proposals and promote a proposal that will fund important land acquisition and wildlife protections without encouraging destructive offshore oil drilling.   In addition, the funding for encouraging development in urban areas and preservation of farmland is a good first step but not enough to truly encourage smart growth and discourage sprawl-inducing projects.

The President’s budget request includes a much needed increase in funding for endangered species protections, but is still inadequate to fully fund the placement of declining species on the  endangered species list and for designating critical habitat.  U.S. PIRG is cautiously optimistic about the $100 million of funding for salmon protection in the Pacific Northwest, but action still must be taken to save the salmon on the Columbia and Snake Rivers, including the removal of the four dams on the Lower Snake River.

Contrary to an earlier Presidential pledge to protect these areas, the President’s budget request includes funding for logging and roadbuilding in some of the last remaining unprotected wilderness areas in National Forests.  The proposed timber user fee will not begin to cover the true Forest Service timber sale costs, and will only promote the sale of our Heritage Forests to the highest bidder, instead of their protection for the American public.

EPA (Environmental Protection Agency) Clean Water Program:  While U.S. PIRG is pleased that the Administration continues to fund the Clean Water Action Plan and has finally begun to recognize the importance of controlling nonpoint source runoff, we are disappointed that additional focus has not been placed on improving enforcement mechanisms.

Higher Education: The President's FY’00 Budget request cuts Federal student aid programs and abandons the commitment that was made to the families who are struggling the most to pay for college. The President has squandered the opportunity to maintain the Pell Grant program as the cornerstone of student aid programs.  The 4 percent increase in the maximum award will do nothing to restore the lost purchasing power of the Pell Grant.  The President’s budget request uses $2.3 billion in offsets from higher education and student loan programs while freezing budget authority for Title IV aid programs that benefit students.
