Health Coverage Drops In Wake of Welfare Reform; A Disturbing Trend For Working Families
 
WASHINGTON - May 13 - The Food Research and Action Center today praised Families USA's report on declining health coverage due to welfare reform, noting the parallels to recent reports of food stamp coverage declines.

"Families USA's findings that hundreds of thousands of people are losing health coverage as a byproduct of implementation of the 1996 welfare law should be another red flag to those interested in the well-being of low-income working families," said Jim Weill, President of the Food Research and Action Center (FRAC). "The significant drops in health coverage among people eligible for benefits have many of the same causes as the drops in food stamp coverage among eligible families. Either loss is a blow to low-income working families. Losing both is a cataclysm--it means working poor families lack both adequate food and access to medical services. While those outcomes were not intended under the 1996 welfare law, in reality it cannot be called welfare reform if families, when they move from welfare to work, are hungrier and sicker. Both the federal and state level governments must act to reverse this course."

The Families USA report, "Losing Health Insurance: The Unintended Consequences of Welfare Reform," is the first national study to examine the connection between welfare reform and health insurance. It found that as a direct result of welfare reform an estimated 675,000 people were uninsured in 1997--62 percent of them children. Most of those children were likely still eligible for Medicaid and should not have lost coverage.

The loss of health coverage mirrors a trend in Food Stamp Program caseloads, where declines are out pacing drops in poverty numbers. According to U.S. Department of Agriculture data, the January 1999 Food Stamp Program participation average (18,487,582) was more than seven million persons below that for January 1996 (25,897,912). While some improvement in the unemployment rate in recent years has contributed to Food Stamp Program caseload declines, less positive factors account for the major portion of the drops: interaction with the new Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) Program as well as cuts in Food Stamp Program eligibility for some jobless, childless adults and most legal immigrants. (For more information on January 1999 food stamp participation, click here.)

The trends are cause for concern. "Several state and local studies of the status of families leaving cash welfare have found a higher incidence of hunger than when they were on welfare," said Weill.

Emergency food providers see the problems of health and hunger impacting those persons turning to food pantries and soup kitchens. In "Hunger 1997: The Faces and Facts," Second Harvest, the National Food Bank Network, reports: nearly half of the households receiving food assistance with someone in poor health have unpaid medical bills and are avoiding getting needed medical care; households which missed meals are more likely to have health care debts.
