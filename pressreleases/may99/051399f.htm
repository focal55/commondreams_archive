Senate Gun Vote: Despite Littleton, Gun Rights Clout Still Strong
 
WASHINGTON - May 13 - Rejecting public outrage over the recent Colorado high school shooting massacre, the Senate yesterday handed gun rights advocates its first victory in the latest battle over firearms control, nixing an effort to require background checks at gun shows.

The measure, defeated 51-47, had been considered the only gun control measure proposed thus far with any likelihood of passage. Authored by Sen. Frank Lautenberg (D-N.J.), the proposal was an amendment to the broader juvenile-justice bill, which is still currently under debate in the Senate. Yesterday's defeat was a major setback for Democrats and gun control advocates, who had hoped to gain momentum for tougher firearm restrictions in the wake the Littleton shooting.

Republicans, on a mostly party line vote, supported the position of the National Rifle Association, which has lobbied hard against tougher gun show restrictions. The NRA and gun rights groups have traditionally maintained close ties with Congress, spending millions in lobbying expenditures and political contributions to gain clout with federal lawmakers. Between 1991 and 1998, gun rights groups have given nearly $30 million in soft money, PAC, and individual donations to federal candidates and parties. Contributions have overwhelmingly favored the GOP, which received about 74 percent of those dollars.

In fact, the NRA's political clout looks to have played a large role in the vote opposing tougher gun show standards. On average, those who voted against Lautenberg's amendment received an average of $10,478 in contributions from gun rights groups, while those who voted in favor of gun show restrictions received only $297 on average. Of the 49 Republicans who rejected the amendment, 31 had received more than $10,000 in contributions from gun rights groups. In contrast, only three of the 41 Democrats who voted in favor of background checks reported contributions of $1,000 or more from gun rights groups.
