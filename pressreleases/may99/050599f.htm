Greenpeace Says European Union Chemicals Policy Endangers Environment And Health
 
BONN - May 6 - Analyses of the hazards to people and the environment from only 19 of the approximately 50,000 chemicals available on the European market have been concluded in the past six years. Although major risks have been acknowledged for 14 of these 19 chemicals, to date the EU has not taken countermeasures in one single case. At a press conference with science journalists in Bonn, Greenpeace today drew attention to this state of affairs in European policy on chemicals in the run-up to the meeting of EU Environment Ministers next weekend in Weimar.

Manfred Krautter, Greenpeace's chemicals expert, says, "The current state of EU law on chemicals is a disaster. Consumers in the EU come into daily contact with tens of thousands of chemicals and no one knows how dangerous they are. Even substances known to be dangerous are not banned. There is no precautionary protection of the environment and health according to the principles of the European Union."

The Community regulation for assessing and controlling chemicals came into force six years ago. Its aim was to assess chemicals on the market and, in the event of risks, to take countermeasures. The reason for the sluggishness in implementing the regulation is the complicated assessment procedure which, under pressure from industry, has been inflated into a catalogue 700 pages long. Despite having spent up to ten million German marks in analyzing a single substance, only rarely have the escalated standards of the assessment procedure been met. Axel Singhofen, Greenpeace's expert in EU chemicals policy, says, "Without full risk assessments for chemicals being made, the chemicals industry is given a warrant to market them - even though they might well be dangerous." At its present snail's pace, assessment of chemicals in the EU will take up to 25,000 years. This makes acting in a precautionary way on the basis of existing suspicions impossible."

EU chemicals policy has blatantly failed. The chemical industry is marketing increasingly hazardous substances. Soft-PVC products like children's toys contain toxic softeners, computers emit gases from carcinogenic flame retardants, additives in plastics cause hormonal changes, etc. The proposal for reforming European law on chemicals recently submitted by the German Presidency of the EU Council contained only marginal improvements.

Greenpeace is calling on the Environment Ministers meeting in Weimar at the weekend to make chemicals subject to mandatory licensing. Substances which have not been analyzed, or are toxic or poorly degradable, may not in general be allowed. Greenpeace furthermore proposes there be a simplified, less expensive assessment procedure for chemicals, one which serves to protect consumers and the environment as a matter of precaution, and today presented a plan for such a reform.
