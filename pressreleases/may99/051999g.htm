British Columbian First Nations to Present Anti-Logging Resolution at Home Depot Investors Meeting
 
ATLANTA - May 19 - An hereditary chief of British Columbia's Nuxalk Nation, along with a singer of traditional songs, are en route to Atlanta to tell Home Depot shareholders, at the company's May 26 annual meeting, how old growth logging is destroying their traditional homeland. Chief Qwatsinas and the singer Xanuse are leaving their homes in the small fishing village of Bella Coola, located at the top of a fjord along BC's remote central coast, to appeal directly to Home Depot CEO Arthur Blank to stop selling products made from ancient forest wood. Home Depot is the world's largest retailer of old growth products.

"Home Depot is selling away our nation by the board foot," said Qwatsinas. "Loggers are cutting down the ancient trees of our homeland that have been standing since time began. They are cutting down the trees in the valley we call 'Ista,' where our legends say the first woman came to Earth. These are our most sacred places - and everything is being cut down and sold at Home Depot. We are concerned about the destruction of the environment and the animals, and how this affects our way of life as indigenous people."

Canadian clear-cut logging company Interfor, which operates on traditional Nuxalk territory, is Home Depot's largest supplier of hemlock and cedar products.

Unless Home Depot shuts them out, Qwatsinas and Xanuse plan to speak at the shareholders meeting to support a resolution being presented by Trillium Asset Management for Home Depot to develop a plan to end the sale of old growth wood entirely. The two will also voice their support for this resolution at a May 26 press conference in front of Atlanta's Cobb Galleria, where the meeting is to be held, at 9 AM just before doors open.

While many companies have stopped using and selling old growth wood, Home Depot has made no plans to phase out these products. The wide array of old growth wood Home Depot carries includes hemlock, cedar and Douglas fir from the temperate rainforests of British Columbia, redwood from California, lauan and ramin from Southeast Asia, and mahogany from the Amazon.

"When a scant 20 percent of our ancient forests survive worldwide, and only about 4 percent in the United States," said Rainforest Action Network Old Growth campaign director Michael Brune, "Home Depot's continued sales of products made from the planet's last remaining old growth forests is simply barbaric." Old growth forests are forests in which no commercial logging has occurred.

In December 1998, 27 U.S. corporations - including IBM, Dell, Kinko's, Nike, 3M, Levi-Strauss, Mitsubishi Motors America, Mitsubishi Electric America, and others - announced their commitment to stop selling or using old growth wood. Europe's largest home improvement center, B&Q, has nearly completed removing old growth wood from its shelves.
