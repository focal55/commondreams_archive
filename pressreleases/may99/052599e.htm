It Is Rocket Science: Another Aborted Missile Defense Test
 
WASHINGTON - May 25 - The U.S. Army is reporting that a high altitude anti-missile defense system (Theater High-Altitude Area Defense or THAAD) test planned for today had to be aborted due to unknown technical difficulties.

"Today's aborted test underscores what we've learned in the last 6 test failures – missile defense is extraordinarily complex and we cannot risk American security on an unproven concept," said John Isaacs, President of Council For A Livable World, a leading arms control organization. "Instead of rushing to deploy an ineffective national missile defense system, we need to carefully and prudently continue research and development to ensure that it will work."

The THAAD system has cost $3.8 billion to date and has failed 6 out of the last 6 tests. Overall, exo-atmospheric missile defense systems have failed 14 out of 16 tests. A General Accounting Office investigation discovered that the two earlier test "successes" were manipulated to ensure a hit – including heating the target and flying it sideways for broader exposure to the heat-seeking device. Moreover, the tests do not reflect the difficulty in deploying a complex national or theater missile defense that can overcome the full range of decoys and countermeasures possible.

"If a baseball player struck out this often he'd be cut from the team," Isaacs said. "Instead we're signing him to a bigger contract."

"The real key to American security are the arms control agreements that have kept the peace for more than 40 years and led to real nuclear weapons reductions, coupled with the threat of overwhelming, devastating retaliation. Before we deploy any national missile defense system, we need to be sure that it will work every time, that it's affordable, and that it will ensure the safety of American families," Isaacs concluded.
