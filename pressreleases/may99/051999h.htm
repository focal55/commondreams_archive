Toxic Pollen Threatens Monarchs; Gene-Altered Corn May Harm Beloved Butterfly
 
CAMBRIDGE, MA - May 19 - Entomologists at Cornell University have found significant adverse effects in monarch butterfly caterpillars that were fed pollen from genetically modified corn. Since about half of the summer monarch population migrates through the US Corn Belt, where millions of acres of engineered insect-resistant Bt corn now grow, America's most beloved butterfly may be imperiled.

"This is not the first suggestion of potentially severe ecological effects of Bt corn," said Margaret Mellon, Agriculture and Biotechnology Program Director at the Union of Concerned Scientists. "EPA rushed into allowing 20 million acres of this corn to be planted without thoroughly assessing its risks."

The Cornell laboratory study, published this week in Nature, found that monarch caterpillars eating Bt-corn pollen had significantly higher death rates and stunted growth compared with those eating normal pollen. Nearly half the caterpillars that consumed the toxic pollen died after four days.

"The fall migration of monarch butterflies from Canada to central Mexican forests is a natural wonder. For many school children and adults, these butterflies are an emblem of nature," said Jane Rissler, Senior Staff Scientist at UCS. "The loss of monarchs is too high a price to pay for engineered corn."

The Cornell study is not the only one to suggest that Bt crops are far from benign. Swiss scientists last year showed detrimental effects on beneficial insects that ate corn pests that had been fed Bt corn. Other scientists have shown that Bt toxins accumulate in the soil and may adversely affect soil ecosystems.

Some of the 19 species of butterflies and moths on the US Endangered Species List may also be at risk from the toxic pollen--if they eat plants near Bt-corn fields.

The Environmental Protection Agency must take action to protect the monarch butterfly and prevent other adverse ecological effects by denying any further approvals and renewals of Bt corn. The agency must also begin consultations with the Department of Interior about the potential dangers of toxic corn pollen to endangered or threatened butterflies and moths.

"It's time to stop looking at agricultural biotechnology through rose-colored glasses," said Margaret Mellon. "This is a novel technology that poses serious risks to the environment."
