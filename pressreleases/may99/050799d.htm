U.S. Department of Agriculture Study Asserts that Global Warming is a Threat U.S. Farmers Can Afford to Meet
 
WASHINGTON - May 7 - The U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) acknowledges in a new report released today that global warming threatens U.S. farmers and that a "clear rationale exists for taking measures to reduce our global warming pollution." Also according to the report, farmers may even benefit or would, at most, only be modestly impacted by U.S. compliance with an international treaty to slow global warming.

The conclusions of the USDA report, "Economic Analysis of U.S. Agriculture and the Kyoto Protocol," are in stark contrast to doomsday scenarios portrayed by the American Farm Bureau and an American Petroleum Institute-funded report by WEFA, Inc. It criticizes such studies as "flawed," saying they reach "unreasonable conclusions" by predicting energy price increases not supported by reality, and by assuming farmers and markets will do nothing in response.

Global warming is caused primarily by pollution from burning fossil fuels that builds up in the atmosphere and traps more of the sun’s heat near the Earth’s surface. Its likely effects on agriculture, according to the USDA report, include changes in growing seasons, water availability, soil moisture and precipitation, extreme weather events, and the spreading of pests and diseases.

The USDA report analyzed the cost to farmers if the U.S were to comply with the international global warming treaty reached in December 1997 in Kyoto, Japan, by joining in a pollution permit trading system that prices emissions at $14 to $23 a metric ton by the year 2010. It finds that "the resulting impacts on the U.S. agricultural sector are small and in some cases negligible." For example, soybean and rice production might decline by 0.1 and 0.9 percent respectively, and livestock production by a twentieth of a percent or less. These projections do not count benefits to farmers from global warming pollution reductions.

Even before accounting for possible tax relief for farmers, and new chances to supplement their income through conservation tillage or growing crops to make alternative fuels, USDA predicts that net farm income could be reduced only by .5 percent (that is, 50 cents per $100), while consumers would wind up paying an extra .05 percent for farm products (or 5 cents per $100).

"Moreover, even under more pessimistic assumptions – higher tradable permit prices and higher energy prices – the effects on agriculture are far less than asserted by other analyses released over the past two years," says the report, issued today by USDA’s Office of the Chief Economist.

The USDA report makes "very conservative assumptions" about the rate at which farmers and others would adopt more energy-efficient technology in response to slightly higher prices for energy. Assuming the high end of the predicted range for pollution permits, $23 a metric ton, it estimates those price increases at the equivalent of a few cents per gallon (3.3 percent for gasoline, 5.2 percent for diesel fuel, from less than 1 percent to almost 3 percent for electricity depending on the region, 1 percent for phosphate fertilizer, and less than 4 percent for nitrogen fertilizer). Those predictions are far lower than those made by petroleum industry advertising and industry-funded studies.

In addition, contrary to other models it criticizes as less complete, the USDA report predicts that crop markets could be expected to support higher prices for farmers’ crops as a result of any increased production costs. It also finds that "addressing climate change could create opportunities for farmers to supplement their income" through tilling methods that keep more carbon in the soil, or by growing crops used to make fuels, energy, and chemicals.

For example, a farmer could be compensated for conservation tillage, use of winter cover crops, rotational grazing, or planting marginal agricultural lands in trees. In addition, it says, using trees, crops, and agricultural wastes to produce power, fuels or chemicals, could both reduce global warming pollution and help supplement farmers’ incomes.

The USDA report predicts only a "minimal" impact on U.S. competitiveness in foreign markets. "Because the overall costs of compliance will be low, incentives to shift production to countries having no, or less stringent, emissions targets will be similarly small," it says.

"Importantly, this result does not factor in the benefits to agriculture of avoiding the consequences of climate change," the report notes. It says that uncontrolled global warming could force farmers to pay for expanded irrigation and flood control systems, change to new varieties of crops, or even move their farming operations.

While rising concentrations of global warming pollution in the atmosphere "are unlikely to impair the ability of the United States to produce enough food to feed itself and others through the next century," the USDA report says, "climate-induced shifts are likely to reduce the ability of some communities to obtain their livelihoods from agriculture."
