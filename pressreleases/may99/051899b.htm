ICBL to Hold Press Briefing on Campaign to Ban Landmines
 
WASHINGTON - May 18 - News Advisory:

What:
U.S. PRESS BRIEFING -- LANDMINE MONITOR REPORT 1999 BY THE INTERNATIONAL CAMPAIGN TO BAN LANDMINES

When:
10-11 a.m., Wednesday, May 19

Where:
Lisagor Room, National Press Club, 529 14th St. N.W., Washington, D.C.

What:
Representatives of the International Campaign to Ban Landmines (ICBL) will brief U.S.-based media on the Landmine Monitor Report 1999: Toward a Mine-Free World, which was released earlier this month in Mozambique at the First Meeting of States Parties to the 1997 Mine Ban Treaty (a.k.a. Ottawa Treaty). Particular attention will be given to the United States chapter of this report and to U.S. policy against banning antipersonnel landmines.
Who:
Speakers include:

*Jody Williams, 1997 Nobel Peace Prize Laureate. Williams has just returned from the first diplomatic meeting on the ban treaty in Mozambique and from the Hague Appeal for Peace. Williams coordinated the ICBL from 1991-1997, and currently serves as the ICBL's Ambassador.

*Stephen D. Goose, Human Rights Watch Arms Division. Chief editor of the Landmine Monitor Report 1999 and researcher of Landmine Monitor's U.S. country report. Goose led the ICBL delegation to the diplomatic conference in Maputo.

*Jerry White, Landmine Survivors Network (LSN). Chair of the ICBL's Working Group on Mine Victim Assistance. White was injured by a landmine in Israel.

*Holly Burkhalter, Physicians for Human Rights. Co-Chair of the Grassroots Taskforce of the U.S. Campaign to Ban Landmines.

------ Landmine Monitor is an unprecedented initiative by the International Campaign to Ban Landmines (ICBL), 1997 Nobel Peace Prize Laureate. It is the first time that non-governmental organizations (NGOs) are coming together in a coordinated, systematic and sustained way to monitor a disarmament or humanitarian law treaty, in this case the 1997 Mine Ban Treaty, and to assess more generally the efforts of the international community to resolve the global landmine crisis. Researchers in more than eighty nations gathered information to produce this 1,100-page book in just a six month period. To date, 135 governments have signed the 1997 Mine Ban Treaty and 81 have ratified. It became binding international law on March 1, 1999. The United States refuses to sign the treaty until 2006, and only if alternatives to AP mines are found.

For more information, contact: Mary Wareham, Human Rights Watch, 202-612-4321
