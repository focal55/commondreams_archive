Exxon's Avowed Anti-Environmental Position Dominates Merger Talks; Exxon and Mobil Shareholders Show Increasing Concern for Global Warming
 
DALLAS, TX - May 27 - The Exxon/Mobil fossil fuel powerhouse made it abundantly clear today that they are not going to join the growing consensus of business leaders concerned about the threat of global warming.

Pressured by shareholders at today’s meeting, the leaders of both Exxon and Mobil refused to accept responsibility for their contribution to global warming and remained firm in their defense of their participation in public misinformation campaigns.

Shareholders at both companies voted on a global warming resolution that asked the company to report what actions they are taking to address global warming and what potential liabilities shareholders may face as a result of inadequate actions. The measures also asked the company to report what types of efforts the company funds to downplay the threat of global warming.

Despite the opposition of the companies’ boards to this resolution, 5.4 percent of Exxon shareholders supported it, nearly a full percentage point more than a similar resolution on global warming earned last year. Shareholders at the Mobil meeting supported the resolution with 5.18 percent of shares voted.

"This growing support indicates that shareholders are becoming increasingly concerned about the risks global warming poses not only to the planet’s health, but also to the health of shareholder value," said Sister Pat Daly, Domincan Sisters of Caldwell, NJ. "Now both the science and momentum are on our side."

The Exxon resolution comes at a time when a growing number of companies have re-examined their position on global warming. Last year, British Petroleum (and hence Amoco) and Royal Dutch Shell withdrew from the Global Climate Coalition (GCC), an industry-funded group fighting to stall action on reducing greenhouse gas emissions, citing "irreconcilable differences" on global warming.

Furthermore, Ford Motor Company may be may be close to joining BP and Shell in withdrawing its membership from the GCC (See Detroit News, May 9, 1999). Exxon and Mobil still fund the GCC, which spent over $13 million in 1997 on a public advertising campaign against the Kyoto Protocol.

Further isolating Exxon/Mobil as the biggest remaining global warming skeptic, Texas Governor George Bush, lead contender for the Republican Presidential nomination, recently acknowledged that "I'm becoming more convinced that the science proves there's global warming." (See Ft. Worth Star Telegram, May 14, 1999).

"While our competitors are exploring new ways of meeting the world’s energy demands with alternatives to oil, Exxon/Mobil are exploring new ways to cast doubt on the problem," said Father Michael Crosby of the Province of St. Joseph of the Capuchin Order and the lead filer of the Exxon global warming resolution. "We hope that by the time they abandon this Flat-Earth mentality that our competitors won’t have left us in the dust."
