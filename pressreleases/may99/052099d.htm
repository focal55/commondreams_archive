Physicians Escalate Gun Control Demands
 
WASHINGTON - May 20 - "Today's school shooting in Conyers, Ga., confirms Americans' worst fears. There are just too many guns available in today's society to keep our kids safe," said Robert K. Musil, Ph.D., executive director and CEO of Physicians for Social Responsibility, the Nobel Peace Prize-winning organization that in recent years has stepped into the gun control debate. "It is time for the Congress to enact strict legislation now to stop the slaughter and end this public health epidemic," Musil said.

Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR) called today for a ban on handguns, the leading cause of firearms fatalities and injuries. "We supported such legislation in 1994 when only a few Senators were interested. It is time now to act boldly to prevent further predictable, preventable tragedies. And, of course, Congress must immediately pass all of the responsible and rather mild gun legislation still before it. We must stop unregulated private sales of firearms at gunshows and on the Internet, make guns childproof, regulate guns as consumer products, and improve firearm injury surveillance," said Musil. "And in light of this morning's shooting, it may even be time to take a second look at restrictions on certain classes of long guns that obviously are not used for sport." Musil added.

According to PSR current estimates are that in addition to over 34,000 firearms fatalities in the U.S. each year, gun injuries run in the hundreds of thousands annually with total costs including medical care topping $4 billion annually. "Our physicians see too often and understand too well the cost to our citizens in injuries, death, grief, trauma, and medical care over a lifetime that plagues gunshot victims and their families. We must call a halt," Musil explained.

The factors that lead to youth alienation and violence or suicide are complex, states PSR. Nevertheless, easy access to guns throughout American society is the key factor in the high incidence of deaths and injuries revealed by epidemiological data gathered by the Centers for Disease Control (CDC). "It is guns, not television or computers, that ultimately turn what would be schoolyard shouting matches or slugfests into senseless slaughterhouses," added Musil.
