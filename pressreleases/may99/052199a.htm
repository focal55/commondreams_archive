Senate Passes Juvenile Crime Bill S. 254; Wellstone Opposed Measure Over Disproportionate Minority Confinement Issue
 
WASHINGTON - May 21 - Senator Paul Wellstone voted against the "Violent and Repeat Juvenile Offender Accountability and Rehabilitation Act of 1999" (S. 254) which passed the Senate yesterday by a vote of 73-25. Wellstone opposed the bill primarily because it would eliminate the seven-year old federal requirement that states address disproportionate minority confinement (DMC) in their juvenile justice systems. An amendment offered earlier by Wellstone and Senator Edward Kennedy which sought to maintain the requirement was tabled, and effectively defeated, by a vote of 52-48 after lengthy and spirited floor debate. Only three Republicans joined Senate Democrats in support of the DMC requirement.

"A key part of this bill undermines efforts now underway across the land to deal with the disproportionate confinement of minority youth in our nation's jails. Otherwise there is much good in this bill, and but for the gutting of this core requirement on a vital civil rights question, I would have supported it. However, this was a civil rights question and a civil rights vote," Wellstone said.

It is well documented that in nearly every state, minority youth are over represented in every stage of the juvenile justice system, particularly in confinement. Current law directs states to identify the extent to which disproportionate minority confinement exists, to assess the reason that it exists, and to develop intervention strategies to address the causes for DMC. As a result, 40 states to date are implementing or developing promising intervention plans to address this national problem. The Juvenile Crime bill, S. 254, would significantly weaken this requirement, greatly reducing the likelihood that states will take steps to identify and correct the problem. The current law does not require and has never resulted in the release of juveniles, and does not require numerical quotas for arrest or release of any youth from custody based on race.

In a series of questions put to Republican opponents of the existing DMC requirement, Wellstone asked whether "when the police are out there in the streets, and we get to which kids are searched on the streets and which kids are not, you don't think that has anything to do with race? When we get to the question of which kids are arrested and which kids are not, you don't think that has anything to do with race today in America? When we get to the question of the evaluation of youth by probation officers, you don't think that has anything to do with race? When we get to the question of the decision whether to release or detain by a judge, based upon who has the money and who does not have the money to put up a bond, you don't think that has anything to do with race? And when we get to the question of sentencing, you don't think that has anything to do with race, Senators? You are sleepwalking through history," Wellstone declared.

Following the vote, Wellstone said that he will continue to speak out and advocate on the issue of disproportionate minority youth confinement in America despite the passage of S. 254. He expressed hope that the elimination of the requirement will be dropped in the conference report after the House takes up the Juvenile Crime Bill (S. 254).

Wellstone offered several other amendments in an effort to improve the Juvenile Crime bill during the course of debate. Two amendments from his Mental Health Juvenile Justice Act (S. 464) were accepted as part of S. 254 which would allow states to use existing federal block grant funds for the coordination and training of justice system personnel in mental health assessment and intervention efforts. A Wellstone amendment that was defeated along party lines would have provided resources for the counseling of children who witness domestic violence, and outreach and training of community professionals about the issue of children witnessing domestic violence. A fifth Wellstone amendment to S. 254 which also failed mostly along party lines would have provided matching grants to states and localities to hire 100,000 needed school counselors, 21,000 school psychologists and 20,000 school social workers. This measure would have helped the country reach the minimum student-to-counselor ratio as defined by the American School Health Association, and was endorsed by the U.S. Conference of Mayors.
