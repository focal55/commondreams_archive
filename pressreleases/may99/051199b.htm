Non-Proliferation, Test Ban Efforts in Jeopardy One Year After Indian & Pakistani Nuclear Blasts
 
UNITED NATIONS, NEW YORK - May 11 - The United States risks having substantially reduced influence in its efforts to promote the non-proliferation of nuclear weapons in South Asia and elsewhere, if it fails to ratify the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT) by this fall, when an important international conference on the treaty's entry into force will be convened, according to a new report released today.

The report, Accelerating Entry into Force of the Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty: The Article XIV Special Conference, recommends consideration of the imposition of economic sanctions upon any country that tests in the future, among other measures. The report's primary author, Ambassador George Bunn, is former general counsel to the U.S. Arms Control Disarmament Agency. He also helped negotiate the nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), which is the focus of a major international meeting beginning this week at the UN.

The report includes an in-depth analysis of the purpose, procedure, and power of the Special Conference on accelerating the CTBT's entry into force, provided for in Article XIV of the treaty. Only those countries that have signed and ratified the Treaty will have voting power. The report also recommends action by governments in advance of the Special Conference to encourage its entry into force, which requires that 44 specific countries ratify the treaty, including the Permanent-5 nuclear powers, plus India, Pakistan, and Israel.

"The anniversary of the Indian and Pakistani nuclear test explosions should remind all countries that the longer they wait to ratify the CTBT, the sooner more tests may occur. The CTBT is vital to curbing a nuclear arms race in South Asia, as well as renewed American-Russian-Chinese nuclear arms competition," says Daryl Kimball, Executive Director of the Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers and co-author of the report. The CTBT bans all nuclear test explosions and would help curb the development of new and more deadly nuclear bomb types.

"Most disturbing is the failure of the Senate to approve U.S. ratification of the treaty in advance of the Special Conference," says Kimball. "It has been almost three years since the U.S. signed, but prospects for ratification are in jeopardy because of a small, but powerful minority blocking treaty consideration," he added.

Despite overwhelming public support, the endorsement of military leaders, and strong Senate backing, the Clinton Administration's repeated calls for Senate approval of the CTBT have thus far not overcome Foreign Relations Committee Chair Senator Jesse Helms' staunch opposition to bringing the treaty to the full Senate for a vote, or even to hold hearings in his committee.

"The powerful arguments in favor of the CTBT are only as good as our political leaders' will to end nuclear testing and to curb nuclear proliferation. The Clinton Administration must bolster its anemic efforts to press for Senate approval of the CTBT if the U.S. is to ratify," warns Kimball. "The United States' lack of leadership will be very apparent at the Special Conference if the U.S. fails to ratify, which would relegate it to 'observer' status."

Kimball's concern about the failure of the United States and others to ratify the CTBT thus far reflects the views of the 17 non-governmental organizations of the Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers, an alliance which includes prominent groups such as: the Arms Control Association, the Federation of American Scientists, Lawyers Alliance for World Security, Natural Resources Defense Council, Physicians for Social Responsibility, the Henry L. Stimson Center, the Union of Concerned Scientists, and others.

"From now on, it will be the countries that have ratified the treaty that will call the shots," says Ambassador Bunn. "Only those countries which have ratified the Treaty will be able to participate in decisions at the Special Conference about how to accelerate ratification and entry into force of the CTBT, " he added.

"The Article XIV Special Conference can take a number of steps to accelerate ratifications," says Rebecca Johnson, Executive Director of the Acronym Institute in London and co-author of the report. "One measure that can be pursued is to extend financial assistance to some developing countries that have ratified to offset their share of costs allocated for monitoring nuclear test ban compliance worldwide. A wide variety of proposals can be pursued, ranging from an agreement to impose sanctions against countries that test nuclear weapons, to the creation of new incentives for ratification, such as facilitating international monetary support to countries that have ratified the CTBT." The report examines seven areas of action that could be adopted at the Conference.

The report also urges that the first Special Conference is held in New York, rather than in Vienna, in order to attract foreign ministers and greater international attention to the event. Unlike Vienna, every CTBT signatory has a diplomatic mission in New York. Scheduling the Special Conference in New York in late-September or early-October between the General Assembly and the sessions of its First Committee would increase the likelihood that the foreign ministers will participate in the Conference and that it gains international attention.

Since September 24, 1996, when the CTBT was first signed, 151 countries have joined the treaty and 34 have ratified, including 17 of the 44 states that must ratify to trigger entry into force. If, as is likely, all 44 states do not ratify by September 24, 1999 -- the third anniversary of the opening for signature of the CTBT -- the Secretary General will convene the first Special Conference on CTBT Entry Into Force at the request of a majority of the ratifying states. The Conference may be re-convened every year until the CTBT enters into force.

"As the world marks the one year anniversary of the Indian and Pakistani nuclear test explosions this week, let us not forget that the Prime Ministers of both countries have stated that their countries would not be the ones to prevent the treaty from coming into force," said Ambassador Bunn. "The U.S. and other leading states must ratify the treaty in order to help prompt India and Pakistan to join," he added.

"The future credibility of the NPT, which will be reviewed again in April 2000, will be influenced by whether and how effectively the CTBT has been implemented. If entry into force is held up by some of the nuclear-weapon states that committed to pursue it, there will be charges of 'backing out of the deal' from some of the non-nuclear-weapon states to whom the promise was made," the authors warn. "This can only provide aid and comfort to those around the world who may be seeking to acquire or improve nuclear weapons and leave open the possibility that the progress achieved toward a test ban -- both real and symbolic -- will be lost."
