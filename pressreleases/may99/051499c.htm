Senate Approved Limited Gun Show Restrictions; Despite Littleton, Gun Rights' Advocates Clout Still Strong
 
WASHINGTON - May 14 - Reversing its decision from two days ago, the Senate on Friday approved a Republican-backed measure that would require mandatory background checks at gun shows but would also impose a 24-hour time limit in which they can be performed.

However, analysis of today's 48-47 vote -- in which Republicans claimed to have orchestrated a major policy shift - shows that gun rights groups maintained their clout among those supporting the bill. Of those voting in favor of the GOP amendment, 27 lawmakers had received at least $10,000 or more in political contributions from gun rights groups - which signed off grudgingly on the legislation. In contrast, 38 Democrats who voted to kill the amendment had received no contributions from the gun rights lobby,

On Wednesday, Senate Republicans voted to kill an amendment which would have required mandatory background checks at gun shows within three to five days - a standard under federal law. The defeat was considered a major setback for Democrats and gun control advocates who had hoped the recent Colorado high school shooting would create momentum for firearm restrictions.

The National Rifle Association had specifically opposed stricter gun show regulations in its lobbying in recent weeks and months. In fact, the NRA and gun rights groups have traditionally maintained close ties with Congress, spending millions in lobbying expenditures and political contributions to gain clout with federal lawmakers. Between 1991 and 1998, gun rights groups have given nearly $9 million in soft money, PAC, and individual donations to federal candidates and parties. Contributions have overwhelmingly favored the GOP, which received about 74 percent of those dollars.

Republican lawmakers have contended Wednesday's vote against strict background checks had little to do with the NRA's clout. However, the NRA and other gun rights groups signed off on Friday's amendment, which Democrats have called a massive attack on the Brady Bill, federal legislation that first enacted background checks for gun buyers.

On average, lawmakers who voted Friday in favor of limited background checks received about $10,000 in campaign contributions from the gun rights lobby. In contrast, those voting against the measure received an average $1,000 in gun rights contributions.
