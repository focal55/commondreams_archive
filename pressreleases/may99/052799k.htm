Congressional Leaders Cite Test Ban Treaty as Next Step to Dealing With Chinese Nukes; Senator Dorgan Calls for Prompt Senate Action
 
WASHINGTON - May 27 - Following the release earlier this week of the "Cox Committee Report" on the transfer of sensitive U.S. nuclear technology, members of Congress are speaking out in support of prompt Senate ratification of the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT) and are pointing out that the test ban will minimize China’s ability to field reliable, new types of nuclear weapons based on stolen technology and/or its own weapons research. The Treaty must be ratified by the U.S. and other key states before it enters in force and its extensive verification system is in full effect. Earlier this week, a Democratic Senator also made it clear that he will lead efforts to make sure the Senate considers and approves the Treaty this year, despite the fact that the Senate leadership has thus far refused to add it to the schedule.

Two members of the Cox Committee: Representative John Spratt (D-SC) and Representative Christopher

Cox (R-CA) specifically addressed the role of the CTBT in slowing China's nuclear weapons modernization. Senator Byron Dorgan spoke about the need for prompt Senate action on the Treaty. The following are excerpts from their statements:

In response to Tuesday’s release of the Cox Select Committee on nuclear and commercial transfers to China, Congressman John Spratt said, in part:

"In the end, none of our security measures, counter-intelligence efforts, or export controls is likely to be totally foolproof or completely adequate. The Chinese are not on par with the United States, but they are capable, and they do have other technology sources, like Russia. The chief purpose of our controls is to impede and slow down the pace at which China develops nuclear weapons and missiles. One of the surest devices to this end is the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT). I offered an amendment in mark-up to underscore this fact, but the committee did not accept it. Nevertheless, the CTBT would impose major impediments on China, while imposing minor impediments on us. Without saying it, this report makes that fact more clear than ever."

Speaking to reporters on Tuesday, Congressman Chris Cox himself acknowledged the utility of the Test Ban Treaty in preserving our strategic interests:

QUESTION: "Can you address something Mr. Spratt said earlier about including some language dealing with the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty in the report, and having that then taken out? What was he referring to?"

COX : "Actually I didn't hear that. Did he say that at our..."

QUESTION: "He said -- he had said something with regards to wanting to put something in with regards to the test ban treaty."

COX : "Well, what I did hear John say, which I agree with fully, is that the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, if it were enforced, would be a means of preventing the more rapid weaponization and deployment of new PLA nuclear weapons. But at the same time we have to recognize the rather sad track record of the People's Republic of China in honoring the agreements they have signed."

Also this week, Senator Byron Dorgan chastised the Senate for not taking action on the CTBT and hinted that he will lead efforts to make sure the Senate considers and approves the Treaty this year.

CTBT. He said in a Senate floor speech on May 24:

"...the Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty is here in a committee [Senate Foreign Relations] without movement. There were no hearings on the treaty in the last session of the 105th Congress. We are now 5 months into the 106th Congress. I very much want our country to do the right thing: Ratify that treaty before September of 1999, when the committee will be formed of the countries that are signatories to that treaty and who have ratified that treaty, about how it will be brought into force and how it will be verified.

"I intend to work with a number of my colleagues to see if we are able, in the coming weeks, to speak with some aggressiveness on this issue here on the floor of the Senate and, on behalf of the American people, to make the case that we ought to have the opportunity to vote on the ratification of the Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty. We ought to do it soon.

"I have seen the agenda that has been offered by the Majority Leader as to what he hopes to bring to the floor to the Senate before Memorial Day, before the Fourth of July. This is not on it. It must be. It should be. I hope it will be, because this is a critically important issue to our country and to the world."

"The Senate has been derelict in its duty to consider and approve the CTBT and the Cox Committee report makes it clear that America is safer with the CTBT in force than without it. To deny ratification and global implementation of the Treaty is to deny our nation a vital defense against nuclear modernization by China and nuclear proliferation elsewhere around the world," concludes Daryl Kimball, executive director of the Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers.
