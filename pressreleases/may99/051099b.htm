Wal-Mart's 'War on Women Workers' Exposed in Nationwide Protest
 
WASHINGTON - May 10 -Working women across the country exposed Wal-Mart's "War on Women Workers" in nationwide protests the week of Mother's Day, May 9, 1999. The workers asked consumers to give working moms a real Mother's Day gift - a fair and safe workplace - by not shopping at Wal-Mart for Mother's Day.

 While Wal-Mart employs more women than any other retail store – more than two-thirds of its 800,000 employees are women – the company ranked last among retailers in terms of equity and fairness to women by a study promoted by the Institute for Women's Policy Research.

Wal-Mart Wages War on Women Workers

 Fifty years ago, women were second class citizens in the workplace. At Wal-Mart, women face second-class treatment every day, just like they did 50 years ago. Recent court cases reveal a grim picture of sexual harassment and discrimination at Wal-Mart stores in every part of the country. Juries have awarded millions of dollars to Wal-Mart women workers who have filed harassment and discrimination claims against the retailing giant. Wal-Mart's war on working women makes it impossible for working moms to have the dignity and respect on the job they deserve.

Wal-Mart Leaves Working Moms without Health Care

 Wal-Mart's War on Women Workers leaves 425,000 employees, most of them women, without health coverage from Wal-Mart. Only 38% of Wal-Mart employees – as compared with a national average of more than 60% – are able to participate in the company's health care program due to the strict eligibility requirements, huge co-pays, and big deductibles. A Wal-Mart executive bragged that the company is able to shift the cost of insuring their employees to taxpayers – admitting that many Wal-Mart workers get their health care benefits from state or federal government programs.

Wal-Mart's Move into the Supermarket Industry Threatens Women's Jobs

 The supermarket industry is a primary source of retail jobs for women. Half a million women across the country work in supermarkets where they have a voice in the workplace and affordable family health care benefits. Having exhausted their general merchandise retail market, Wal-Mart is making a greed-driven move to take the profits and the good paying jobs of the supermarket industry away from working women. If Wal-Mart is successful in its intended takeover of the supermarket business, thousands of women will face lower wages, loss of affordable health care benefits and the loss of a voice in the workplace.

Mother's Day 1999 – Give Working Moms a Real Gift: Don't Shop at Wal-Mart

 The Women's Network of the United Food and Commercial Workers Union (UFCW) led the protest to mark Mother's Day 1999 as a time to honor working moms by providing a fair and safe workplace, along with decent family medical care and living wages.

"Working moms are the backbone of the retail industry. To truly honor them this Mother's Day, we are exposing Wal-Mart for waging war on working women. Women from across the country have taken action against the sexual harassment and discrimination many women feel on the job in Wal-Mart's stores. Wal-Mart set its sights on destroying union retail jobs by dragging down wages and benefits in the grocery industry. Today, working moms are saying NO! to Wal-Mart," said UFCW International Vice President Susan Phillips.

Working Moms Take a Stand Against Wal-Mart

 The UFCW official expressed pride that, "Working moms are taking a stand. We are giving working moms a real Mother's Day gift and not shopping a Wal-Mart where women are treated like second-class employees."

 The UFCW, with 1.4 million members, is one of the largest unions in the U.S. The union represents over 900,000 workers in the retail industry. The UFCW Women's Network is an organization of UFCW members focusing on issues of particular concern to working women and their families.
