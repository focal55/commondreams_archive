CDF Applauds Senate Passage of Gun Restrictions But Overall Juvenile Crime Bill Falls Short
 
WASHINGTON - May 21 - The Children's Defense Fund (CDF) today praised the tougher gun protections to keep children safe contained in the juvenile crime legislation approved by the Senate, but expressed concerns about the overall bill, in particular the measure's lack of guaranteed funding for programs to prevent juvenile crime and the repeal of provisions in existing law that require states to address the disproportionate confinement of minority youth.

"This legislation takes a major step forward to reduce children's access to guns, which have too often been used in crimes by children and against children," said Children's Defense Fund President Marian Wright Edelman. "Safety locks on new handguns and closing the gun show loophole where convicted felons and children can purchase guns has been long overdue." According to CDF nearly 13 children are killed by guns in America every day, almost a classroom of children every two days.

"It is shocking that the final gun vote, 51-50, was so close," said Edelman. "This is not about the Second Amendment; it's about adult responsibility. If an adult brings a gun into the home, that adult has the responsibility to ensure that children do not have ready access to it. Adults who sell guns also should be responsible for making sure that those weapons aren't purchased by individuals who are not allowed to purchase guns. It is time that the Congress protects children, not guns."

CDF remains disappointed that the juvenile crime bill guarantees less than 35 percent of the overall funding in the bill for prevention measures and eliminates current provisions aimed at fairness in the juvenile justice system by requiring states to review whether higher numbers of minority children are confined in the juvenile system through intentional or unintentional discriminatory actions.

"While funds for prevention activities were increased during floor debate on the legislation, there is no guarantee that such funds will actually be provided. Too often, we have seen the illusion of funding provided in an authorization bill, but very little funding actually appropriated when spending bills are passed later in the year to carry on prevention," said Edelman. CDF cited the 1994 crime bill, which authorized nearly $4 billion in prevention funding, but over the next five years only $150 million was actually appropriated for prevention activities.

CDF expressed disappointment that an amendment proposed by Sen. Boxer (D-Calif.) to increase funds for after school programs was rejected. "Violent juvenile crime peaks between 3 p.m. and 8 p.m., the after-school hours when children are unsupervised and more likely to engage in at-risk behavior like smoking, drinking, sex, or crime. In every community, children go home alone after school. It is unfortunate that Sen. Boxer's amendment, which would have funded after-school programs in every state, was rejected largely along party lines. This amendment would have helped local communities to reduce youth violence by providing mentoring and positive activities for youth after school, particularly in low income areas," said Edelman.

Edelman said, "It is also extremely disappointing that the Senate rejected an amendment by Senators Wellstone (D-Minn.) and Kennedy (D-Mass.) to retain a requirement in current law that states review their juvenile justice systems to see if discrimination is occurring that results in disproportionate minority confinement."

According to the Children's Defense Fund, minority children are twice as likely to be arrested as non-minority youth, seven times more likely to be placed in a detention facility than non-minority youth, and one in seven African American males will be incarcerated before the age of 18. Minority children ages 10-17 represent 15 percent of the age 10-17 U.S. population, but make up 26 percent of children who are arrested, 32 percent of children who are referred to juvenile court, 46 percent of children placed in long-term juvenile detention institutions, and 52 percent of all cases waived to criminal court.

"Studies have shown that a number of children of color have been treated more harshly for the same or similar offenses at each stage of the juvenile justice process -- from arrest to disposition. Our system of justice ought to be color-blind," said Edelman. The Children's Defense Fund noted that the amendment defeated by the Senate was included in legislation passed last year by the House by a vote of 413-14.

"While a number of provisions were improved in the juvenile crime bill before it passed the Senate, we hope that the measure can be strengthened before final action takes place. During the weeks ahead, we will be working with House members for a stronger juvenile crime bill, which protects children from guns, protects children in the juvenile justice system from harm, provides significant investments in prevention efforts, and ensures that states work to implement a color-blind juvenile justice system," said Edelman.
