Monsanto And Novartis Genetically Engineered Maize Harms Butterflies: Greenpeace Calls For A Ban
 
AMSTERDAM - May 20 - Greenpeace today repeated its call for a ban of all releases of genetically engineered plants into the environment as new scientific findings published in today's edition of the scientific journal Nature show that pollen from genetically engineered (GE) maize produced by Monsanto, Pioneer and Novartis, with their Bt-maize, may harm butterfly caterpillars.

The study, conducted at Cornell University, New York, USA, shows that caterpillars of the Monarch butterfly feeding on plant leaves, which were dusted with pollen from transgenic Bacillus Thuringiensis (Bt - a soil bacteria) maize, ate less, grew slower and died faster. After four days, 44 percent of them had died compared to none of the caterpillars that didn't feed on the pollen.

The Monarch butterfly, considered to be a "flagship species for conservation" according to the researchers, is native to America and Africa and occasionally migrates to Europe. It appears quite likely that the detrimental effects of Bt-maize also apply to other butterfly species. Maize pollen can disperse with the wind over long distances.

Neither Monsanto nor Novartis had provided information on the effects of Bt-maize on butterfly caterpillars prior to the approval of these genetically modified organisms (GMOs) and authorities in the USA and the European Commission had not requested them. Both Monsanto and Novartis downplayed the results, claiming that not too many Monarchs lived in maize fields anyway and demanding that the detrimental effects had so far only been shown in the laboratory.

"Such reactions are the precise opposite to precaution and follow exactly the same patterns of denial these companies have employed for decades, when health and environmental effects of their chemical pesticides were exposed", said Greenpeace spokesperson Isabelle Meister. "However, in the case of these GMOs we are talking about living toxins that can reproduce in nature and transmit their dangerous traits to wild species. We cannot consider GMOs harmless until harmful effects are fully proven." Greenpeace demands that authorities in the USA, Argentina, Canada and the European Union take immediate action and prohibit the growing of these GE maize crops.

Previous scientific studies have shown that Bt crops may also harm other insects such as springtails, which feed on funghi and debris in soil and the green lacewing, that feeds on insects such as the European corn borer and the bollworm which the Bt-maize is designed to kill. Further, the Bt-toxin released from the transgenic plants during decomposition may persist in soil for a considerable time.

Bt-Maize is grown in the USA on approximately 10 million hectares this year, minor quantities are also grown in Canada, Argentina, Germany and Spain. It is banned in other European countries such as Austria, Luxembourg and Norway.
