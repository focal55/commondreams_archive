China: A Military Threat To The United States?
 
WASHINGTON - May 26 -With the release of the Cox Commission report, many Republicans are pointing to China as the next great threat to U.S. national security. A number of Members of Congress have been hyping the "Chinese threat" ever since the Soviet Union broke apart and the "Soviet menace" disappeared.

In reality, there is much less to the Chinese "threat" that many are claiming.

While the Chinese have engaged in espionage to learn U.S. nuclear secrets, it still lags far behind the United States in nuclear weapons and most measures of military power.

For example, while the U.S. has 6,000 nuclear weapons capable of hitting China, the Chinese have only about 20 nuclear weapons capable of hitting the United States. Moreover, each of these 20 Chinese missiles are unfueled, without their warheads mated and not very accurate.

In addition to this 300 - 1 advantage in longer-range nuclear weapons, U.S. advanced technology and training provides an added advantage. In terms of power projection, the U.S. can sends tens or hundreds of thousands of our forces overseas in a relatively short time; the Chinese have no such overseas power projection.

China vs United States
The numerical comparisons:

U.S. strategic nuclear weapons capable of hitting China: 6,000

Chinese strategic nuclear weapons capable of hitting the U.S.: About 20

Total U.S. nuclear weapons (deployment, reserve or awaiting dismantlement): 12,070

Total Chinese nuclear weapons: 400

U.S. ICBM's: 550

Chinese ICBM's: 18-24

U.S. ballistic missile submarines: 18

Chinese ballistic missile submarine: 1
U.S. long-range strategic bombers: 174

Chinese long-range strategic bombers: 0

U.S. Gross Domestic Product: $8.1 trillion

Chinese Gross Domestic Product: $639 billion.
U.S. military budget: $270 billion

Chinese military budget: $36.6 billion

U.S. aircraft carriers: 11 (plus one in refit)

Chinese aircraft carriers: 0


(Source: The Military Balance 1998/99, International Institute for Strategic Studies (IISS) Nuclear weapons figures from Carnegie Endowment for International Peace.)
