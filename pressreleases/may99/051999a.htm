Greenpeace: Commission's Climate Change Communication A Two Year Step Backwards
 
BRUSSELS - May 19 - Europe's climate change policy would be put back two years by the latest Commission communication, released today, which should be rejected by the EU Council and Parliament, Greenpeace said today.

"This communication is not so much preparing for implementing the Kyoto Protocol as preparing for another half-decade of inaction," said Michel Raquet of Greenpeace's European Unit. "It will not reduce emissions of greenhouse gases, nor does it promote EU leadership on this issue - leadership which the world badly needs at this juncture."

Racquet said the Commission's communication does not effectively promote early implementation of the EU wide measures required to implement the Kyoto protocol. Instead it relies on vague commitments for voluntary industry agreements "as soon as possible" and even vaguer references to having an EU wide emission trading scheme, possibly by 2005 after possibly having a consultation process in the year 2000, he said.

In 1997 the Commission identified policies and measures for reducing CO2 emissions alone by around 800 million tons, 17 per cent below the 1990 level by 2010. Much of this action was already cost-effective at that time. "Less than two year later, this potential seems to have been dramatically, and seemingly arbitrarily reduce by 25 per cent," said Raquet.

The Commission also uses a figure of 50 Euros per ton of CO2 saved to justify purchasing emission certificates from outside the EU under the Kyoto protocol. "The Commission omits to explain that purchasing emission certificates from outside the EU under the trading provisions of the Kyoto Protocol is likely to actually allow emissions to grow," said Greenpeace Climate Policy Director Bill Hare. The EU has been under pressure from the US to allow the sale of so called "hot air" emission permits by countries such as Russia and the Ukraine, whose economic collapse has drastically reduced its CO2 emissions rather than any genuine policy implementation.

"The Commission has also failed to recommend action to reduce the huge subsidies that are still going into fossil fuel use in the EU and which would reduce emissions significantly if removed," said Raquet. Close to $US15 billion per year since 1990 has gone to the fossil fuel and nuclear industries in subsidies from the EU and Western European governments, according to a 1997 economic analysis for Greenpeace by the Vrij Universitat of Amsterdam.

"If the EU adopts these recommendations they will simply acknowledge its failure to protect their own citizens from the devastating impacts of climate change, and relinquish its leadership on this issue in future negotiations," Raquet said.
