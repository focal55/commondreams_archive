Thurgood Marshall Journalism Award Winners Announced: ABC-TV's Nightline and Eric Bates Of The North Carolina Independent To Be Honored By DPIC
 
WASHINGTON - May 19 -Nightline's superb coverage of the National Conference on Wrongful Convictions and the Death Penalty in 1998, and Eric Bates's investigative piece, "Shadow of a Doubt," for the North Carolina Independent, earned high praise from the judges for the Death Penalty Information Center's third annual Thurgood Marshall Journalism Awards. The awards are presented for excellence in portraying and analyzing the problems associated with capital punishment.

This year a special award is being presented to Clint Eastwood for his film "True Crime," depicting the danger of executing the innocent. Mr. Eastwood produced, directed and starred in this film in which a journalist uncovers the crucial evidence of a condemned man's innocence.

The award in the broadcast media category will be given to Richard Harris (Senior Producer), Terry Moran (Correspondent), Artis Waters and Diana Pierce (Producers) of ABC-TV's Nightline for their in-depth look at the real-life danger of mistaken executions. Nightline, in exploring the unique national conference addressing this issue, combined sharp insight with face-to-face conversations among those who spent years of their lives under an erroneous sentence of death. This piece continues the long and highly-acclaimed tradition of Nightline's thoughtful commentary and outstanding portrayal of current events.

The award in the print category will be presented to Eric Bates, Staff Writer for the Independent, one of the nation's outstanding investigative publications. Bates's article, "Shadow of a Doubt," concerned the conviction and death sentence of David Junior Brown, scheduled to die in North Carolina in late 1998. The article was the result of an exclusive investigation into legal documents covering 18 years, and exposed not only flaws in Brown's prosecution, but in the state's entire system of justice. Shortly after Bates's article, Brown's execution was stayed, with the presiding judge citing information contained in the article. Eric Bates has written for a number of prominent national publications and served as editor of Southern Exposure.

This year's awards will be presented at the Annual Awards Dinner of Death Penalty Focus of California on May 20 at the Miramar Sheraton Hotel in Santa Monica, California. Mike Farrell and Shelley Fabares are the Co-chairs of this event honoring individuals who have made outstanding contributions to human rights.

The Thurgood Marshall Journalism Awards are named in honor of the late Supreme Court Justice who believed that the death penalty would be stopped once people understood how it works in practice. "The question with which we must deal," Justice Marshall wrote, "is not whether a substantial proportion of American citizens would today, if polled, opine that capital punishment is barbarously cruel, but whether they would find it to be so in light of all information presently available." The Marshall Awards are bestowed annually and are accompanied by a prize of $3,000 for each of the categories of print and broadcast journalism.

The distinguished judges for this year's Awards were actor and social activist, Mike Farrell, New York Daily News columnist, Jim Dwyer, and New York Times reporter, Don Terry.

The Death Penalty Information Center is a non-profit organization which focuses its research on the problems of capital punishment in the United States. Entries for next year's awards must be published or produced in 1999, and should be submitted to the Death Penalty Information Center by January 30, 2000.
