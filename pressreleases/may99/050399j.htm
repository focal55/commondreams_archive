More Than 120 Members of Congress Support New Right to Know Bill
 
WASHINGTON - May 3 - Representatives Henry Waxman (D-CA) and Jim Saxton (R-NJ) introduced the Children’s Environmental Protection and Right to Know Act of 1999 today, a bill that would increase public access to information about toxic chemicals in communities, in the workplace and in consumer products. The bill was introduced with the support of 123 original cosponsors, the highest number of original cosponsors of any environmental legislation introduced so far this session.

“We applaud Representatives Waxman and Saxton and the other bill supporters for their leadership in protecting children from environmental risks,” said Allison LaPlante, U.S. PIRG Environmental Advocate. “This legislation will prevent toxic pollution and enhance the public’s Right to Know about toxic hazards in their communities, workplaces and homes.”

The Children’s Environmental Protection and Right to Know Act will expand the 1986 Community Right to Know law by adding information on how toxic chemicals are used in the workplace, transported through communities and put into products, especially those intended for use by children. The bill will add new sources of pollution that are currently exempt from reporting, including solid waste and medical waste incinerators, and the oil and gas industry. The bill will also require reporting on extremely hazardous substances such as dioxins, mercury and lead, which currently escape reporting.

“This bill is being introduced as the U.S. EPA also considers expanding reporting under the federal Right to Know Program’s Toxics Release Inventory. More than 400 local, state and national groups and 35,000 individuals have urged EPA to strengthen their current proposal to include complete reporting on mercury, lead, dioxin and other persistent or bioaccumulative toxins,” said LaPlante.

The toxic chemical “use reporting” in the Waxman/Saxton bill is based in part on a successful law passed in 1989 in Massachusetts. Massachusetts recently reported that between 1990 and 1997 state industries decreased their toxic chemical releases to the environment by 80 percent, reduced toxic chemical waste generated by 41 percent and cut overall toxic chemical use by 24 percent. However, U.S. EPA data show a national increase in toxic chemical waste generation in recent years.

“Strong Right to Know laws in Massachusetts and New Jersey have shown that shining the public spotlight on toxic chemical use encourages companies to prevent pollution,” said LaPlante. “This bill is modeled on tried and true pollution prevention and reporting programs.”

“We urge every member of Congress to join this bi-partisan team in supporting the Children’s Environmental Protection and Right to Know Act and work to make it a top priority during this session,” said LaPlante.
