Scientists Urge Consideration of Humanitarian Truce
 
WASHINGTON - May 5 - The following was released today by the Federation of American Scientists:

In light of the dangers of mass starvation in Kosovo, the Federation of American Scientists (FAS) is urging the Administration to consider the possibility of negotiating a Humanitarian Truce, lasting 30 to 60 days, during which bombing, fighting, and ethnic cleansing would be stopped, troops would stand down, aid workers, food, and medical supplies would be permitted to enter, and Kosovars would be permitted free movement across borders.

The peaceful period could facilitate negotiations on both an international peace-keeping force and a long-term solution and, above all, would help avert otherwise impending starvation.

The need for this truce is becoming ever more evident. On May 2, NATO Commander General Wesley Clark advised the world that 820,000 internally displaced people inside Kosovo are "...largely cut off from any sources of food or supplies, and communications, and living in conditions of hardship and danger." Physicians for Human Rights has said: "Mass death may be imminent."

While such a truce would not be monitored immediately by armed international monitors, there would be the eyes and ears of the aid workers, and the truce would be conditional on the halt in fighting and ethnic cleansing. Perhaps the whole truce could be overseen by the same countries that would be leading candidates for supplying the eventual international forces.

In any case, even an agreement on an international security force might not bring those forces into the field in time to save the lives of families now running out of food -- which could easily lead to the loss of many more lives than are being lost to atrocities. Under these circumstances, the difficulties of a Humanitarian Truce may be less than the difficulties of pursuing the present course or other alternatives.

How far is this from the Administration's position? At the moment, the Administration has put off the problem of the future of Kosovo and is concerned with establishing the conditions under which refugees could go home. But it is not focused on the dangers to internal refugees, which require a greater urgency.

On May 4, the President said "...under the right circumstances, we would be willing to have a bombing pause, but we would need acceptance of the basic principles (evidently of the occupation of Kosovo) and at least the beginning of withdrawal of Serb forces."

The Humanitarian Truce could be organized to fulfill all those conditions except the "basic principles" under which the international force would be designed -- whether, for example, NATO-led, or just with NATO troops, or with NATO troops not involved in military activities, or with no NATO troops at all, etc. And the statement appears to recognize that the bombing halt would not await, in any case, the formation, arrival, and deployment in Kosovo of the international force designed to monitor the withdrawal of Serb forces to Serbia.

Could NATO get the bombing started again, at the end of the truce period, if all went badly? One thing is clear: the opening up of Kosovo is going to provide enormous new evidence of atrocities and signs that many families have permanently lost their adult males. Under these circumstances, one could expect new political impetus to take further dramatic steps, if necessary.

But the bombing is not going well anyway. It does not cause Milosevic physical pain, and he might easily be -- probably is -- more interested in maintaining power and not giving up Kosovo than he is in preventing bombs from falling on Yugoslavia. It may feel good to us to continue bombing -- but do we want to continue to downplay the well-being of almost 1,000,000 Albanians stuck in Kosovo?

If one views Serbia as a kidnapper with ethnic Albanian hostages, NATO is in the position of a SWAT team that has moved -- albeit understandably -- in a precipitous way. The kidnapper has turned out to be more cruel, and more resistant to pressure than expected. The SWAT team feels, in part, that its reputation for good judgment, and success, has become at issue. But, in fact, the immediate problems of 820,000 people must be placed in the forefront of moral considerations.

It is true that the long-term return of the refugees is also a key factor. And it is argued by the Administration that refugees will not return unless the international force is "NATO-led" and/or has heavy weapons, and so on. This may have some truth to it, but it seems one factor among many, and there may be other ways to achieve their security.

In general, it is important to observe that the correlation of forces has changed as a result of the bombing, the displacement of refugees, the political situation here and in Serbia, and many other factors. With this in mind, a Humanitarian Truce would permit a factoring in of these new elements. For example, Serbia is now in desperate economic condition and will need a release of sanctions and new investment. Here is an economic lever as important as military pressures and one less likely to provoke strong resistance.

The Humanitarian Truce we have in mind is not a "bombing pause" but a bilateral stand-down of force and fighting. And it appears to be something that has not yet been considered. And since the situation inside Kosovo is likely to be even worse than we realize -- and new press reports daily are bearing this out -- we want the Administration to give this new approach some consideration if it believes that negotiations are likely to drag on.
