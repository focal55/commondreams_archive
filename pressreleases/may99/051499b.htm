Pocahontas' People Return to Jamestown, VA; Trail of Hope Bolsters Mattaponi Tribe's Efforts to Defeat Reservoir Project
 
WASHINGTON - May 14 - Upwards of one thousand people are expected to converge on Jamestown, Virginia this Saturday to show support for the Mattaponi American Indians who are opposing a reservoir that would flood lands sacred to the tribe and threaten their fishing rights.

"We are returning to Jamestown because here is where it all started. Now, 400 years later, we are still fighting to protect our land, our fishing, our culture," said Carl Custalow, Assistant Mattaponi Chief and tribe spokesman. Jamestown is the site of the first permanent English settlement in America, established in 1607.

The City of Newport News, VA proposes to build a 1500 acres reservoir that would divert as much as 75 million gallons per day of water from the Mattaponi River, just up stream from the Mattaponi Tribe's reservation. The reservation was established in 1658, by treaty negotiated with the Jamestown settlers and later signed by King Charles II of England. The Mattaponi Indians are direct descendents of Chief Powhatan and his daughter, Pocahontas.

The King William Reservoir project, according to Newport News, is needed for drinking water in future years, however, the Tribe disputes that claim. The tribe is concerned that changes in the Mattaponi River's salinity, as a result of the massive water withdrawals, will harm their fishing rights. The Tribe has operated a shad hatchery on the river since 1917, and the fishing has cultural significance to the tribe as well as being an important source of food. Additionally, the reservoir would flood more than one hundred identified archeological sites of cultural significance to the Tribe, including gravesites.

"Newport News believes they can offer us money to compensate us for the harm done by this reservoir. But what is the price of preserving one's culture?" said Custalow. "This project threatens our existence; we must oppose it."

"In returning to Jamestown, we are calling on Virginians and all Americans to speak out against injustice, to speak out against the King William Reservoir," stated Custalow.

The State of Virginia issued a permit for the project in December 1997, however, the reservoir still requires approval from the United States Army Corps of Engineers. A decision by the Corps is expected in June.
