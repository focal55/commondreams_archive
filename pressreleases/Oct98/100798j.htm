Secretary Babbitt Opens Huges Swath Of Arctic Alaska To Oil Development
 
WASHINGTON - October 7 - Greenpeace today called the opening up of the National Petroleum Reserve-Alaska (NPR-A) to oil exploration a move that will further global warming. Interior Secretary Bruce Babbitt released his Record of Decision today making this decision final.

"We are facing a climate emergency, not an energy emergency," stated Dan Ritzman, Greenpeace climate specialist. "The Clinton Administration and Secretary Babbitt know that we can not afford to burn even one quarter of known fossil fuel reserves without risking dangerous climate change. Against this backdrop, it is irresponsible to open up the NPR-A to oil and gas leasing."

Today's decision stands in sharp contrast to Babbitt's earlier statements on climate change. Last fall, Secretary Babbitt sharply attacked oil companies for opposing necessary preventive action on climate change, and has also recognized climate change as "the largest, most pervasive and ominous threat that we have ever confronted in this civilization."

"Babbitt cannot at the same time be concerned about global warming and open up the NPR-A for oil development," said Ritzman. "The Secretary is putting the economic desires of multinational oil companies first, at the expense of the climate, the environment, and public process."

The NPR-A is a 23-million acre expanse of wilderness in the northwest corner of the Alaskan Arctic, and is currently the largest expanse of undeveloped public land left in the U.S. Today's decision opens the door for leasing in the northeast section of the Reserve, an area the size of Indiana. Eighty-seven percent of the land in this section will be made available to oil and gas leases. The Reserve was set aside in 1923 by President Harding, designated for use in case of national energy emergency.

The Reserve encompasses a vast area of rolling foothills, wild rivers and extensive coastal plain wetlands which provide critical habitat for millions of waterfowl, caribou, Arctic peregrine falcons, beluga whales and other wildlife.

The northeast portion of the reserve, affected by today's decision, includes two key habitats: Teshekpuk Lake and the Colville River. Teshekpuk Lake is a globally significant molting area for Pacific brant and other waterfowl. Each year, nearly 22% of the world's Pacific brant population fattens up on lush wetland plants next to large deep lakes. No other comparable area is available to this brant population along Alaska's northern coast.
