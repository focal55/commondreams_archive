Smog Threat ’98: Report Shows Over 5200 Violations of EPA’s New Smog Standard This Summer Groups Call for Cuts in Pollution from Power Plants and Cars
 
WASHINGTON - October 8 - A report released today by U.S Public Interest Research Group (PIRG) and the Clean Air Network documented more than 5200 violations of the new federal health standard for ozone pollution or “smog” this summer.  The report, “Smog Threat ‘98” found that on more than three out of every four days this summer, unhealthy levels of air pollution were recorded in the United States.

“Americans should be outraged that, all too often, their air is unsafe to breathe.   This report is a wake-up call to take immediate action to clean up old power plants and cars,” said Rebecca Stanfield, Energy Advocate for U.S. PIRG.

The report compiled and analyzed air quality data recorded between April and early September for twenty-six states and Washington, DC, where such information is readily available to the public.  More limited data from an additional fifteen states is also included, while nine states failed to provide sufficient air quality data to be included.

“Clearly, air pollution is a nationwide problem affecting millions of Americans,” said Jayne Mardock, Director of the Clean Air Network.  “We have a duty to solve this problem to protect ourselves and our children’s health.” Among the report’s findings are:

·At least 5208 violations of the new health-based smog standard occurred between   April 5 and September 14 of this year.   

·Although data is not available for every site for this time period, the smog standard was violated somewhere in the United States on 126 out of 163 days, or 77% of the days between April 5 and September 14.

·Among the 26 states and Washington, DC where data is available from early April through early September, the new standard was violated this year 19 times more frequently (3847 times) than the old standard (200 times).

·Among the same states, there were 27% more violations of the new smog standard reported in 1998 (3847) than were reported in 1997 (3026). 

·California has had more known violations of the new eight-hour smog standard than any other state this year, with 1025 violations. The following  “dirty dozen” states had the highest number of unhealthy smog days: CA, NC, TX, GA, PA, NJ, MD, NY, IN, OH, VA, FL (see attached chart.)

"More than six million people with asthma live in areas projected to exceed the new smog standard,"  said Dr. Alfred Munzer, Director of Pulmonary Medicine at the Washington Adventist Hospital, and past President of the American Lung Association.   “For asthma patients, a smoggy summer means missed work, staying indoors instead of playing outside, and in too many cases, spending the day at the emergency room instead of at summer camp.  This report demonstrates that we need action to protect those most vulnerable to air pollution," he continued.   

In 1997 the EPA adopted a new, tighter smog standard based on an overwhelming body of medical evidence showing that the old standard was too weak to protect people from health impacts of breathing smog.  Under the new standard, the air is considered dangerously polluted when smog concentrations reach .085 parts per million over an eight-hour period.

“It is not enough to have tough health standards,” said Stanfield.  “People will continue to become sick and die prematurely from air pollution until the standards are met, and this means polluters need to start cutting their emissions immediately,” continued Stanfield.

The groups called on Congress and EPA to take action to reduce smog from two of its primary sources:  the electric power industry, and motor vehicles. Each of these sources emit approximately one-third of the smog-forming nitrogen oxide (NOx) pollution emitted in the U.S. each year.  In September, the EPA took a major step toward cleaner air when it adopted a new rule requiring summertime cuts in pollution from power plants in 22 eastern states and Washington, DC.  That rule will reduce summertime NOx pollution by 1.1 million tons over the next five years.  However, the rule does not reach most states, does not require year-round reductions in NOx, and does not require cuts in the three other major power plant pollutants: sulfur; mercury; and carbon dioxide.  

“Congress should expand upon EPA’s new rule by mandating year-round reductions in the four major air pollutants from power plants across the nation and eliminating the “lethal loophole” in the Clean Air Act that exempts old dirty power plants from emissions standards,” said Stanfield. 

The other major source of smog-forming air pollution is motor vehicles.  By the end of this year, EPA will propose new standards for automobile emissions from cars, trucks and sport-utility vehicles made in the year 2004 and beyond.  Driven by tougher standards in California, auto makers have developed cars that have zero or near-zero emissions.  Moreover, low-sulfur gasoline is available, which, if sold nationwide, would cut smog-forming NOx pollution from motor vehicles dramatically.

"EPA must continue taking aggressive steps to clean the air, starting with adopting nationwide clean car, truck and sport-utility vehicle emission standards that match the tough California clean car program," said Mardock.  "The technology exists for a new generation of low and non-polluting cars, and the public health need is indisputable," she continued.
