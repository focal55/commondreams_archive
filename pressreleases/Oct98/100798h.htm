Proposed Flag Amendment Rejected Once Again; ACLU Says the Measure Dead for the Year
 
WASHINGTON - October 7 - In a tremendous victory for free speech and the Bill of Rights, the Senate today rejected a move to bring the "flag desecration" amendment to the floor with limited debate. The American Civil Liberties Union immediately claimed victory, saying that the failed procedural move means that the proposed constitutional amendment is dead for the year.

In an effort to appease the amendment's supporters -- who had launched a multi-million dollar campaign -- Majority Leader Trent Lott, R-MS, sought the unanimous consent of the Senate to pass the amendment. But Senator Robert Kerrey, D-NE, objected, sealing the amendment's defeat for the year. Congress is scheduled to adjourn for the year this weekend.

"Today's action demonstrated once again that the U.S. Constitution is not for sale," said Solange Bitol, an ACLU Legislative Counsel. "For the second time, the Senate has stood up to enormous pressure and rejected amending the Bill of Rights."

The ACLU said, however, that it expected the sponsors of the measure to be back next year, pushing for an amendment that would allow Congress to forbid "desecration" of the flag. But the ACLU said that no matter the odds, it would continue to strenuously fight to preserve Americans' right to freedom of speech.

This year, in fact, the amendment's proponents had told reporters that the only penalty they sought for flag desecrators was that they receive a ticket "with a requirement that they take a class to teach them about the flag." The ACLU called such a cavalier approach to changing the Bill of Rights -- the document that embodies the country's most cherished values -- deeply troubling.

In urging senators to reject the proposed amendment, the ACLU also stressed the parallels between China, a country known for its violations of human rights, and congressional efforts to adopt flag protection legislation. In one of its first actions after coming to power last year, the China-appointed Hong Kong legislature made defacing the Chinese and Hong Kong flags a crime punishable by up to three years in prison or a fine of more than $6,000.

"Voting to unravel the First Amendment by passing a proposed constitutional amendment to ban desecration of the flag would have instead desecrated what the flag represents and what millions of Americans have died to defend," Bitol said.
