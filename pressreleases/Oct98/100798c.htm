Clean Air Trust Releases Memo on Motor Vehicle Pollution
 
WASHINGTON - October 7 - The following was released today by Clean Air Trust:


As you may know, on November 5 the state of California is likely to approve a far-reaching plan to crack down on pollution from motor vehicles.

A key component of the plan would require that nearly all sport utility vehicles, minivans and other "light trucks" meet the same tailpipe standards as passenger cars. (Right now they don't.)

The car companies are bitterly resisting this cleanup proposal --presumably because the companies make big profits on these vehicles.(If they had to clean up, the companies might have to give up some of that profit margin, or raise prices slightly.) The California Air Resources Board reports the auto industry is making a bigger stink over cleaning up SUVs than it is over a plan to require much stricter tailpipe standards for passenger cars.

We expect the California decision to be a prelude to an even bigger battle over national standards for motor vehicles. By late this year, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency is expected to propose stricter motor vehicle standards, starting in 2004. Once again, the SUV/minivan/pickup issue is sure to be a flash point.

The attached fact sheet summarizes the "light truck" issue. Thanks for your attention. And please don't hesitate to call 202-785-9625 with any questions.

DIRTY DOUBLE STANDARD: POLLUTION FROM SPORT UTILITY VEHICLES AND OTHER "LIGHT TRUCKS"

Most people don't know it, but popular vehicles such as the Chevy Suburban, Tahoe and Yukon are legally allowed to spew out three to five times as much smog-forming pollution as today's passenger cars.In effect, they have a license to pollute.

Why such a double standard?

It's because the government officially considers sport utility vehicles, minivans and pickup trucks to be "light trucks." And "light trucks" are allowed to pollute much more because they were once used mostly on farms and construction sites.

These weaker standards remain in place even though "light trucks"now account for nearly one of every two family vehicles sold -- and even though most of them are used simply for shopping and commuting. No wonder our air remains too dirty.

The Environmental Protection Agency has noted that making "light trucks" meet the same tailpipe standards as passenger cars would bean extremely effective way to reduce smog caused by motor vehicles --in fact, as effective as cutting pollution from "low- emission" vehicles in half.

The EPA may set tougher standards for light trucks as part of a plan to set new controls for passenger vehicles starting in model year 2004. But the car companies are fighting any effort to make them clean up their act. (By the way, they also keep making bigger and bigger vehicles in part to evade pollution controls.)

It doesn't have to be this way. These bigger vehicles pollute more because the car companies simply use cheaper and less effective pollution control devices.

The American public expects EPA to protect public health. This means requiring the car companies to make SUVs, minivans and pickup trucks as clean as passenger cars. It's time to end this dirty double standard.

The vehicle pollution standards are complex, but essentially the heavier the vehicle, the more it's allowed to pollute. A big Suburban, for example, can emit nearly three times as much nitrogenoxides (a main cause of smog), 56 percent more hydrocarbons(including cancer-causing chemicals) and 47 percent more carbon monoxide (which can cause heart problems) than even the heaviest passenger cars.

Even most lightweight sport utility vehicles are dirtier than automobiles. For example, a lightweight SUV can legally emit 75 percent more nitrogen oxides than a big passenger car. These SUVs, minivans and pickup trucks probably will pollute more even if the car companies brand them as "low-emission" vehicles.
