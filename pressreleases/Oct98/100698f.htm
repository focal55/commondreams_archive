Sierra Club Gives Sen. Al D'Amato A 21 Balloon Salute; Environmental Group Says "D'Amato is Full of Hot Air"
 
WASHINGTON - October 6 - Appalled at Senator Al D'Amato's (R-NY) attempts to greenwash his record on clean air this election year, Sierra Club activists gave the Senator a 21 'hot air' balloon salute today. After years of consistently voting against clean air protections, Senator D'Amato attempted to portray himself as a leader on this critical issue by holding a hearing on his stalled acid rain prevention bill -- a bill which D'Amato has allowed to languish since its introduction. Activists were so outraged by D'Amato's grandstanding and green scamming, they delivered a bouquet of 21 balloons which read "D'Amato's Hot Air" to his office.

"Al D'Amato has a long record of votes against America's air, and now he's trying to cover them up with an election-year fig leaf," said Kathryn Hohmann, Sierra Club's Director of Environmental Quality. "Senator D'Amato is polluting the democratic process with his hot air by claiming to be an environmental leader.  This isn't leadership -- this is a cynical election year stunt."

Senator D'Amato has a long history of votes against clean air and public health. In 1995, he supported regulatory reform legislation that would make it nearly impossible to implement new clean air safeguards. And he's voted to slash the budget for enforcement of clean air laws -- in 1995 he voted to cut 1.5 billion in funding from the Environmental Protection Agency, the key agency in charge of enforcing clean air rules. D'Amato also opposed a "right to know" measure that would help inform Americans about the toxic pollution emitted by big companies in the air we breathe.  And in 1990, Sen. D'Amato voted to gut the enforcement program at the heart of the Clean Air Act.

"Senator D'Amato should spend less time talking about clear air and more time working to protect it," said Hohmann. "This is a classic case of  election year environmentalism."

An analysis of Senator D'Amato's voting record on all environmental issues, as tracked by the non-partisan League of Conservation Voters, reveals a record that's spotty at best. And it tends to peak every six years, right in time for elections.
