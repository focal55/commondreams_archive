Dirty Four Told To Stay Out Of Arctic Refuge; Watchdog Group Details Pattern Of Pollution And Environmental Irresponsibility
 
WASHINGTON - October 13 -

The four companies that want to drill in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge have a dirty and dangerous track record and should pledge not to drill there, according to a new report released today by the Public Interest Research Groups (PIRGs).

“The Dirty Four: The Case Against Letting British Petroleum, Arco, Exxon and Chevron Drill in the Arctic Refuge,” documents a long list of spills, leaks, accidents, violations, and explosions.  It focuses on the four companies that have expressed interest in opening up the coastal plain of the Refuge for oil drilling and exploration.

“Our report shows that BP, Arco, Exxon and Chevron are some of the world’s worst polluters,” said Athan Manuel, director of the U.S. PIRG’s Save the Arctic Wilderness campaign.  “Their dirty track record is incompatible with an area as pristine and fragile as the Arctic Refuge.”

Each company pledges to protect the environment and respect the rights of native peoples.   For instance, BP pledges “no accidents, no harm to people, and no damage to the environment,” and Chevron boasts of a “commitment to preserving the culture and environment of the community  they [Chevron employees] share.”    But this brief list of pollution incidents and safety hazards casts serious doubt upon any such claim.

  • A BP subcontractor near Prudhoe Bay was ordered to pay a $15 million fine for violating the Clean Water Act.  The BP subcontractor was found guilty of illegally injecting hazardous waste back into the groundwater at the company’s Endicott Oil Field.  BP had touted Endicott as an “environmentally friendly” oil field.
  • BP is responsible for the second largest oil spill in California history, a 400,000 gallon spill that covered twenty square miles near Huntington Beach, California, in 1991.
  • Arco is a Potential Responsible Party (PRP) for three toxic waste Superfund sites in Prudhoe Bay.  Arco is responsible for  the nation’s largest Superfund site, in Montana, and recently agreed to pay the state $215 million in damages and fines. 
  • Twenty eight people have died as a result of explosions or fires at Arco facilities.
  • Almost ten years after the Exxon Valdez oil spill,  the worst ecological disaster in U.S. history, most wildlife populations show little or no sign of recovery.
  • In February 1998, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) accused Exxon of nearly 200  violations of the Clean Air Act, and demanded $4.7 million in fines.
  • The President of Chevron U.S.A. appeared in federal court in May 1992 to plead guilty to 65 violations of the Clean Water Act and pay $8 million in fines, for illegal discharges from the company's offshore oil- and gas-production platform "Grace" off the California Coast.
  • Chevron has paid more than $70 million in fines, settlements, and penalties since 1980, and is a  PRP for more Superfund sites than any other oil company.

“The public needs to know that behind the oil industry rhetoric lurks a long list of corporate irresponsibility,” continued Manuel.   “ Today we call on the oil industry to finally act as good corporate citizens and cancel their drilling plans for the Refuge.”

In addition to urging companies to cancel their drilling plans, PIRG is asking each company to endorse the Arctic Refuge Wilderness Bill.  The bill would designate the coastal plain as wilderness, and prohibit any development or drilling.

The coastal plain of the Arctic Refuge is one of  America’s last unspoiled places. It remains untouched by roads, development or trails. The coastal plain’s 1.5 million acres are the biological heart of the Refuge, and support large populations of caribou, musk oxen, bears, wolves, and migratory birds. It is also the only area along the entire American Arctic not open for oil and gas drilling.

The Refuge is also the home of the Gwich’in people.  The Gwich’in, which means people of the caribou, have lived in and around the Refuge for 20,000 years.    They depend on the caribou for food, shelter, and as a link to their traditional way of life.   According to the Department of the Interior, oil drilling in the Refuge would harm the caribou herd.

The report can be found at PIRG’s web site: http://www.pirg.org.

U.S. PIRG is the national lobbying office for the state Public Interest Research Groups.  PIRGs are non-partisan, non-profit environmental and consumer watchdog organizations that are active across the country.
