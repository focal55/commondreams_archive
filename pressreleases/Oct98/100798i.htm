House Rejects Congressional Leadership's Dangerous Wilderness Legislation Omnibus Public Lands Bill Fails by Vote of 302-123 in the House
 
WASHINGTON - October 7 - Some of America's last wilderness areas are still off-limits to developers -- at least for another couple of months. By a convincing margin, the House of Representatives today voted 302-123 to reject a 481-page Omnibus public lands bill filled with provisions that threatened our National Forests, opened sensitive wildlife areas to development, and threatened wilderness in our National Parks.

"We are very pleased that the House has turned back the Congressional leadership's efforts to undermine our environmental laws and open up our last wilderness areas to development. The American people want to see our National Parks, forests and wilderness protected for future generations, not sold to the highest bidder," said Melanie Griffin, director of Sierra Club's Land Protection Program.

"The Sierra Club is particularly pleased that Representative Sherwood Boehlert (R-NY) stood firm in his opposition to this dangerous piece of legislation and led many mainstream Republicans in protecting the environment," added Griffin. "This was a great victory for America's wilderness and public lands. Representative Boehlert along with wilderness champions Representative George Miller (D-CA) and Bruce Vento (D-MN) deserve great credit for ensuring that this bad bill did not pass."

Just days before the Congress was set to adjourn, Representative Jim Hansen (R-UT) introduced H.R. 4570 -- the Omnibus National Parks and Public Lands Act -- which bundled scores of initiatives affecting America's parks, forests, and public lands into one bill.   Bypassing the normal legislative process, the bill -- which bunched legitimate and destructive legislation together -- was brought to the floor today without any hearings or committee action and with only one hour of debate.

"This is not the way to do the nation's business," said Griffin. "Their only hope of passing unpopular provisions to clearcut forests, pave wilderness, and override environmental laws is to bury them deep in Omnibus bills like the one that was rejected today," she explained. "We are hopeful that today's vote will send a strong message to the Congressional leadership.  Hidden or exposed, bills that undercut our environmental protections will be defeated.

Among the provisions that conservationists object to in H.R. 4570 are measures that would remove protection from over 125,000 acres of currently protected wild lands in Utah, Georgia and Alaska, allow mining, roads, an airport and a hydropower project on sensitive public lands, and grant easements and rights of ways across many protected public lands, including some of the most sensitive wildlife habitat in Alaska.

"We hope that the Congressional leadership will work constructively to pass the popular measures in this bill, and will abandon their efforts to undermine environmental laws," concluded Griffin.
