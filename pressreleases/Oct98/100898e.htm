Sierra Club Condemns failure To Provide Equal Contraceptive Coverage
 
WASHINGTON - October 8 - The Sierra Club today denounced the House of Representatives for its failure to provide contraceptive coverage to federal employees.  An amendment to the Treasury Department appropriations conference report, offered by Rep. Steny Hoyer (D-MD) would have rectified a gross disparity in health care coverage between female and male federal workers. The amendment, which failed ona 202-226 vote, would have ensured that health insurance plans that cover prescription drugs and devices cannot exclude prescription contraceptives from their coverage.

"This is an issue of equity and fairness, as well as environmental sustainability," emphasized Carl Pope, Executive Director of the Sierra Club. "The Sierra Club believes that universal access to a full range of family-planning and reproductive health services, in tandem with programs that empower women, are necessary to achieving a stable population level in the U.S. and abroad so that we can protect our environment for future generations."

A recent report noted that U.S. women earn 75 cents for every dollar that men earn, yet women of child-bearing age pay 68% more than men in health care costs --- mostly related to reproductive health care. Women are earning less, but paying more in health care costs.

"Insurance companies are subsidizing population growth by paying for Viagra, yet many refuse to cover the contraception that women need to help them plan their families," stressed Pope. "It would be laughable if it weren't such a gross example of gender inequity."

Currently, a large majority of health plans used by federal workers cover prescription drugs and devices, yet exclude prescription contraceptives from coverage.  The inadequacy of contraceptive coverage through current Federal Employee Health Benefit plans is clear --- 80% of Federal Employee Health Benefit plans do not cover the leading reversible birth control methods --  oral contraceptives, diaphragm, IUD, Depo-Provera, or Norplant.

"Making contraceptives affordable will prevent unintended pregnancies and ensure a more sustainable domestic population level which is important for the U.S. environmental health," Pope added. More than half of the 6 million pregnancies that occur in the U.S. each year are unintended.

The amendment to provide for contraceptive coverage was introduced by Rep. Nita Lowey (D-NY) and originally passed both the House and Senate.  The amendment was removed from the bill in the conference committee that reconciles differences between House and Senate versions of legislation.  Tonight's vote would have instructed the conference committee to put this critical environmental amendment back into the Treasury Department appropriation for 1999.

     "We applaud the leadership of Nita Lowey for introducing this legislation,  and for standing up for gender equity and a sustainable population," concluded Pope. "Too bad that her fellow Representatives did not follow her fine example."
