World Trade Organization Ruling Called A Blow to Endangered Sea Turtles
 
WASHINGTON - October 12 -

Editor's Note--The World Trade Organization today issued its final ruling on a US law protecting endangered sea turtles from being drowned in shrimp nets. The WTO initially ruled against the US turtle protection law last April, saying it was an unfair restraint of trade. The US Trade Representative appealed and today that appeal was denied. The following is a statement by David Schorr, Sustainable Commerce Program Director, World Wildlife Fund:

The WTO panel ruling in the "Shrimp-Turtle" case constitutes a blow to endangered sea turtles, and against the development of international environmental norms and practices. The law condemned by the WTO is a legitimate, non-protectionist measure to help prevent the extinction of a critical marine animal. The U.S. acted in accordance with existing international environmental laws, and in a manner consistent with efforts to build cooperative international responses to environmental crises. In this context, the Appellate Body ruling reveals the profound bias of the WTO against environmental policies, and in favor of "free trade" at any cost.

The law at issue is an important part of U.S. efforts to encourage the use of "turtle excluder devices" wherever sea turtles are in danger of drowning in shrimp trawling nets. These devices-known as TEDs-are simple, inexpensive, and proven effective. The U.S. has been working for several years to establish international agreements regarding the adoption of TEDs, and has provided foreign aid to support their purchase and use abroad.

As set out in amicus briefs prepared by WWF, U.S. actions in this case were consistent with emerging international legal norms requiring the conservation of sea turtles and other endangered marine life. Sea turtles are given the highest level of protection by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES), and are further protected under the U.N. FAO Code of Conduct on Responsible Fisheries and under the Bonn Convention on Protecting Migratory Species of Wild Animals, among others. But the WTO-operating behind closed doors-refused to receive WWF's brief, and has issued a ruling that {virtually ignores} {discounts} international environmental law and the means by which it is developed.

The Appellate Body ruling also perpetuates a contorted interpretation of the WTO's own charter. Article XX of the WTO Agreement plainly states that governments are free to limit trade in non-protectionist ways when doing so is necessary to protect the environment. But the WTO remains an institution captured by the special interests of multinational corporations and free trade technocrats. Today's ruling proves that the WTO is simply not competent to decide issues that require a mature balance between liberalized trade and the pursuit of other legitimate policy goals.

The way we trade and invest across borders increasingly determines the quality of life on Earth-for people, for turtles, and for our entire biosphere. But the WTO remains badly off course. The Clinton Administration has repeatedly promised to help build an international trade regime that does not sacrifice environmental and social values to the dogma of free trade. The time has come for renewed White House leadership and for prompt action to make this promise a reality.
