The Senate: Who Does It Represent?
 
WASHINGTON - January 8 -

ALVIN THORNTON, athornton@fac.howard.edu
Chair of the Political Science Department at Howard University, Thornton said: "The U.S. Senate is unrepresentative of the population it's supposed to represent. In the early days, they were appointed by state legislatures; today, they are elected by the people, but they remain distant from them because of the money factor."

MATT KELLER, mkeller@commoncause.org
http://www.commoncause.org
A lobbyist at Common Cause, Keller said: "The central problem is the amount of money that it takes to get into the Senate. The average winning campaign costs $4.5 million -- generally, the person with the most money wins. A senator needs to raise $15,000 per week, every week for six years, to get re-elected. The result of this is that it is becoming a rich man's club. You either have to be rich or know a lot of wealthy people to get in. Forty percent of the Senate are millionaires, while the average income of a family of four is $35,000 per year. It was never quite as disproportionate as it is now. And if you're not rich, you have to spend time with a lot of people who are, and that doesn't give you a real world view. We say we have representative democracy, but we don't."

BARBARA REYNOLDS

A syndicated columnist, Reynolds said: "There are no African Americans in the Senate, yet African Americans are a subtext and a pretext on virtually every issue. Andrew Johnson was impeached largely because he didn't want to give them their 40 acres and a mule. Rehnquist, who will preside over the Clinton trial, has written in favor of the `separate but equal' argument which banned us from most major institutions of society. Strom Thurmond became a Republican because he didn't want civil rights. Clinton is being judged almost exclusively by whites -- some of whom are themselves racists."

JOHN GLOSTER, vhns60a@prodigy.com
http://pages.prodigy.com/DCSTATEHOOD
The Chair of the D.C. Statehood Party, Gloster said: "The citizens of the District of Columbia do not have a vote or a say in the entire impeachment process -- or on any issue of national importance, even though D.C. has a larger population than Wyoming. If D.C. had representation in the Senate, there would likely be at least one African American in the Senate. Even our aspirations on our local affairs are often overruled by the Senate or the House of Representatives. Most recently, D.C. citizens voted on a referendum regarding the legalization of marijuana for medical use. Based on exit polls, it won overwhelmingly, but it was quashed by Rep. Bob Barr (R-Ga.) who would not even allow the votes to be counted."
