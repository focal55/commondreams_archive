Physicians Call for Stronger U.S. Leadership on Toxic Chemicals
 
WASHINGTON - January 8 - Delegates from more than 100  countries will meet next week in Nairobi, Kenya, to participate in  the Intergovernmental Negotiating Committee on Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs). This is the second of five meetings scheduled to conclude in the year 2000 with a legally binding international treaty that will govern the way that countries use and dispose of toxic chemicals.

According to Physicians for Social Responsibility (PSR), the U.S. is dragging its heels on several key issues. POPs are linked to numerous health problems including cancers, birth defects, immune suppression, and reproductive disorders. Health professionals want the U.S. to take the lead in calling for the phase-out and ultimate elimination of the initial 12 pollutants listed in the treaty, including dioxin, PCBs and the pesticide DDT.

"The U.S. is not exerting the leadership needed to get rid of these harmful chemicals once and for all," said PSR environment director Sharon Newsome. "The goal of the treaty must be POPs elimination. The U.S. should lead the way in this worldwide effort to protect the public health."

In particular, PSR is calling for the use of DDT to be phased out while reducing the scourge of malaria at the same time. "As a physicians group, we understand that it is important to address both of these problems together." PSR is also calling on the U.S. and other industrialized countries to commit the necessary financial assistance to help developing countries, especially in areas such as malaria control projects and research on drugs.

PSR serves as Secretariat for the International POPs Elimination Network (IPEN), a group that now includes more than 125 NGOs on six continents. IPEN leaders, including PSR staff members Sharon Newsome and Karen Perry, will represent NGO concerns at negotiations in Nairobi. "We know that putting alternatives in place to phase out some of the 12 POPs will take time and may be expensive, but it will greatly improve the health of present and future generations," said Karen  Perry, associate director of PSR and coordinator of the IPEN coalition. "Getting a good agreement down on paper -- one that has as its goal the phase-out of these chemicals by specified and reasonable dates -- will drive the implementation of safe and effective  alternatives."
