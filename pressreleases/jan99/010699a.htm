New Web 'Member Profiles' Identify The Money Behind the 106th Congress
 
WASHINGTON - January 6 - Will 1998's big campaign contributors get their money's worth from the 106th Congress?

As the new Congress convenes today, the non-partisan Center for Responsive Politics unveils a free Internet resource at www.crp.org  that will help reporters and the general public answer that question for themselves.

The Congressional Campaign Finance Profiles show in an easy-to-follow graphic format where members of the new Congress got the money to run their campaigns.

Sources of campaign contributions based on the most current Federal Election Commission data are displayed by industry and geography. The profiles break down the amounts raised from political action committees and individual donors of at least $200 and list the top overall contributors to every lawmaker.

Scorecards showing how well each member complied with the legal requirement to identify the employer and occupation of individual contributors are provided.

The profiles also link to images of the personal financial disclosure filings for every member who served in the 105th Congress, as well as summaries of contributions by any leadership PACs a member is affiliated with. Links to the legislation each member sponsors and a link to send e-mail to the lawmaker are provided. Voters who don't know who represents them can search for their lawmaker by zip code.

The feature packages the most current data in a user-friendly format. Other highlights include:

-- Expanded detailed lists of the top contributors to each lawmaker, as well as the top industries that gave to each member.

-- The addition of profiles for the non-voting House delegates representing the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, American Samoa, Guam, and the U.S. Virgin Islands.

-- A list of all new members of the House and Senate.

"These profiles let anyone - anywhere in the country - keep tabs on their own representatives in Congress," said Larry Makinson, the Center's executive director. "By profiling each member's cash constituents, we're making it possible for voters back in the districts to see who else their lawmakers in Washington may be listening to."

The Center's Website at www.crp.org also provides searchable databases that allow users to do their own campaign finance research, as well as a searchable lobbying database with all federal lobbying expenditures in 1997 - the last full year for which data is available. The Center's reports, publications, and Capital Eye newsletter also are available online.
