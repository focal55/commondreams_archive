Texas Tops List of Most Anti-Choice States
 
WASHINGTON - July 6 - The Texas Legislature, led by GOP frontrunner George W. Bush, enacted more anti-choice laws than any other state in the country thus far in 1999, according to a new mid-year analysis of state legislative activity on reproductive rights produced by The National Abortion and Reproductive Rights Action League (NARAL) Foundation.

The analysis showed that the Texas Legislature, which passed seven anti-choice bills in the 1999 session alone, tops a list of the country's most actively anti-choice state legislatures that includes Virginia, Arizona, Iowa and Florida.

"George W. Bush and the Texas Legislature have waged a relentless assault on the freedom to choose, stripping away women's reproductive freedom and curtailing their options at virtually every turn," said NARAL President Kate Michelman. "Yet, as a candidate, Bush adopts a centrist facade, insisting he is no threat to the freedom to choose. As the record now shows, Bush and the Texas Legislature are indeed leaders when it comes to restricting the reproductive rights of women."

Among the report's other findings:

-- The level of anti-choice activity among state legislatures as a whole remains high, with 408 anti-choice measures being introduced thus far in 1999, up from the 335 introduced in all of 1998. The number of anti-choice measures enacted thus far (45) is also on pace with last year's mid-year count (46).

-- Anti-choice lawmakers are using a wide variety of methods to restrict reproductive choice for American women. Whereas most states already have numerous restrictions on reproductive choice already on the books, including parental involvement laws and bans on public funding, anti-choice state legislators have turned their attention to other ways in which to restrict choice, such as bills that single out abortion providers for special regulation.

-- Pro-choice legislators are achieving more success in promoting proactive legislation designed to reduce unintended pregnancy and the number of abortions, with 291 pro-choice measures introduced thus far in 1999, up from 130 in all of 1998. Similarly, there have been 29 pro-choice measures enacted thus far in 1999, up from 22 enacted in all of 1998.

-- States are moving aggressively to redress inequities in insurance coverage of contraceptives. In 1999, 61 bills requiring insurance coverage for contraception have been introduced in 32 states and six states (CT, GA, ME, NV, NC, VT) have enacted bills requiring insurance coverage for contraception, representing
a 600 percent increase over 1998 when only Maryland enacted a law requiring such coverage.

-- Although bans on so-called "partial-birth" abortion continue to grab headlines, such legislation comprises only a small percentage of anti-choice measures enacted. Of the 45 anti-choice measures enacted, only one, in North Dakota, was a new ban on so-called "partial-birth" abortion.

"While we are pleased that pro-choice lawmakers are seeing some success in passing legislation that will improve women's health and prevent unintended pregnancy, we remain alarmed at the vast number of anti-choice bills being introduced and passed," said Michelman. "Anti-choice state legislation continues to far outpace preventative pro-choice legislation."

"Instead of devoting their attention on laws that expand women's options and improve women's health, lawmakers continue to focus on curtailing reproductive health options and jeopardizing programs that make a real difference in the prevention of unintended pregnancy and the lives of American families," continued Michelman. "I urge all lawmakers, federal and state, to join NARAL in our efforts to improve women's health and prevent unintended pregnancies, thereby reducing the number of abortions in this country."

The NARAL's Foundation's mid-year review of state legislative action in 1999 is a precursor to Who Decides? A State-by-State Review of Abortion and Reproductive Rights, an annual, book-length publication produced by The NARAL Foundation which will address not only 1999 legislative activity, but also the cumulative status of reproductive rights in the states. For a full report on mid-year legislative activity, please contact NARAL's Communications Department at 202-973-3032.

FIVE WORST STATES

The following is a list of the five Worst States thus far in this legislative session, determined by the total number of enacted anti-choice bills.

-- One law mandates parental involvement in a minor's decision to terminate her pregnancy and requires that a minor seeking an abortion endure a 48-hour waiting period before an abortion and requires the creation and distribution of state-prepared materials (SB 30);

-- one law makes it a felony for any adult, including a relative, who is more than three years older than a 14-16 year-old to take the 14-16 year-old out of state without parental consent (HB 1428);

-- one law singles out abortion providers for special regulations (HB 2085);

-- one law prohibits family planning funding for organizations that offer abortion procedures, prohibits the use of family planning funds to dispense prescription contraceptives to minors without parental consent, and funds abstinence-until-marriage education (HB 1);

-- one law prohibits the state Child Health Plan from covering reproductive services except prenatal care and care related to diseases, illnesses, or abnormalities related to the reproductive system (SB 445);

-- one law prohibits school-based health centers receiving specified grants from providing reproductive services, counseling, or referrals (HB 2202); and,

-- one law denies a tax exemption to certain not-for-profit organizations that perform, refer for, or assist specified organizations that perform or refer for abortion (HB 541).

-- One law promotes abstinence-until-marriage education (SB 1047);

-- one law prohibits specified funds for state family planning services to be used for performing, assisting, encouraging, or making direct referrals for abortions (HB 2717); and,

-- one law prohibits school divisions or participating providers in the Medicaid program, or any other program operated by the Department of Medical Assistance, from providing family planning, pregnancy, or abortion services (HB 2360/SB 1199) (Note: Identical signed bills VA SB 1199 and VA HB 2360 were treated as one bill for the purposes of the Five Worst States count).

-- One law singles out abortion providers for special regulations (HB 2706);

-- one law funds abstinence-only education (HB 2480); and,

-- one law prohibits funds for community health centers from being used for abortion or abortion referral and counseling (SB 1001).

-- One law limits public funding of abortion to cases of life endangerment, reported rape or incest, or fetal anomaly and supports abstinence-only education (HF 760);

-- one law prohibits state university hospital and clinic funds to be used for abortions or the operation of an early termination of pregnancy clinic unless necessary to preserve the woman's life, the fetus has anomalies, or the pregnancy is the result of reported rape or incest (SF 464); and,

-- one law supports abstinence-only education (HF 737).

-- One law mandates parental involvement in a minor's decision to terminate her pregnancy (SB 1598); and,

-- one law creates anti-choice license plates, payment for which funds private organizations that provide services and counseling to pregnant women who are committed to placing their children for adoption, and such organizations may not perform, refer for, or counsel about abortion (HB 509).
