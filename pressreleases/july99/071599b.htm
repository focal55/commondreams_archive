Nader Calls for Real HMO Reform
 
WASHINGTON - July 15 - "If civil society does not reassert itself over the arrogance of giant HMOs, the quality of our health care and our democracy will continue to be compromised," Ralph Nader wrote today in a letter to the Senate and House leadership.

Calling for real reform of "a monetized HMO establishment intent on undermining the health of patients and the professionalism of physicians and nurses," Nader outlined the following bare-bones elements of a worthwhile reform plan:

-- HMO accountability in the civil justice system. "There is little doubt that the tragic record of wrongdoing and serious neglect at HMOs today springs in part from the fact that for 125 million Americans with private industry coverage there is no real remedy when wronged," Nader wrote. "If convicted thieves simply had to give back the money they stole, the number of bank robberies would increase decisively. The possibility of damages -- compensatory, non-economic and punitive -- is precisely the legal leverage patients need to force HMOs to provide medically-necessary care."

-- Doctor, not HMO, control over medical decisions. "Only doctors should determine medical necessity, not corporate bureaucrats," Nader wrote.

-- Independent review procedures, outside of HMO control. "When HMOs pay for reviews and choose when such reviews are warranted, patients will have no legitimate appeal," Nader argued. "A private review system (one in which the third-party reviewer is not a public entity) keeps the control with HMOs that contract out for reviews and the physicians who decide them."

-- Executive salary caps for HMOs that service Medicare patients or receive tax money. "Obscene HMO extravagance is a stark contrast to the penuriousness HMOs show toward patients," Nader noted. "If patients are to suffer under HMO rationing of medically necessary services, HMOs that receive public monies must not be allowed to squander precious dollars on lavish executive compensation packages."

-- Consumers' right to band together. "Government and health industry envelopes, sent to people for customary purposes, such as bills or government checks, should contain, at no expense to sender, a postage-paid envelope/letter that invites paid memberships from consumers to state-chartered private consumer health action groups," Nader wrote.
