Wellstone Introduces Legislation to Address Impact of Family Violence on Children
 
WASHINGTON - July 1 - As part of a longstanding commitment to combat domestic abuse and seek solutions for reducing youth violence, Senator Paul Wellstone introduced legislation today to address the impact of family violence on children. Wellstone 's "Children Who Witness Domestic Violence Protection Act" is a comprehensive step toward addressing the many problems children and families face when violence makes its way into the home. The bill is an outgrowth of many community meetings and work done in Minnesota and around the country by Wellstone's wife, Sheila, a nationally-recognized leader in the fight against domestic violence.

"For too many children, the first exposure to violence is not on TV or in the movies or in their neighborhoods, but in the home," said Senator Wellstone. "Domestic abuse happens in every part of the country, in all kinds of homes, to all kinds of people, regardless of class, race, ethnicity, religion, education level, or sexual orientation. Children who are exposed to this violence are at a high risk of becoming perpetrators of violence or victims themselves. As part of our efforts to reduce violence in our schools and our communities, we have no choice but to address the impact of family violence on children."

The legislation would provide funds for mental health services for children who witness domestic violence, training for school staff, police officers and child welfare workers on how to identify, treat and deal with children who have witnessed domestic violence, supervised "Safe Haven" visitation centers where parents who are battered can visit their children, and short-term crisis nurseries available for children and families impacted by domestic violence. The funds will be made available to states, non-profit organizations and other local agencies to design and carry out these initiatives in ways that work best for each community. A summary of the bill is available.

Over 3 million children in the United States witness domestic violence in their homes each year. In Minnesota, 17% of 6th graders, 21% of 9th graders, and 18% of 12th graders reported they had been physically abused or had witnessed family violence. (Source: MN Dept of Children, Families, and Learning) These children are at a high risk for aggression, depression, learning difficulties, school failure, delinquency, and even suicide. The attitudes a child develops concerning the use of violence and conflict resolution in their own relationships are also affected. Further, children living in homes where domestic violence occurs are at a greater risk of being abused themselves.

"Women are battered every day all over this country, but it's rare to hear about the children who live in these homes that are wracked by violence," said Sheila Wellstone. "This issue of children witnessing domestic violence just kept coming up in meeting after meeting in Minnesota with advocates and women who have been abused. It has become clear that, as we work to end the cycle of violence, we need to take a comprehensive approach that takes into account all of the damaging effects of violence on families. We need to address this issue together, as a community."

Senator Wellstone was optimistic about the chances to pass the Children Who Witness Domestic Violence Protection Act. "When my colleagues take a look at the problem, I think we'll get the support to pass this bill. Passing this legislation would be a huge step forward toward stopping the cycle of violence in our homes and in our communities.
