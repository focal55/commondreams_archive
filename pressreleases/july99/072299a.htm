New Poll Shows Young Americans Choose Strengthening Social Security Over Tax Cuts, Guaranteed Benefits Over Investment Gamble
 
WASHINGTON - July 22 - Shattering the foundation for a common stereotype of young adults, new survey research focusing exclusively on 18-34 year olds finds that this group has remarkably positive views of Social Security. Eighty-one percent support the program, including a broad cross-section of young minorities and Whites, Republicans and Democrats, and blue and white-collar workers.

Conducted by Peter Hart Research Associates, a leading Washington opinion-research firm, the poll finds that younger generations place a high priority on strengthening and protecting Social Security benefits. In fact, young people say that it is more important to provide a "decent, guaranteed" benefit than to "increase rates of return" for Social Security (55% vs. 39%).

Voicing their position on a conflict that has divided Washington, a large majority of young adults would rather use the budget surplus to strengthen Social Security than provide a tax cut (59% to 32%). Even more would use the surplus for Medicare instead of a tax cut (67% to 27%).

"Ironically, this research turns conventional wisdom on its head," said Hans Riemer, 2030 Center Director. "Young people, in fact, are just like everyone else-they think Social Security works, they want their benefits to remain intact, and they don't want politicians playing games with their future."

The poll, commissioned by the 2030 Center, a public policy institute for young adults, addresses broad questions about Social Security as well as the nuts-and-bolts of three leading reform plans. In what should be a warning to many pundits and politicians, widely touted plans to replace Social Security with private investment accounts fall short in the eyes of young adults.

Two approaches that reduce guaranteed benefits in order to fund private investment accounts lose heavily to a third approach that provides full guaranteed benefits but no private accounts. Nearly 70% of young adults endorse the guaranteed benefits plan, while the plans that experts call "privatization" win less support (55% and 50%) and much higher opposition (25% vs. 41% and 45%).

Deflating claims by some Generation X interest groups that a "generational war" is brewing, 85% of young people say that seniors "need and deserve" the Social Security benefits they receive, so it is not "unfair" to young people. Moreover, young adults think that the basic system remains relevant for their generation: on a more than two-to-one basis, they say that Social Security can work for young people in the future if Congress will act to strengthen the system's finances today.

The 2030 Center is a public policy institute dedicated to improving young adults' economic circumstances. Copies of the poll and summary report, Young Americans and Social Security, are available at www.2030.org or by contacting the 2030 Center.
