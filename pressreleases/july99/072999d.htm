Chet Atkins, Greenpeace and Renowned Marine Experts Sound Alarm on Today's Threats to Whale Survival
 
BOSTON - July 29 - Marking 25 years of activism to protect whales, the environmental group Greenpeace today called attention to the major threats the world's whale are now facing, and the need to establish a Global Whale Sanctuary.

At a press conference today at Boston's New England Aquarium, Greenpeace was joined by some of the nation's most prominent whales experts and advocates, including: Karen Steuer of the Yarmouthport-based International Fund for Animal Welfare; Dr. Roger Payne, a renowned whales authority and creator of the Discovery Channel film, "In the Company of Whales;" Chester G. ("Chet") Atkins, former US congressman from Massachusetts; and Brian McDonald, a director with the New England Aquarium and President of the North East Whale Watching Association.

Greenpeace Executive Director Kristen Engberg discussed the history of the whale conservation movement globally, and why it is critical to stop commercial whaling by the only two nations still conducting the practice, Japan and Norway.

"Most people believe the whales have been 'saved,'" Engberg said, "but today they face renewed threats to their survival. Commercial whaling by Norway and Japan is increasing each year, despite a moratorium that's been in place since 1986. Even more troubling is the fact that these nations also want to see a resumption of international trade in whale products, which would almost certainly encourage other nations to resume killing whales for profit."

Negotiators will debate lifting current ban on whale trade at the next meeting of the Convention on the International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES), to be held in April 2000 in Nairobi, Kenya.

"We are finally starting to see whale populations stabilize worldwide," said Chet Atkins. "Now, instead of letting commercial whaling destroy what we have fought so hard for, it is time for the leaders of this country to go to CITES demanding that the trade ban be continued."

Brian McDonald, of the North East Whale Watching Association said whale watching has been a financial boon to New England – once the whaling capital of the world – and also provides a new platform for critical whale research.

Greenpeace activists worldwide are calling on the International Whaling Commission (IWC), the only body authorized to manage the world's whales, to establish a Global Whale Sanctuary to keep whales off limits to commercial whalers everywhere. Greenpeace collected over two million petition signatures in the early 1990s, in a successful effort to create a whale sanctuary around Antarctica.
