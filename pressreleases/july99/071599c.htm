Statement of Common Cause Acting President Donald J. Simon On George W. Bush's Refusal to Participate In The Presidential Public Financing System
 
WASHINGTON - July 15 - George W. Bush's decision not to participate in the presidential public financing system means that he is turning his back on the principal post-Watergate reform law that was intended to take the White House off the auction block, to limit spending in presidential campaigns, and to diminish the role of fundraising in our elections.

Governor Bush's decision signals that the 2000 presidential election may be the most expensive ever. The fundraising - and potential corruption - will be out of control.

At the heart of the Governor Bush's fundraising machine is a small and secretive group of insider money men who each have committed to raising $100,000 blocks of money for the campaign. These so-called "Pioneers" are the new class of political power brokers whose status and access are determined by the big money they bring to the campaign.

The "flexibility" Governor Bush claims he seeks by opting out of the public financing system is being purchased at the expense of the law designed to safeguard the integrity of our presidential elections.

What George W. Bush and his money men are really "pioneering" is the next generation of the political arms race, where the sky's the limit on spending and on special-interest money in our presidential primaries.

As bad as Governor Bush's decision today is, it also raises the question of what will happen down the road when his fundraising machine is unleashed in the world of soft money - where there are no rules, and where any amount of money can be raised from any source - and where all of the scandals of the 1996 election occurred.
