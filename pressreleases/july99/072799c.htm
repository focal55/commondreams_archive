Wellstone Introduces Major Legislation to Provide Fairness in Substance Abuse Treatment
 
WASHINGTON - July 27 - U.S. Senator Paul Wellstone introduced legislation today that will end discrimination in insurance coverage for Americans who suffer from the disease of drug and alcohol addiction. The Drug and Alcohol Addiction Recovery Act of 1999 ensures that private health insurance companies cover the costs for drug and alcohol addiction treatment services at the same level that they pay for treatment of other diseases.

Today, even if treatment is available and accessible, it is often unaffordable, as many health plans do not cover the costs for drug and alcohol addiction treatment at the same level that they pay for treatment of other diseases.

"For too long, the problem of drug and alcohol addiction has been viewed as a moral failing, rather than as a disease. Some forms of addiction have a genetic basis, and yet still we try to deny the serious medical nature of this disease. Nearly all of us know someone who has experienced drug or alcohol addiction, within our families or our circle of friends. This bill is a big step toward ensuring that those suffering with the diseases of drug and alcohol addiction can get the treatment needed to help save their lives," Wellstone said.

Similar to Wellstone's Mental Health Parity Bill that was introduced earlier this year, this bill does not mandate that health insurers offer drug and alcohol addiction treatment benefits. What it does is prohibit discrimination by health plans who offer substance addiction treatment from placing unfair and life-threatening limitations on caps, access, or financial requirements for services that are different from other medical and surgical services.

The bill prohibits limits that differ from those that are applied for other covered illnesses on:
* Annual and lifetime caps
* Co-payments and deductibles
* Hospital days and outpatient visits

Small business employers with 25 or fewer employees are exempt under the Wellstone bill. The measure also provides protection for states with stronger mental health or substance abuse parity laws.

"This bill will save lives and health care dollars. Addiction to alcohol and drugs is a disease that affects the brain, the body, and the spirit, but we know that treatment works. We must provide adequate opportunities for the treatment of addiction in order to help those who are suffering, and to prevent the health and social problems that it causes. To do that, we must end the discrimination of addiction treatment by health plans today," Wellstone said.

In Minnesota, the State Alcohol and Drug Agency has extensively evaluated the effectiveness and costs of their substance abuse programs. It has reported an abstinence rate of 64% for clients six months after treatment, with medical and psychiatric hospitalization reduced. Minnesota has estimated that it has saved $22 million in annual healthcare costs by providing alcohol and drug treatment.

Alcoholism and drug addiction are painful, private struggles with staggering public costs. According to studies released by the National Institute for Drug Abuse, the direct and indirect costs of alcoholism alone in this country exceed $245 billion each year in medical claims, medical disability, injuries, theft, absenteeism, and decreased productivity. Assuring access to treatment will not only combat this insidious disease, it will save health dollars. Several studies have shown that for every $1 spent on treatment, more than $7 is saved in other health care expenses.
