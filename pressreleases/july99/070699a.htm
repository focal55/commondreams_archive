World's Coral Reefs Will be Devastated Within 30 Years if Global Warming is Not Checked
 
WASHINGTON - July 6 - Greenpeace today released a report entitled "Climate Change, Coral Bleaching, and the Future of the World's Coral Reefs." This scientific study concludes that if global warming is not stopped the world's coral reefs could soon be lost. By early next century, continued greenhouse gas pollution will result in coral bleaching that will devastate reefs from the Florida Keys to the Australian Great Barrier Reef to the Indian Ocean.

"The world's coral reefs are sending out an S.O.S. Unless the United States accelerates its efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by switching to renewable energy sources, the next generation of children may never see beautiful, healthy coral reefs," said Iain MacGill, Greenpeace Climate Issues Specialist.

Coral bleaching is a condition that can seriously damage or kill entire reef systems. Corals contain microscopic plants called zooanthellae, which vibrantly color the corals' tissues and provide the corals with food. When corals become stressed, as from rising ocean temperatures, they expel the zooanthellae and turn white or "bleach." 1998 was the warmest year on record, and brought with it the most widespread and severe coral bleaching to date. Every reef system in the world's tropical oceans was affected, and in some places like the Indian Ocean, entire reef systems were destroyed.

In this new scientific study, conducted by Professor Ove Hoegh-Guldberg, one of the world's leading experts in coral bleaching, two of the leading climate projection models used by the United Nation's Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change are used to predict the future impact of continued global warming on coral reefs. He concludes that as early as 2030, the severe coral bleaching events that shocked the world's scientific communities in 1998 could become an annual occurrence.

"The loss of coral reefs would have devastating consequences, adversely affecting millions of people around the world," added MacGill "Coral reefs provide large sources of income for the tourism and fishing industries, ingredients for new drugs and coastal protection from extreme weather events, their loss potentially costing trillions of dollars."

Increasing levels of greenhouse gas pollution from the burning of coal, oil and gas is increasing global temperatures to record levels. Climate Change, Coral Bleaching, and the Future of the World's Coral Reefs concludes that warmer ocean temperatures are the primary cause of the rise in intensity, frequency and extent of coral bleaching. If greenhouse gas emissions are not dramatically reduced, coral reefs will likely be one the first casualties of global warming in the new millennium.

Greenpeace is the leading independent organization that uses peaceful and creative activism to protect the global environment.
