Republicans Seek To Eliminate Protections For Children Abused In Jails and Juvenile Facilities; Parents Of Children Who Died In Adult Jails Oppose Pending Federal Legislation
 
WASHINGTON - July 20 - Parents of children who died in adult jails expressed outrage today at H.R.1501, legislation pending in Congress that would eliminate protections for children from physical and sexual abuse in correctional facilities. Sen. Paul Wellstone (D. Minn.) is offering legislation that would preserve the protections.

"After what happened to my son, I can't believe that Congress would be so uncaring," said Rita Horn, whose 15-year-old son Robbie committed suicide in a county jail in LaGrange, Kentucky. "Without the legal system stopping kids from being held in the jail, Robbie's death could be repeated again and again."

Following her son's death, Horn sued to stop Oldham County from holding children in the local jail. Jails records showed that 1,390 children had been held over a 4-year period, most for minor violations and "status" offenses like truancy and curfew violations. The lawsuit resulted in a consent decree in 1985 in which county officials agreed to no longer hold children in the jail.

However, under a provision sponsored by Rep. Tom DeLay (R-22nd TX) amending the Prison Litigation Reform Act, all consent decrees which were in effect on the date the original Act was passed in 1995 would automatically be terminated. Under the DeLay amendment, which passed with only ten minutes of floor debate and was not addressed at all during Judiciary Committee hearings, children could again be held in the Kentucky jail where Robbie Horn died.

To counter the DeLay provision, Sen. Paul Wellstone is offering legislation that would exempt children and those with mental illness from the Prison Litigation Reform Act. Wellstone's legislation would insure that consent decrees protecting children from harmful practices remains in place. "We must insure that when a child is incarcerated in the United States, the child is safe and the facility meets minimum constitutional standards," said Senator Wellstone in offering his amendment. "No society can call itself civilized which turns a blind eye to the plight of children like Robbie Horn and Chris Peterman."

"The DeLay provisions would immediately wipe out protections in scores of counties around America where children have been abused and mistreated in adult jails and juvenile facilities," said Mark Soler, president of the Youth Law Center, a public interest law office that has represented children over the past twenty years. "Thousands of children could again be locked up under dangerous, often horrific conditions."

Over the past two decades, Robbie Horn's case has not been the only tragedy. In Ironton, Ohio, a 15-year-old girl ran away from home overnight, then returned to her parents, but was put in the county jail by the juvenile court judge "to teach her a lesson." On the fourth night of her confinement, she was sexually assaulted by a deputy jailer. More than 500 children had been incarcerated in the jail over the previous three years, most for skipping school and other minor offenses. Since county officials entered into a consent decree more than 15 years ago, no children have been held in the jail.

Not all consent decrees involve adult jails. In Philadelphia, children in the juvenile detention facility were regularly beaten by staff, often with paddles, chains, or other objects. In Portland, Oregon, the juvenile facility was acknowledged by local officials to be unsafe, unsanitary, a fire hazard, and "reeking of urine." In Solano County, California, children were put in isolation for days at a time, with no access to showers, exercise, recreation, or medical staff. All of these practices were ended through consent decrees with local officials.

Other cases have involved state facilities. In South Carolina, nine children in the state correctional facility who had attempted suicide were transferred to the state mental hospital. There each child was subjected to long periods of solitary confinement. Several of the children were also placed, naked or in paper gowns, in four-point restraints, hands and feet bound to the four corners of their beds. State doctors forcibly injected several of the children with vitamins or psychotropic drugs as part of "aversive therapy." In Oklahoma, children in the state institutions were regularly beaten by staff. In Arizona, children in the state correctional facilities were put in solitary confinement, handcuffed, or put in four-point restraints. In California, disabled children did not receive special education services. Again, all of these practices stopped when public officials entered into consent decrees.

All of these consent decrees -- in cases involving adult jails, juvenile detention facilities, and state correctional institutions -- would immediately be dissolved if the DeLay provision in H.R. 1501 becomes law.

"When my son Chris was beaten and killed in the Boise jail, I swore his death would not be in vain," said Janice Peterman, whose 17-year-old son was jailed for failing to pay $73 in traffic fines. Over a 14-hour period, Chris Peterman was tortured and finally murdered by other prisoners in the cell. More than 650 children had been held in the jail over a 3-year period, 42% for traffic offenses and 17% for "status" offenses. In response to a class action lawsuit, local officials agreed to end incarceration of children in the jail. Mrs. Peterman said, "I turned my grieving for my son into work to protect other children from tragedy. This would rob my work of meaning, and turn today's children into victims just like Chris."

"If the DeLay amendment passes, it will eliminate an important weapon to stop the kinds of atrocities experienced by Chris Peterman and Robbie Horn," stated Vincent Schiraldi, Director of the Justice Policy Institute. "Our research shows that youth in adult facilities are five times more likely to be sexually assaulted and eight times more likely to commit suicide than youths held in juvenile facilities."
