Greenpeace Activists Sentenced to Jail for Los Angeles Ship Protest
 
WASHINGTON - July 2 - Three members of the international environmental organization Greenpeace were sentenced today to a month each in jail for their part in a protest over the importing of timber from Canada's ancient forests to the Port of Long Beach in California. Omi Hodwitz, Pat Finn, and Bernard Mcleod were given this sentence for trespassing aboard the Thorseggen, a ship carrying over 8,000 tons of newsprint cut from Canada's Great Bear Rainforest. The Great Bear Rainforest, located on the west-coast of British Columbia, is the largest remaining unprotected tract of ancient temperate rainforest on Earth.

Over the course of the three day protest, activists – including those sentenced – hung from the ship's crane bearing a banner that read, "Stop Destroying The Great Bear Rainforest."

According to Mathew Jacobson, Forest Issues Specialist at Greenpeace, "The American people have made it clear that they oppose the destruction of the Earth's last remaining ancient forests. The purpose of the action aboard the Thorseggen was to show the connection between the destruction of ancient forests abroad and the U.S. marketplace. A large percentage of the Earth's ancient forests wind up in lumber, newsprint, and even toilet paper in the U.S. The destruction of these forests is unnecessary, and alternative sources for these products are readily available."

Following the action, Fletcher-Challenge, the manufacturer of the newsprint, made contact with Greenpeace to initiate dialogue and address Greenpeace's concerns.

Both Fletcher-Challenge and Norsk, the owner of the Thorsaggen, requested that the case be dropped, but the state of California persisted in its quest for the conviction of the activists.

In a letter presented to the Judge today, Norsk requested that the activists not be sentenced to jail time.

"We're certainly disappointed in the State's decision to take this strong a stand against what was clearly a non-violent exercise of our first amendment rights, but we stand by our actions," said Omi Hodwitz. "While we disagree with the charges, we were well aware of the risk we were taking, and we're prepared to face the consequences. Eighty percent of the Earth's original ancient forests have already been degraded or destroyed. If this is part of the price we have to pay to protect the remaining twenty percent, we are prepared to do so."

The three activists were released on bond pending appeal.
