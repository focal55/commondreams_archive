 Clean Air Trust Hails European Union Decision to Require Low Sulfur Gasoline
WASHINGTON - July 1 - The nonprofit Clean Air Trust today hailed this week's decision by the European Union to require California-style low-sulfur gasoline in Europe.

"This will lead to a major reduction in the smog that plagues many European cities," noted Frank O'Donnell, executive director of the Trust. "The United States should follow suit to fight the persistent smog in this country," O'Donnell added.

The European Parliament and the Council of Ministers agreed June 29 to require low-sulfur gasoline throughout Europe by 2005 along with stricter tailpipe pollution standards for cars and light trucks. The shift to low-sulfur gasoline was bitterly opposed by the oil industry, which is fighting a similar move to reduce the sulfur content of U.S. gasoline.

Major health and environmental groups, state and local air quality regulators and car companies are all urging the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency to require California-style low-sulfur gas in the U.S. A recent analysis by state and local governments noted that moving to low-sulfur gas would produce the clean-air equivalent of removing 54 million cars from the roads. The EPA is expected to propose new gasoline sulfur standards by the end of the year.

O'Donnell noted that more than 30 states -- including Utah, Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Missouri, Mississippi, Arkansas, and most of the East -- have already experienced smog levels above new federal health standards this year.

"Low-sulfur gas would mean fewer and less severe smog episodes," O'Donnell said, noting that sulfur impedes the performance of catalytic converters. Low-sulfur gas also would permit introduction of more efficient and advanced technologies that will be sold in
Europe and Japan, which already has low-sulfur gasoline.
