Gov. George W. Bush and Gun Control
 
WASHINGTON - August 27 - Following is a statement released today by Handgun Control Inc.:

This is not a time for hemming and hawing. Gov. Bush (R-Texas) should stand tall and tell us exactly where he stands on the issue of guns. So far, his answers have been evasive and inconsistent. In the wake of the Littleton shooting, he said he supported background checks on all sales of guns at gun shows 1/8 under present law, background checks are not done on buyers purchasing guns from "unlicensed dealers" operating at gun shows 3/8 and then proceeded to oppose specific bills in Austin and in Washington that would have done just that:

-- In Texas he opposed gun show legislation this year that was supported by many Texas police chiefs and sheriffs. He insisted that the matter should be resolved by Congress.

-- But then, during the U.S. Senate debate on guns in May, he supported an amendment sponsored by Sen. Larry Craig (R-Idaho), later defeated, that would have stopped far short of mandatory checks at gun shows.

-- Later, during the House debate in June, after first indicating support for the Senate-passed gun show provisions, he supported an amendment by Rep. John Dingell that, by reducing the time allotted for background checks at gun shows to 24 hours for sales by licensed gun dealers, would have actually weakened existing law with respect to gun shows.

Today outside Atlanta, near the site of another major shooting, Bush again said he supports background checks at gun shows. But does he support the Senate-passed gun show measure or not? That's a critical question. Bush's clear and unequivocal support for strong gun show legislation could help to break the current impasse in Congress.

On a more positive note, Gov. Bush today reportedly said in Atlanta that he supports raising the age for handgun possession from 18 to 21. Federal law presently forbids gun stores from selling handguns to juveniles, but in most states youths 18 and over can purchase handguns from unlicensed dealers at gun shows or even from total strangers. Earlier this year, a proposal to bar sales of handguns to youths under the age of 21 was endorsed by key House Republicans, including Speaker Hastert and Henry Hyde, the Chairman of the House Judiciary Committee. The issue, however, was never brought to the House floor, despite pledges by both Hastert and Hyde. The issue subsequently died, despite a Clinton administration report indicating that 18, 19 and 20-year olds have the highest rate of handgun homicides of any age group. If Mr. Bush truly supports a hike in the age of handgun possession, he should encourage House and Senate conferees to revive the issue when they meet again next month.

On a third gun issue, Bush said he supports Congressional restrictions on high capacity ammunition magazines, but he stopped short of any specifics. The measure passed earlier this year by the U.S. Senate would stop the further importation of such clips, but California just passed a law prohibiting the resale of such magazines.

------
Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, is the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works to enact stronger federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban handguns. Founded in 1974, HCI has more than 400,000 members nationwide and works with local groups around the country to enact and protect reasonable gun control laws. More information about HCI and its affiliated organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on our website at www.handguncontrol.org.
