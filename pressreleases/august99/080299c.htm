Colorado Reinstates State Background Gun Checks
 
WASHINGTON - August 2 - Yesterday, for the first time since last April, the state of Colorado conducted its own, state-level background checks for all over-the-counter gun purchases.

Since April, Colorado had been using the National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS), a computerized database maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation, which provides information for criminal background checks. But after the horrific killings of three girls, Leslie, Katheryn and Rebecca Gonzales by their father, Simon Gonzales, in Castle Rock, Colo., just three weeks ago, the state decided to go back to a state-level instant criminal background check that might have blocked Mr. Gonzales from buying a gun. The FBI's "Instant Check" System, which executes background checks for gun
dealers in about half the states, did not locate a domestic abuse restraining order against Mr. Gonzales that would have precluded him from buying a gun.

Before Colorado switched to the NICS system in April, the Colorado Bureau of Investigation (CBI) used a state background system that screened applicants wanting to purchase a firearm for criminal information, including warrant data, arrest data, probation and parole data, juvenile data and domestic abuse data. The NICS system, while providing a federal database to immediately check prospective firearm purchasers for felony convictions and other barriers to purchase, does not include some state and local police records, such as records of persons convicted of certain offenses, like stalking, and does not include outstanding misdemeanor warrants on domestic violence, felony arrests or records of involuntary commitments to mental hospitals. Despite warnings that NICS might not catch all illegal gun purchases because of its limited access to state criminal resources, the Colorado Legislature allowed their state-level checks to expire in April.

The switch is already paying off. In its first day of state background checks, the Colorado Bureau of Investigation denied eight gun purchases out of 68 requests, two of which would not have been denied had the state still been using NICS.

"Colorado made a smart choice to reinstate the state-run background check," said Bob Walker, president of Handgun Control. "The system is off to a good start: eight rejections on the first day. Most importantly, the new check denied two applicants who would have slipped through the cracks on the federal NICS system. If this rate continues, Colorado law enforcement will stop about 3,000 prohibited purchasers a year, many of whom could potentially walk away with a gun instantly, like Simon Gonzales did, if they were still using the federal system."

"We urge other states that switched from a state background system to NICS last November (Delaware, Missouri and New York) -- in addition to the 25 states that only use the federal system -- to follow Colorado's example and back up the NICS check with their own checks," Walker added.

Handgun Control also supports legislation introduced in the current Congress to reinstate the federal Brady Waiting Period. On November 30, 1998, the five-day waiting period for handgun purchases required by the Brady law expired. It was replaced by the NICS system, where most background checks are completed within minutes, without a waiting period. Besides allowing more time for local police to conduct a thorough, comprehensive criminal background check, the waiting period allows gun purchasers considering crimes of passion or impulse suicides a "cooling off" period to protect themselves or their victims.

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, is the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works to enact stronger federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban handguns. Founded in 1974, HCI has more than 400,000 members nationwide and works with local groups around the country to enact and protect reasonable gun control laws. More information about HCI and its affiliated organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on the Web site at www.handguncontrol.org.
