Business Leaders Call For Nuclear Weapons Reduction To Mark Hiroshima Anniversary; Savings Should Be Applied To Public Health And Other Domestic Needs
 
WASHINGTON - August 3 - As the anniversary of the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Japan approaches, the members of Business Leaders for Sensible Priorities believe it is time for America to reduce its huge nuclear arsenal and put some of the monies saved into domestic needs.

More than 50 years after the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings and 10 years after the fall of the Soviet Union, the United States still maintains a nuclear arsenal of 12,000 weapons, at an annual cost to taxpayers of more than $30 billion. Meanwhile, our nation has public health, education and child care needs that are unmet because of a lack of funding.

BLSP military advisor Capt. James Bush, a former nuclear missile submarine commander, and Dr. Mohammad Akhter, executive director of the American Public Health Association, discussed these issues at a morning press briefing at the National Press Club.

"These weapons have no tactical value; their only use is deterrence," Capt. Bush explained. "It makes no sense to keep our nuclear stockpile at Cold War levels. We should reduce that stockpile to 1,000 weapons and save taxpayers about $15 billion a year."

"A nation's security does not depend solely on the number of bombs in its arsenal or warships in its fleet," Dr. Akhter said. "No nation has ever fallen because it did not have enough weapons. Nations fall from within. The threats to America's national security now come mainly from within - violence, drug abuse and peoples' lack of access to health care. Investing in our health and the health of the next generation will help us guarantee our future security."

Dr. Akhter noted, for example, that 43 million Americans still lack health insurance, and that America spends only about 17 cents per person each year to prevent cardiovascular disease, the nation's leading killer that consumes $274 billion each year in medical costs and lost productivity. He added that investments in public health can yield important returns by reducing the costs of treating people who later develop more serious ailments because they could not get appropriate care.

Business Leaders for Sensible Priorities is a non-partisan, national public education campaign designed to increase Americans' knowledge of how their tax dollars are spent. The campaign currently has 500 business executives and retired military officers on its membership rolls.
