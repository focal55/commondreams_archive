Democrats Threaten to Bring Senate to Standstill over Nuclear Test Ban Treaty
 
WASHINGTON - August 31 - Yesterday The New York Times reported that Senate Democrats are threatening to bring Senate business to a halt this fall if no action is taken on the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT), an international agreement that would ban all underground nuclear explosions and create a worldwide monitoring system to ensure compliance.

The CTBT was signed by President Clinton nearly three years ago and has since been signed by 152 countries. Since the President signed the treaty, it has languished in the Senate Foreign Relations Committee because Chairman Senator Jesse Helms has refused to allow hearings on the treaty despite calls from every Democratic Senator and members of his own party. According to The New York Times, Clinton Administration officials plan to push the treaty more publicly in the coming weeks.

Daryl Kimball, executive director of the Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers, has worked on nuclear test ban issues for the past 10 years. He is a leading expert on the history, negotiations and legislation leading to the current congressional impasse on its ratification. Kimball has commissioned numerous bipartisan public opinion polls that show public support for the treaty at 82 percent and support for the treaty in Helms' home state of North Carolina at 75 percent.

Tom Zamora Collina, director of International Security Programs for the Union of Concerned Scientists, is an expert on U.S. nuclear weapons and nuclear testing policy and has written extensively about the U.S. nuclear weapons Stockpile Stewardship Program and test ban verification.

Armed with the public opinion data, these leaders in the disarmament community captured solid support from Senate Democrats and have organized voters across the country to contact the White House and Congress to urge action on the treaty.

If the CTBT is not ratified by this October, the United States will have no voting privileges at a meeting of treaty signers in Vienna, Austria scheduled for Oct. 6-8. The conference's goal is to explore ways to accelerate the treaty's entry into force. Disarmament experts fear the opportunity to encourage India and Pakistan to join the test ban treaty may also be squandered if the U.S. fails to ratify. This is a remarkable turn of events since it was the United States that spent over ten years negotiating the treaty's fine points and was the first country to sign it.
