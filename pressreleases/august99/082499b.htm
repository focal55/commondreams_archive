Local Coalition Calls for FCC Action to Protect the Public in the Digital Age
 
BOSTON - August 24 - Media activists and leaders from Boston's non-profit community will join forces with People for Better TV (PBTV) and Allston's Citizens' Media Corps (CMC) in organizing efforts here to protect the public interest in the digital television age.

The coalition noted that in the Telecommunication Act of 1996, Congress gave broadcasters $70 billion worth of additional public airwaves for digital TV broadcasts. In May, broadcasters in the nation's top ten television markets, including Boston, began using digital TV. On June 3, People for Better TV filed a petition with the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) asking for public hearings on how the broadcasters should compensate communities.

At an open workshop to be held at St. Elizabeth Hospital's Seaton Auditorium at 6 p.m. on Wednesday, Aug. 25, participants will discuss what broadcasters should offer to the public in return for the $70 billion worth of public airwaves they received. PBTV is organizing similar outreach efforts in a number of U.S. cities in order to gather support for FCC hearings on digital TV.

"In the interest of fairness to the American public, the FCC should schedule public hearings on the public interest obligations of these giant corporations which own the nation's television stations as they stand on the brink of a $70 billion windfall in publicly owned air rights," declared Mark Lloyd, national coordinator of PBTV.

"We're not talking about widgets here. As media becomes increasingly viewed as a commodity to be bartered without concern for its power to shape the culture, its potential to stimulate civic dialogue and diverse, democratic debate dwindles. The statistics are striking: increasing loss of diversity in media ownership and plunging voting statistics and civic participation have a direct relationship," said Steve Provizer of the Citizens' Media Corps (CMC).

In addition to the CMC, other coalition members in Boston of PBTV include; the League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC), the Massachusetts Office of the National Council of Churches, the Homeless People's Network, the Jewish Organizing Network, and the League of Women Voters.
