New Survey Shows Even SUV Owners Support Tougher Standards For Polluting Vehicles
 
WASHINGTON - July 3 - On the final day for the public to weigh in on landmark new auto pollutionstandards, U.S. PIRG delivered the last of more than 100,000 post cardscollected this summer from citizens across the nation to the White Houseand EPA. Acording to a PIRG survey released at the event, 87% of SUV andlight truck owners surveyed supported tightening pollution andmiles-per-gallon standards for SUVs and other light trucks. The pressconference featured "the Exterminator," a 24-foot long inflated SUV thathas been touring the nation as part of U.S. PIRG’s summer campaign.

"Now is the time for EPA to make all cars clean cars, even the biggest anddirtiest SUVs of all," said Rebecca Stanfield, Clean Air Advocate U.S.PIRG. "If EPA listens to the people and not the polluters, fewer Americanswill get sick or die prematurely from air pollution," continued Stanfield.

The postcards call on EPA to strengthen its proposal by requiring allpassenger vehicles to meet the same pollution standards as cars, includingthe larger sport utility vehicles (SUVs) and diesel powered vehicles.

"The American people want cleaner cars and trucks and cleaner gasoline,"said Paul Billings, Deputy Director, Government Relations for the AmericanLung Association. "EPA should require auto makers and oil refiners to givethe people what they want as soon as possible. Then we can all breatheeasier," Billings concluded.

The State PIRGs surveyed 1396 owners of SUVs, minivans and light trucks in32 states to determine the extent to which they were aware of the pollutionloopholes for their vehicle when they purchased it, whether they would havepaid a modest premium for a clean, efficient vehicle, and whether theysupport changing policy to require SUVs, minivans and other light trucks tomeet the same pollution and efficiency standards as cars. Of SUV and otherlight truck owners surveyed:

· 83% did not know when they bought their vehicle that it was allowed topollute up to three times more than regular cars;

· 81% would have been willing to pay $200 more to get an SUV, minivan orpickup truck that would have been as clean or cleaner than a car;

· 78% did not know that a typical SUV emits one third more global warmingpollution than a regular car due to the excess fuel consumption;

· 80% would have been willing to pay $200-$450 more for an SUV, minivan orpickup that would emit one third less global warming pollution and savemoney at the gas pump; and

· 87% thought that car companies should be required to make SUVs, minivansand pickups that meet the same pollution and miles-per-gallon standards ascars.

"If the Administration is serious about stopping global warming, theywill reject any bills that prohibit a move to more efficient cars andSUVs," said Katherine Silverthorne, Staff Attorney for U.S. PIRG. "EvenSUV owners agree that automakers should be required to build clean andefficient SUVs," she continued.

Automobiles are responsible for one-third of the pollution that causessmog, which sends tens of thousands of Americans to emergency rooms eachsummer. Under EPA’s proposed new standards, the average car would emitnearly 90% less smog-forming pollution than the average car under currentstandards, and some SUVs and light trucks would be held to clean carstandards. However, EPA’s proposal gives bigger SUVs a break by allowingthem two years of extra time to clean up, and the biggest and dirtiest SUVsof all would continue to be allowed to emit as much as three times morepollution than cars.

Current law also allows SUVs and other light trucks to use more gasolineper mile than cars. While the Administration is charged with updatingmiles-per-gallon standards, Congress has passed a rider for the past fouryears prohibiting the Administration from changing the standards. U.S.PIRG and Sierra Club called on President Clinton to veto any spending billsthis year that contain a freeze on miles-per-gallon standards.

"The days of the automaker's dirty little secrets are numbered," said AnnMesnikoff, Director of Sierra Club's Clean Car Program. "The EPA'sproposed clean air rules for SUVs are a big step in the right direction,but we must also raise miles per gallon standards -- the biggest singlestep we can take to curb global warming," she continued.

This summer the State PIRGs’ Clean Air Now campaign spoke to Americansabout auto pollution. As part of the campaign, the PIRGs toured the nationwith a giant inflated SUV dubbed "the Exterminator." The Exterminator metlocal activists for rallies and press events in more than 25 states.
