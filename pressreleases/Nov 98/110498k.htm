ACLU Vows to Continue Fight As Hawaii, Alaska Voters Reject Same-Sex Marriage
 
NEW YORK - November 4 -

Expressing disappointment over the outcome of state ballot measures to ban same-sex marriage in Alaska and Hawaii, the American Civil Liberties Union said today that it would continue the fight to ensure legal and social recognition for lesbian and gay relationships.

The referenda in these two states were closely watched during the elections because these were the first two states to come close to recognizing same-sex marriages, and because it was widely believed that the federal Constitution might require other states to recognize those marriages.

In Hawaii, despite a fiercely fought battle, voters approved by 69-29 percent "Question 2," a constitutional amendment to expand the power of state lawmakers to restrict marriages to opposite-sex couples. In Alaska, voters approved by 68-32 percent "Measure 2," an initiative that would amend the state constitution to say that a "valid" marriage in Alaska can exist only between one man and one woman.

"As disappointing as today's results are, it's important to keep this issue in perspective," said Matthew Coles, Director of the ACLU's National Lesbian and Gay Rights Project. "Over the past 25 years, we've made incredible progress in getting governments, corporations, universities, and many churches to recognize lesbian and gay relationships."

Coles noted several recent high points, including a successful challenge to a Maryland ban on same-sex intimacy just last month, and, in late 1997, an agreement with New Jersey authorities to allow lesbian and gay couples to adopt children on equal footing with married couples.

In addition, ACLU lobbying efforts this year helped to defeat a Congressional measure to deny federal funding to San Francisco because the city expects its contractors to provide benefits to the partners of gay employees. Opponents also beat back a Congressional attempt to ban lesbian and gay couples from jointly adopting children in the District of Columbia.

"As long as our nation attaches a wealth of economic and legal consequences to civil marriages," Coles said, "denying those same benefits to lesbians and gay men is nothing short of outright discrimination, and we will fight it."

Vanessa Y. Chong, Executive Director for the ACLU of Hawaii, agreed. "This is so disappointing for a state that has a good civil rights record, but we knew it was going to be a difficult battle."

"Our fight now moves to the state legislature to lobby for the extension of equal rights and benefits," she added. "In the same vein, we expect to work with others to ask the State Supreme Court, despite this amendment, to extend all the rights and benefits to same-gender couples."

The Hawaii amendment reached voters following a landmark ruling in Baehr v. Miike, a case brought in 1991 by two lesbian couples and one gay male couple after they were denied their applications for marriage licenses. In 1996, a state trial judge ruled that the state had "failed to present sufficient credible evidence . . . that the public interest in the well-being of children and families, or the optimal development of children would be adversely affected by same-sex marriages."

In response, state lawmakers agreed last year to let the voters decide on a constitutional amendment that would give lawmakers the power to restrict marriages to opposite-sex couples. At the same time, lawmakers reached a compromise plan that also would give same-sex couples a portion of the rights and benefits available to married couples.

Alaska's Measure 2 came in part as a response to a ruling in February in which a Superior Court Judge refused to throw out a challenge to a 1996 state law that banned same-sex marriage. The judge in that case said choosing a life partner is a fundamental right protected by the Alaska Constitution.

Lawmakers then introduced a constitutional amendment asking voters if same-sex marriage should be banned in Alaska. In July of this year, the Alaska Civil Liberties Union and the ACLU's Lesbian and Gay Rights Project sued the state, saying that Measure 2 would radically alter underlying principles of equality and that it amounted to an illegal revision of the state constitution.

Jennifer Rudinger, Executive Director of the AkCLU, said approval of Measure 2 represents a "sharp break" from Alaska's longstanding traditions of equality and privacy for all. "Our whole system of government in Alaska, as set forth in the Constitution, is based on the fundamental notion that all people are entitled to equal protection under our laws," she said. "It is very unfortunate that a blatantly discriminatory clause has now been added to a constitution that was one of the strongest in the nation in terms of its explicit protections for civil liberties."

As of January 1998, bills banning same-sex marriage were blocked in 24 states; 25 states signed them into law. States that Passed Anti-gay Marriage Laws (25): Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Kansas, Maine, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Mississippi, Montana, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah and Virginia.

States that Blocked Anti-Gay Marriage Bills (24): Alabama, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Hawaii, Iowa, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Texas, Vermont, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, and Wyoming.

"At this moment, gay men or lesbians can lose their homes to the government when a partner becomes ill, can be barred from intensive care units when their partners are desperately ill, or can lose everything when a partner dies, all because society refuses to recognize their relationships," Coles said.

"One has only to think of the civil rights movement of the sixties to realize that change does not come all at once," he added. "In fact, during this election nearly 40 percent of the electorate in South Carolina voted to reject a measure to finally remove from the state constitution a ban on interracial marriages. That should give us a little perspective on the battle before us."
