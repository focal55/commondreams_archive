Greenpeace Scales Obelisk To Demand Real Progress From Climate Summit
 
BUENOS ARIES - November 2 -

Greenpeace climbers this morning scaled the 70 metre Obelisk in the centre of Buenos Aires displaying their demands for international climate negotiations starting today in the Argentinian capital. The climbers entered Argentina's main nat ional monument and displayed a banner reading "Save the Climate - clean energy now" supported by five huge windmills which Greenpeace action teams had installed around the monument overnight.

The protest comes at the opening of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) which will continue until November 13th. "The key question is whether governments will support renewable energy solutions to climate change or whethe r they will continue to pay lip service only and protect fossil fuel and nuclear interests," said Corin Millais, renewable energy campaigner with Greenpeace International.

"There is a real danger here, that a thick blanket of jargon and details will pave the way for more coal, more oil and more nukes which will further delay the necessary phase-out of fossil fuels. We know already that we can only burn a quarter of the k nown fossil fuel reserves if we want to avoid dangerous climate change," said Millais.

High on the agenda are the inclusion of forests which may absorb some carbon dioxide (CO 2), the extent to which industrialised countries can trade carbon emissions among themselves and the design of a Clean Development Mechanism (CDM). The CDM is supp osed to transfer money and technology to the developing world in order to improve energy efficiency, resulting in cost-efficient greenhouse gas reductions.

But the CDM can be a new polluting tool. To be environmentally sound, it must supply massive support for renewable energy forms to meet the demands of the developing world. However, the mechanism can be abused as well, providing an option for industrie s to dump nuclear and fossil fuel power stations in the developing world. In the worst case, the United States for example could build nuclear and marginally improved coal power plants abroad which are not acceptable domestically and in return receive cre dits to increase their domestic CO 2 emissions.

"Greenpeace demands that the CDM is designed as an instrument to address the problem of climate change and not as an export subsidy for ailing industries in industrialised countries. That means support for renewable energy such as wind power and solar and no money for nuclear and misleadingly named clean-coal power plants," said Millais.

The concept of acquiring carbon credits by planting trees is equally problematic. While the general idea looks like a win-win concept, quite the opposite may be the effect. Under present regulations, the concept could actually provide an incentive for countries to cut down valuable old-growth forest, replacing it with fast growing eucalyptus trees. "The scientific basis for an inclusion of trees into the protocol is simply not there and it does not tackle the main problem which is rising CO 2 emissions " says Bill Hare, head of the Greenpeace delegation in Buenos Aires. "You don't start digging a well in your yard if your house is already on fire", said Hare who opposes inclusion of premature forest projects before a proper scientific assessment by the International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has been finalised.

Finally, the conference will discuss how much of the reductions each country will have to achieve domestically. At present, the free for all trading schemes as proposed by the US could actually lead to further emissions of CO 2. They provide an incenti ve for some countries to inflate emission targets in order to convert the difference between real and projected emissions into hard currencies Russia alone could look at a windfall profit of several billion dollars if they trade their high allowance to th e carbon-hungry US.
