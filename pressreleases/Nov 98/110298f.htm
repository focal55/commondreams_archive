This Weekend's Christian Coalition Voter Guide Plan Falters; Clergy Refuse to Distribute Guides
 
No home for voter guides - Unsolicited Guides Appear on Church Doorsteps in Iowa, Alabama, Florida, and North Carolina and in Restaurants in Tennessee

WASHINGTON - November 2 - The Interfaith Alliance (TIA) is being flooded by inquiries from pastors and clergy who are receiving unsolicited voters guides from area Christian Coalition activists. According to one pastor, guides are "miraculously" appearing on doorsteps and in church foyers. In West Tennessee, Interfaith Alliance members discovered thousands of Tennessee voter guides in area restaurants next to the free daily publications. Earlier this month, TIA's local chapters around the country sent "Dear Pastor" letters alerting fellow clergy to the unethical and deceptive voter guide campaign of the Christian Coalition. As a result, several area clergy who identify as both conservative and moderate have refused to distribute voter guides.

Concerned that their multi-million dollar voter persuasion campaign is being foiled, this week local Christian Coalition chapters sent unsolicited letters to clergy demonizing the local Interfaith Alliances spreading misinformation about TIA's mission and agenda. In several letters, the Coalition tries to assure clergy that their partisan voter guides are "legal" and that the centrist Interfaith Alliance is a "liberal and leftist" organization (TIA does not take a pro or con position on issues like abortion, vouchers, or gay marriage.) The Christian Coalition's letters go further, calling into question the faith and legitimacy of the ministers, rabbis and imams who signed the Interfaith Alliance's letters. (Letters available on request)

"The Christian Coalition's misrepresentations about The Interfaith Alliance and the local clergy who disagree with them is not only unethical, it is uncivil. The truth is they are running scared. But I can understand why, they stand to lose power and influence if they don't deliver the Republican Party voters as they promised. At their Road To Victory conference in September, Senate Majority Leader Trent Lott asked the Coalition to defeat six Democratic Senators. They have invested over $3 million dollars in an effort to keep their power in Washington, they have a lot at stake," stated Rev. Welton Gaddy, executive director of The Interfaith Alliance.

Under the auspices of local Interfaith Alliances, religious leaders sent over 25,000 fellow clergy around the country. These letters were signed by religious leaders from more than 30 denominations including: Roman Catholic, Islam, African Methodist Episcopal, African Methodist Episcopal Zion, Reform and Conservative Judaism, Southern Baptist, Presbyterian (USA), Church of the Brethren, Seventh Day Adventist, Evangelical Lutheran Church of America, United Methodist Church, United Church of Christ, Christian Church Disciples of Christ, and Episcopalian to name a few. Example letters are available on the website.

The Interfaith Alliance is a non-partisan centrist organization of Muslims, Jews, Christians and other faith traditions dedicated to promoting the positive and healing role of religion in public life. With a membership of 80,000 and a grassroots network of 100 clergy-led alliances around the country, TIA is encouraging people of faith and goodwill to be involved in civic life to promote civility, mutual respect and cooperation.
