Voters Make Clean Money the Law in Massachusetts and Arizona
 
WASHINGTON - November 4 -

--Teleconference Scheduled for 2pm EST--

Resounding victories for comprehensive campaign finance reform initiatives in Arizona and Massachusetts have left voters reclaiming their democracy, and wealthy special interests scrambling. With all the votes counted, voters in Massachusetts approved Question 2 -- "The Clean Elections Law" -- by a 2-1 margin with 66 percent of the vote in favor, while Proposition 200 -- "The Citizens Clean Elections Act" -- passed with 51 percent approval in Arizona.

Arizona and Massachusetts are now poised to join Maine and Vermont in offering candidates for state-level office a chance to break the hold special interests have on public officials through their campaign contributions. Both referenda provide an alternative system of campaign financing that gives all qualifying candidates who agree to take little or no private money and who agree to limit their campaign spending, a fixed amount of money from a publicly-financed pool.

"The people of Massachusetts have spoken loud and clear; they want to end big money's dominance in the Bay State's election campaigns," said David Donnelly, campaign manager for Mass Voters for Clean Elections. "The Massachusetts state legislature should listen to this overwhelming mandate from the voters."

"Arizona is leading the way for national reform," said Kaia Lenhart, political director of Arizonans for Clean Elections. "If sweeping reform can happen in a conservative state like Arizona, it can happen anywhere."

The impressive victories in Arizona and Massachusetts are consistent with a national trend in this year's elections; when offered the opportunity to vote for sweeping change in the way we finance campaigns, voters are not afraid to act. Victories for comprehensive reform in Arizona and Massachusetts were joined by the reelection of reform leader Sen. Russ Feingold (D-WI) and the defeat of Republican Sen. Lauch Faircloth of North Carolina, a well-known friend of big-monied interests, further demonstrating the resiliency of the reform issue among voters.

"The victories in Arizona and Massachusetts for Clean Money Campaign Reform prove that voters have the political will to enact far-reaching campaign finance reform, whether they are liberal or conservative, Democrat or Republican, business or labor," said Ellen Miller, executive director of Public Campaign.

"It is now up to the reform community to demonstrate further that Members of Congress can be held accountable for not supporting the kind of sweeping reform measures the voters want."

The victories also provide strong momentum to local, grassroots efforts in other states working to bring Clean Money reforms to their own electoral systems, as popular support for comprehensive reform is as robust in other states across the country as it is in Arizona and Massachusetts. Missouri is slated to put Clean Money to the ballot test in 2000, while New Mexico will introduce Clean Money Campaign Reform legislation in January. In addition, at least 40 other states are working toward Clean Money reform, including Washington, Oregon and Wisconsin, as they continue to build and broaden their state coalitions and educate voters about the problems of money in politics.

Question 2 in Massachusetts, led by Mass Voters for Clean Elections, was endorsed by the entire Massachusetts congressional delegation, the first time any initiative received such endorsement in the Commonwealth. Former Governor Michael Dukakis, former Lieutenant Governor Evelyn Murphy, former state GOP chair John Sears, Common Cause Board Chair and former Harvard University President Derek Bok and former CEO of Stride Rite Corporation Arnold Hiatt all endorsed the measure as well. More than 6,000 volunteers statewide worked toward the overwhelming victory.

Arizonans for Clean Elections turned in 195,000 signatures to have Proposition 200 placed on the ballot -- 82,000 more signatures than needed to qualify. The proposition also enjoyed the broadest coalition of support of any referenda in state history, gaining the endorsement of the Arizona League of Women Voters, Arizona Common Cause, the state chapter of the American Association of Retired Persons and popular former Governor Rose Mofford.

Maine and Vermont passed similar legislation in 1996 and 1997, respectively. Maine enacted it into law through a ballot initiative, Vermont through a vote in the state legislature.

Public Campaign is a non-profit, non-partisan organization working on behalf of comprehensive campaign finance reform.
