The Whale Hunt: Makah Police Chief Has Long Record Of Civil Rights Violations
 
WASHINGTON - November 3 -

The Sea Shepherd Conservation Society has obtained documents detailing a history of civil rights violations and use of excessive force by Makah Chief of Police Lionel Ahdunko. Makah tribal police are currently under investigation by the Clallam County Sheriff's office in connection with violence against anti-whaling activists in a November 1 incident on the reservation. The FBI is weighing possible federal charges against the tribe for violating the activists' civil rights.

While Ahdunko was Chief of Police for the Washoe Tribe of Nevada and California in 1997, the Washoe Dresslerville Community Council requested emergency police protection from Ahdunko's tribal officers. In a letter to the Bureau of Indian Affairs dated August 10, 1997, the Council told BIA "We are compelled to take this action due to reports that some Dresslerville community residents are arming themselves for protection against the Washoe Tribal Police and that this situation is highly unstable and volatile, requiring immediate attention.

"The DCC [Dresslerville Community Council] excludes the Washoe Tribal Police from the Dresslerville Community effective August 11, 1997, and request that BIA immediately detail officers until this situation is resolved."

After filing a grievance against Ahdunko on October 29, 1997, following a Special Tribal Council Meeting, the Washoe Tribe voted to terminate Ahdunko's employment as Chief of Police.

Numerous civil rights violations and affidavits charging the use of excessive force by the Washoe Tribal Police under Ahdunko were referred to the FBI and the Internal Affairs branch of the Bureau of Indian Affairs, according to Karen L. Winters, an attorney in Minden, Nevada. These include affidavits of injuries sustained by individuals while in police custody.

"This is the tip of the iceberg," said Sea Shepherd Director Lisa Distefano, who was injured by Makah tribal police in last Sunday's incident. "Ahdunko has a long record; the Makah Tribal Council has a lot to answer for, and they will have to do so in court. It is outrageous that they would put tribal members and their children at the mercy of this 'peace officer,' whose record was known to the Tribal Council and the Bureau of Indian Affairs before they hired him."

Documentation of charges will be made available at a press conference to be held November 4, unless there is a whale hunt.
