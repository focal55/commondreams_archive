Environment A Winning Issue From Sea To Shining Sea
 
WASHINGTON - November 4 - Candidates who campaigned on protecting the environment won races from California's redwoods to the New York islands.  Sierra Club efforts contributed to the victories of pro-environment candidates in 38 out of 43 (88%) priority races.   These efforts included paid advertising, free media, financial contributions, volunteers, and staff donated to Senate and House races.

        "Yesterday, voters spoke loud and clear.   And today, Congress is greener.  Clearly, the public wants the next Congress to protect America's environment,"  said Chuck McGrady, President of the Sierra Club.  "Voters yesterday said `no' to divisive partisan politics and `yes' to solving the problems that affect their daily lives -- including cleaning up our air and water and fighting sprawl."

Sierra Club priority candidates include:
Barbara Boxer and Gray Davis in California
Chuck Schumer in New York
John Edwards in North Carolina
Patty Murray in Washington State
Russ Feingold in Wisconsin
Mark Udall in CO-02
Tom Udall in NM-03
Neil Abercrombie in HI-1
Jay Inslee in WA-1

In addition, Sierra Club won all three of its independent expenditure campaigns:
Brian Baird in WA-03;
Dennis Moore in KS-03 (the first time a Democrat has held the seat in 50 years)
Parris Glendening in the Maryland Governor's race.

        "The contrast  between these candidates and their opponents was like the difference between a clear day and a smog alert," McGrady noted.

        The Sierra Club Political Committee contributed $500,000 in direct and in-kind contributions to these candidates.  It also conducted a massive get out the vote program that contacted 250,000 members, close to half of the Club's membership.

        The Sierra Club also conducted a campaign to educate the public about environmental problems, and where their elected officials stand on these issues.  To inform the public and encourage them to contact their elected officials about the environment, the Club broadcast television and radio ads in 40 markets describing the records of  25 incumbents.  The Club  also distributed one million postcards to the public that they could mail to lawmakers, urging these legislators to vote to protect the environment. These activities were part of the Club's ongoing program of grassroots lobbying and public education.

        Finally, the Club mailed and distributed one million voter guides to inform the public about the candidates' positions on critical environmental problems.  These well-documented guides did not advocate the election or defeat of any candidate.
