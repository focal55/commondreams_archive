 Nader: Senate to Celebrate the Fourth by Giving Corporate Wrongdoers Even More Immunity from Accountability
WASHINGTON - July 4 - The following was released today by Ralph Nader:

As the country celebrates Independence Day, which commemorates the individual rights that our forefathers fought to secure from King George III, the United States Senate is set to vote next week of legislation that would limit the power of individual jurors to punish irresponsible companies whose dangerous products injure or take the lives of innocent Americans.

Consumer advocate Ralph Nader stated, "The Revolution was a revolt against tyranny, especially arbitrary and oppressive restrictions on our 'unalienable rights.' One such restriction that provoked the Colonies' to rebel against King George III was the Stamp Act -- which virtually closed down the civil court system. Next week, in dishonor to our founding fathers, the new so-called 'congressional revolutionaries' will vote on cruel legislation that will stamp out critical parts of state laws and make the courthouse door less accessible to many Americans."

Nader was referring to the "Product Liability Reform Act of 1998," which the Senate is scheduled to vote on next week. That legislation would make it harder for jurors to award punitive damages against criminally negligent companies, bar suits against long-lasting products, pressure wrongfully injured people to take unfair settlements, and make most stores and distributors -- in addition to biomaterial suppliers -- immune from liability. The bill is completely tilted against consumers in favor of corporations, as evidenced by a special provision that allows businesses to continue to use America's courts without limitation -- while imposing severe restrictions on the ability of injured Americans to receive justice in their own courtrooms. Moreover, it would override and federalize existing State laws by taking away long standing common law rights.

The leaders of the American Revolution relied on the common law to fight for an open and independent court system in which citizen jurors would be the decision makers. Indeed as Thomas Jefferson stated, Americans were resisting attempts by the British Crown "to submit us at one stroke to a whole system no particle of which has its foundation in the common law." That is precisely why the Declaration of Independence deplored "attempts at depriving us, in many cases, of the benefits of trial by jury" and "abolishing our most valuable laws."

Nader continued, "In celebration of Independence Day, the Senate is planning to vote on legislation that would grant corporate wrongdoers even more immunity from punishment and accountability. Over 200 hundred years ago, our founding fathers fought and died for the right of trial by jury. Next week, U.S. Senators, their campaign coffers loaded with business cash, will vote on whether to limit the power of American jurors to hold irresponsible corporations accountable. Citizens could commemorate our nation's birthday by contacting their Senators immediately at 202-224-3121 and asking them to oppose this legislation."
