IRS Takes Tax Resister to Court — Pacifists Take to the Streets! Brooklyn Protest Against Militarism
 
NEW YORK - March 2- Demonstrators will march through downtown Brooklyn March 5 in support of long-time war tax resister Ed Hedemann and in opposition to U.S. militarism.

Since 1972, Hedemann has protested the use of federal income taxes to finance war and weapons by paying to nonprofit groups and agencies the entire amount due on his federal income taxes. "As a pacifist," he explains, "I can't support any institution that is dedicated to killing and destruction." Now he's being sued in federal court by the IRS to force him to turn over his financial documents. A first hearing is scheduled for March 5, the day of the planned protest.

The protest has been organized by the War Resisters League, a 75-year-old pacifist organization. Each year the War Resisters League publishes "Where Your Income Tax Money Goes," a pamphlet featuring a pie-chart graph of the new federal budget. The pie chart has shown the majority of federal tax monies going to the Pentagon. The organization therefore supports war tax resisters who, like Hedemann, refuse to contribute to what they see as a death machine.

Last December 11, against the backdrop of renewed U.S. air strikes against Iraq, Hedemann was served with an Order to Show Cause why he should not be compelled to turn over his financial records to the IRS. Just days after unleashing the largest military action since the Gulf War, which took nearly 2,000 Iraqi lives and cost U.S. taxpayers more than $500 million, President Clinton asked Congress for an additional $100 billion for the Pentagon over the next six years. "This is the largest military build-up since the unprecedented peace-time military spending of the Reagan era," comments Ruth Benn, Director of the WRL National Office.

Hedemann began withholding federal taxes in 1972 as a protest against the Vietnam War. He has continued the practice since then because, he says, "Fifty to 65 percent of federal income taxes have been spent on present and past military programs through the Department of Defense and related agencies." Since he began refusing to pay the IRS, he says the agency has "routinely sent me threatening notices and levies, called me at home, harassed organizations I work for and looked for nonexistent bank accounts and property. But this is the first time they've ever taken me to court. I guess they're in desperate need of more money to pay for all those cruise missiles."

There are an estimated 8,000 to 10,000 people in the U.S. who are refusing to pay some or all of their federal taxes because of opposition to war. In the past 30 years only a half dozen war tax resisters have been taken to court; the last such case was nine years ago.

Hedemann files all his tax returns, and pays state, local, and social security taxes. But when he files his federal returns, he sends, instead of a check, a note of explanation as to why he is sending the entire amount due to organizations such as Voices in the Wilderness, which provides material aid to Iraqi civilians; the New York Times Neediest Cases fund; and a documentary film project on the U.S.-aided murder of Jesuit priests in El Salvador.

The planned march will pass both the military recruiting center and the IRS office in downtown Brooklyn, showing the multiple ways that militarism impacts the lives of ordinary New Yorkers.

A spokesperson for the War Resisters League said, "While Army and Navy recruiters are redoubling their efforts to attract young people to the military and the US is engaged in a slow motion war against the people of Iraq, the reasons for war tax resistance are painfully clear. The Pentagon would rather address global problems with cruise missiles than diplomacy while the dollars it consumes are desperately needed by communities here at home."


Schedule:

Leaflet in front of U.S. military recruiters at Flatbush Avenue and State Street
Walk along Flatbush to IRS Building at 625 Fulton Street
