Environmentalists Endorse 1999 Shareholder Resolutions
 
WASHINGTON - March 8 - Today a coalition of eight environmental groups, led by Greenpeace and Friends of the Earth, today endorsed a slate of 74 environmentally friendly shareholder resolutions filed at companies such as Exxon, Maxxam, and Kodak.

Shareholder resolutions, which are proposals that are introduced by and voted upon by a company's shareholders, are an increasingly important tool in challenging corporate threats to the environment. These resolutions were endorsed at the start of the 1999 corporate proxy voting season.

Endorsed resolutions advocate improved environmental policies, performance, or disclosure; or the elimination of environmentally harmful projects or practices. Major categories of environmental resolutions include: endorsing environmental codes of conduct, global warming, environmental reporting and standards, toxics issues, nuclear issues, and project-specific resolutions at resource extraction companies. For example, the coalition endorsed ten resolutions on climate change while another seven questioned the oil projects that fuel global warming.

"Challenging and changing corporate behavior is key to environmental progress," said Carwil James, of the Greenpeace Climate Campaign. "Because of their economic power, corporations now have the type of power once only held by governments, but more effort is needed to hold them accountable."

The endorsing groups are calling on people and institutions all over the country to claim their power as shareholders -- through pension plans, mutual funds, or endowments -- and demand healthy communities and corporate accountability.

Groups as diverse as the Free Burma Coalition and the Good Neighbor Project endorsed the resolutions. Though groups may not endorse or have expertise on every individual resolution on the slate, most organizations directly work on the targeted environmental issues, or run campaigns on individual corporations. For example, groups which have long fought to stop the destructive Three Gorges Dam in China, are now using shareholder power to target companies like Morgan Stanley Dean Witter, who backs the controversial dam.

"Environmental groups are forging stronger and closer alliances with shareholders such as religious investors and union pension funds," said Michelle Chan-Fishel coordinator of Friends of the Earth's Green Investments program. "These shareholders have long known that corporate responsibility is a shareholder issue, not just an activist demand."

Greenpeace is the leading independent organization that uses peaceful and creative activism to protect the global environment.
