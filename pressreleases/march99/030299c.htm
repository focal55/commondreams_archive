Ten Commandments Display To Be Removed From Courthouse In Lumpkin County, Georgia; County Heeds Legal Warnings, Avoids Church-State Lawsuit
 
WASHINGTON - March 2- The Lumpkin County, Ga., County Commissioner has decided to remove a Ten Commandments display from the local courthouse after a threatened lawsuit by civil liberties groups.

Americans United for Separation of Church and State and the Georgia affiliate of the American Civil Liberties Union contacted the county to warn that legal action could result unless the Commandments display was removed.

After the Georgia Americans United chapter received complaints from local citizens, Americans United wrote to County Commissioner Charlie Ridley on Feb. 22, explaining that the display was in violation of the First Amendment of the Constitution because it amounted to government endorsement of religion.

In a Feb. 25 reply, County Attorney William M. Brownell, Jr. explained, "Upon careful consideration of this issue, the Commissioner has agreed to take down the Ten Commandment display from the Lumpkin County Courthouse."

"Lumpkin County clearly made the right decision," said the Rev. Barry W. Lynn, executive director of Americans United. "Government officials need to learn that the Ten Commandments is a religious text and should not be manipulated as some kind of political football.

"By posting one faith's religious text on a courthouse wall, Lumpkin County was promoting a specific faith through official state action," Lynn added. "I've got a commandment for government officials: Thou shalt keep church and state separate."

Americans United is a religious liberty watchdog group based in Washington, D.C. Founded in 1947, the organization represents 60,000 members and allied houses of worship in all 50 states. The group also has over 40 state and local chapters.
