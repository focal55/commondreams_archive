Albright In Indonesia; Analysts Available
 
WASHINGTON - March 4 - With Secretary of State Madeleine Albright visiting Indonesia, prominent critics of U.S. policy in the region are available to comment on U.S.-Indonesian relations:

JEFFREY WINTERS, (847) 491-2630, (773) 281-8285, jaw@nwu.edu
Associate professor of political economy at Northwestern University and author of "Power in Motion: Capital Mobility and the Indonesian State," Winters said: "Albright's approach to the opposition remains highly deferential to the government in power. This is a mistake since the Habibie government has no legitimacy whatsoever. On East Timor, the U.S. has a long and dishonorable record, but the U.S. now should aggressively seek a UN peacekeeping solution leading to a referendum in East Timor. Otherwise, it only increases the chances that Jakarta will engineer a violent outcome."

ALLAN NAIRN, (212) 662-3649, anairn@igc.org
Author of the forthcoming "Our Kind of Guys: The U.S. and the Indonesian Military," Nairn said today: "The U.S. has been the main sponsor of the Suharto regime. At the time of the East Timor invasion, they supplied 90 percent of the arms, and Ford and Kissinger approved the invasion. Until last spring, the U.S. was training the most notorious units of the army on advanced sniper techniques and psychological warfare. There is still $106 million of U.S. weapons sales this year. While Indonesia has made clear its desire to withdraw from East Timor, they have launched attacks by paramilitary units allied with the Indonesian military."

ISABEL GALHOS, (613), 233-3840, bella@jungle.ottawa.on.ca, http://www.easttimor.com
A representative of the National Council of Timorese Resistance, Galhos said: "What is most important right now is the disarmament of the paramilitary and the need for the UN to be on the ground to prevent the violence.... Since the invasion of East Timor in 1975, one-third of the East Timorese population has been killed, that's over 200,000 people. I was three years old when the invasion happened, most of my family was killed. My story is typical; most people have had family members killed by the military. The first year, they killed people randomly, then they moved people to concentration camps, where many children died of malnutrition."

LYNN FREDRIKSSON, (202) 544-6911, etandc@igc.org , http://www.etan.org
Washington representative of the East Timor Action Network, Fredriksson said: "As Albright meets for the first time with Timorese resistance leader Xanana Gusmao, still under house arrest and considered by many the Nelson Mandela of East Timor, it is crucial to recall that Indonesian-supported paramilitary units are terrorizing the civilian population of East Timor. Secretary Albright should take a firm stand with President Habibie and General Wiranto and press for the immediate opening of East Timor to international monitors, and an immediate halt to the arming of these brutal units." ETAN can arrange interviews with East Timorese activists, including Nobel Laureate Jose Ramos-Horta. Contact John Miller at (718) 596-7668.
