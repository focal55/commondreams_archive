Prime Minister of India Declares Continued Commitment to Nuclear Weapons; Abolition Plea by International Delegation of Physicians Fails to Persuade Prime Minister and Defense Minister of India
 
WASHINGTON - March 3 - A delegation of physicians met last week in Delhi with Indian Prime Minister A.B. Vajpayee and Defense Minister George Fernandes. The delegation, organized by International Physicians for Prevention of Nuclear War, urged India to sign the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty and to take a leadership role in the worldwide campaign to abolish nuclear weapons.

The visit to India came the day after the conclusion of historic talks between Prime Minister Vajpayee and Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif of Pakistan. The countries signed an agreement affirming their moratorium on nuclear testing and setting up confidence building measures to reduce the possibility of accidental nuclear war between them The agreement emphasized the need to solve all issues, including Kashmir, by peaceful means.

But despite the agreement between the two countries, India remains firm in her refusal to sign the CTBT. According to members of the physicians' delegation, the Prime Minister and Defense Minister of India expressed their desire to eventually get rid of nuclear weapons, but said it was impossible as long as the U.S. and other countries continued to maintain their own nuclear arsenals.

"We had some substantive and productive discussions," said Dr. Ira Helfand, Past-President of Physicians for Social Responsibility and the only U.S. representative on the delegation. AI think India's leaders recognize the dangers of nuclear weapons, but they believe that as long as nuclear weapons are essential for U.S. security, then nuclear weapons are essential for Indian security as well."

According to Dr. Helfand, Pakistan built nuclear weapons as a deterrent against India. India wanted them as a deterrent against China. China built them as a deterrent against the U.S. and Russia. He says that it will be impossible to make progress toward nuclear abolition as long as the U.S. maintains the position of Ado as I say, not as I do.@ Indian leaders are quick to point out that Article VI of the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPF) binds the U.S. to eliminate nuclear weapons. "The acquisition of nuclear weapons by India and Pakistan is a powerful indictment of the failure of the U.S. to live up to this obligation," says Dr. Helfand.

In addition to dialogues with the key decision makers, the group participated in a two-day seminar with leading academics, military analysts and opinion leaders who raised significant issues and diverse perspectives on the prospects for nuclear disarmament. The delegation also attended a Peace Conference, in Karachi, Pakistan, which included members of Parliament and retired military leaders, as well as many doctors, academics, activists from Pakistan and a delegation of about 30 from India.
