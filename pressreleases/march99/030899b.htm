Press Breakfast With Authors Of "Social Security: The Phony Crisis"
 
WASHINGTON - March 8 - The big secret in the rush to "save" Social Security is that, according to the projections accepted by everyone in the debate, Social Security does not need to be saved. But pretending that it does serves political purposes for President Clinton, as well as many Republicans and Democrats in Congress.

Drs. Dean Baker and Mark Weisbrot, co-authors of "Social Security: The Phony Crisis" (1999, University of Chicago Press), will provide a briefing on the facts underlying the supposed need to "reform" Social Security. Baker is Senior Research Fellow at the Century Foundation (formerly the Twentieth Century Fund) and the Preamble Center, and Weisbrot is Research Director of the Preamble Center.

Date: Tuesday, March 9, 1999

When: 9:30 a.m. (Breakfast will be served)

Where: National Press Club, 529 14th Street N.W., Club Room

Who: Drs. Dean Baker and Mark Weisbrot

As Ralph Nader wrote in his invitation to this breakfast, "The basic facts about Social Security -- the ones known to all the economists and actuaries most familiar with the Social Security system -- have gotten lost in the din over how to 'save' it. And these facts show that Social Security does not need to be saved. There is no significant problem with Social Security's finances."

According to the best projections of the Social Security Board of Trustees, Social Security will be able to pay all promised benefits until 2032. Even after that, incoming taxes will be sufficient to pay real (inflation-adjusted) benefits that are higher than those of today, for the rest of the 75-year planning period. And the revenue that would be needed to make up the projected shortfall after 2032 amounts to less than 1% of our national income over the next 75 years.

The arguments used by those who are pressing for the "reform" of Social Security turn out, upon examination, to be surprisingly empty. They rely on various popular confusions over the status of the Social Security Trust Fund, a conflation of Social Security and Medicare (they are separate programs with separate funding sources), and other rhetorical and accounting tricks.

No RSVP is necessary. All are welcome.

Breakfast will be served
