Report Shows Bush Could Have Raised $92 Million If 'Reform' Proposals Are Adopted
 
WASHINGTON - September 9 - A congressional proposal to increase campaign contribution limits from $1,000 to $3,000 would have enabled presidential candidate George W. Bush to raise up to $92 million from his existing donors in the first half of this year, according to a new report released today by the U.S. Public Interest Research Group (U.S. PIRG). The report projects that raising the limits from $1000 to $3000 could increase the funds coming from $1000 donors from 58% of the total money raised to 81%. Money raised from contributions less than $200 could decrease from 8.1% of the total to 3.5%.

Gov. Bush has spoken in favor of raising the current limits, and early in September, the House will vote on an amendment to the Shays-Meehan bipartisan reform bill, offered by Rep. Edward Whitfield (R-KY), that would triple existing contribution limits.

"Even with the existing $1000 limits, the percentage of funds from donors giving the maximum contribution is astounding. Wealthy individuals who can afford to give the maximum contribution are more and more influential in determining who runs for office and who wins party nominations," said Julia Hutchins, U.S. PIRG democracy advocate. "Tripling the contribution limit would further diminish the role of small donors, whose participation represents the political voice of ordinary Americans," she continued.

Data from the report also demonstrates that $1000 donors are a minute proportion of all Americans. Contributions of $1000 make up 80.4% of all itemized funds raised from individual donors, but these donors only represent one in every 5,000 Americans.

U.S. PIRG is continuing its efforts to reduce the powerful influence of wealthy special interests in elections. The organization opposes any further weakening of reform legislation, especially amendments that would increase hard money contribution limits.

"In the midst of the biggest 'wealth primary' ever, with presidential candidates raising record amounts of money in hard dollars, it is inconceivable that politicians would consider compromising contribution limits in exchange for a ban on soft money." said Hutchins. "Increasing the $1000 limits is not reform, but a rewiring of the current system that lets the same big money in through new channels," she concluded.

PIRG's democracy campaign director Derek Cressman expressed concern that raising the current contribution limits would jeopardize the presidential voluntary spending limits, as more candidates would choose to forego federal matching funds if they could raise more from their wealthiest donors. "It would send a signal that it's okay for the wealthy minority to unduly influence elections," said Cressman. "Even if accompanied by a ban on soft money contributions to parties, allowing fat cats to give even more money to candidates is campaign deform, not reform," he added.
