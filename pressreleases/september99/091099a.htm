For Sale: US Arms Technology; Licenses for Manufacturing and Technical Assistance Worth $10.7 Billion
 
WASHINGTON - September 10 - While the public relations machine at the State Department touts the U.S. government's recent efforts to curb the global market in illicit small arms, and while members of Congress and Washington pundits wail over the reported transfer of U.S. nuclear technology to the Chinese, the U.S. continues to license the export of small arms and conventional weapons technology to nations like Indonesia every year. In 1998 alone, the State Department allowed the sale of $10.7 billion in weapons production technology to 59 governments, according to an annual report issued by the Administration.

Last year licenses were approved to sell Indonesia almost $2.5 million in manufacturing and technical assistance, the software, blueprints, instructions and manufacturing rights necessary to make American designed weapons. As the current situation in East Timor shows, selling weapons, much less the means to make them, to dictatorial, repressive regimes is a dangerous and often ill-fated activity. M-16 armed Indonesian soldiers are making East Timor burn, and the U.S. is virtually powerless to stop it.

Several other human rights abusing governments were permitted to receive large amounts of technical assistance last year as well, including Turkey, Colombia, and Saudi Arabia. When will those countries use U.S. weapons and arms making capabilities in ways that go against U.S. foreign policy goals?

Controlling and tracking weapons once they have been sold is hard enough, but it will be that much harder to keep tabs on arms proliferation when we have sold the know-how and rights to foreign countries to build the weapons themselves. If countries like Indonesia can build their own small arms, how can we know what they build, much less where the weapons go and for what purpose they are used? At least if the U.S. and other arms exporters control the means of weapons production, they can partially control the flow and use of arms.

Meanwhile, Congress and the "experts " continue to complain about U.S. nuclear technology being sold to China, but then ignore the export of U.S. conventional weapons technology. The sad irony, if not contradiction, is that conventional weapons and technology are far more likely to be used against U.S. troops in future conflicts than nuclear weapons and know-how.

If these patriotic politicians and commentators were truly concerned with America's security, they would also want to crack down on the export of U.S. conventional weapons technology. The reason that they do not could have something to do with the fact that manufacturing and technical assistance licensed over the last three year was worth $35 billion. It is hard to turn the spigot off with that kind of money coming out of the faucet.
