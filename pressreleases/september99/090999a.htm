World's Population of Refugees, Asylum Seekers and Internally Displaced Totaled More than 30 Million at End of 1998
 
WASHINGTON - September 9 - The world's population of refugees, asylum seekers, and internally displaced persons totaled more than 30.4 million at the end of 1998 -- an increase of more than 500,000 over 1997, according to the new World Refugee Survey released today by the U.S. Committee For Refugees (USCR).

The annual Survey is the most comprehensive resource for statistics and information on refugees, asylum seekers, and internally displaced persons around the world.

As in last year's Survey, Sudan accounts for the largest number of the world's uprooted people -- more than 4.3 million Sudanese have been forced to flee because of the continuing bloody civil war in the south and east. One out of every eight refugees and displaced persons in the world are Sudanese, the Survey found.

Overall, five groups account for nearly 45 percent of the world's refugee and displaced population, the USCR Survey found: Sudanese, Palestinians (3.8 million), Afghans (2.6 million), Iraqis (1.5 million); and Colombians (1.4 million).

"It is the world's shame that we close the 20th century knowing that political conflict, ethnic strife, war, and persecution have left more than 30 million civilian victims fighting for their survival," USCR Executive Director Roger Winter said. "It will be the world's continuing shame as we enter the 21st century if the world's fortunate ignore the plight of the uprooted. As we enter a new century, I ask that we recommit ourselves to creating safe conditions for refugee return, to promote reconciliation of war-torn societies, and to prevent the inequities and injustices that cause displacement."

Highlights of the new World Refugee Survey include:

-- The 13.4 million refugees worldwide at the end of 1998 were slightly fewer than the 13.5 million documented in 1997. The 13.4 million refugees in 1998 were also more than 4 million fewer than the 17.6 million documented at the refugee population's peak in 1992.

-- The 17 million internally displaced people worldwide at the end of 1998 represented an increase of some 600,000 over 1997.

-- Eighty percent of all uprooted people in Africa are currently seeking assistance and protection in countries that themselves are experiencing internal armed conflict.

-- Nearly two-thirds of all refugees worldwide are being hosted in Middle East countries -- 43 percent -- or African countries -- about 21 percent.

-- Nearly 830,000 refugees voluntarily returned to their homelands during 1998. More than half of these repatriations occurred in three countries -- Liberia, Bosnia and Hercegovina, and Afghanistan.

USCR's annual World Refugee Survey -- now in its 41st year -- carefully documents conditions for the world's refugees and internally displaced persons in 120 countries and territories and provides detailed statistics and expert analysis on the state of refugees and IDPs on a country-by-country and regional basis.

This year's Survey also includes a powerful "Decade In Review" by Kathleen Newland, senior associate at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. Newland calls the 1990s a decade of experimentation for the international community as it sought new ways to respond effectively to the needs and issues of refugees. Unfortunately, Newland writes, these innovations have been, for the most part, ad hoc and crisis driven, "leaving a new but largely unplanned and unexamined system of humanitarian response."

A free copy of the World Refugee Survey is available to reporters. Highlights of the Survey can also be accessed at www.refugees.org.

------
The U.S. Committee for Refugees (USCR) is a nonprofit, nongovernmental, humanitarian, organization that defends the rights of refugees, asylum seekers, and displaced people worldwide.
