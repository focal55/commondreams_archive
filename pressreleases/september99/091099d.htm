AFSC Urges Support For Angolan Grassroots Peace Effort
 
"We, the Angolan people, should take full responsibility for the solution of our own problems." -- The Manifesto for Peace in Angola

PHILADELPHIA - September 10 - The American Friends Service Committee is urging international support for a grassroots peace initiative emerging from religious and non-governmental organizations in Angola that offers hope for ending nearly 40 years of war and social upheaval there.

The Manifesto for Peace in Angola, an initiative to build a consensus for peace based on a "patriotic vision of social justice and national equity" has been gaining momentum in Angola and abroad. The document has currently garnered over 1,000 signatures and is being circulated in that country by the Angolan Group of Reflection for Peace (Grupo Angolano de Reflexão para a Paz/GARP).

"Given Angola's recent history of war and refugees, this document reflects a deep need in the Angolan people for lasting peace," wrote AFSC Executive Director Kara Newell in a letter to Daniel Ntoni-Nzinga, the Quaker International Affairs Representative for Southern Africa, who has joined those providing leadership in promoting the initiative. Newell praised the manifesto as "a surge of hope for Angola's future."

"The Angolan manifesto is also a challenge to the rest of us not to be afraid to seek peace and justice outside the confines of government and ideology, and to have greater faith in civil society," Newell wrote.

Daniel Ntoni-Nzinga stated that Angolan citizens hold the key to healing the deep rifts that have torn at the African nation.

"The Angolan people believe that the only way to peace is through negotiation; the belligerents assume that war is the way to reach peace, even if this means the destruction of one part of the nation by the other," he said.

The manifesto sets forth fundamental principals necessary for peace, including social justice, good governance and mutual respect for the diversity of people and cultures there. The document rejects the possibility for military solutions to the Angolan conflict and cites political, military and international forces that seek control over the country's wealth of oil and diamonds as fueling the conflict.

"We have reached the extreme stage of suffering, social humiliation and total perversion of the use of power. Hence, we have come to the conclusion . . . that we, the Angolan people, should develop a common ground for the causes as well as for the consequences of the military and political conflict we are facing," the manifesto states.

The document calls for a central role for members of civil society - leaders of non-governmental civic and religious organizations -- who would establish conditions for the inclusiveness and safe participation of Angolans in the process of national dialogue for peace. The document calls for an immediate cease fire between government and rebel organizations' (UNITA and FLEC) armed forces and the immediate opening of formal communication and mediation among belligerents. The manifesto also calls for the opening of humanitarian corridors to assist people affected by famine and violence and the use of portions of governmental and rebel military budgets to fund peace initiatives.

###

The American Friends Service Committee is a Quaker organization that includes people of various faiths who are committed to social justice, peace, and humanitarian service. Its work is based on the Quaker belief in the worth of every person and faith in the power of love to overcome violence and injustice.
