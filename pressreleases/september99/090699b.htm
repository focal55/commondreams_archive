Dili Press Conference on Links Among East Timor Militias, TNI/ABRI, U.S. Government
 
NEW YORK - September 6 - News Advisory:

Date:
Tuesday, Sept. 7, 8 a.m. (Dili, East Timor time;
Monday, Sept. 6, 8 p.m., U.S. Eastern time)

Place: Dili, East Timor.
Exact location to be announced shortly before 8 a.m.

Details:
At 8 a.m. on Tuesday, Sept. 6 in Dili, award-winning journalist and human rights activist Allan Nairn will disclose new information on the links among the East Timor militias, TNI/ABRI (the Indonesian Armed Forces), and the U.S. government.

Nairn was injured while attempting to stop the Nov. 12, 1991 Santa Cruz massacre. He has since been banned from Indonesia and Timor as a "threat to national security" (see bio below).

A diplomat told Nairn on Monday that the militias have been searching for him in Dili.

Nairn will be releasing advance copies of a report based on an extended investigation in the U.S., Indonesia, and East Timor. The report is based on interviews with Western and Indonesian military and intelligence officials, including two Kopassus (Special Forces) Generals, a former military intelligence (SGI) commander in Timor, and Western intelligence officials with access to classified intercepts. It relies on and cites numerous internal documents, including:

-- a classified account of a crucial meeting relating to the militias between Gen. Wiranto and a senior U.S. military official

-- a discussion of Indonesian armed forces (TNI) torture from an internal Kopassus assessment of the current situation in East Timor

-- An internal U.S. cable from the U.S. State Department to U.S. CINCPAC (Commander in Chief Pacific) that praises the human rights performance of Lt. Gen Prabowo, Gen. Hendro Priyono, Gen. Feisal Tanjung, and other U.S.-trained TNI/ABRI officials

-- Internal U.S. and Indonesian personnel records relating to Intel, Kopassus, and other TNI officers trained by the United States

-- Notes from a briefing for TNI officers by a senior U.S. military official in which the official discusses plans for a new U.S. training facility in Indonesia

-- the personal phone records of Aitarak militia leader Eurico Guterres

-- internal bank records from the pro-militia FPDK

If copying facilities can be found, copies of and excerpts from selected documents will be made available to the press and public.

Nairn's report will form the basis of an article to be released this week by The Nation magazine (New York).

Since much of East Timor is now under seige by the Indonesian army and militias, the location of this event will not be announced until shortly beforehand.

Please Note: No Timorese or Indonesian organization is involved in organizing this event, nor is any international organization with offices in Indonesia or East Timor.

While in Timor on assignment for The New Yorker magazine, Allan Nairn was injured while attempting to stop the Nov. 12, 1991 Santa Cruz massacre. His skull was factured by TNI/ABRI troops wielding U.S. M-16 rifles.

After the massacre Nairn, (together with Amy Goodman of WBAI/Pacifica Radio (U.S.)) was banned from Indonesia and occupied Timor as "a threat to national security." The ban has since been personally reaffirmed by the TNI commander, General Wiranto. During the November, 1994 APEC summit Nairn was arrested twice by Indonesian military intelligence while trying to enter East Timor. He later succeeded without their knowledge.

In March, 1998, during the uprising against Suharto, Nairn entered Indonesia without army permission. On March 16 he published an article in The Nation magazine (New York) and later held a press conference in Jakarta in which he disclosed documents showing that, in defiance of an understanding with the U.S. Congress, the U.S. military was training the Indonesian armed forces in urban warfare, Psy Ops, and "advanced sniper technique." The East Timor Action Network (ETAN), together with members of the U.S. Congress, held a concurrent press conference on the documents in Washington, D.C. The day after his press conference, Nairn was arrested and deported from Indonesia. During interrogation he was told that if he returned to Indonesia or Timor he would be jailed for six years on charges of defying the ban and "practicing journalism without permission."

The training expose led to an outcry in Congress that resulted in suspension of the program, known as JCET (Joint Combined Exchange and Training), in Indonesia.

Earlier this year, in defiance of the ban, Nairn again entered Indonesia without army permission. He has been in occupied East Timor since August.

Nairn has covered military and human rights issues since 1980. His reporting from Guatemala, El Salvador, Haiti, Indonesia, East Timor and other places, has won the George Polk Award, the RFK Journalism Award, The DuPont-Columbia Broadcast Journalism Award, the CPB (Corporation for Public Broadcasting (U.S.)) silver medallion and other honors

Since the Santa Cruz massacre Nairn has campaigned internationally to halt the supply of arms and training to the Indonesian armed forces. He is a leader of the East Timor Action Network (U.S.) and is organizing Justice for All, a grassroots human rights group.
