FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE
CONTACT: Human Rights Campaign
AIDS Death Rates Stablize While New HIV Infections Continue Unabated; HRC Urges House and Senate to Make Public Health Needs a Priority
 
WASHINGTON - September 2 - In a letter today, the Human Rights Campaign urged the House and Senate Appropriation Committee leaders to develop a responsible plan to immediately pass and adequately fund the FY 2000 Labor, the Health and Human Services, and Education Appropriations bill. The letter highlights the sobering statistics on HIV/AIDS reported this week at the National HIV Prevention conference, sponsored by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

"The statistics underscore the reality that the HIV/AIDS epidemic remains a public health emergency and they highlight the importance of putting public health interests ahead of short-term political goals," said HRC's Political Director Winnie Stachelberg.

CDC officials and other researchers reported that in 1997 AIDS deaths declined by 42 percent, while in 1998 they dropped by only 20 percent. Equally concerning, new HIV infections continue unabated and the available evidence does not suggest that this number is declining. The numbers for people of color are even more troublesome. AIDS deaths dropped 35 percent in 1997 and 17 percent in 1998 for African Americans, while comparable reductions for whites are 51percent and 22 percent.

Young gay men are also at high risk. A study in New York City this year indicated that large numbers of young gay men age 15 to 22, became infected with HIV within the last two years. In San Francisco, the percentage of gay men who report using condoms all the time has dropped from 70 percent to 60 percent. Gay men who use drugs intravenously had the highest percentage of new infections in 1998 and 1999.

"We are on the verge of sacrificing an entire new generation of gay men to this epidemic", said Stachelberg. "There is a certain complacency in our community and on Capitol Hill resulting from the misperception that HIV/AIDS has become a manageable disease."

CDC officials also attributed the declining of AIDS deaths to the relatively short duration during which the available drugs are effective before the virus becomes resistant to them. The complexity of a treatment program known as HAART (highly active antiretroviral therapy) also contributes to reducing its benefit. The treatment program usually includes three to five drugs designed to decrease the viral load and may also include medications to prevent or treat particular opportunistic infections. Multiple daily doses of seven or eight different drugs, with various nutritional and timing requirements, makes HIV treatment complicated, costly and very difficult to maintain for many people.

"The clear message from Atlanta is that the HIV epidemic is far from over and that prevention remains the only currently available cure. In order to be effective, efforts to prevent HIV infection must reach the hundreds of thousands of Americans who have HIV and don't know it or who are engaging in high-risk behavior," said Stachelberg in the letter.

The issue for lawmakers is to decide how to pay for it. The current allocation for the FY 2000 Labor/HHS/Education Subcommittee, which funds HIV/AIDS related programs such as the Ryan White CARE Act, is $17 billion (18 percent) below President Clinton's FY 2000 Budget request and $15 billion (16 percent) below the comparable FY 1999 enacted levels. In the Senate, the Labor/H bill is slightly higher, with its current allocation at $8.4 billion (9 percent) below the President's FY 2000 Budget request and $4 billion (5 percent) below the FY 99 enacted level.

"Appropriators have a clear choice to make", said Stachelberg, "We can further deny HIV treatment and prevention needs and hope that more effective, simpler and cheaper treatments will become available soon. Or we can begin the new century with a comprehensive, adequately funded strategy to prevent new infections, care for those who are sick, and avoid sacrificing a new generation of young people to this epidemic".

The Human Rights Campaign is the largest national lesbian and gay political organization, with members throughout the country. It effectively lobbies Congress, provides campaign support, and educates the public to ensure that lesbian and gay Americans can be open, honest, and safe at home, at work, and in the community.
