Sen. Landrieu Flip-Flops On Protecting Forests; Senator's Actions Don't Live Up to Her Rhetoric
 
WASHINGTON - September 9 - Senator Mary Landrieu (D-LA) flip-flopped on a major environmental vote today, voting to allow timber companies to cut down National Forests without considering how the logging would harm wildlife and wildlife habitat. Landrieu cast her anti-environmental vote despite signing a letter just weeks ago opposing the very logging proposal she voted for today.

"If Senator Landrieu felt strongly enough to sign a letter opposing forest destruction, she should have stuck with that position when she voted," said Barbara Vincent, vice chair of Sierra Club's Delta Chapter, which covers Louisiana. "Why tell people you support forest protection and then turn around and vote the other way?"

On July 15, 1999, Landrieu wrote to the top Republican and Democratic Senators overseeing the Interior Department budget, asking them to remove a rider that could wipe out wildlife habitats in America's National Forests. However, when the Senate today voted on removing that rider, Sen. Landrieu voted to keep the rider in the bill.

"In July, Senator Landrieu wrote that she cared about healthy forests, but when push came to shove, she failed to stand up for America's wildlife and National Forests," said Sean Cosgrove, Sierra Club's forest policy specialist. "Senator Landrieu's vote allows the Forest Service to ignore science and fire up the chainsaws. In a vote that pleases the timber industry, Senator Landrieu has uprooted our nation's laws, turned science on its head and mocked the public's demand that Congress protect our National Forests."

The letter reads in part: "We are writing to encourage you to remove Section 329 ... By limiting decisions to the `available' science, Section 329 turns a blind eye to the information we need to make the best possible management decisions on our public lands. Section 329 inappropriately interferes with the judicial process, and reverses years of USFS and BLM management direction without any public involvement or debate."

Current law requires that the Departments of Agriculture and Interior gather and consider scientific information about wildlife populations when they plan logging and other destructive activities. However, Sen. Slade Gorton (R-WA) slipped a rider into the Interior Department appropriations bill that would allow those departments to ignore the law. Specifically, the rider would allow the departments to revise public land management plans, and to issue new leases, without considering how those actions would threaten endangered species. The Senate today voted on an amendment sponsored by Sens. Charles Robb (D-VA) and Max Cleland (D-GA) to eliminate that harmful rider and uphold the law requiring that the forest managers use scientific information about wildlife populations. Although Sen. Landrieu had signed a Robb-Cleland letter asking that the rider be removed, she voted against their amendment, which failed on a 45-52 vote.
