U.S. Government Turns Its Back on Dioxin Elimination in Global Pollution Treaty
 
GENEVA - September 9 - The United States is deliberately backing away from action to eliminate the highly toxic chemical dioxin, a retreat that will likely cause this dangerous substance to proliferate in the environment, Greenpeace charged today.

In Geneva, Switzerland, where the United Nations is meeting to work out a global treaty to eliminate certain toxic chemicals including dioxin, U.S. State Department negotiator Brooks Yeager clarified the U.S. position. He said the State Department will oppose any treaty language that would "aim to eliminate" dioxins or require changes in any U.S. regulations, even though the treaty would impose such changes in many other countries.

"Only a few governments, including the U.S. and Canada, are standing in the way of totally eliminating POPs by pretending risks from these chemicals can be managed, when there is no safe level of exposure," said Greenpeace senior campaigner Jack Weinberg. "The current U.S. position would result in dramatic increases in dioxin emissions in the developing world. It will encourage developing countries to repeat the mistakes of the industrialized world by embracing obsolete pollution control systems instead of adopting the dioxin-free technologies that exist today."

The goal of the U.N. treaty is to craft a legally binding agreement to eliminate chemicals known as persistent organic pollutants (POPs), such as dioxins, DDT, and PCBs. Dioxins are unintentional by-products of the manufacture and incineration of polyvinyl chloride, or PVC (vinyl).

The U.S. position comes as a particular blow to two citizens of Mossville, Louisiana, a predominantly African American community situated among several chemical facilities involved in the manufacture of PVC. David Prince and Edgar Mouton traveled to Geneva to tell their community's story to the United Nations Environment Program. They charged that "the U.S. government appears to be more interested in preserving the lives of polluting industries than preserving the health of their citizens." Prince was one of 28 Mossville citizens tested earlier this year for blood dioxin levels by the federal Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry. The tests found blood dioxin levels in Mossville residents that far exceed the national average.

Cancers, respiratory problems, reproductive disorders are among those illnesses associated with dioxin contamination, and which are occurring with alarming frequency in Mossville.
