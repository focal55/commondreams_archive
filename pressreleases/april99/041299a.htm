Nation's Most Endangered Rivers of 1999: Sprawl Called One of Worst Threats to Face Rivers in Years American Rivers Calls on State, Local, Federal Governments to Mitigate Sprawl Damage
 
WASHINGTON - April 12 - Sprawl is one of the most pervasive and menacing threats to face the nation’s rivers in years, according to American Rivers, a national river conservation organization.

In a series of press conferences held around the country today, American Rivers announced this year’s list of the ten most endangered rivers in the country as part of the release of its 14th annual report, America’s Most Endangered Rivers of 1999.

This year’s America’s Most Endangered Rivers report demonstrates for the first time how devastating unbridled development is to the nation’s rivers and catalogues areas where sprawl and rivers are on a head-on collision course.

“We’ve all seen and felt sprawl - from lengthy commutes and crowded schools to polluted air and lost open space. It is seeping into every aspect of our lives. But few people understand the unique and devastating impacts of unplanned, rapid growth on rivers or the hardships that communities face from a wholesale degradation of rivers across the country. We have made great progress in cleaning up our rivers in the 25 years since the Clean Water Act, but sprawl is threatening to reverse those gains,” said Rebecca Wodder, President of American Rivers.

Today, American Rivers called on local and state governments to reduce the damage sprawl causes to rivers. Governments must encourage re-development of high-density areas and make them more attractive and livable-in part by restoring urban riverfronts-to limit the amount of new impervious cover spreading out over the land. Governments must also encourage use of mass transit and carpools to reduce the need for more roads and parking lots. Further, local governments must set zoning standards for site development that reduce runoff and protect riverside lands.

State and federal agencies should protect and restore rivers that are impaired by excessive water withdrawals. This can often be accomplished by encouraging water conservation by pricing water sensibly, as well as through existing laws such as the Clean Water Act, the Endangered Species Act, and state water laws. Water allocation plans must recognize the needs of fish, wildlife, and humans.

Sprawl chews up riverbanks, wetlands and floodplains, critical components of a river ecosystem that provide wildlife habitat, filter sediment and toxins, and act like sponges to absorb floodwaters. Paving over the land with roads, parking lots, rooftops, and anything else that can’t absorb rainwater creates “impervious cover” and dramatically increases the amount of polluted runoff flowing into rivers and streams. Impervious cover causes more frequent and severe flooding and leads to streambank erosion and habitat loss. According to the Center for Watershed Protection, when 10% of a watershed is sealed under such cover, the rivers and streams that receive its runoff will be degraded. And when 25-40% of a watershed is paved over, its rivers and streams can often no longer support fish and wildlife and are not safe for human uses.

In the Seattle area, uncontrolled development is sucking excessive amounts of water from the Cedar River and runoff is polluting the water that remains-destroying the river’s fragile salmon and steelhead runs. Chicago’s rampant growth is overloading the suburban area’s sewage treatment system, forcing an increasing number of regular discharges into the Fox River to get rid of the excess.

While sprawl increases water pollution, it also increases human demand for water. Desperately in need of water to serve exploding populations, urban areas are pumping water directly from nearby rivers - or from area aquifers that feed rivers - at alarming rates and with devastating impacts for fish and wildlife.

The suburbs of Atlanta are diverting more and more water from the Coosa and Tallapoosa headwaters which feed an Alabama river basin, one of the richest sources of freshwater aquatic animals in the world. Sierra Vista, Arizona is depleting groundwater so quickly that flows in the San Pedro River, one of the country’s most important migratory bird flyways, are already down by 30%. Salt Lake City’s wasteful water consumption is reducing flows in the Bear River and threatening one of the world’s most remarkable bird refuges.

“The rivers flowing across our country are precious beyond words. Few people realize that the United States supports the greatest diversity of freshwater animal species in the world. But at the same time, freshwater animals are the most threatened group of species in the country. The loss of these species is a warning to us. If sprawl continues to ravage rivers, we will lose not only wildlife but also our drinking water supplies and economic and recreational opportunities,” added Wodder.

Ironically, the same rivers that are now being ravaged by sprawl are in some regions serving as an effective antidote to sprawl, according to Wodder. Rather than being eyesores and dumpsites, urban rivers are actually helping to reverse sprawl by drawing people and businesses back to the core of the city.

“Healthy rivers are the lifeblood of our communities. Virtually every major city and thousands of towns across the country rest on the banks of a river. Today, more and more of us want to get out of our cars and back into nature, and our local rivers offer the most appealing and convenient destinations. A river renaissance is sweeping the country: urban and rural communities everywhere are rediscovering and revitalizing the waterways that flow through them. As rivers and riverside parks become cleaner and more attractive, people of all ages, cultures, and income levels can enjoy a heightened quality of life,” stated Wodder.

While sprawl puts rivers at risk all over the country, a host of other chronic threats continue to damage waterways, including mining waste which pollutes and buries waterways, dams which block rivers and kill fish, and riverbank manipulation which destroys plantlife and animal habitat.

One of the gravest problems this year is occurring on the lower Snake River in Washington State, named the most endangered river in America. Over a million salmon and steelhead once migrated up the Snake every year. But today, the river’s salmon runs are close to extinction because four dams on the lower river have destroyed its natural flow.

American Rivers’ 14th annual report examines and ranks rivers that this year face the most serious and immediate degradation and provides an overview of the state of rivers today. The purpose of the report is to call attention to these threats and mobilize communities around protecting and restoring locally and nationally significant waterways.

1999 MOST ENDANGERED RIVERS IN AMERICA

1. LOWER SNAKE RIVER, Washington State Threat: federal dams Dams on the lower Snake River have brought salmon and steelhead to the brink of extinction. Since the early 1970's, fish populations, once numbering over a million, have dropped by nearly 90%. By the end of this year, the Clinton Administration must produce a Snake River salmon recovery plan. Scientists agree that the cornerstone of the plan must include removal of four dams on the lower Snake to let the river flow naturally.

2. MISSOURI RIVER, Midwest Threat: channelization, dams, bank stabilization, poor grazing practices Once one of the world’s most dynamic rivers, much of the Missouri has been channelized into a barge canal, blocked by gigantic dams, and cut off from its floodplain by flood control levees. Now the river’s few remaining wild sections are in trouble from efforts to stabilize banks by lining them with rock, overgrazing fragile riversides, and dams that disrupt the river’s flow and kill fish.

3. ALABAMA-COOSA-TALLAPOOSA RIVER BASIN, Georgia, Alabama Threat: sprawl, water withdrawals, pollution Atlanta, the most sprawling city in the country, is growing so fast it threatens the health of nearby water sources, including the headwaters of the Coosa and Tallapoosa Rivers, which feed a major river basin in the state of Alabama -- home to one of the most biologically diverse areas in the world.

4. SAN PEDRO RIVER, Arizona and Sonora, Mexico Threat: sprawl, groundwater pumping With most of Arizona's riverside areas already damaged, the San Pedro is the last best example of a rich and healthy natural community in the southwestern US. The exploding community of Sierra Vista, AZ and the Army base of Fort Huachuca are pumping water out of the region’s aquifer - which maintains the river’s year-round flows - faster than it can be replenished.

5. YELOWSTONE RIVER, Montana, North Dakota Threat: bank stabilization, flood control One of the nation’s last freely meandering rivers, the Yellowstone is turning into an armored channel lined with rock. The Army Corps of Engineers is permitting more and more property owners to stabilize the river’s banks with rocks and build flood control levees. As the river becomes an armored channel, wildlife habitat is destroyed, and a blue-ribbon trout fishery is under assault.

6. CEDAR RIVER, Washington State Threat: sprawl, water withdrawals Seattle sprawl may destroy what is left of the Cedar River’s fragile salmon and steelhead runs. The Cedar River contains some of the highest quality fish habitat left in the Lake Washington watershed. Seattle’s proposed plan to take up to 70% of the river’s water to fuel suburban growth could have devastating impacts on the fish.

7. FOX RIVER, Illinois, Wisconsin Threat: sprawl, pollution, state agency inaction Sewage and suburban runoff from the Chicago area are polluting the Fox River and threatening drinking supplies for 200,000 people. The amount of treated sewage regularly released into the river from wastewater treatment plants -- already ill equipped to handle Chicago's growth -- is expected to increase by 50% by the year 2010.

8. CARMEL RIVER, California Threat: sprawl, water withdrawals, dams Monterey County, the fastest growing county in the state, is developing the watershed at an extremely rapid rate. As a result, more and more water is being pulled out of the river- much of it illegally - to serve exploding populations, harming wildlife and their habitat. The county is taking a knee jerk reaction to the water supply problem by proposing to build a new dam -- without considering other more cost effective and environmentally preferable alternatives such as water conservation.

9. COAL RIVER, West Virginia Threat: mountaintop removal coal mining The Coal River is threatened by the largest mountaintop mine ever proposed in West Virginia's long history of coal mining. The coal would be extracted by decapitating entire mountaintops and dumping millions of tons of leftover rock and soil into the valleys and rivers below.

10. BEAR RIVER, Utah Threat: sprawl, water withdrawals, proposed dam Wasteful water consumption in Salt Lake City is reducing flows in the Bear River and damaging one of the world’s most remarkable bird refuges. Instead of creating incentives to control wasteful water practices, the water district wants to build a dam that would cost taxpayers millions of dollars and may reduce water to the refuge even more.
