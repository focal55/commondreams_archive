Grassroots Groups Take Action for Neighborhood Improvements
 
CHICAGO - April 9 - Grassroots groups from across the country will take their efforts to improve communities to the capital in early April when for three days they share local strategies and successes with each other as well as demanding action from public officials.

Bank redlining, abandoned buildings, overcrowded schools, rampant crime and drugs and joblessness are a few of the ills the National People's Action (NPA) coalition, made up of about 1,000 leaders of 302 grassroots neighborhood groups from 38 states, will address from Saturday, April 10-Monday April 12, from their base at the 28th Annual Neighborhoods Conference at the Washington, D.C., Renaissance Hotel, 999 9th St., N.W.

"We will be in Washington to win policy changes that improve communities," says Gale Cincotta, NPA national chairperson. "Every time NPA goes to D.C., there's a showdown and the result is always domestic policy that is more responsive to the needs of our nation's neighborhoods."

Groups that participate in NPA work year-round for the betterment of their own neighborhoods: meeting local police chiefs, getting banks to reinvest by making home and small business loans available, seeking improvements to local schools, and developing local jobs programs, for example. Coming to the capital provides an opportunity to seek improvements that affect communities nationwide.

Highlights of the weekend include:

Plenary on Sunday, April 11 at 2 p.m. in the Grand Ballroom of the Washington Renaissance Hotel with guests Secretary of Education Richard Riley, Fannie Mae CEO Franklin Raines, and FHA commissioner Bill Apgar;

CRA/FHA briefing with Banking and Housing staff Monday, April 12 at 1:30 p.m. in Room 2222 Rayburn House Office Building;

meeting with U.S. Secretary of Education staff at the Department of Education at 1:30 p.m. Monday, April 12;

and Mass Civil Disobedience events starting Sunday, April 11 at 4 p.m. and Monday, April 12 at 9 a.m.

Direct action is an integral part of NPA's approach to winning policy victories. In past years, that has meant visiting the suburban home and downtown office of Housing and Urban Development Secretary Andrew Cuomo, the Department of Education, Alan Greenspan's home and others. Recent results of non-violent direct action targeting these officials will be announced at the group's plenary session, 2 p.m., Sunday, April 12.

In the recent past, NPA has worked with the Federal National Mortgage Association, Fannie Mae, to win a billion-dollar program to buy loans made by local banks through CRA agreements, won an agreement from the Department of Justice to allow local police to share civil forfeiture proceeds with community groups, developed a job creation partnership with Marriott International, met with Drug Czar Barry McCaffrey and with Environmental Protection Agency Administrator Carol Browner.

National People's Action is best-known as the group that created the Community Reinvestment Act in the 1970s. Since then it has been using direct-action tactics to win victories for neighborhoods nearly every year.
