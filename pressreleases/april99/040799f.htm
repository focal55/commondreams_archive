Grant Clemency to Native American Political Figure, United Methodist Advocacy Board Asserts
 
WASHINGTON - April 7 - The General Board of Church and Society appealed to US President Bill Clinton to immediately grant executive clemency to an internationally-recognized Native American political prisoner, serving more than two decades in federal prison.

At the March biannual meeting, the 63-member international board unanimously adopted a resolution calling for widespread support for clemency for Leonard Peltier, a Native American civil rights activist, who was sentenced for the murder of two FBI agents. The resolution emphasized that the parole hearings have concluded that he was "both falsely accused and falsely convicted."

GBCS General Secretary Thom White Wolf Fassett has personally spoken to Clinton and US Attorney General Janet Reno requesting clemency for Peltier. The general secretary was also urged by board members to contact South African President Nelson Mandela and ask him to join in the call for Peltier’s release.

A outspoken activist in American Indian Movement (AIM), a Native civil rights movement, Peltier was sentenced in 1977 to two consecutive life terms. "He is the only Native American political prisoner from the activist era of the 1970s that is still languishing in the prison system," said Fassett, who is Native American.

On June 26, 1975, two FBI drove to an AIM encampment known as Tent City on the property of the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota. They were searching for a Native American man, who was suspected to have stolen a pair of used cowboy boots. Witnesses testify that tensions were high and consequently a gunfight ensued and the two agents and one Native American were killed. Although Peltier was present during gunfight, he denies shooting the agents and/or ordering the shooting.

Leonard Peltier and other AIMD members were at the reservation to provide activism and support to Oglala people, whose land was under threat of government mining.

Human rights activists charge that Peltier was framed and convicted, despite reportedly no supporting evidence.

The 8th Circuit Court of Appeals final decision determined that FBI misconduct and judicial impropriety was involved in Peltier’s trial and previous appeals. "The US Government must share the responsibility with the Native Americans for the June 26, 1975 firefight," concluded Judge Gerald Haney, a member of the Appellate Court in two of Peltier’s appeals in 1984 and 1986.

"He was scapegoat used by a system seeking to "settle up" or even the score against vocal Native Americans," said Fassett. "Since he was a well-known Native American figure, he was specifically targeted as a suspect."

Peltier filed for executive clemency five years ago, but has not received a personal response other than a file form indicating that the petition was under review by the US Justice Department.

Peltier’s ailing health and lack of appropriate health care in the prison system, the board members cited, is another reason for immediate clemency. The activist is suffering from complications from a maxilla-facial surgery and is reportedly in pain.

The case has attracted international attention and he has been recognized by human rights organizations, such Amnesty International. In addition, he was recognized as a human rights defender at the 50th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in Paris.
