CRA Exemption for Small Banks Would Devastate Access to Credit in Rural America
 
WASHINGTON - April 19 - A new analysis conducted by the National Community Reinvestment Coalition (NCRC) reveals that Sen. Shelby's (R-Ala.) amendment to a financial modernization bill being considered by the Senate would exempt 72 percent of all rural, small banks from the Community Reinvestment Act (CRA).

More than 3,870 non-metropolitan banks under $100 million in assets would no longer be obliged to reinvest in their communities by making loans and investments to small business, farmers, working-class, and minority borrowers. "Small banks have not earned an exemption from CRA since they do not behave like the community-centered banks making lots and lots of loans that Sen. Shelby would like us to believe," asserts John Taylor, president and CEO of NCRC.

A NCRC tabulation of the CRA ratings from 1990 through 1998 shows that all small banks (rural and urban) fail CRA exams out of proportion to their numbers. While small banks comprise 60 percent of all banks, they have received 90 percent of the worst CRA ratings, substantial non-compliance since 1990 (26 out of 29 substantial non-compliance ratings according to the FFIEC web page). Likewise, small banks gathered 71 percent of the needs-to-improve ratings (211 out of 197 ratings) in that time period.

Small banks received Satisfactory ratings in proportion to their numbers, but did not earn Outstanding ratings in proportion to their numbers. Small banks earned 66 percent of all Satisfactory ratings and 55 percent of all Outstanding ratings from 1990 to 1998.

"If small, rural banks are exempt from CRA, small farmers will experience a deep drop in access to loans," exclaims NCRC Board member Shelly Sheehy of the Muscatine Center for Strategic Action in Iowa. "I bet if you conducted an experiment in which CRA was removed and put back in place a few years later, an even greater percentage of small banks would fail their CRA exams. It is unconscionable to remove CRA for small, rural banks -- they are 85 percent of the non-metro lenders in my state," Sheehy continues.

NCRC reviewed the impact of the exemption in Senator Shelby's home state ofAlabama, and found that 71 percent of all non-metropolitan banks would be exempt. NCRC also found that small rural banks have spotty community reinvestment records. In a sample of all FDIC exams (available over the Internet) of rural Alabama banks in 1998, NCRC found that banks with Satisfactory ratings only had loan-to-deposit ratios of 47 percent in contrast to banks with Outstanding ratings that had loan-to-deposit ratios of 71 percent. This means that the majority of banks in Alabama that have undergone CRA exams in the last few years loan less than half of what they receive in deposits.

One "Satisfactory" bank, Escambia County Bank, had an abysmal loan-to-deposit ratio of only 22 percent. In contrast, First State Bank of Dekalb had a loan-to-deposit ratio of 85 percent although it had $12 million less in assets.

CRA exams revealed the reasons behind the differences in performance. For example, First State met credit needs in several ways including participation in the development of affordable housing for elderly and disabled individuals, sponsorship of the "Banking Is" financial literacy program at local high schools, and the establishment of electronic credit accounts for small and medium-sized businesses. In contrast, Escambia County Bank, the lender with the dismal loan-to-deposit ratio, has not established partnerships with local public agencies or developers of affordable housing, does not participate in government-guaranteed loan programs, and "typically requires 20 percent equity in the property (home), which precludes some low-income families from borrowing," according to the CRA exam. This lender and others similarly situated in Alabama and across the country would be exempt from CRA under Senator Shelby's amendment.

NCRC Board Member Hubert Van Tol of the Wisconsin Rural Development Center states, "CRA exams provide important insights to community residents as to how well banks serve their community. While one bank is engaging in several innovative marketing and product strategies, another one down the street may still be requiring 20 percent downpayments despite the demonstrated success of low-downpayment programs. Free market economists and policy-makers should love CRA exams; they reveal to consumers, borrowers, and depositors which banks offer the best products and the best prices."

On average, the small bank CRA exams reviewed by NCRC were 8 pages long. This is hardly the stuff of regulatory burden and paperwork as maintained by the opponents of CRA.

"We have been doing our best to educate Sens. Shelby and Gramm," states Taylor. "We have given their respective staff everything they have asked for because we hope to demonstrate the tremendous benefits of CRA to them. The small bank exemption and the safe harbor provision in the financial modernization bill authored by Sen. Gramm would be the death knell for CRA."

The safe harbor provision in Sen. Gramm's bill would require federal banking regulators to consider comments on CRA aspects of a bank merger application only if the person commenting can prove that banks that have passed their CRA exams should no longer receive at least a Satisfactory rating. This would effectively end public comments in many cases since citizens would have to prove that multi-state banks fail their CRA exams in each and every state. Legitimate public concerns about local branch closing or changes to loan programs would be dismissed.

"Sen. Gramm feels that during the merger application process, banks pay groups off to say nice things about the banks. The Senator has not yet offered one example of this, while community organizations can point to the billions of dollars that have flowed to neighborhoods as a result of CRA. Let's not kill the most effective tool to revitalize neighborhoods over a phantom problem," concludes Taylor.

The National Community Reinvestment Coalition (NCRC) is the nation's CRA trade association of more than 700 community reinvestment organizations dedicated to revitalizing inner city neighborhoods and rural areas by promoting partnerships between lenders and community leaders.
