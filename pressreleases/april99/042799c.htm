National Project Offers Hope After Littleton
 
MADISON, WI - April 27 - In the wake of the tragedy in Littleton, Colorado, people across the nation are searching for answers and asking what can be done to reduce youth violence. For some, it means planning "Safe Night" parties for youth.

"These horrific episodes of youth homicide can and must call us to action as a country," said Deborah Prothrow-Stith, MD, a nationally recognized leader in the field of adolescent violence prevention and Professor at the Harvard School of Public Health.

Dr. Prothrow-Stith and others across the country are carrying a message of hope for those who are concerned about their own communities and wondering what they can do to help prevent violence.

"Violence is preventable, not inevitable," said Linda Lantieri of Resolving Conflict Creatively Program National Center, an initiative of Educators for Social Responsibility.

To help curb youth violence, Lantieri and Educators for Social Responsibility have gotten involved in one national program that can have an impact. It’s called Safe Night USA – a national effort to address the issue of youth safety one night at a time. Safe Night USA is teaching and encouraging people across the country to hold "Safe Nights" for youth. A Safe Night is a party, planned by youth and adults, with time to learn how to deal with anger and confrontation appropriately.

"We want people to know that there are things we can do in our communities to make a difference and schools have an important role to play," Lantieri added.

"The idea is for youth to come to a party and have a good time. During the party, they learn useful skills for resolving conflict peacefully," explained Olu Sijuwade, the program’s founder. At a Safe Night party, three rules are enforced: no weapons, no alcohol or drugs, and no arguments. "We chose those three rules because weapons, alcohol or other drugs and arguments are the three most common factors that lead to acts of violence," he added.

Sijuwade, who works for the Milwaukee Health Department, started Safe Nights in Milwaukee in 1994 as a tool to deal with the city’s rising youth homicide rate. Since that time, the program has been credited as a key factor in a 60% drop in Milwaukee’s youth homicide rate.

Now, the Safe Night concept is spreading across the country. On June 5, cities and towns in every state and Puerto Rico will hold Safe Night events, all connected through a live show on PBS and BET. Safe Night USA Project Director Maria Alvarez Stroud is enthusiastic about the overwhelming interest she has seen.

"From Alaska to Maine, communities of all sizes are joining the effort – some to prevent violence, others to intervene and stop a growing problem," she said. In Miami, for example, Safe Night planners expect 500 youth to attend a concert and beach party on June 5 with conflict resolution skill training in the middle. The same night, seven Safe Night parties will be held in Berkeley, California, with some focusing on violence prevention and others on conflict resolution. In Detroit, the goal is to have 150 Safe Night parties on June 5.

Organizers say the concept can work well in rural areas, too. In Kentucky, for example, the goal is to have Safe Night events in all 122 counties, many of which are predominantly rural. In Paducah, which was stunned by a school shooting in 1997, a Safe Night is being planned for June 5. "We are still grieving and our Safe Night event will help us in the healing process," said event organizer Nancy Snook.

"It’s an excellent way for neighborhoods and communities, as well as our entire country, to come together on the same night to show support for our youth and their safety, and offer them skills for managing their emotions and resolving conflict nonviolently," Alvarez Stroud added.

Lantieri concurs. Her organization, which specializes in teaching conflict resolution skills in schools, has joined a number of other national youth-oriented organizations in supporting the effort. In addition to Educators for Social Responsibility, partners include National 4-H Council and Boys & Girls Clubs of America, as well as The American Academy of Pediatrics and others.

Alvarez Stroud tempered her enthusiasm with a note of caution about seeing Safe Night as a complete solution to issues of youth violence. "Safe Night appears to be an effective violence prevention strategy, but it’s not a cure-all. Safe Night alone is not going to make all problems go away. It has to be in conjunction with other prevention strategies and a commitment to work together on the local level."

Dr. Prothrow-Stith agreed. "America clearly has a problem and not one of us is able to hide. It’s a problem that we can prevent," she said. "We must use the memory of all the murdered children and the pain and anger invoked by the current tragedy to invest in prevention. It is overdue."

Joanne Cantor, a communications professor at the University of Wisconsin and author of the recent book Mommy, I’m Scared, noted the power of the media. "While we struggle for answers, this horrible event focuses our attention on the awesome power of the media for both good and ill," she noted. "We question the influence of the media as a contributing factor in this tragedy, while at the same time, the media have been instrumental in uniting us as a nation in our grief."

On its own, Safe Night is a great idea, asserts Cantor. "What the live broadcast adds is the sense of national connectedness. What I especially like about the live show is that this is a case where the media can actually be used in a positive way, to help prevent violence."

There is still time to join the national effort by planning a Safe Night event in conjunction with the national celebration on June 5.
