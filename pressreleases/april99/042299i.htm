Pitt Hunger Strikers Plan To Meet With Chairperson
 
PITTSBURGH - April 22 - The Equal Rights Alliance (ERA) met today with Dr. Robert Gallagher, Vice Chancellor for Student Affairs. Our representatives were informed that if the hunger strike ended, J.W. Connolly, Chairperson of the University of Pittsburgh Board of Trustees would meet with alone with two hunger strikers on Friday, April 23 at 2:00 PM in room 159 of the Cathedral of Learning. The administration requested two specific student strikers by name. During the meeting with Gallagher, we requested to include additional hunger strikers, alternative hunger strikers or non-hunger strikers (e.g. a lawyer, media representative, faculty, staff, or community member). Dr. Gallagher was extremely doubtful that these simple requests would be allowed, but said he would ask Connolly. We also personally told Mr. Dunkelman, the Secretary of the Board of Trustees about our desire to meet with Connolly without restrictions. At the close of business on Thursday, we received no word on whether or not Connolly had considered our inquiries about a modified meeting, or a meeting without the condition of ending the hunger strike. We hope to resolve these issues Friday at 2 PM.

The hunger strikers, who begain striking on Monday, April 12th, were pleased to hear from Connolly and we will not pass up the opportunity to keep the lines of communication open. We will make every attempt to meet with Connolly on Friday, but we will not end the hunger strike in order to do so. Despite experiencing symptoms such as headaches, extreme lethargy, dizziness and irritability, the strikers wish to reiterate that they will not eat until the board agrees to an open forum. By not addressing our request for a public forum, the Board has demonstrated that they do not wish to be held publicly accountable for their decisions, which affect the entire University, the City of Pittsburgh, and local ordinances across Pennsylvania. Speaking alone and in private excludes other members of the Board, the media and the community. These actions are in opposition to the founding principle of this nation-democratic accountability. It is not unreasonable to expect that when the faculty, students, staff, community and City government have universally condemned Pitt's actions, the Board ought to at the very least explain its decision. So the question is not as Pitt spokesperson, Ken Service, put it "Should the Board of Trustees grant a meeting every time students decide to have a hunger strike?" but rather, what kind of system does the University of Pittsburgh have that forces students to go on hunger strike for 12 days in an attempt to talk with the people who have made decisions that go against the wishes of the faculty, staff, undergraduates, graduate students, community and City government?

According to Ken Service, all avenues for getting a meeting with the Trustees have been tried. Arranging the open forum is a simple matter. It can be called by J.W. Connolly (Chairperson of the Board), Chancellor Nordenberg, or at the written request of five members of the Board. Members of the Equal Rights Alliance were even visibly present at the February 18th meeting of the Board and the issue could have easily been added to the agenda by any one of the members; they chose to adjourn instead. Dr. Gallagher said he didn't think Chancellor Nordenberg "ever even entertained the possibility" of using his authority to set up the public forum.

J.W. Connolly already knows how we feel and we know how he feels about this issue-he is the only trustee who has publicly spoken about it (See the interview in the March 4, 1999 issue of the University Times, which available at http://www.pitt.edu/utimes/issues/990304/13.html.) When the University Times asked on March 4, 1999, "How do you feel when you hear people making accusations that you're homophobic, that the board is homophobic?" J.W. Connolly responded, "Are you asking me if I like girls? Is that what your question is?" We are hunger striking in order to gain an open, public forum with the board of trustees so that each individual member can be held accountable for their actions.

The University has repeatedly stated that domestic partner benefits need to be mandated at the state level and that Pittsburgh cannot equate domestic partners with spouses. We do not dispute the fact that Pennsylvania should act; however the University of Pittsburgh itself is directly discriminating against gays in two ways. First, Pitt recognizes and accepts domestic partners-they even offer them limited benefits. They specifically chose, however, not to extend medical benefits, despite the fact that married couples receive them. Second, Pitt chose the criterion of marriage, knowing full well that is was a standard gays couldn't legally meet. Pittsburgh's Human Relations Commission determined that because of this, Pitt discriminates against its gay employees. An analogous situation would be if the police decided to hire only people over six feet tall. While there is no law against height discrimination, such a requirement has a disparate impact on women and is therefore discriminatory.
