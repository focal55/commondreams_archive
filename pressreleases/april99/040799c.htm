Death Penalty Activists To gather For National Conference
 
WASHINGTON - April 7 - Working to take "faith based anti-death penalty activism to a new level," opponents of capital punishment will gather in San Antonio, Texas, for a series of meetings, strategy sessions and training workshops. Leading voices against the death penalty, including Sr. Helen Prejean, former New Mexico Governor Toney Anaya and Arun Gandhi, peace activist and grandson of Mahatma Gandhi, will address the gathering.

"Living our Faith: Organizing the Religious Community Against the Death Penalty" will be held April 8-11 at the San Antonio, Texas Airport Hilton.

"From individual congregations to state councils of churches, from national religious bodies to the Pope himself, it is clear that the religious community has made substantial strides in making the abolition of the death penalty a priority," said Pat Clark, national representative for AFSC's Criminal Justice program and an organizer of the event.

The conference will include a major focus on anti-death penalty efforts in Texas, the state that leads the nation in the number of executions each year. As a part of the religious witness against the death penalty, religious leaders are planning to send a delegation from the conference to hold a prayer service with death row inmates at the Huntsville Prison on Sunday, April 11.

Clark noted that the past two years has seen a record pace in executions in the U.S. and an all time high number of death sentences since the reinstatement of the death penalty in 1972.

"Yet these numbers do not tell the full story," she said. Pointing to increased activity by abolitionists in many parts of the country as well as the numbers of murder victims family members speaking passionately against the death penalty, Clark believes that there is a growing awareness of the inhumanity of executions.

"There is growing international outrage over America's peculiar penchant for state-sponsored killing of its own citizens," she said.

For example, Clark noted, His Holiness the Dalai Lama recently sent a message of support to the Religious Organizing Against the Death Penalty Project in which he called state-sponsored death "a form of revenge" and said that human beings are not violent by nature.

"Human beings become violent because of negative thoughts which arise as a result of their environment and circumstances," the Dalai Lama wrote in his statement. "We need to explain the importance of the practice of love and compassion for our own survival and to try to minimize those conditions which foster murderous tendencies, such as the proliferation of weapons in our societies. These are things even private individuals can work towards."

"My overriding belief is that it is always possible for criminals to improve and that by its very finality, the death penalty contradicts this. Therefore, I support those organizations and individuals who are trying to bring an end to the use of the death penalty," the Dalai Lama stated.
