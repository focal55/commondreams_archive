Pork Futures May Be Down In Iowa, But Not In Congress
 
WASHINGTON - April 22 - Faced with the necessity of paying for the war in Kosovo, some congressional leaders are talking publicly of going far beyond the Administration's request for $6 billion to appropriate another $6 - $12 billion for some of their pet Pentagon projects.

"There are so many needs in the military," said House Appropriations Chairman C.W. Bill Young, R-Fla. "This would be a great opportunity."

If the congressional porkers succeed, it will be the second time in fiscal 1999 that they have used cries of readiness shortfalls and pressing defense needs as a cover for their pork projects.

In October 1998, Congress passed the first emergency supplemental appropriations bill for fiscal 1999 as part of the Omnibus Appropriations bill. The White House only requested $1.1 billion to fix readiness problems, but Congressional leaders turned the bill into an $8.3 billion grab bag of pork and pet projects, such as base construction, ballistic missile defense, and anti-drug activities.

Following that increase, the Pentagon was handed a further huge hike in funds for fiscal 2000. Earlier this year, President Clinton announced that he was boosting the planned fiscal 2000 budget by $12.6 billion in budget authority (and $112 over six years). The Republican-passed fiscal 2000 Budget Resolution saw the President's raise and added still another $8.3 billion in budget authority.

Thus even before Republicans try add another $6 - $12 billion for the Pentagon beyond the $6 billion requested for Kosovo, the fiscal 2000 military budget authority has already been increased from the $271.8 billion fiscal 1999 enacted budget authority (before the first "emergency" supplemental) to $290 billion, a jump of $18.2 billion.

There is little doubt that a few states will particularly benefit from this largess: Mississippi, Florida and Pennsylvania, all represented by key leaders in position to bring home the bacon. The only difference from last year is that Georgia pork may suffer from the departure of Speaker Newt Gingrich.

The Administration Request
On April 19, the Administration formally sent a request for emergency Kosovo funding totaling $6.049 billion. The details of the request are as follows:
$5.458 billion - Defense Department funding
$3.014 billion - continued air operations
$287 million - costs of operations to date
$698 million - munitions replacement, such as cruise missiles
$850 million - contingency reserve funds for readiness
$274 million - costs related to bombing operations in Iraq
$335 million - refugee assistance
$.591 billion - Department of State funding
$386 million - refugee assistance ($721 million with DOD money)
$150 million - assistance to Albania, Bulgaria, Montenegro, etc.
$55 million - embassy security

Already some of the nation's major newspapers are weighing in against this grab for money.

A New York Times editorial of April 21, 1999 stated: "The war in Kosovo ought not to be the occasion for budget gamesmanship in Congress."

A Washington Post editorial of April 20, 1999 argued: "There should be no piggybacking on this operation -- no larding of other defense costs onto the Kosovo bill."
