Labor Board Could Make Food Lion Pay Union's Costs of Defending Against Dismissed Suit
 
WASHINGTON - April 8 - Taking action that could lead to recooping substantial legal bills incurred in defending against an "abuse of process" suit that was dismissed on March 23 by a South Carolina court, the United Food & Commercial Workers Union (UFCW) has filed an unfair labor practice charge against Food Lion with the National Labor Relations Board.

The charge, known as a "Bill Johnson's" charge after a Supreme Court decision, alleges that Food Lion "brought and continuously maintained over the course of several years a meritless state law 'abuse of process' action" against the Union "in retaliation for the UFCW's assistance to Food Lion employees" who were engaged in collective activities.

In the past, where the NLRB has found that a company retaliated against a union by filing a meritless lawsuit, it has usually ordered reimbursement of the union's costs of defending against the legal action.

Food Lion first filed the suit on Feb. 12, 1993, in the Court of Common Pleas in Greenville, S.C. The UFCW moved the case to the federal court in Charleston, S.C. hearing a suit brought by Food Lion employees with financial assistance from the union. The federal court sent the "abuse of process" claim back to the Court of Common Pleas in July 1998, which was dismissed two weeks ago by Judge Joseph J. Watson.

The employees' suit charged Food Lion unlawfully terminated employees to keep them from vesting in the firm's profit-sharing plan and improperly denied employees their health benefits. The company claimed that information developed out of voluntary interviews with Food Lion employees led to a U.S. Department of Labor investigation of "off-the-clock work" at Food Lion and $16 million in back pay and fines in 1993; the filing of private wage-and-hour suits involving more than 1,000 employees, and an ABC-TV "Prime Time Live" report in November 1992 exposing unsanitary food handling practices resulting from the same scheduling pressures that caused off-the-clock work.

Under federal labor law, employees have the legal right to take action for their mutual aid and protection, such as by forming a union or filing lawsuits against their employer alleging improper employment practices. The NLRB charge alleges Food Lion retaliated against the UFCW for providing assistance to employees in filing such lawsuits.
