Religious Leaders, Peace Groups Demand "Lift the Sanctions on Iraq"
 
NEW YORK - April 22 - Convinced that the U.N.-imposed blockade of Iraq is cruel and illegal, Roman Catholic, Protestant, Muslim leaders have joined forces with national peace and human rights organizations to organize a national day of actions protesting the sanctions.

On April 27, hundreds of community activists across the United States will challenge the sanctions by attempting to mail aspirins, bandages, lead pencils, school notebooks, and chlorine bleach to Iraq via the U.S. Postal Service (which is banned from mailing anything of value to Iraq). At 2:00 that afternoon, church leaders including Auxiliary Bishop P. Francis Murphy of the Archdiocese of Baltimore; Rev. Thom Whitewolf Fassett, Board of Church and Society, United Methodist Church; and Rev. John Dear, Fellowship of Reconciliation, will join Kathy Kelly of Voices in the Wilderness, Gordon Clark of Peace Action, and Dr. Hassan Ibrahim, All Dulles Area Muslim Society Committee at the National Press Club in Washington, DC, to add their voices to the growing chorus demanding an end to the blockade.

"The overwhelming suffering of the Iraqi people—particularly the children who are completely innocent—compels us to seek an end to the Iraqi sanctions," said Bishop Murphy.

Other critics of the sanctions include Rep. John Conyers (D-MI), who wrote a letter to President Clinton last year asking that economic sanctions against civilians be delinked from military sanctions. The letter was signed by a total of 43 members of Congress.

According to U.N. officials, the sanctions on Iraq are the most severe trading restrictions ever imposed on any nation and are responsible for the death of an estimated one million Iraqis. UNICEF has reported that as many as 5,000 children die each month as a result of the sanctions regime.

A spokesperson for Voices in the Wilderness, a group that has sent more than 20 delegations to Iraq in defiance of the sanctions, said, "More and more people in the United States understand that embargos are acts of war just as deadly as bombs."

Organizers expect postal officials to rebuff the attempted mailings but hope that their actions will highlight the absurdity of the U.S. policy. In many cities, organizers will deliver the rejected packages to local congressional offices, asking that elected representatives help them get the materials to Iraq. In other cases, they will be delivered to groups like Voices in the Wilderness which ship material aid to Iraq in violation of the ban.

The day of Post Office actions and press conference falls in the midst of a week of coordinated student actions on more than 100 campuses nationwide. In addition, the DC-based Education for Peace in Iraq Center is sponsoring a series of Congressional lobby days during that week.

The national day of actions has been organized by the American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee, American Friends Service Committee, Fellowship of Reconciliation, Pax Christi USA, Peace Action, Voices in the Wilderness, and War Resisters League. Together, these groups represent 100,000 members nationwide.
