Greenpeace Welcomes Delay In New Amoco Oil Project; Launches New Web Site For Shareholders
 
WASHINGTON - April 7 - Greenpeace today welcomed news that BP Amoco would be forced to postpone construction on its Northstar offshore oil development, due to delays in obtaining federal permits. Greenpeace is actively campaigning against Northstar, and has mounted several legal challenges in an effort to stop the project, which if completed could be the first producing offshore oil development in the Arctic Ocean.

The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers is continuing to deal with concerns from the U.S. Department of Interior and other agencies about the Northstar project. Until these concerns are addressed, the Record of Decision on the Final Environmental Impacts Statement for Northstar cannot be issued. Only after the Record of Decision is rendered can permits be granted. Given that the winter construction season in the Arctic is nearly over, BP Amoco has said that it will not have sufficient time to begin construction on Northstar this year.

"Finally, the federal agencies are stopping to look at all of the problems with Northstar - from global warming to the projected one chance in four of a major oil spill. New oil development in the Arctic is just a bad idea," said Dan Ritzman of Greenpeace Alaska.

The BP Amoco setback comes on the heels of an announcement by CEO John Browne, that the company will merge with the Los Angeles based Atlantic Richfield Company (ARCO). "Recent announcements have cloaked BP Amoco with an air of invincibility," said Dan Ritzman of Greenpeace Alaska. "This postponement shows that BP Amoco cannot pre-empt a federal permitting process that examines the major environmental risks involved in Northstar."

Opposition to Northstar and BP Amoco's continued push for new oil exploration is developing around the world. Greenpeace has recently established Shareholders Against New Exploration (SANE BP), to challenge BP Amoco to stop new oil development and act on its rhetoric to stop global warming caused by burning fossil fuels. CEO Browne said, in a 1997 speech at Stanford University: "If we are all to take responsibility for the future of our planet, then it falls to us to begin to take precautionary action now."

Today the newly-formed group launched its website, www.sanebp.com, as a forum to discuss concerns and share information about BP Amoco's business strategy. "Despite saying the right things about global warming, BP Amoco continues to do the wrong things," said Iain MacGill of SANE BP. "With this kind of information, we believe more and more shareholders will make their objections heard."

BP Amoco's Annual General Shareholder's Meeting will be held on April 15, 1999 in London.

BP Amoco's proposed merger with ARCO will cost the company around $26 billion. BP spent $198 million alone in restructuring costs on its acquisition of Amoco. "If BP Amoco was serious about protecting the earth from dangerous global warming, the company would be investing seriously in renewable energy technologies such as solar and wind power instead of new oil developments," said MacGill.
