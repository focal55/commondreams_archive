Texas Should NOT Deregulate the Electric Utility Industry While Texans are Being Asked to Bailout Utilities’ Stranded Costs, Utility Holding Companies are Spending Billions on Foreign Acquisitions
 
AUSTIN, TX - April 15 - A new study released today by Public Citizen shows that the ratepayers of five Texas electric utilities are paying too much for their electricity. Making matters worse, at the behest of the incumbent electric utilities, ratepayers will be mandated by the legislature to bail out the bad investments or so-called, stranded costs, of the electric utilities.

Action must be taken now, as the laws governing the electric utility industry are rewritten, to provide protections for consumers. Unless these protections are included in the deregulation bills currently being debated in the legislature, the holding companies will use their vast empires to pick the pockets of consumers for other utility services, such as gas, telecommunications and Internet access. They will also use their network of affiliates and their ability to cross-subsidize these subsidiaries to chill competition. Unless carefully restructured, with prohibitions against affiliate abuses, we will be left with unregulated monopolies for all of our important utility services.

"Utility holding companies are notorious for ripping off ratepayers, hence the nickname "combines." Just like farmers using their combines to harvest West Texas wheat, utility holding companies are corporate combines harvesting money from ratepayers," said Tom Smith, State Director of Public Citizen’s Texas office.

The report, Money Harvest: The Utility Holding Companies of Texas Are Ripping Off Ratepayers, demonstrates how holding companies are reaping extra profits for electricity services and using the excess profits for purchasing foreign companies. Three utility holding companies in Texas have been using ratepayer money to acquire additional companies located throughout the world. Central and South West has recently acquired companies in Brazil, Mexico, and the United Kingdom. Reliant Energy has recently acquired companies in Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, El Salvador, India, and Mexico, and has acquired a portion of a company headquartered in the Netherlands. Texas Utilities has recently acquired companies in Australia, China, Chile, Finland, Mexico, Norway, Sweden, and the United Kingdom.

Smith charged, "When Texas Utilities Co. agreed to pay $10.4 billion for Britain’s Energy Group PLC, it was one of the largest foreign acquisitions ever. These are the same guys coming hat-in-hand to the Legislature saying they need a ratepayer-funded bail-out to remain competitive."

The Texas Legislature may force ratepayers to hand over an estimated $4.6 billion to the subsidiaries of the three utility holding companies in the form of a stranded cost bailout for bad investments in power plants. Central Power & Light could receive a stranded cost bailout of $1.6 billion, or $2,550 per customer. Houston Lighting & Power could receive $1.5 billion, or $960 per customer. Texas Utilities Electric Company could receive $1.5 billion, or $620 per customer. Some of this money will then flow to the three utility holding companies, which can use it to further expand their empires.

"Ratepayer robbery was a common sport among the utility holding companies of the Roaring Twenties. This report shows that the utility holding companies of Texas are back to their old tricks," said Wenonah Hauter, Director of Public Citizen’s Critical Mass Energy Project in Washington, DC. "In addition, Texas ratepayers are being asked to pay billions of dollars to bail out utility investment in plants that produce power that is too expensive to sell," added Hauter.

The report analyzes the electric bills paid by the ratepayers of these five electric utilities since 1989. The report shows that, while ratepayers have had to pay high electricity bills, $9.6 billion of ratepayer money has been given to the three utility holding companies that own these utilities. Central and South West Corporation, which owns Central Power & Light Co. and West Texas Utilities Co., has received $1.7 billion in profits. Reliant Energy, Inc., which owns Houston Lighting & Power Co., has received $2.7 billion in profits. Texas Utilities Company, which owns Southwestern Electric Service Co. and Texas Utilities Electric Co., has received $5.2 billion in profits.

According to the 68-page report, ratepayers of Central Power & Light company are paying an extra $245 per year for electricity. Ratepayers of West Texas Utilities Company are paying an extra $162 per year. Ratepayers of Houston Lighting & Power Company are paying an extra $218 per year. Ratepayers of Southwestern Electric Service Company are paying an extra $72 per year, and ratepayers of Texas Utilities Electric Company are paying an extra $239 per year. These profits should have been used to pay down their bad investments in power plants.

"By keeping electricity bills high, the utility holding companies have been receiving ratepayer money to fuel the expansion of their empires," said Charlie Higley, a Senior Energy Analyst in Public Citizen’s Washington office and author of the report. "Because ratepayers have to buy electricity, we’re being forced to pay the high rates while the utility holding companies rake in higher profits."

The report concludes that Texas ratepayers would pay lower electricity bills if their electric utilities were not owned by holding companies. The report recommends that Texas lawmakers should prohibit holding companies from owning electric utilities. It also recommends that Texas lawmakers should not force ratepayers to bail out the stranded costs of electric utilities. The report recommends increased authority for the Public Utility Commission in Texas for prohibiting affiliate abuses.
