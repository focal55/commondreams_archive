Public Health And Environmental Groups Issue 'Clean Air' Report Cards To Eight Southern Governors; Governor’s Summit Just Lip Service Unless Followed by Decisive Action
 
ASHEVILLE, NC - April 7 - Public health and environmental advocates issued a report card today evaluating the performance of eight Southern governors on air quality issues. The grades given the governors are based on their respective positions on four clean air initiatives that will directly impact mountain air quality. Combined, the Governors received a failing or incomplete score 80% of the time.

The report cards are being issued as the Southern Governors meet in Asheville at the governors’ Summit on Mountain Air Quality, convened by North Carolina’s Governor Hunt. The stated purpose of the summit is to “[a]ssemble Southern Appalachian States to achieve a common understanding, at the highest levels of state government, of the nature of mountain air quality problems and to establish a basis for cooperative efforts to address those problems.”

While the public health and environmental advocates voiced support for the summit, they expressed skepticism about its likely outcome. “We are pleased to see the Governors come together to demonstrate a concern for this problem, but the best way to measure progress is past performance,” said Elizabeth Ouzts of the North Carolina field office of U.S. Public Interest Research Group (U.S. PIRG). “As these report cards indicate, the Governors have been more talk than action when it comes to clearing the air in the Southern Appalachians.”

The organizations scored the Governors based on support for four major clean air initiatives including: (1) Support for closing a loophole in the Clean Air Act that exempts older coal-burning power plants from meeting modern pollution standards; (2) Support for EPA’s “SIP Call” regulations that will dramatically reduce nitrogen oxide pollution from power plants in 22 eastern states; (3) Support for proposed federal visibility regulations to address the problem of pollution-generated haze; and (4) Willingness to sign a Memorandum of Understanding (“MOU”) between states and the Department of Interior to involve federal land managers in the permitting process for proposed projects that have the potential to diminish air quality in the national parks. Each Governor received a “pass,” “fail,” or “incomplete,” for each of the four initiatives.

Of the 32 scores given to the eight Governors, 26, or 80% were failing or incomplete. The Governors of West Virginia, Alabama, South Carolina and Virginia received all failing or incomplete scores. The Governors of Tennessee, Georgia and Kentucky have supported and complied with the EPA’s regional smog rule, while the Governors of North Carolina and Tennessee have signed the Memorandum of Understanding giving federal land managers a voice in air permitting proceedings. Finally, the Governor of North Carolina has pledged his support for the EPA’s proposed regional haze regulation.

“Air quality in the Southern Appalachians is getting worse,” said Jonathan Birdsong of the Izaak Walton League of America. “It is time to stop talking about clearing the air and really start doing it.”

The Southern Appalachian Mountains are facing a serious threat from high levels of air pollution. Ground-level ozone or “smog” is threatening the health of people and the ecosystems in the Appalachians, which attract millions of visitors to the South each year. Last summer, monitors in the Great Smoky Mountains National Park set a new record, registering violations of federal health standards for ozone smog on 44 days. In addition, the Southern Appalachians are home to the worst visibility conditions in the nation, due to pollution-generated haze, and the rate of acid deposition in these mountains is among the highest in the country. Studies conducted by the U.S. Forest Service show that visibility in the Southern Appalachians declined by 60% on average since 1948, with an 80% decline in summertime visibility.

For the past seven years, the southern states have met through a voluntary process known as the Southern Appalachian Mountains Initiative (SAMI). Although SAMI was established to identify and recommend reasonable measures to remedy existing and to prevent future adverse effects from air pollution on the Southern Appalachians, the process has not yet produced any tangible results.

“This summit shows that the governors recognize the seriousness of the issue,” according to Danielle Droitsch of the National Parks and Conservation Association. “But if they go home at the end of the day without progress toward the needed actions to remedy this problem, we will be no better off. Clean air will require actual commitments to take concrete steps that will reduce pollution.”

U.S. PIRG is the national lobby office for the State PIRGs, a network of state-based public interest watchdog groups.
