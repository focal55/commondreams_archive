Environmental Groups Oppose Polluter-Protection Y2K Bills
 
WASHINGTON - April 20 - Pending Year 2000 (Y2K) bills sponsored by Senators Hatch, Feinstein and McCain and Rep. Tom Davis could actually increase the risk of environmental hazards due to Y2K failures at chemical plants, nuclear utilities and other facilities, argued national environmental groups in a letter delivered to Congress today.

"Congress shouldn't give polluters a Y2K 'get-out-of-jail-free' card," said Allison LaPlante, U.S. PIRG Environmental Advocate. "Instead, industry should be encouraged to fix its Y2K problems. While we join the Congress in encouraging companies whose computer failures could harm the environment to act now to make their systems Y2K compliant, we oppose polluter protection bills that would have the opposite effect."

"Passage of these anti-environmental bills granting special legal protections and lawsuit immunity will increase the risk of environmental accidents from Y2K failures, not reduce it," she added. The proposals would remove important incentives for companies to fix potentially devastating Y2K computer processing problems, and would shield companies from full accountability for environmental harm caused by their Y2K failures.

Y2K processing problems in mainframe computers and embedded chip systems have the potential to harm the environment and affect public health. While the full extent of environmental problems that may result from Y2K failures is not known, EPA has said that "[d]evastating effects could occur through such problems as accidental contamination of drinking water, the release of harmful pollutants into the air, and the inappropriate distribution of chemicals and toxins into the community." A recent report from the U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board stressed special concern that the Y2K readiness efforts of small to medium-sized chemical facilities are "less than appropriate."

"These bills perversely reward firms that have done the least to solve their Y2K problems," said LaPlante. "We are calling on Congress to stop this outrageous special interest legislation for which the environmental and public health stakes are so high," said LaPlante.

The proposed Y2K legislation may be voted on as soon as later this week. Among the groups opposing the legislation were U.S. PIRG, Natural Resources Defense Council, the National Environmental Trust, Union of Concerned Scientists, Friends of the Earth, Defenders of Wildlife, Alliance for Justice and Americans for the Environment.
