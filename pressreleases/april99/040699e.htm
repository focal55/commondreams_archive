More Than 400 Groups And 30,000 Citizens Call For Expanded Right-To-Know About Most Dangerous Pollution
 
WASHINGTON - April 6 - More than 400 national and local groups and 30,000 individuals called on the U.S Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to strengthen a proposal to expand the public’s Right to Know about mercury, dioxin and other “persistent bioaccumulative toxins.” The EPA will close a three-month public comment period tomorrow on a proposal which sets stricter public reporting requirements, but does not require full disclosure of environmental releases of these substances.

“Nearly one year ago, Vice President Gore and EPA Administrator Browner promised to provide communities with vital information about dangerous toxic chemical pollution, and today citizens from across the country are calling on them to keep their promise,” said Allison LaPlante, U.S. PIRG Environmental Advocate. “EPA’s proposal is a step in the right direction, but significant amounts of dangerous pollution will still go unreported to the public and policy makers.”

Releases of substances like mercury, dioxin and leadtoxins linked to cancer, birth defects, reproductive disorders and other serious health problemsare often not reported to the EPA or the public. Persistent bioaccumulative toxins are highly toxic in small quantities, remain in ecosystems for long periods of time and build up in human and animal tissue. Due to a loophole in the federal Right to Know reporting law, releases of these chemicals escape reporting because reporting thresholds are set too high. Under the Community Right to Know Act’s Toxics Release Inventory (TRI), facilities currently are required to report chemical releases only if they “manufacture or process” at least 25,000 pounds, or “otherwise use” 10,000 pounds of a given TRI chemical. Under EPA’s proposal, reporting thresholds for certain chemicals would be lowered to 100 pounds or 10 pounds, depending on the chemical.

“One of the biggest weaknesses in EPA’s proposal is its failure to provide us with vital information about emissions of lead, a substance linked to physical and mental development problems in children,” said LaPlante. “1.7 million children in the U.S. already suffer from lead poisoning, and there is no safe level of exposure. We are calling on EPA to take action on this issue immediately.”

The EPA’s current proposal will require stricter reporting on the environmental releases of less than 20 chemicals considered to be persistent and bioaccumulative toxins. The proposal does not include stricter reporting for substances like lead, arsenic and cadmium.

“Although this expansion will provide the public with needed information, even a reporting threshold of ten pounds for a substance like mercury would leave the public in the dark about harmful levels of emissions,” said LaPlante. “A fraction of a teaspoon of mercury can contaminate the fish in a 25 acre lake. Industries should be required to tell us about any mercury released into the environment.”

“Public information is one of the most powerful tools for protecting ourselves from exposure to these dangerous toxic chemicals,” said LaPlante. “We applaud the Clinton Administration and EPA for going forward with this critical expansion, but, along with thousands of citizens across the country, we urge them to strengthen their proposal. We have a long way to go to ensure complete access to information about these particularly dangerous chemicals in our communities.”

U.S. PIRG delivered 30,480 individual comments to the U.S. EPA’s public docket today calling for a strengthened Right to Know proposal.
