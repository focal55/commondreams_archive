Greenpeace Activists Today Symbolically Returned Highly Carcinogenic Asbestos To Its Rightful Owner: Shipping Company P& O Nedlloyd In Hongkong
 
HONG KONG - April 12 - Greenpeace activists today symbolically returned highly carcinogenic asbestos, contained in a sealed drum to its rightful owner, shipping company P& O Nedlloyd, in Hongkong and urged the company to stop dumping toxic waste ships in China.

The action coincides with negotiations of a technical working group on the Basel Convention in Geneva, Switzerland, seeking to address issues of waste dumping from rich industrialised countries into less industrialised countries. The asbestos sample was collected by Greenpeace in January from a scrapyard in Lianhuashan, Panyu City, Guangdong Province, where a P&O Nedlloyd vessel, Botany Bay, was being dismantled.

P&O Nedlloyd, an Anglo-Dutch container-shipping company, operates 112 owned and chartered vessels. The company sold ten vessels for scrapping in Asia last year. Two such decommissioned vessels - Botany Bay and Encounter Bay - were tracked down by Greenpeace in southern China earlier this year.

The P&O Nedlloyd vessels, sent to China for scrapping without first being decontaminated, contained high levels of hazardous substances, including heavy metals and asbestos. These toxic materials will pollute the environment and endanger the health of workers during the scrapping process as witnessed by Greenpeace investigation teams.

"We are against the export of hazardous waste to Asia and oppose this form of waste dumping. Vessels such as the Encounter Bay are hazardous waste in the form of a ship as they structurally contain highly toxic and hazardous substances including asbestos and heavy metals," said Dr Howard Liu, campaigner of Greenpeace China.

Greenpeace is not against shipbreaking but demands that all ships-for-scrap should be properly decontaminated before being sent to non-OECD countries for breaking and urges the shipbuilding industry to build ships that are toxics-free. "When a ship reaches the end of its life span, it has to be scrapped. Many industrialised countries are sending their waste vessels to scrapyards in Asia because labour costs are low and environmental and safety standards are lax. The world should condemn this immoral practice of profiting at the expense of the health of workers and the environment," Liu stressed.

According to recent press reports, United Kingdom medical authorities have warned that up to 500,000 people in Europe could die from asbestos-related cancers in the next three decades.
