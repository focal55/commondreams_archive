Public Campaign Ad Targets Members Of Congress For Being 'Leashed' To Their Special Interest Contributors; The Ad The New York Times Refused to Print
 
WASHINGTON - April 21 - In an effort to keep pressure on the problems of the campaign finance system, Public Campaign launched a bold paid ad in today’s Washington Post which highlights Members of Congress who look out for the economic interests of their major-industry campaign donors.

The ad is based on six reports researched and written by Public Campaign last year as part of their “Golden Leash Awards” program. The ad heralds this year’s awards, the first of which will be given next week, on April 29. The ad specifies how much money individual lawmakers have taken from vested interests and how those Members have carried the water for these interests through legislative activity, while citizens suffered as a result. In the ad, winners of last year’s Golden Leash are morphed with the bodies of dogs and accompanied by common commands such as “Fetch,” “Beg” and “Heel,” illustrating that Members of Congress are at the beck and call of their campaign contributors.

“We are demonstrating, in graphic illustration, the fundamental problem with today’s electoral system, where wealthy special interests - through their campaign contributions - have greater influence over politicians than the public,” said Ellen Miller, executive director of Public Campaign.

Launched in January 1998, the Golden Leash is awarded every six weeks to Members of Congress based on a strict set of criteria. The ad will also appear in the April 26 edition of Roll Call, and is available for view on Public Campaign’s Website at www.publicampaign.org/leashad.html. The complete Golden Leash Award section can also be found online at www.publicampaign.org/goldenleash.html.

The New York Times declined to print the ad, apparently because the newspaper found it offensive.

“I’m offended by elected officials who take money from special interests, and then legislate on their behalf,” Miller said. “We need to confront lawmakers head-on about their quid pro quo behavior.”

Public Campaign is a non-profit, non-partisan organization promoting comprehensive campaign finance reform.
