Fact Sheet of Seven Deadly Loopholes that Allow Juveniles EasyvAccess to Guns
 
WASHINGTON - April 26 - The following was released today by Handgun Control Inc.:

Six days after the Littleton shooting, Americans are asking how juniors in high school could amass the firepower to commit the massacre.

Attached is a factsheet from Handgun Control listing the "seven deadly loopholes" that enable juveniles to have easy accss to firearms. Federal legislation addressing these loopholes is expected to be introduced -- or reintroduced -- very shortly.

The Seven Deadly Loopholes that Give Children Easy Access to Firearms
In the school shootings of the 1997-98 school year, and in the incidents that occur every week in which a child brings a gun to school, the child usually gets the gun from home.

On April 24, federal investigators announced that the firearms used in the Columbine High School massacre were most likely obtained legally by the shooters in private, unregulated sales. Determining who sold them the guns and whether or not those sales were indeed legal may be extremely difficult because it is unlikely that a "paper trail" exists for investigators to follow.

Whether a gun is brought to school from home or purchased legally from a private seller, these cases illustrate the gaping loopholes in our current firearms laws which enable minors -- and criminals -- to get firearms. These loopholes exist, in large part, thanks to the gun lobby which continues to resist any attempt to close them.

1. Parents are not required to keep loaded firearms out of the reach of children. Only 16 states presently have Child Access Prevention (CAP) laws which hold gun owners criminally responsible if a child gains access to an unsecured firearm and uses it to injure themselves or someone else. CAP laws not only protect children against the criminal use of a gun by a child but also protect against unintentional shootings by children.

2. So-called "private collectors" can sell guns without background checks at gun shows and flea markets. The Brady law requires that federally licensed gun dealers initiate and complete a background check before they sell a firearm, whether the sale takes place at a gun store or at a gun show. No such requirement extends to individuals who call themselves "private collectors" at gun shows and other public venues. In most states, these individuals are legally able to sell one, five, 10 or 20 guns at gun shows without conducting a background check or following any of the state's firearms sales laws. Recent studies have shown that gun shows have become a significant source of guns for illegal users, including children.

3. Private gun owners do not have to keep records with respect to whom they sell their guns. Federal law requires that gun stores keep record on guns that are sold so that police can trace firearms recovered in crime. Private citizens, however, are not required to inform the police or keep any records when they resell a gun. As a result, law enforcement's ability to trace how a gun got into a youth's -- or a criminal's -- hands is severely hampered. Furthermore, this loophole facilitates the flow of guns into the illegal market via "straw purchasers." Straw purchasers are individuals who are legally able to buy guns and who buy guns for resale to illegal buyers. Straw purchasers are able to guy guns in large quantities from gun stores and then resell them on the street to illegal buyers with little fear of being caught. Only about a dozen states have laws requiring that private sales of firearms be recorded and only three states limit the number of handguns that can be purchased at any one time.

4. Gun manufacturers and stores are not required to lock up their guns when the factory or store is closed. There are no security standards for gun stores. Some guns stores lock up their guns in a vault when the store is closed, but the vast majority do not. As a result, thousands of guns are stolen from stores every year. In 1998, California passed tough new security standards for gun manufacturers and distributors aimed at blocking thefts, but no such standards exist in other states.

5. Gun manufacturers are not required to make guns that are safer and less accessible to children an other unauthorized users. Manufacturers have the ability, using existing technology, to make guns that cannot be used by unauthorized users. Simple internal locking devices, including combination locks, have been available for years, but manufacturers have not taken advantage of even these simple, inexpensive devices. They have not even taken steps to make their products safer by including simple features on all guns, such as load indicators which tell the user that the gun is still loaded. guns are not regulated for safety by the Consumer Product Safety Commission of any other federal agency, making it the only unregulated consumer product in America today. As a result, many guns manufactured today lack even the most basic kind of safety devices.

6. In most states, juveniles of any age can purchase of be in possession of assault weapons. Although juveniles 18 and younger are prohibited by Federal law from purchasing handguns, neither the Federal government nor most states restrict the purchase and ownership of long guns. Although this loophole allows teenagers to hunt with rifles and shotguns, it also allows them to possess semi-automatic Uzis, AK-47s, AR-15s and other assault weapons manufactured before 1994 and "grandfathered" under that year's assault weapon ban. One gun used in the Colorado massacre was apparently a TEC-9, an infamous assault pistol that was also used in the 1993 massacre at a San Francisco law firm in which eight people died and six were wounded.

7. Young adults aged 18-21 can legally purchase handguns. Under federal law, young adults in this age bracket cannot purchase handguns from federally licensed dealers. However, a dangerous loophole allows them to purchase handguns and even assault pistols in a "private" transaction from any private individual, including the so-called "private collectors" who sell many firearms at gun shows. Young men in this age bracket commit a high percentage of the nation's violent crime but are still legally permitted to buy the weapons most typically used in those crimes.

Handgun Control, chaired by Sarah Brady, is the nation's largest citizens' gun control lobbying organization. Based in Washington, D.C., HCI works to enact stronger federal, state and local gun control laws, but does not seek to ban handguns. Founded in 1974, HCI has more than 400,000 members nationwide and works with local groups around the country to enact and protect reasonable gun control laws. More information about HCI and its affiliated organization, the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence, can be found on their Web site at www.handguncontrol.org.
