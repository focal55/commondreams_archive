New EPA Standards Soft on Light Trucks and Dirty Diesels; Automakers Propose Escape Hatch for New Pollution Standards
 
CAMBRIDGE, MA - April 30 - The proposed "Tier 2" emission standards expected to be released later today or tomorrow by the Environmental Protection Agency will decrease the allowable amount of smog-forming pollutants in automobile exhaust and sulfur in gasoline, but extend pollution breaks for light trucks and diesel engines. Meanwhile, the auto industry announced a proposal this week that would weaken the new tailpipe standards.

"The 100 million Americans living in areas with chronic smog should not have to wait 10 years for cleaner air," said Michelle Robinson, Senior Advocate for UCS's Transportation Program. "And if the auto industry gets its way the public may never get the auto pollution relief they deserve."

The counterproposal unveiled this week by the Alliance for Automobile Manufacturers would stall tighter pollution standards for several more years, provide the escape hatch of a so-called independent body review, and put the fate of the program in the hands of an uncooperative, hostile oil industry. UCS estimates that the average new vehicle would emit 40 percent more of the smog-forming pollutant nitrogen oxide over the next decade if the Alliance's proposal is adopted.

Though UCS applauds EPA's proposal as a step in the right direction, it is concerned that the proposal gives light trucks such as SUVs, mini-vans, and pick-ups 10 years to lower pollution to levels they can meet today. Although the vast majority of light trucks sold are being used as passenger cars, they continue to be allowed to exceed passenger car emission standards. Using existing and affordable technology, California pollution control engineers modified a Ford Expedition and showed that it could meet proposed Tier 2 standards even under "work truck" conditions.

"If light truck loopholes had never existed, it would be like taking 40 million cars off the road today," said Roland Hwang, Transportation Program Director. "It's time emission standards caught up with engineering capabilities."

The proposal's retention of special pollution breaks for diesel is counterproductive. While diesel decreases global warming pollution because of its fuel economy, its fine particulates exacerbate serious respiratory illness. And growing evidence links diesel exhaust to cancer. Other technologies, including fuel cell, electric and improved gasoline vehicles, can reduce global warming pollution without sacrificing air quality.

"Americans can have global warming reductions and air quality gains," said Hwang. "Tolerating diesel's pollution because of its fuel economy potential is unwise and unnecessary."
