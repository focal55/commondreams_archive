NPA Actions Win Victories; More Planned for Monday
 
WASHINGTON - April 12 - The following was released today by National People's Action:

A meeting with the Secretary of Labor and the satisfaction of sending a message opposing deregulation of the financial services industry to U.S. Sen. Phil Gramm (R-Tex.) were the outcomes of Sunday afternoon demonstrations by the National People's Action coalition (NPA).

More than 1,000 leaders of 302 grassroots neighborhood groups based in 38 states that participate in NPA, in town for their 28th annual conference at the Renaissance Hotel, made their concerns heard at the homes of Gramm, 4201 Yuma St., N.W., and Secretary of Labor Alexis Herman, 700 7th St., S.W. Both were home. No one ever came to the door at the Gramm house, but Herman sat down to meet with five of the group's leaders and agreed to further negotiations on their job and welfare to work issues.

"Philly the Kid" Gramm was wanted by NPA for trying to kill the Community Reinvestment Act, the federal anti-redlining law NPA groups got Congress to pass in 1977. Gramm has targeted the CRA as part of his campaign to get "financial modernization" legislation passed in the Senate. The CRA is a law that has yielded $1 trillion in home mortgage, small business and small farm loans. Since CRA passed banks have been required to consider the needs of the communities where they operate.

Gramm's modernization proposal would both kill the CRA and ease other restrictions on members of the financial services industry, allowing them to increase profits -- while leaving consumer protections stuck in the 1970s.

The NPA position is that consumer protections need to be updated as well. The group advocates expanding the Community Reinvestment Act. The group dubbed the Secretary of Labor "Herman the Hermit" for her refusal to meet with community groups. Bureaucratic tie-ups at the Department of Labor have increased the difficulties for many who tried to get jobs or job training rather than smoothing the way. Some examples of problems leaders laid out in a brief discussion with her at her apartment that lead to further meeting included the stories of Michael Bess and Cesar Maldonado.

Bess, who is paralyzed on one side, through a maze of Department of Labor restrictions, regulations, and requirements was sent to typing training. Maldonado, a youth from Chicago, applied for a summer job only to learn that under current regulations federally-funded summer job programs are only available to young people who have one of six "barriers." In other words, the jobs program is available to dropouts, young felons, and others -- but not to young people with a clean record.

Also at the NPA conference Sunday:

-- Franklin Raines, the head of the Federal National Mortgage Association, Fannie Mae, announced that a partnership between community groups that participate in NPA and Fannie Mae had resulted in $1 billion more being available for affordable housing and other loans in underserved communities.

-- Federal Housing Administration Commissioner Bill Apgar committed to debarring lenders with a high rate of foreclosure on loans they make through the FHA's 100 percent guaranteed loan program.

-- Secretary of Education Richard Riley told the group he would work to get school construction financing legislation passed through Congress to build new schools to relieve overcrowding in the neighborhoods where NPA groups are based.

On Monday, the group plans to continue its winning strategy of direct action by departing the Renaissance Hotel first thing in the morning and visiting others who have declined politer invitations to meet.

They also plan to attend a 1:30 p.m. Monday briefing on the CRA and FHA issues in Room 2222 Rayburn House Office Building with several Members of Congress and staff of housing and banking committees.

"We learned a long time ago that we care more about the neighborhoods where we live than anybody else," said Gale Cincotta, NPA National Chairperson. "Many public officials do respect the expertise of neighborhood residents when it comes to deciding what's best for our own communities. Those who don't, may get a taste of some NPA medicine."
