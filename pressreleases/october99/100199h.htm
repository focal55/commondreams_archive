Union of Concerned Scientists: Lessons Learned from Japanese Nuclear Accident
 
WASHINGTON - October 1 - The following was released today by the Union of Concerned Scientists:

Nuclear accidents can happen at U.S. nuclear fuel processing plants:

"U.S. nuclear fuel factories use very similar technology to those in Japan, with nearly identical controls against accidents. The Japanese accident clearly shows that safety measures on this dangerous technology are not foolproof," said David Lochbaum, Nuclear Safety Engineer for the Union of Concerned Scientists.

The best way to prevent a similar accident in the United States:

"We should assume an accident can occur. This mindset forces reasonable precautions, such as the development of contingency measures and emergency plans. These steps minimize the chances of an accident, lessen the duration of an accident, and limit the consequences to plant workers and the public from an accident. An emphasis on safety is the most cost-effective insurance premium available," said Lochbaum.

Protecting human health in the event of an accident:

"There has been a debate in the United States for over a decade as to whether potassium iodine pills should be stockpiled in case of a nuclear accident. Following Chernobyl, Poland distributed potassium iodine pills to children. Russia did not. The incidence of thyroid cancer among children in Poland is much, much lower than in Russian children. The pills are absorbed by the thyroid, thus blocking the intake of radioactive iodine gas released in a nuclear accident. The United States has been debating this health issue for years. The debate protects no one. This issue should not be debated -- it should be resolved," said Lochbaum.

On President Clinton's call for a safety review:

"In the past two years, we've seen almost daily reports of broken safety equipment and procedures not being followed at U.S. nuclear fuel processing plants. These reports were made to the U.S. Department of Energy and to the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission. President Clinton asked for a safety review to be performed by these same agencies, which have a track record of overlooking known safety problems. What we need is for the regulators to do their jobs," said Lochbaum.

------
David Lochbaum is one of the nation's top independent experts on nuclear power. He worked in the nuclear power industry for nearly 20 years before joining UCS in 1996. He has authored numerous reports, including "The Good, the Bad and the Ugly: A report on Safety in America's Nuclear Power Industry," "Three Mile Island's Puzzling Legacy," and the book "Nuclear Waste Disposal Crisis."
