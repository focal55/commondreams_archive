Missile Defense Test Results Insufficient for Decision to Deploy; Only Three Tests Scheduled before June 2000 Judgement on NMD
 
WASHINGTON - October 1 - ON OCTOBER 2, the first intercept test of the proposed National Missile Defense (NMD) system is scheduled to take place, when a target missile is fired from Vandenberg Air Base in California and a ground-based interceptor, launched from Kwajalein Missile Range in the Marshall Islands, will attempt to hit it

This will be the first of three tests scheduled to take place before June 2000, when the Pentagon will determine whether those results justify certifying the NMD as ready on technical grounds to deploy. The Pentagon has said if one of the three succeeds, that would justify a provisional decision to deploy. Shortly after the Pentagon’s assessment, the Clinton Administration will decide whether to deploy the NMD based on four factors: technical feasibility; cost of the system; the status of the threat; and the impact on arms control and relations with Russia.

"This test is fine as an early development test. But the fact is that the Pentagon has set the bar so low that it could move toward deployment next summer based on the results of this test alone. This tells you that politics--not technology--is driving this program," said Tom Collina, Director of the Arms Control Program at the Union of Concerned Scientists.

This first test will not be a complete system appraisal, but will focus on the Exoatmospheric Kill Vehicle (EKV), the "bullet" that is intended to hit the target missile. The booster missile that will launch the EKV is not the one that will be used in the actual system, but instead a surrogate that will be guided by a GPS locator beacon on the target warhead. According to the Pentagon, once the EKV is within range ("in the basket"), it will home in on the target by itself.

Pentagon officials have said this test will have one mock warhead (the target) and one balloon decoy. The interceptor's temperature-sensitive infrared sensors will have to differentiate between the warhead and the balloon. Only one of the three tests planned for before next June will be a "integrated full system" test, where all system elements work together, and all three will use surrogate components.

"The idea that you can decide to build the most complicated system ever designed by humankind after only three partial tests doesn't strain credulity, it breaks it," said Charles Ferguson, Senior Research Analyst at the Federation of American Scientists. "As a former US naval officer, I can attest that even the simplest systems on US warships are tested more times than the proposed NMD system before making a deployment decision."

"In duck hunting terms, they are shooting at a mallard from about ten yards away, and there's an unpainted cardboard decoy nearby. That's an understandable first test for one component, but you can't base a decision to deploy on it," said Stephen Young, Deputy Director of the Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers.

The Coalition to Reduce Nuclear Dangers is a non-partisan alliance of 17 nuclear non-proliferation organizations based in Washington, D.C. *The views expressed in this advisory do not necessarily reflect those of every member organization of the Coalition.
