Greenpeace To Send International Team To Japan Nuclear Accident Site
 
AMSTERDAM - October 1 - Greenpeace will send a team to Japan, at the request of Greenpeace Japan, to attempt to gather further information about the nuclear accident at the uranium processing facility at the Tokaimura reprocessing plant, in Japan. The environment group said the three person team; one from Holland and two from Germany, would leave Amsterdam today for Tokyo. "We will be attempting to get a clearer picture of the extent of radiation levels and contamination in the areas surrounding the plant," said Diederik Samsom, a nuclear engineer and qualified radioprotection officer from Holland, who will be leading the Greenpeace team. "To date there seems to be a lack of clear information from the company (JCO, a subsidiary of Sumitomo) and the Japanese government on these questions," said Samsom. "Safety will be our highest priority, as it has been during previous nuclear contamination monitoring around the nuclear reprocessing plants at La Hague in France and Sellafield in the UK where Greenpeace has measured levels of radioactivity being discharged into the ocean by the two plants," said Samson. "Greenpeace hopes that its role as independent watchdog, can complement the work of regulatory authorities and non-government organizations in Japan."

According to an International Atomic Energy Assessment (IAEA), the level of [contamination] at Tokaimura reaches grade 4 on the IAEA classification system. The Three-Mile Island accident was graded 5, while Chernobyl attained the top level 7. Greenpeace Japan held a demonstration in front of the Science and Technology Agency (STA) in Tokyo at 10 am to expose the lack of security inherent in all nuclear industry's activities. "Yesterday's accident at the Tokaimura nuclear facility is the devastating evidence that the nuclear industry worldwide is unable to apply the high standards of safety they claim to adopt", said Sanae Shida, Executive Director of Greenpeace Japan. Meanwhile the controversial cargo of plutonium-MOX fuel, manufactured by British Nuclear Fuel Ltd (BNFL) in the UK, arrived this morning at the Takahama reactor. Greenpeace warned that loading the MOX fuel into the Takahama reactor would increase the risk of nuclear accidents. "BNFL's doctoring of safety checking data was revealed by the UK's Independent newspaper only two weeks ago and today Japan witnesses the most serious accident in the history of the Japanese nuclear industry. One nuclear tragedy is enough for Japan, we do not need any more plutonium". The nuclear accident occurred at 10h35 am, 30th September, at a test facility in the JCO Ltd.'s uranium processing plant located in Tokaimura, Ibaraki Prefecture, on the Pacific coast of Japan. It is the center of Japan's nuclear research and includes reprocessing facilities, enrichment plant, and laser isotope enrichment. The facility is a commercial plant where enriched UF6 gas is converted into UO2 powder for further processing. Instead of loading batches of 2.4kg of uranium in each lot, at least 16kg of uranium dioxide was processed at a time, which is the likely cause of the accident. Initially, three workers were contaminated and one of them was taken to the National Institute of radiological Sciences in Chiba City in critical conditions. The latest figures show that 39 people, including civilians, have been contaminated by radiation. Initially, an atmospheric radiation count of 0.84 millisievert/hour 10,000 times of the annual dose limit) was monitored. The Japanese Science and Technology Agency (STA) has announced that it is a criticality accident in an uncontrolled nuclear chain reaction. The local government has evacuated the 350 meters area around the plant while all the people living in a 10 kilometers range have been asked to stay indoors. The Tokaimura site has a range of nuclear facilities, including a plutonium reprocessing plant that suffered a fire and explosion in April 1997, which at the time was Japan's most serious accident. The operators of the plant are currently considering reopening the plant later this year, despite having no need for any more plutonium. Japan currently has a stockpile of over 5 tons of plutonium inland, with a further 30 tons in Europe, waiting to be shipped to Japan.
