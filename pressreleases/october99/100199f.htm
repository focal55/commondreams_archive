Arts, First Amendment Supporters Rally At Brooklyn Museum
 
WASHINGTON - October 1 - Artists will join forces with First Amendment supporters in front of the Brooklyn Museum of Art tonight, united in their anger over Mayor Giuliani's decision to cut off the museum's funding because it refused to cancel an exhibition he objects to.

Participating in tonight's rally are artist Chuck Close; Joseph Amrhein, the owner of Pierogi 2000 gallery in Brooklyn and a champion of young minority artists; artist and MacArthur Fellow Pepon Osorio; playwright Wendy Wasserstein; actress Susan Sarandon; actress and former NEA chair Jane Alexander; performance artist Reno; and others.

The Mayor's censorious blow takes aim against the city's second largest museum, which houses a rich and diverse collection that the New York Times has called "some of the greatest works of Egyptian, Asian, American, American Indian and European art." Giuliani's move is widely viewed as a bid for Catholic and conservative upstate votes in his race for the U.S. Senate.

"Mayor Giuliani has taken a political hammer to two great pillars of New York's cultural life - the museum and the First Amendment," said Barbara Handman, Vice President and New York Director of People For the American Way Foundation and a 1998 winner of the National Medal for the Arts for her lifelong arts advocacy. "The mayor is wrong about the First Amendment and wrong about the works he wants to condemn."

The target of Giuliani's judgment, a painting by British-born Roman Catholic Chris Ofili called "Holy Virgin Mary," illustrates the perils of political art criticism. Ofili's use of elephant dung in this painting, as in his other works, is inspired by his explorations of the culture of Nigeria, where both his parents were born, and where, according to the Washington Post, "excrement is often incorporated into religious artwork." He explained his use of elephant dung in his paintings to the New York Times, "It's a way of raising the paintings up from the ground and giving them a feeling that they've come from the earth rather than simply being hung on a wall."

"An artist's work is both that artist's vision and a mirror that reflects ourselves - complete with our beliefs, hopes, and biases," said Ronald Feldman, PFAWF Board Member and the owner of Ronald Feldman Fine Arts, Inc. "Ofili's painting, which the Mayor has condemned sight unseen, will be understood by others as a depiction of the enduring belief in and search for holiness in the midst of a debased and debasing culture."

Giuliani's action against the museum is being denounced as an unconstitutional abuse of government power to ban certain kinds of artistic expression. At the same time, it also violates the principle of religious freedom, since it seeks to ban works that some take to be critical of religion.

"The same constitutional principles that forbid government from establishing any religion also mean that government must not create a favored status only for artistic expression that reflects a conventional or orthodox view of religion," said Handman. "The Mayor is free to speak out about his own religious beliefs and interpretations, but he must not use the power of his office to enforce them on the rest of us."
