War Resisters Declare Third Annual "Pentagon-Free Day"
 
NEW YORK - October 1 - In cities and rural communities across the country, anti-militarist organizers will declare A Day Without the Pentagon and protest the growing militarization of our culture, foreign policies that favor military solutions to diplomacy, and domestic policies that favor the military bureaucracy over human needs spending.

Many of the actions taking place across the country as part of this year's A Day Without the Pentagon campaign—now in its third year—focus attention on military recruiters. The Pentagon is directing more money into its recruiting drives and expanding its presence in local communities because the Army, Navy, and Air Force are falling short of their recruiting goals. The military focuses on poor communities and communities of color, in the hope that young people from those areas will view enlistment favorably because they have fewer civilian opportunities—a practice dubbed the "poverty draft."

"Across the country, in high schools, shopping centers and playgrounds, recruiters try to lure young people into the military with promises of employment and educational opportunities," said Chris Ney, Disarmament Coordinator for the 76-year-old War Resisters League. "They don't tell young people they're joining an institution that curtails their legal rights and that can send them on kill-or-be-killed missions."

The War Resisters League argues that the military's expanded recruiting efforts will allow the Pentagon to extend its influence over educational and other organizations serving young people. Schools that refuse to allow military recruiters access to school buildings and student records face funding cuts. Some schools seek to limit the expression of alternative views that challenge the recruiters' promises, despite laws guaranteeing equal access for anti-military opinions.

A recently-publicized practice allowing soldiers to return to their old neighborhoods shortly after completing basic training and act as recruiters is a deceptive, and potentially dangerous, recruiting gimmick, say A Day Without the Pentagon activists. "When the entire country is concerned about community and school violence, shouldn't we challenge the notion that soldiers are good role models for our children?" asked Joanne Sheehan, Chair of War Resisters International and one of the protest organizers. "These soldiers can't tell recruits about the reality of military life because they have not experienced that reality. We want young people to consider all of their options, including their potential for success without the use of violence."

Organizers calculate that current and past military spending totals $1.7 billion per day. If redirected to human needs, one day's Pentagon spending could build 200 new schools or provide supportive housing for 136,000 homeless people or shelter and counsel 56,000 battered women or offer Pell grants for 1 million college students.

Ney added, "In the past year we've seen the military turn a humanitarian disaster in Kosovo/a into a disaster, continue to bomb and starve the people of Iraq, and seek the largest increase in military spending since Reagan was president. Meanwhile our nation's schools have become a bloodbath. Its time to break the cycle of violence."
